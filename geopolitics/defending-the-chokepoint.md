# Defending the Chokepoint

### How the Iran War Strengthens America's Adversaries

---

Iran is dangerous. This is not in dispute. A regime that massacred thousands of its own protesters in January, that has spent decades funding proxy networks from Lebanon to Yemen, that was inching toward nuclear breakout capability — this is not a government anyone should be naive about. The hawks who warned for years that Iran posed a genuine threat to regional stability were not wrong about the diagnosis.

They were wrong about the treatment.

One month into Operation Epic Fury, the largest U.S. military campaign since Afghanistan, the strategic ledger is coming into focus. And it does not read the way its architects promised. The war has not made America safer, has not stabilized the Middle East, and has not weakened the adversarial coalition it was supposed to deter. It has done the opposite on all three counts. The question is no longer whether Iran was a problem. It is whether the United States just solved that problem for Russia, China, and Iran itself — at its own expense.

---

## The Beneficiaries

**Russia.** The most immediate and measurable winner. Moscow built its 2026 federal budget on oil at roughly $60 a barrel. The war sent Brent crude past $120. According to the KSE Institute, Russia stands to gain between $45 billion and $151 billion in additional budget revenues this year, depending on how long the conflict lasts. In the central scenario — a three-month war — the windfall reaches $97 billion in extra budget revenues, exceeding Russia's entire 2025 fiscal deficit. This is not an abstraction. It is the capital that sustains Russian military operations in Ukraine. Every week the Strait of Hormuz stays closed, the Kremlin's war chest refills. American munitions expended over Tehran are, through the intermediate mechanism of oil prices, financing Russian artillery shells fired at Kharkiv.

Nor is Moscow merely a passive beneficiary. U.S. officials have confirmed that Russia is providing Iran with satellite imagery and intelligence on the positions of American warships and aircraft. Iran's foreign minister has not denied this, stating that military cooperation with Russia and China continues. The relationship between Moscow, Beijing, and Tehran — once a loose alignment of convenience — is hardening into structured coordination under the pressure of a common American adversary.

**China.** Beijing's gains are quieter but potentially more consequential. Start with the immediate: China was Iran's largest oil customer before the war, purchasing roughly 13% of its crude imports at discounted prices under a 25-year, $400 billion cooperation agreement. Chinese-linked tankers have moved at least 11.7 million barrels through the strait since late February, many going dark to avoid tracking. Iran has explicitly granted Chinese vessels preferential passage — and demanded that other ships pay tolls in yuan.

This is where the strategic damage deepens. Deutsche Bank published a note last week warning that the conflict could be "the catalyst for erosion in petrodollar dominance and the beginnings of the petroyuan." Lloyd's List confirmed that at least two vessels have already paid transit fees in Chinese currency, brokered by a Chinese maritime services company. Iran's parliament is drafting legislation to formalize the toll system. For a country that has sought for years to internationalize the yuan in energy trade, the war is delivering what no amount of diplomacy could: a live, functioning proof of concept for non-dollar oil settlement at the world's most critical chokepoint.

Beyond currency, the Pentagon is offering Beijing a free masterclass. As the Peterson Institute's analysis noted, observing U.S. naval operations in the Gulf in real time — carrier movements, missile intercept patterns, logistics flows, interceptor-to-drone cost ratios — is strategically invaluable as China contemplates scenarios involving the Taiwan Strait. The more the United States deploys and reveals its operational playbook in the Gulf, the better calibrated Beijing's own military planning becomes.

**Iran.** The regime that was supposed to be destroyed is instead discovering leverage it didn't know it had. Bloomberg Economics' Middle East lead observed that Tehran has been "taken aback by how successful its Hormuz strategy has been — by how cheap and comparatively easy it is to hold the global economy hostage." Iran's entire defense budget is less than $10 billion annually — roughly 1% of America's. Yet with naval mines, coastal missile batteries, and drone swarms, it has taken 20% of the world's oil supply offline and triggered the largest emergency reserve release in IEA history. The interceptor-to-drone cost ratio runs approximately 106 to 1. The asymmetry is devastating.

The war has also handed the regime a domestic rallying narrative. The same population that was in the streets protesting their government in January is now subject to foreign bombardment — a dynamic that historically consolidates support for embattled regimes, not weakens it. And Iran's new leverage is durable: even after the war ends, every state in the world now knows that Hormuz can be weaponized cheaply, effectively, and with global consequences. That knowledge does not get un-learned.

---

## The Dollar Under Fire

The petrodollar system — born from the 1974 Saudi-American bargain in which Riyadh priced oil in dollars and recycled surpluses into U.S. assets in exchange for security guarantees — is arguably America's most strategically valuable financial architecture. It underpins dollar reserve status, keeps U.S. borrowing costs low, and gives Washington outsized leverage through sanctions.

The Iran war is stress-testing every pillar of that arrangement simultaneously. The U.S. security umbrella for Gulf infrastructure has been visibly punctured — bases attacked, oil fields hit, desalination plants struck. The maritime security guarantee for Hormuz has failed. Gulf economies that were diversifying into tourism and finance hubs are watching those investments burn. Deutsche Bank's strategist warned that damage to Gulf economies "could encourage an unwind in their foreign asset savings held largely in dollars."

And at the choke point itself, the yuan is flowing. Iran's toll-booth system is crude — literally and figuratively — but it is establishing a precedent. Ships are submitting to IRGC vetting, providing documentation, crew manifests, and cargo details, then receiving escort through Iranian territorial waters. The currency of settlement is Chinese. This is happening now, not in a scenario paper.

The irony is acute. The petrodollar was built on the premise that American military power would guarantee the free flow of Gulf oil. The war was launched, in part, to demonstrate that power. Instead, it has demonstrated its limits — and created the conditions under which alternatives to the dollar system are being built in real time.

---

## The Losers

The accounting is brutal. The Pentagon has requested $200 billion from Congress — unprecedented for a single supplemental. The war is burning roughly $1 billion per day. CSIS estimated $3.7 billion in the first 100 hours alone. The Penn Wharton Budget Model projects total economic impact reaching $210 billion. And Rep. Thomas Massie asked the question no one in the administration has answered: "Is this the first $200 billion? Does this turn into a trillion?"

To pay for it, House Republicans are exploring cuts to federal health care spending — on top of the nearly $1 trillion in Medicaid reductions already enacted. The primary target is eliminating ACA cost-sharing reductions for low-income Americans. The Congressional Budget Office estimates this would save $30 billion while increasing the uninsured by 300,000. Americans are being asked to pay for a war that over 60% of the country opposes by giving up health coverage.

Europe faces its second energy crisis in four years. Gas storage entered this conflict at just 30% capacity after a harsh winter. The ECB has postponed rate cuts and raised inflation forecasts. Germany and Italy face technical recession. The war is also choking supply chains beyond energy: helium shortages threaten semiconductor and medical device manufacturing; pharmaceutical supply chains dependent on petrochemicals face price spikes; an Iran-linked cyberattack crippled medical device manufacturer Stryker's operations.

The Global South is paying the steepest relative price. The Philippines declared a state of emergency. Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nigeria, and Vietnam face severe fuel shortages. Fertilizer prices have surged 30%, threatening food security in import-dependent economies. These are the countries that have no strategic petroleum reserves, no alternative energy sources at scale, and no political voice in the decision to launch the war.

---

## The Structural Trap

Grant the hawks their best case. Assume every Iranian nuclear site is destroyed, the IRGC is degraded beyond reconstitution, and the regime collapses into something less hostile. Even then, the war does not solve the underlying problem.

The lesson of Hormuz is now proven and permanent: a state with a $10 billion defense budget can hold the $100 trillion global economy hostage by controlling a narrow waterway through which fossil fuels flow. This vulnerability is structural, not contingent on who controls Tehran. It exists because the global economy runs on a fuel that must be extracted from a handful of geographies and shipped through physical chokepoints. No amount of military spending changes that geometry.

The war has also demonstrated a second structural truth: the U.S. military, for all its overwhelming power, cannot simultaneously project force and guarantee the commercial safety of the waterways it is fighting in. The Strait of Hormuz is both the battlefield and the supply line. You cannot bomb a country and keep its shipping lanes open at the same time. This is not a failure of execution. It is a contradiction embedded in the strategic logic.

---

## The Alternative

There is a class of investment that addresses every vulnerability this war has exposed: renewable energy. Not as a climate aspiration. As a national security strategy.

Half the war's direct cost — $100 billion — invested in domestic solar, wind, and battery storage would build approximately 100–125 GW of new capacity. That is three times the United States' annual solar installations. It would create over 575,000 jobs, predominantly in Republican-voting states. It would generate electricity for 25 to 30 years. And it would return $3 to $8 for every dollar invested, according to the International Renewable Energy Agency.

More importantly, it would devalue the weapon Iran just discovered. Every gigawatt of renewable capacity is a gigawatt that does not depend on tankers navigating a war zone. A world that runs on domestically generated electricity from sun and wind is a world in which Hormuz is a geographic feature, not a strategic chokepoint. The strait's leverage exists precisely because the global economy is addicted to what flows through it. Reduce the addiction, and you reduce the leverage — permanently, without firing a shot.

This is not speculative. It is already happening. Ember's data shows that since the war began, all installed solar and wind worldwide has avoided roughly 330 TWh of gas-fired generation, saving over $40 billion. China — which has invested massively in clean power over the past decade — is better cushioned from the gas market volatility this war has caused than any Western economy. Beijing saw this coming. Washington did not.

Deutsche Bank's own note, after warning of the petroyuan, concluded with a quietly devastating observation: the biggest threat to dollar dominance is not China's currency. It is "a permanent shift away from globally traded oil and gas." In other words, the best defense of the petrodollar is not a war to keep oil flowing. It is a transition that makes the oil irrelevant.

---

## The Measure of Strategy

The measure of strategy is not whether you can destroy a threat. It is whether your response leaves you stronger or weaker than before.

By that standard, Operation Epic Fury is failing on its own terms. The United States is spending $200 billion it does not have, cutting health care to fund it, depleting strategic reserves, revealing its operational playbook to China, financing Russia's war in Ukraine through oil prices, accelerating the petroyuan, consolidating the very regime it sought to topple, and demonstrating to every future adversary that a narrow waterway and cheap drones are sufficient to hold the global economy hostage.

Iran is a problem. This war is not the solution. It is a $200 billion experiment in making every problem worse while creating several new ones. The chokepoint is being defended. The question is: defended for whom?

---

*The author holds that Iran's nuclear ambitions and regional destabilization programs represent genuine threats to international security. The argument presented here is not that the threat is imaginary, but that the chosen response is counterproductive — enriching adversaries, eroding American financial architecture, and deepening the structural vulnerability it purports to address. A fraction of the war's cost, redirected toward energy independence, would accomplish what military force cannot: making the chokepoint irrelevant.*
