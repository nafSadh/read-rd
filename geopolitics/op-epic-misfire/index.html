Operation Epic Misfire

Defending the Chokepoint — How the Iran War Strengthens America’s Adversaries

One month into the largest U.S. military campaign since Afghanistan, the strategic ledger does not read the way its architects promised. Russia is banking a $45–151 billion oil windfall that funds the Ukraine war. China is gaining real-time intelligence on U.S. naval operations while the petroyuan emerges at the Strait of Hormuz. Iran itself has discovered that a $10 billion defense budget can hold the $100 trillion global economy hostage.

This is not a pacifist argument. It is a strategic one. Sun Tzu taught that the supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting — not because fighting is immoral, but because it is expensive and uncertain. Churchill championed radar, blockades, and Bletchley Park as weapons that multiplied power without squandering it. The question is never whether a threat is real. It is whether the response leaves you stronger or weaker.

Position

Iran’s nuclear ambitions and regional destabilization are genuine threats. The war is the wrong weapon — enriching adversaries, eroding petrodollar architecture, and deepening the structural energy vulnerability it purports to address. Half the war’s cost in renewable energy would accomplish what military force cannot: making the chokepoint irrelevant.
