I
I · The Leak 3

A Default Set to Public

Anthropic uses an off-the-shelf CMS for its blog. Assets uploaded to it are public by default unless a user explicitly changes the setting.

Two security researchers — Roy Paz (LayerX Security) and Alexandre Pauwels (Cambridge) — discovered a publicly searchable data store linked to the blog.

Inside: close to 3,000 unpublished assets — images, PDFs, audio files, and crucially, a structured draft blog post announcing a new model.

Anthropic attributed the exposure to "human error" in CMS configuration and pulled public access after Fortune notified them.

I · The Leak 4

What the Draft Revealed

The key document was a structured draft blog post — complete with headings and a publication date — for a planned product launch.

It named the model Claude Mythos and described a new tier called Capybara — larger and more capable than Opus.

The draft described Mythos as Anthropic's "most powerful AI model ever developed" and flagged unprecedented cybersecurity risks.

Also exposed: details of an invite-only CEO summit in an English countryside manor, and other internal materials including an employee's parental leave document.

Accidental or strategic?

Some observers note the leak's timing is suspiciously convenient — free hype ahead of a potential IPO, similar to OpenAI's Q*-era leaks.

II
II · The Model 6

A Fourth Tier: Capybara

Until now, Anthropic offered three tiers: Haiku (small/fast), Sonnet (balanced), and Opus (largest/most capable).

Capybara sits above Opus — the draft describes it as "larger and more intelligent than our Opus models, which were, until now, our most powerful."

Mythos is the specific model name within the Capybara tier. Training is reported as complete.

The model is extremely compute-intensive and expensive to run. Anthropic says it's working on efficiency before general release.

II · Capabilities 7

Dramatically Higher Scores

Anthropic's draft said Mythos gets "dramatically higher scores" versus Opus 4.6 on coding, academic reasoning, and cybersecurity.

The company confirmed it has "meaningful advances in reasoning, coding, and cybersecurity."

The model was described as "by far the most powerful AI model we've ever developed" — language Anthropic doesn't typically use publicly.

Step change, not increment

Anthropic's spokesperson called it "a step change" — suggesting a genuine capability jump, not just an iteration on the Opus line.

III · Cyber Risk 9

Far Ahead in Cyber Capabilities

The leaked draft warned Mythos is "currently far ahead of any other AI model in cyber capabilities."

It said the model "presages an upcoming wave of models that can exploit vulnerabilities in ways that far outpace the efforts of defenders."

Anthropic is privately briefing senior government officials that large-scale AI-assisted cyberattacks become substantially more likely once models at this capability level reach wide distribution. [Axios]

A Dark Reading poll found that 48% of cybersecurity professionals now rank agentic AI as the #1 attack vector for 2026 — above deepfakes and everything else.

III · Context 10

Not the First Warning

In February 2026, OpenAI released GPT-5.3-Codex and classified it as "high capability" for cybersecurity tasks — the first model to receive that rating under its Preparedness Framework.

The same week, Anthropic's Opus 4.6 demonstrated an ability to surface previously unknown vulnerabilities in production codebases — acknowledged as a dual-use capability.

In November 2025, Anthropic reported that a Chinese state-sponsored group had been using Claude Code to infiltrate ~30 organizations before detection.

The pattern

Each generation of frontier models has pushed the cyber risk threshold further. Mythos appears to be the biggest single jump yet.

How the leak moved markets and fits the competitive landscape
IV
IV · Market 12

Cybersecurity Stocks Slump

CrowdStrike dropped 7.5% before lunch on the day after the leak was published. Other cybersecurity names sold off broadly.

The fear: frontier AI models that can find and exploit vulnerabilities at scale could commoditize offensive capability, overwhelming defenders.

However, stocks bounced sharply the following Monday. Palo Alto Networks CEO Nikesh Arora bought $10M in shares — a confidence signal that the defense side of AI also benefits.

Two-sided trade

The bull case: if Mythos-class models make attacks more likely, enterprises must modernize defenses immediately — growing the market for security vendors.

IV · Competition 13

The Frontier Race: Q2 2026

OpenAI reportedly finished pre-training its own frontier model, codenamed "Spud", around the same time as the Mythos leak.

Both Anthropic and OpenAI are reportedly positioning for IPOs later this year — making capability announcements strategically important.

Meanwhile, Z.AI's GLM 5.1 offers 94% of Opus 4.6's coding performance at $10/month — open-source, built on Huawei hardware. The cost floor is dropping fast.

OpenAI also updated Codex with new plugins for Slack, Figma, GitHub, and Gmail — competing directly with Claude Code's ecosystem.

The pattern for the rest of 2026: frontier labs racing to demonstrate capability while managing political and regulatory consequences.

Takeaways 14

What Matters Here

1. Capability jump is real. This isn't incremental — a new tier above Opus, with Anthropic using language like "step change" and "by far the most powerful."

2. Cyber risk is the headline. Anthropic is self-reporting that its own model outpaces defenders. Government briefings are happening. This is new territory for responsible disclosure by an AI lab about its own model.

3. Release will be staged. Cyber defense orgs get early access. General availability depends on efficiency improvements and safety assessments.

4. IPO-adjacent timing. Whether the leak was accidental or not, Mythos lands in a competitive moment where both Anthropic and OpenAI are racing to demonstrate frontier leadership ahead of potential public offerings.

5. The cost of frontier is rising. Capybara is described as more expensive than Opus. The gap between frontier and commodity AI is widening even as open-source closes from below.
