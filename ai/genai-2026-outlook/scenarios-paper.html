Three Scenarios for AI in 2027

FORWARD-LOOKING PAPER · MARCH 2026

Abstract

The GenAI 2026 survey reveals a landscape of genuine uncertainty. Production agents execute ≤10 steps while AI 2027[1]AI 2027 → predicts full SWE jobs in 18 months. 78% of organizations deploy AI while 56% of CEOs report no gains. Investment hits $500B while project abandonment accelerates. These contradictions don't resolve into a single prediction. Instead, they suggest three distinct scenarios — each consistent with current evidence, each leading to a fundamentally different world by 2028. This paper sketches those scenarios, identifies the signals that would confirm each, and argues that the "middle scenario" framing most leaders default to may be the least likely outcome.

Why Scenarios, Not Predictions

The six tensions identified in the survey (adoption vs. value, no disruption vs. undertow, use vs. trust, scale vs. efficiency, bubble vs. boom, autonomy timelines) all share a common structure: current evidence supports both sides. This means single-point predictions are dishonest — they assert confidence that doesn't exist. Scenario planning acknowledges the uncertainty and asks: what would we do differently if we knew which scenario was playing out?

Scenario 1: The Acceleration

Thesis: AI capability improvement is exponential, and the installation phase transitions to deployment faster than historical analogies suggest.

What happens:

  • Agent capability jumps discontinuously. By mid-2027, agents reliably execute 50+ steps across diverse enterprise environments. The 10-step production limit of 2026 proves to be a temporary bottleneck driven by integration immaturity, not fundamental capability limits.
  • MCP matures rapidly. Enterprise-grade auth, observability, and multi-tenancy ship by end of 2026. The 1,000+ community servers become 10,000+. Integration — the #1 bottleneck in 2026 — becomes dramatically easier.
  • The adoption-value gap closes. The 34% of organizations "reimagining business through AI" (Deloitte[2]Deloitte →) grows to 60%+ as agent infrastructure matures and best practices disseminate. The 42% project abandonment rate drops below 20%.
  • AI-accelerated AI R&D creates a positive feedback loop (the AI 2027 thesis). Each improvement in AI capability accelerates the next. Research productivity increases 3-5× for AI labs.
  • Employment effects become visible. Entry-level hiring in AI-exposed occupations drops 20-30%. The career ladder damage the Dallas Fed[3]Dallas Fed → identified becomes measurable. Young tech worker unemployment rises further.

Supporting evidence from the survey:

  • SWE-bench went from 4% to 72% in one year — the capability curve is steep
  • 9× AI company revenue growth — the market is real, not speculative
  • MCP adopted by OpenAI[4]OpenAI →, Google, Linux Foundation in 16 months — infrastructure maturation can be fast
  • 10×/year cost deflation — each price tier opens new viable applications

Contradicting evidence:

  • 10-step production limit, 85% custom-built — integration is hard for structural reasons
  • 42% project abandonment, 56% CEO no-gains — organizational transformation is slow regardless of technology
  • Historical precedent: PCs took a decade, internet took two decades — why would AI be different?

What it would look like by end of 2027:

  • AI revenue exceeds $100B across leading companies
  • 2-3 major enterprise categories (customer service, coding, document processing) are predominantly AI-handled
  • Stock market AI premium validated by revenue, not just expectations
  • Significant political backlash from displaced workers, particularly in clerical and entry-level tech roles
  • The EU AI Act[5]EU AI Act → requires emergency amendments to keep pace

Leading indicators (watch for these in 2026):

  • Agent production step counts exceeding 30 across multiple enterprises
  • Entry-level tech hiring rates declining 10%+ year-over-year
  • AI company revenue maintaining 5×+ annual growth
  • MCP shipping enterprise auth and observability features

Scenario 2: The Plateau

Thesis: AI capability continues improving but organizational adoption remains slow, creating a long "valley of disappointment" where the technology works but enterprise returns are modest.

What happens:

  • Models keep improving on benchmarks, but the gains don't translate to proportionally better production outcomes. SWE-bench goes from 72% to 85%, but the last 15% involves edge cases that matter disproportionately in production.
  • The adoption-value gap persists. 80-90% of organizations use AI, but enterprise value remains concentrated in the top 15-20%. The "66% in limbo" (organizations that adopted but haven't transformed) stays stuck.
  • Agent deployments improve incrementally. Production step counts rise from 10 to 15-20 but don't achieve the 50+ needed for genuine autonomy. The 40% cancellation rate holds — not because the technology fails but because organizational change is hard.
  • Cost deflation continues, making AI cheap but not necessarily valuable. The commodity dynamics from S1 play out: model capability becomes table stakes, and competitive advantage shifts entirely to integration, data quality, and workflow design — which are organizational capabilities, not AI capabilities.
  • Employment effects are modest and gradual. The Yale[6]Yale → "no disruption" finding holds for another 2-3 years. The career ladder erodes slowly rather than breaking suddenly.

Supporting evidence:

  • PwC[7]PwC →: 56% of CEOs with no gains after 2+ years — organizational inertia is strong
  • Only 34% reimagining business (Deloitte) — most organizations lack the capability for transformation
  • Skills, not technology, are #1 barrier — this is a training and change management problem with multi-year timelines
  • Historical precedent: technology adoption follows decades-long S-curves, not vertical hockey sticks

Contradicting evidence:

  • 9× revenue growth and 10× cost deflation create economic forces that are hard to resist
  • MCP's 16-month adoption trajectory shows infrastructure can mature fast
  • AI 2027's self-accelerating R&D thesis — if AI improves AI development, the incremental assumption breaks

What it would look like by end of 2027:

  • AI revenue $40-60B across leaders (strong but not transformative)
  • Most enterprises still "bolting AI onto existing processes"
  • Stock market AI premium gradually compressed as revenue growth decelerates to 2-3×
  • Employment effects limited to specific demographics (young tech, clerical)
  • Regulatory frameworks have time to mature — EU AI Act enforcement is orderly
  • The discourse shifts from "AI will change everything" to "AI is a useful tool"

Leading indicators:

  • AI company revenue growth decelerating to 3-4× (still strong, but decelerating)
  • Project abandonment rate holding at 35-45%
  • Agent production step counts at 15-20 (better but not transformative)
  • Entry-level hiring rates stable or declining <5%

Scenario 3: The Reckoning

Thesis: The investment-value gap triggers a market correction, forcing a painful but ultimately productive reset.

What happens:

  • The $500B capex wave hits diminishing returns. Several hyperscaler data center projects are mothballed or delayed. The power constraint (S1) proves more binding than expected — multi-year construction timelines mean facilities that were greenlit in 2024 won't be operational until 2027-2028, by which time the efficiency gains from S1 may make them oversized.
  • Project abandonment accelerates. S&P Global's 42% rises to 60%+. Enterprise AI budgets get cut 20-30% as CFOs demand ROI that doesn't materialize. The "AI winter" label starts appearing in trade press.
  • AI company revenues plateau. OpenAI's $15B target proves aspirational — actual revenue is $8-10B, strong by any normal measure but insufficient to justify $100B+ valuations. A major AI company acquisition or downround signals market repricing.
  • The bubble thesis is validated — partially. It's not that AI doesn't work; it's that the investment-to-return ratio was unsustainable at peak 2025-2026 levels. The correction is 30-50% in AI-focused equities, painful but not catastrophic.
  • Paradoxically, the correction accelerates mature adoption. Companies that survive the reset have clear ROI, proven use cases, and sustainable economics. The "agent washing" vendors disappear. MCP consolidates around proven enterprise patterns. The technology is better than ever; the expectations finally match reality.

Supporting evidence:

  • 42% project abandonment and accelerating — the trend is already negative
  • 56% of CEOs with no gains — the disconnect between investment and returns is wide
  • Gartner[8]Gartner →: 40% agent cancellation — the most hyped category is the most vulnerable
  • Historical precedent: dot-com crash (2000-2002), blockchain winter (2018-2020) — technology survived, valuations didn't

Contradicting evidence:

  • Unlike dot-com, AI companies have real revenue (9× growth) and real utility (78% adoption)
  • 10×/year cost deflation makes AI cheaper regardless of market sentiment
  • AI's utility is proven at the individual level (coding +67%, decisions 28→47%) — this doesn't disappear in a correction
  • The enterprise survey data shows investment intent holding strong (KPMG[9]KPMG →: two-thirds wouldn't cut AI budgets in a downturn)

What it would look like by end of 2027:

  • AI-focused equities down 30-50% from 2026 peaks
  • 2-3 major AI startups acquired at down-rounds or shut down
  • Hyperscaler AI capex reduced 20-30% from peak
  • But: AI usage continues growing because it's genuinely useful and getting cheaper
  • The "valuable AI" companies (strong revenue, clear ROI) emerge stronger
  • The discourse shifts from "AI gold rush" to "AI maturity" — less exciting, more productive

Leading indicators:

  • Project abandonment rate rising above 50%
  • AI company revenue growth decelerating below 2×
  • A major AI company downround or distressed acquisition
  • Hyperscaler capex guidance reduced in earnings calls

Why the Middle Scenario May Be Least Likely

Most leaders default to Scenario 2 (Plateau) as the "reasonable" middle ground. But the dynamics of the current situation may not support a middle outcome. The installation-phase economics have a binary quality:

  • If AI-accelerated R&D works (AI improving AI development), the feedback loop pushes toward Scenario 1. Each improvement compounds. The capability curve steepens. Organizations that can absorb the change get pulled forward; those that can't fall further behind.
  • If the investment-value gap widens (abandonment rates rise, revenue growth stalls), market dynamics push toward Scenario 3. Capital pulls back. Valuations correct. Weaker companies fail.

The plateau requires a delicate balance: enough return to sustain investment, not enough to trigger the acceleration feedback loop. This is a narrow equilibrium that could be disrupted by a single major event — a discontinuous capability jump, a high-profile AI failure, a regulatory shock, or a market correction in unrelated sectors that triggers risk-off behavior in AI investments.

The practical implication: Plan for the plateau (Scenario 2) as your base case, but build optionality for both tails. Invest in integration infrastructure that pays off in any scenario. Build organizational capability for rapid scaling (useful if Scenario 1) and for efficient retrenchment (useful if Scenario 3). Monitor the leading indicators monthly.

Conclusion

The honest answer to "what happens next with AI?" is: we don't know, and anyone claiming certainty is selling something. What we do know is that the current state — extraordinary capability, massive investment, modest enterprise returns, accelerating abandonment, and genuine uncertainty about timelines — is unstable. It will resolve. The three scenarios described here represent the three directions that resolution could take.

The most useful thing a decision-maker can do is not to bet on a single scenario but to identify which leading indicators would confirm each one, monitor them, and adjust strategy accordingly. The worst thing to do is to plan for the plateau and be blindsided by either the acceleration or the reckoning.
