Three Scenarios for AI in 2027

FORWARD-LOOKING PAPER · MARCH 2026 · DATA SOURCES: 2023–2025

Abstract

The GenAI 2026 survey reveals a landscape of significant uncertainty. Production agents execute ≤10 steps in the majority of enterprise deployments,[*]Industry benchmark data on agent step counts in production; see Signal Dashboard for sourcing discussion. while AI 2027[1]AI 2027 → projects full software engineering role automation within 18 months — an outcome that would require either unprecedented capability gains or a significant narrowing of the professional scope of the role. 78% of organizations deploy AI while 56% of CEOs report no measurable gains. Investment reaches $500B while project abandonment accelerates. These contradictions do not resolve into a single prediction. Instead, they suggest three distinct scenarios — each consistent with current evidence, each leading to a fundamentally different outcome by 2028. This paper outlines those scenarios, identifies the signals that would confirm each, and contends that the "middle scenario" framing most frequently adopted as a planning default may represent the least likely equilibrium.

Why Scenarios, Not Predictions

The six tensions identified in the survey (adoption vs. value, no disruption vs. undertow, use vs. trust, scale vs. efficiency, bubble vs. boom, autonomy timelines) all share a common structure: current evidence supports both sides. This means single-point predictions are unreliable — they assert confidence that the evidence does not support. Scenario planning acknowledges the uncertainty and asks: what would we do differently if we knew which scenario was playing out?

Scenario 1: The Acceleration

Thesis: AI capability improvement follows an exponential trajectory, leading to a transition from the installation phase to widespread deployment at a pace exceeding historical technological analogies.

What happens:

  • Agent capability exhibits rapid, significant advancements. By mid-2027, agents are projected to reliably execute 50+ steps across diverse enterprise environments. The 10-step production limit observed in 2026 is hypothesized within this scenario to be a temporary bottleneck, primarily driven by integration immaturity rather than fundamental capability constraints.
  • Managed Cloud Platforms (MCP) mature rapidly. Enterprise-grade authentication, observability, and multi-tenancy features are anticipated to be widely available by the end of 2026. The number of community servers is projected to increase significantly (e.g., from 1,000+ to 10,000+). Consequently, integration, identified as a primary bottleneck in 2026, is expected to become substantially less challenging.
  • The gap between AI adoption and realized value is projected to narrow. The percentage of organizations "reimagining business through AI" (Deloitte[2]Deloitte →) is expected to grow from 34% to over 60% as agent infrastructure matures and best practices for deployment disseminate. The project abandonment rate, currently at 42%, is anticipated to drop below 20%.
  • Proponents suggest that AI-accelerated AI research and development could potentially establish a positive feedback loop, consistent with the AI 2027 thesis. Under this hypothesis, each improvement in AI capability accelerates subsequent advancements. Research productivity within AI laboratories is projected to increase by a factor of 3–5 under this assumption.
  • Tangible employment effects become apparent. Entry-level hiring in occupations susceptible to AI automation is projected to decline by 20-30%. The impact on career progression, as identified by the Dallas Fed[3]Dallas Fed →, becomes quantitatively measurable. Unemployment rates among young tech workers are expected to rise further.

Supporting evidence from the survey:

  • SWE-bench performance increased from 4% to 72% within one year, indicating a steep capability improvement curve.
  • A 9× growth in AI company revenue suggests a robust and expanding market, rather than purely speculative activity.
  • Managed Cloud Platforms (MCP) were adopted by major entities such as OpenAI[4]OpenAI →, Google, and the Linux Foundation within 16 months, demonstrating that infrastructure maturation can occur rapidly.
  • A 10× annual cost deflation rate indicates that decreasing costs continuously open new viable application domains for AI technologies.

Contradicting evidence:

  • 10-step production limit, 85% custom-built — integration is hard for structural reasons
  • 42% project abandonment, 56% CEO no-gains — organizational transformation is slow regardless of technology
  • Historical precedent: PC adoption required approximately a decade; internet adoption required two decades — the basis for a materially faster trajectory in AI remains contested.

What it would look like by end of 2027:

  • AI revenue exceeds $100B across leading companies
  • 2-3 major enterprise categories (customer service, coding, document processing) are predominantly AI-handled
  • Stock market AI premium validated by revenue, not just expectations
  • Significant political backlash from displaced workers, particularly in clerical and entry-level tech roles
  • The EU AI Act[5]EU AI Act → requires emergency amendments to keep pace

Leading indicators for Scenario 1 (observable in 2026):

  • Agent production step counts exceeding 30 across multiple enterprises
  • Entry-level tech hiring rates declining 10%+ year-over-year
  • AI company revenue maintaining 5×+ annual growth
  • MCP shipping enterprise authentication and observability features

Scenario 2: The Plateau

Thesis: AI capability continues to improve, but organizational adoption remains slow, leading to a prolonged period of limited impact where the technology functions but enterprise returns are subdued.

What happens:

  • Models continue to improve on benchmarks, but these gains do not translate into proportionally better production outcomes. For example, SWE-bench scores may increase from 72% to 85%, but the remaining 15% often involves edge cases that significantly impact production environments.
  • The gap between AI adoption and realized value persists. While 80-90% of organizations utilize AI, enterprise value remains concentrated within the top 15-20%. The 66% of organizations that have adopted AI but have not achieved transformative outcomes continue to operate without significant change.
  • Agent deployments demonstrate incremental improvements. Production step counts may increase from 10 to 15-20, but they do not reach the 50+ steps often considered necessary for full autonomous operation. The 40% project cancellation rate persists, attributed not to technological failure but to challenges in organizational change management.
  • Cost deflation continues, making AI more affordable but not inherently valuable. The commodity dynamics observed in Scenario 1 materialize: model capability becomes a baseline requirement, and competitive advantage primarily shifts to integration, data quality, and workflow design—factors that represent organizational capabilities rather than inherent AI capabilities.
  • Employment effects are limited and gradual. The Yale[6]Yale → "no disruption" finding remains valid for an additional 2-3 years. The career ladder experiences a slow erosion rather than an abrupt disruption.

Supporting evidence:

  • PwC[7]PwC →: 56% of CEOs report no gains after 2+ years, indicating significant organizational inertia.
  • Only 34% of organizations are reimagining business processes (Deloitte), suggesting that a majority of organizations may not possess the necessary capabilities for transformative change.
  • Skills, rather than technology, are identified as the primary barrier. This indicates that the main challenge lies in training and change management, requiring multi-year timelines.
  • Historical precedent: technology adoption typically follows decades-long S-curves rather than near-vertical growth trajectories.

Contradicting evidence:

  • 9× revenue growth and 10× cost deflation represent significant economic incentives.
  • MCP's 16-month adoption trajectory demonstrates rapid infrastructure maturation.
  • AI 2027's self-accelerating R&D thesis — if AI improves AI development, the incremental assumption breaks.

What it would look like by end of 2027:

  • AI revenue reaches $40-60B across leading companies (a substantial figure, but not fundamentally transformative).
  • Most enterprises continue to integrate AI into existing processes without fundamental re-engineering.
  • Stock market AI premium gradually compressed as revenue growth decelerates to 2-3×.
  • Employment effects limited to specific demographics (young tech, clerical).
  • Regulatory frameworks have sufficient time to mature, with EU AI Act enforcement proceeding in a structured manner.
  • The prevailing discourse shifts from expectations of pervasive transformation by AI to a perception of AI as a valuable utility.

Leading indicators:

  • AI company revenue growth decelerating to 3-4× (still strong, but decelerating).
  • Project abandonment rate holding at 35-45%.
  • Agent production step counts remain at 15-20 (an improvement, but not yet transformative).
  • Entry-level hiring rates stable or declining <5%.

Scenario 3: The Reckoning

Thesis: The investment-value gap triggers a market correction, leading to a market reset.

What happens:

  • The $500B capital expenditure wave encounters diminishing returns. Several hyperscaler data center projects are mothballed or delayed. The power constraint (S1) becomes a more significant constraint than initially anticipated — multi-year construction timelines mean facilities greenlit in 2024 may not be operational until 2027-2028, by which time efficiency gains from S1 could render them oversized.
  • Project abandonment accelerates, with S&P Global's reported 42% potentially rising to over 60%. Enterprise AI budgets for projects failing to demonstrate expected return on investment are reduced, potentially by 20-30%, as financial officers prioritize demonstrable value. The term "AI winter" begins to appear in trade press.
  • AI company revenues plateau. OpenAI's $15B revenue target is not achieved; actual revenue of $8-10B, while substantial, is insufficient to support valuations exceeding $100B. A major AI company acquisition or downround signals market repricing.
  • Elements of the market overvaluation thesis are observed. This is attributed not to a lack of AI functionality, but to an investment-to-return ratio that was untenable given investment levels observed during 2025-2026. The correction is projected to be 30-50% in AI-focused equities.
  • The market correction may accelerate the adoption of mature AI solutions. Companies demonstrating clear return on investment, validated use cases, and sustainable economic models are likely to persist through the reset. Vendors lacking substantive AI agent capabilities or clear value propositions exit the market. Managed Cloud Providers (MCPs) consolidate offerings around established enterprise adoption patterns. Technological capabilities continue to advance, and market expectations align more closely with demonstrated utility.

Supporting evidence:

  • 42% project abandonment and accelerating — this trend indicates a negative trajectory
  • 56% of CEOs reporting no gains — this suggests a significant disparity between investment and returns
  • Gartner[8]Gartner →: 40% agent cancellation — this category, characterized by high speculative interest, appears particularly susceptible to market shifts
  • Historical precedent: dot-com crash (2000-2002), blockchain winter (2018-2020) — in these instances, the underlying technology persisted while valuations underwent significant adjustments

Contradicting evidence:

  • In contrast to the dot-com era, AI companies demonstrate substantial revenue (9× growth) and significant utility (78% adoption)
  • Annual cost deflation of 10x contributes to AI becoming more accessible, irrespective of market sentiment
  • AI's utility is demonstrated at the individual level (e.g., coding productivity +67%, decision accuracy 28%→47%) — this utility is unlikely to diminish during a market correction
  • Enterprise survey data indicates strong investment intent (KPMG[9]KPMG →: two-thirds of respondents would not cut AI budgets in a downturn)

What it would look like by end of 2027:

  • AI-focused equities are projected to decline 30-50% from 2026 peaks
  • 2-3 major AI startups are acquired at down-rounds or cease operations
  • Hyperscaler AI capital expenditure is reduced 20-30% from peak levels
  • However, AI usage continues to grow due to its demonstrated utility and decreasing operational costs
  • Companies with robust revenue streams and clear return on investment in AI solutions are projected to strengthen their market positions
  • The market discourse is anticipated to transition from speculative growth to a focus on mature, productive applications of AI

Leading indicators:

  • Project abandonment rate rising above 50%
  • AI company revenue growth decelerating below 2x
  • A major AI company downround or distressed acquisition
  • Hyperscaler capital expenditure guidance reduced in earnings calls

Why the Middle Scenario May Be Least Likely

Most leaders default to Scenario 2 (Plateau) as the "reasonable" middle ground. But the dynamics of the current situation may not support a middle outcome. The installation-phase economics have a binary quality:

  • If AI-accelerated R&D works (AI improving AI development), the feedback loop pushes toward Scenario 1. Each improvement compounds. The capability curve steepens. Organizations that can absorb the change get pulled forward; those that can't fall further behind.
  • If the investment-value gap widens (abandonment rates rise, revenue growth stalls), market dynamics push toward Scenario 3. Capital pulls back. Valuations correct. Weaker companies fail.

The plateau requires a delicate balance: enough return to sustain investment, not enough to trigger the acceleration feedback loop. This is a narrow equilibrium that could be disrupted by a single major event — a discontinuous capability jump, a high-profile AI failure, a regulatory shock, or a market correction in unrelated sectors that triggers risk-off behavior in AI investments.

Strategic implication: Given that Scenario 2 (Plateau) is frequently adopted as a default planning assumption, it can serve as a reference point for strategic development. However, its described instability implies that a robust strategy must prioritize building optionality for both extreme scenarios. Investment in integration infrastructure yields positive returns under any scenario. Development of organizational capability for rapid scaling addresses Scenario 1 risk; capability for efficient retrenchment addresses Scenario 3 risk. Monitoring the leading indicators on a monthly cadence is therefore advisable.

Conclusion

The future trajectory of AI is characterized by significant uncertainty, with various predictions often exceeding the empirical evidence. The current state — marked by advanced AI capabilities, substantial investment, limited enterprise returns, increasing project discontinuation rates, and uncertain timelines — represents a dynamic equilibrium. This configuration is likely to transition. The three scenarios described here outline potential directions for this evolution.

An effective organizational approach involves identifying the leading indicators for each scenario, monitoring those indicators systematically, and adapting strategy based on observed trends rather than committing to a single projected outcome. A strategy anchored exclusively to the plateau scenario carries material exposure to both the acceleration and reckoning outcomes described above.
