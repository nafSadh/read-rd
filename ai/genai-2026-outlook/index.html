DISCOURSE SURVEY · MARCH 2026

GenAI 2026 & After

A literature survey of what informed observers are saying about the state and trajectory of generative AI — across technology, adoption, employment, trust, regulation, and economics.

Generative AI in early 2026 is defined by a paradox. The technology has never been better: models that score 72% on SWE-bench (up from 4% a year ago), inference costs collapsing 10× per year, multimodal systems that speak, see, and simulate physics. And yet the gap between what AI can do and what organizations actually get from it has never been wider. 78–88% of companies deploy AI. Only 10–39% report enterprise-level value. 56% of CEOs captured neither revenue nor cost savings.

This survey maps the landscape across 8 dimensions and 53+ sources — industry surveys (McKinsey, Deloitte, PwC, KPMG), economic analysis (Goldman Sachs, Dallas Fed, Brookings, Yale), regulatory coverage (EU AI Act, US state laws), and technology assessment (Stanford AI Index, Epoch AI). Each section is built only after assessing whether sources are sufficient to support its claims. The most interesting findings are not the consensus points but the tensions — the places where credible sources directly contradict each other.

Six Tensions

The most valuable findings in any survey are the contradictions — places where credible sources, looking at the same evidence, reach opposite conclusions. These six tensions define the GenAI discourse in 2026. Each links to the relevant section deep-dive.
  • T1 Adoption vs. value — 78–88% of organizations deploy AI, but only 10–39% report enterprise-level impact. PwC: 56% of CEOs captured neither revenue nor cost savings. The technology works; the organization doesn’t — yet. [S4]
  • T2 No disruption yet vs. undertow — Yale finds “no discernible disruption” 33 months post-ChatGPT. Goldman finds young tech workers’ unemployment up 3pp. Both are true — the aggregate is fine while specific demographics are already hurting. [S5]
  • T3 Use vs. trust — KPMG (48K people, 47 countries): 66% use AI regularly, only 46% trust it, 56% made mistakes. The first major technology to achieve mass adoption while being actively distrusted by most of its users. [S6]
  • T4 Scale vs. efficiency — Bigger compute clusters (9 GW by 2030) vs NVIDIA’s own paper arguing 7B models are 10–30× more efficient for most tasks. Both camps work at the same company. [S1]
  • T5 Bubble vs. boom — $500B+ capex and 42% project abandonment look like a bubble. 9× revenue growth and $15B at OpenAI look like a boom. Both cite valid evidence because both are partially true simultaneously. [S8]
  • T6 Autonomy timelines — When do agents achieve real autonomy? AI 2027 says 18 months. Davenport says 5 years. Karpathy says 10 years. A 6× spread from sources with access to the same evidence. [S3]
