# S4: Workplace Adoption & Productivity

> Everyone uses AI. Almost nobody gets enterprise-level value from it. 78-88% of organizations deploy AI somewhere. Only 10-39% report enterprise-wide impact. The gap between adoption and value is the defining story of 2026.

**Sources:** 10 (Stanford HAI, MIT Sloan, Makebot, Microsoft, IDC FutureScape, ITSM.tools, Anthropic, McKinsey State of AI, Deloitte Enterprise AI 2026, PwC CEO Survey)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Three major enterprise surveys (McKinsey 2025, Deloitte 2026, PwC 2026) plus domain-specific data.

---

## 1. The Adoption-Value Gap

The central finding is a paradox captured by converging data: 78% of organizations use AI (McKinsey, up from 55% in 2023). 88% deploy AI somewhere (PwC synthesis). But only 39% report EBIT impact at enterprise level (McKinsey). PwC: 56% of CEOs captured neither revenue nor cost savings. Only 12% achieved both.

Deloitte's framing: enterprise AI adoption moves at "the speed of business, not the speed of technology." 66% report productivity gains, but only 20% report revenue growth — and 74% are still *hoping* for future revenue impact. The gap between "we use AI" and "AI materially improved our business" is enormous.

## 2. The Evaluation Shift

Stanford HAI: the era of AI evangelism is giving way to the era of AI evaluation. "Can AI do this?" replaced by "How well, at what cost, for whom?" MIT Sloan's Davenport and Bean describe a shift from individual to enterprise-level implementation — copilot-style tools delivering "incremental and mostly unmeasurable productivity gains" for individuals, while organizations struggle to capture value at the system level.

Makebot: LLMs now evaluated by outcomes, not benchmarks. Productivity improvements "most consistently observed in programming, call centers, and narrowly defined workflows." Many large-scale AI initiatives underperform expectations.

## 3. Where Value Materializes

Consistent across sources — AI delivers measurable value in:

- **Coding**: Anthropic data shows 67% more PRs merged/day. Fountain: 50% faster. Rakuten: 7h autonomous coding sessions. TELUS: 500K hours saved. But only 0-20% full delegation — AI as accelerant, not replacement.
- **Customer service**: Call center volume reduction, faster resolution (connects to S3 agent findings).
- **Productivity for junior workers**: Deloitte finds GenAI boosted junior productivity 20-30%, senior only 10-15%. AI as equalizer — closing the skill gap from below.
- **Decision support**: ITSM survey: better decision-making jumped from 28% to 47% as top benefit.

## 4. Where Value Doesn't Materialize

- **Revenue growth**: 20% achieving vs 74% hoping (Deloitte). The belief-execution gap.
- **Enterprise-wide scaling**: Only 10% scaled value enterprise-wide (PwC). Two-thirds of organizations merely layer AI onto existing processes.
- **Open-ended initiatives**: McKinsey: only 6-10% of firms with weak data pipelines realize consistent value.

## 5. The Barriers

Deloitte: insufficient worker skills = #1 barrier (majority of senior leaders). 42% believe strategy is prepared, but feel less prepared on infrastructure, data, risk, and talent.

McKinsey identifies the management gap: high performers are 3× more likely to have senior leadership demonstrating ownership and commitment. The differentiator is not technology access — everyone has that — but organizational readiness.

## 6. The Free-Tool Shadow

ITSM survey: 82% of end-users use free AI tools at work (up from 71% in 2025). Only 2% report no AI use. But only 16% fully trust AI for autonomous operational decisions. Trust increased for 62% but remains shallow — people use AI daily while not trusting it for important decisions.

## 7. What Sources Agree On

1. **Adoption is near-universal** — 78-88% use AI somewhere. The "will we adopt?" question is settled.
2. **Enterprise value is rare** — 10-39% report meaningful impact. The gap is the story.
3. **Coding is the strongest use case** — measurable, consistent gains across multiple studies.
4. **Junior workers benefit most** — 20-30% vs 10-15% for seniors. AI as skill equalizer.
5. **Skills, not technology, are the bottleneck** — consistent across Deloitte, McKinsey, PwC.
6. **Leadership commitment is the differentiator** — 3× more likely in high performers (McKinsey).

## 8. Where Sources Disagree

1. **How much value is real?** McKinsey (39% EBIT impact) vs PwC (only 12% both revenue + cost). Likely reflects different survey populations and definitions.
2. **Timeline to enterprise-wide value**: Deloitte says 6-12 months for scaling to strong ROI. McKinsey high-performer data suggests some achieve it faster. Many never get there.
3. **AI as tool vs AI as transformation**: Microsoft frames "AI coworkers." Deloitte says only 34% are truly reimagining business through AI. The rest bolt AI onto existing processes.
