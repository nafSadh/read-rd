# S5: Employment & Labor Market

> 33 months after ChatGPT: "no discernible disruption" to the broader labor market (Yale). But under the surface: young tech workers already feeling it (unemployment +3pp), the career ladder is breaking, and 6.1M clerical workers have low adaptive capacity.

**Sources:** 8 (Goldman Sachs, Yale Budget Lab, Dallas Fed, Brookings, BLS, TechCrunch/VCs, WEF, National University)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Economic analysis, labor data, adaptive capacity, demographic breakdowns, and both sides of the displacement debate.

---

## 1. The Headline: No Disruption (Yet)

Yale Budget Lab: "No discernible disruption" to the broader US labor market 33 months after ChatGPT. No clear growth in AI exposure among unemployed. Historical precedent: computers took a decade to transform workflows.

BLS approaches AI like any other technology in their projections. Displacement "tends to take longer than technologists expect." Many AI-affected occupations still see employment growth historically. But BLS acknowledges their methods aren't designed to capture "extremely rapid technological change."

## 2. The Undertow: Young Tech Workers

Goldman Sachs: 20-30 year olds in tech-exposed occupations saw unemployment rise ~3 percentage points since start of 2025 — while the broader labor market remained stable. Only 9.3% of companies report using GenAI for tasks that directly affect employment. Historically, tech disruption effects are temporary (~2 years).

Full exposure estimates: 2.5% of US employment at risk with current AI use cases. 6-7% at risk with wide adoption. Unemployment could rise 0.5pp during transition.

## 3. The Career Ladder Problem

Dallas Fed's most consequential finding: AI breaks the traditional career ladder. Codified knowledge (textbook learning, procedures) is automatable. Tacit knowledge (judgment, experience, relationships) is complemented by AI. This means: entry-level workers whose value comes from codified knowledge get substituted. Experienced workers whose value comes from tacit knowledge get augmented.

The career ladder paradox: if AI can do what juniors used to do, how do juniors develop the tacit knowledge that makes seniors valuable? Entry-level development becomes "cost-ineffective" for employers. But without entry-level development, the pipeline of experienced workers dries up.

## 4. Who's Actually Vulnerable

Brookings: 37.1M US workers are highly AI-exposed. But exposure ≠ vulnerability. 70% (26.5M) have HIGH adaptive capacity — high pay, financial buffers, diverse skills. They'll adapt.

The truly vulnerable: 6.1M clerical/admin workers with LOW adaptive capacity. Office clerks (2.5M), secretaries (1.7M), receptionists (965K). Low pay, limited financial buffers, narrow skill sets. These are disproportionately women (79% of employed women in high-risk jobs vs 58% of men).

## 5. The Global Picture

WEF: 85-92M jobs displaced globally by 2030. 97-170M new roles created (net positive in their forecast). 59% of workers need reskilling by 2030. 41% of employers foresee workforce reductions.

The WEF numbers are commonly cited but should be read carefully: "jobs displaced" and "new roles created" don't happen to the same people, in the same places, at the same time.

## 6. The Generational Divide

Workers 18-24 are 129% more likely to worry about AI job loss than older workers. 49% of Gen Z believe AI has reduced the value of a college education. 14% of workers say they've already been displaced. This connects to S4's finding that junior workers benefit most from AI productivity — the same cohort that benefits most individually may be most threatened collectively.

## 7. What Sources Agree On

1. No broad labor market disruption — yet. But early signals in specific demographics.
2. The career ladder is the most consequential structural risk.
3. Exposure ≠ vulnerability. Adaptive capacity matters more than exposure.
4. Clerical/admin workers (especially women) are most at risk.
5. Historical precedent suggests effects take years, not months.

## 8. Where Sources Disagree

1. Timeline: Yale (no disruption yet, may take a decade) vs VCs (budget shifts from labor to AI in 2026). Goldman Sachs is in between (2-year disruption cycles).
2. Scale: BLS (incremental) vs WEF (92M displaced). Methodology differences explain much of this.
3. Will AI be net positive or negative for employment? WEF says net positive (170M created > 92M displaced). Dallas Fed's career ladder analysis is more pessimistic about quality of new jobs.
