Agent-Omit (HKUST(GZ) + DiDi, Feb 2026)[5]Ning et al. “Agent-Omit” (arXiv:2602.04284, Feb 2026) addresses a practical problem: 97.3% of agent token cost comes from thoughts (45.1%) and observations (52.2%), not actions (2.7%). Demystifying Agentic RL

This practitioner-oriented paper (NUS + Princeton + UIUC, Oct 2025)[6]Yu et al. “Demystifying RL in Agentic Reasoning” (arXiv:2510.11701, Oct 2025) systematically investigates three dimensions of agentic RL: data curation, algorithm design, and reasoning mode. The key finding that defies conventional wisdom: a deliberative strategy with fewer tool calls outperforms both frequent tool calling and verbose self-reasoning.

Data: Real > Synthetic

Real end-to-end trajectories dramatically outperform synthetic stitched data for SFT initialization. High-diversity datasets sustain RL exploration.

Algorithm: Exploration-Friendly

“Clip higher” (relax upper bound), overlong reward shaping, and maintaining policy entropy improve training efficiency.

Reasoning: Deliberate > Verbose

Fewer, more strategic tool calls beat both frequent calling (dependency) and long CoT without verification (hallucination).

4B Models Compete

With proper recipes, 4B models achieve strong results on AIME2024/2025, GPQA-Diamond, LiveCodeBench-v6.

OpenClaw-RL — Next-State Signals

OpenClaw-RL (Princeton + NUS, Mar 2026)[7]Wang et al. “OpenClaw-RL” (arXiv:2603.10165, Mar 2026) makes a simple but powerful observation: every deployed AI agent already discards the training signal it needs to improve. After each action, the agent receives a next-state signal (user reply, tool output, GUI change) that encodes both evaluative (how well it performed) and directive (how it should change) information.

The framework is fully asynchronous: policy serving, PRM judging, and training run concurrently with zero coordination overhead. It unifies personal agents (OpenClaw conversations) with general agents (terminal, GUI, SWE, tool-call settings) under a single learning infrastructure.

User re-query signals dissatisfaction. Passing test signals success. Error trace signals failure. Natural process reward, no annotation needed.

Section 01

From Scaffolds to Weights

The most consequential shift in agentic AI between early 2025 and early 2026 is not any single model release. It is a change in where agent capabilities live. In 2024, an agent's ability to plan, use tools, manage memory, and reflect was implemented as external code—prompt templates, retrieval pipelines, orchestration logic. By early 2026, these capabilities are increasingly being internalized into model weights via reinforcement learning.

The formal distinction, laid out most clearly by Zhang et al. in the Agentic RL Landscape survey (TMLR 2026)[2]Zhang et al. “The Landscape of Agentic RL for LLMs” (arXiv:2509.02547v4, TMLR Jan 2026), is between a degenerate single-step MDP (what RLHF does) and a temporally extended POMDP (what agentic RL does). In the single-step case, the model sees a prompt, generates a response, and receives a scalar reward. In the agentic case, the model acts over multiple time steps in a partially observable environment, receiving stochastic feedback from tools and the world, and accumulating rewards over an extended horizon.

What Changed and When

DeepSeek-R1 (January 2025)[1]DeepSeek-R1: Incentivizing Reasoning via RL (arXiv:2501.12948, Jan 2025) was the catalyst. By applying GRPO—a critic-free RL algorithm that uses group-relative advantages—to LLM training, the DeepSeek team demonstrated that self-reflection behavior could emerge without being explicitly trained. The model spontaneously learned to check its own reasoning, backtrack, and verify answers.

Kimi k1.5 (Moonshot AI) reinforced the generalizability. By late 2025, a full ecosystem had formed: Agent-R1 providing formal MDP extensions, scaling law studies characterizing RL behavior across model sizes, efficiency work like Agent-Omit showing agents can learn to reason less when appropriate, and practitioner work like “Demystifying RL” identifying concrete algorithmic recipes.

The Mechanistic Debate

A fundamental open question runs through all these papers: does RL merely amplify capabilities that already exist in the pretrained weights (the “sharpening” view), or does it create genuinely new reasoning patterns (the “discovery” view)? The ARL Landscape survey flags this as unresolved and fundamental. The answer determines what the ceiling of RL training might be.

Section 02 — Zhang et al. TMLR 2026

The Landscape of Agentic RL

This 95-page survey (arXiv:2509.02547v4, published TMLR January 2026)[2]Zhang et al. “The Landscape of Agentic RL for LLMs” (arXiv:2509.02547v4, TMLR Jan 2026) is the canonical reference. 25 authors from 14 institutions, synthesizing 500+ works. The paper explicitly cites Plaat et al. as a predecessor that “emphasizes the core capabilities of reasoning, acting, and interacting”—but argues that a unified RL-centric framework was missing.

The POMDP Formalization

The paper’s most lasting contribution is the formal seven-element tuple: ⟨S, O, A, P, R, T, γ⟩.

Action space decomposition: A_agent = A_text ∪ A_action. Text actions generate natural language without altering environment state. Structured actions (delimited by special tokens <action_start> and <action_end>) invoke tools or modify the environment. Critically, A_action is recursively constructed—a single action may itself be a sequence of primitive actions.

Dual transition dynamics: Text actions produce deterministic transitions. Environmental actions produce stochastic transitions P(s_{t+1} | s_t, a_t). This makes the learning problem fundamentally harder than standard RLHF.

Rich reward structure: Task completion rewards (sparse), step-level progress rewards (dense), and zero otherwise. This contrasts with RLHF’s single scalar.

The RL Algorithm Zoo

The paper catalogs 20+ algorithm variants across three families. The GRPO family has dominated because it eliminates the critic network. Key GRPO mechanism: sample a group of outputs, use their relative rewards to compute advantages. Advantage for sample g:

A_hat(s_t, a_t) = (R(s_t, a_t) - mean(R_group)) / std(R_group)

Sample-efficient but vulnerable to high variance when the group contains similar-quality outputs.

Memory Taxonomy (Table 3)

Perhaps the most underappreciated contribution. Classifies agent memory as: RAG-style external stores (MemoryBank, HippoRAG), RL-guided retrieval (Prospect, Memory-R1, Mem-α), Token-level explicit (MemAgent, MEM1, ReSum, Context Folding), Token-level latent (MemoryLLM, M+, MemGen), Structured (Zep temporal graphs, A-MEM atomic notes, G-Memory hierarchical). The RL-guided category—where RL controls what to store, retrieve, and forget—is the frontier.

Open Challenges

Trustworthiness: Agents have expanded attack surface via tools, memory, planning modules. Multi-agent systems compound risks. Sycophancy is dangerous in agentic settings.

Scaling Training: Agent RL Scaling Law shows longer training improves tool-use frequency, reasoning depth, accuracy. But entropy collapse and capability narrowing threaten larger models.

Scaling Environments: ALFWorld and ScienceWorld insufficient. Solutions: automate reward design (explorer agents), automate curriculum generation (EnvGen-style feedback loops).

The Mechanistic Debate: RL as “amplifier” (surfaces existing capabilities) vs. “creator” (teaches new patterns). Unresolved and fundamental.

Coverage Gap

This survey does not cover the agentic coding tool ecosystem (Claude Code, Antigravity, etc.) or the protocol standardization wave (MCP/A2A). It treats multi-agent systems primarily as research, not production engineering. These are the gaps this survey addresses.

Section 03 — Cheng et al. Nov 2025

Agent-R1 — MDP Formalization

Agent-R1 (arXiv:2511.14460, USTC, 13 pages)[3]Cheng et al. “Agent-R1” (arXiv:2511.14460, Nov 2025) makes the POMDP formalization implementation-ready. Its Table 1 is one of the clearest side-by-side comparisons of static LLM vs. LLM Agent MDP components.

State Space

Static LLM: s_t = (w_p, w_1, …, w_t)—prompt plus generated tokens. LLM Agent: s_t = (w_p, T_1, …, T_k, T_{k+1}^partial)—prompt plus full turns of interaction, where each T_i is an agent action and environment response. Qualitatively different: bidirectional interaction history vs. unidirectional generation.

Transition Decomposition

Two types coexist in a single policy: P_G (generative, deterministic—appending tokens) and P_E (environmental, potentially stochastic—triggered by tool invocations). The RL algorithm must handle both within the same trajectory.

Process Rewards

Beyond final outcome reward r_f, Agent-R1 uses intermediate process rewards r_p for tool-call success. This provides more frequent feedback for credit assignment in long trajectories. Validated on multi-hop QA requiring chained tool calls.

Architecture Taxonomy

Three-level progression: Workflows (human-designed routing/planning) → Agentic Workflows (ReAct-style iterative reasoning-acting loops, but still prompt-engineered) → Fully Autonomous Agents (no predefined workflow, end-to-end RL, proactive environment interaction through action-feedback cycles).

Design Philosophy

Agent-R1 is deliberately modular—designed for “straightforward adaptation across diverse task scenarios and interactive environments.” Different agent tasks share the same RL training loop but need different environment interfaces.

Section 04 — Tan et al. Dec 2025

RL Post-Training Scaling Laws

This paper (arXiv:2509.25300v3, 27 pages)[4]Tan et al. “Scaling Behaviors of LLM RL Post-Training” (arXiv:2509.25300v3, Dec 2025) does for RL post-training what Chinchilla did for pretraining: establishes empirical scaling laws that predict performance. 63 models across Qwen2.5 (0.5B to 72B), all RL-trained on 50,000 math problems.

The Core Formula

log L(N, X) = -k(N) · log X + E(N)

L = test loss, N = model size, X = resource budget (compute C or data D), k(N) = learning efficiency, E(N) = initial performance baseline.

Finding 1: Larger Models Are More Efficient

Consistent across both compute and data metrics. A 72B model extracts more value from the same compute budget than a 7B model. Validated by fitting on 0.5B–32B and extrapolating to 72B with good accuracy.

Finding 2: Efficiency Saturates

k(N) follows a saturation formula: k(N) = k_∞ · (1 - e^{-N/N_0}). Learning efficiency increases with scale but asymptotically approaches a ceiling. A hypothetical 720B model wouldn’t be proportionally better than 72B for RL training. This is the most practically important finding.

Finding 3: Data Reuse Works

“Repeated exposure to a small dataset is nearly as effective as using larger corpora.” Performance governed by total optimization steps, not unique data volume. Teams don’t need massive unique RL datasets—a well-curated small set trained longer can match.

Finding 4: Predictive Across Conditions

Robust for both base and instruction-tuned starting points. Enables compute-optimal allocation: given fixed FLOPs, predict optimal model size and training duration.

The Agentic Scaling Question

The paper flags a key open question: these laws were measured for pure reasoning RL. When agents use tools, “offloading deterministic computations to tools and focusing learning on high-level decision making” should shift the curve upward. Measuring agentic scaling laws is critical future work.

Section 05 — Ning et al. Feb 2026

Agent-Omit — Learning to Skip

Agent-Omit (arXiv:2602.04284, 20 pages)[5]Ning et al. “Agent-Omit” (arXiv:2602.04284, Feb 2026) starts with a quantitative analysis using Qwen3-8B on WebShop: 45.1% of tokens are thoughts, 52.2% observations, and only 2.7% actions. The bottleneck isn’t executing—it’s thinking and reading.

Turn-Level Analysis (Monte Carlo Rollouts)

Thoughts: Front-loaded. Heavy token consumption in early turns for high-level planning. Later thoughts redundant once execution is clear.

Observations: Linear growth from stacking. Early observations critical for reasoning; later ones noise during answer generation.

Accuracy contribution: Early turns critical (high Pass@8). Later turns often fall below Pass@1 baseline—actively hurting performance.

Two-Stage Training

Stage 1 (Cold-start): Forward traversal through trajectories. At each turn, test whether omitting thought/observation preserves accuracy. If yes, mark as omittable. Generate single-turn and multi-turn omission examples.

Stage 2 (Omit-aware RL): Dual sampling generates standard and omission-aware trajectories. Tailored omission reward incentivizes skipping. Theoretical guarantee: deviation of omission policy upper-bounded by KL-divergence.

Benchmarks

Tested on DeepSearch (knowledge-intensive), WebShop (e-commerce), TextCraft (Minecraft long-horizon), BabyAI (grid-world), SciWorld (scientific discovery). Agent-Omit-8B matches frontier agents while achieving best efficiency trade-off.

Strengths

  • First principled turn-level omission for agentic RL
  • Theoretical KL-divergence bound on policy deviation
  • 8B model matching frontier agents on efficiency
  • Evaluated across 5 diverse benchmarks

Limitations

  • Cold-start requires access to full trajectories
  • Binary omission (keep/skip), not partial compression
  • May miss tasks where late observations carry novel info
Section 06 — Yu et al. Oct 2025

Demystifying Agentic RL

This paper (arXiv:2510.11701, NUS + Princeton + UIUC, 24 pages)[6]Yu et al. “Demystifying RL in Agentic Reasoning” (arXiv:2510.11701, Oct 2025) is the practitioner’s guide. While other papers focus on theory or specific innovations, this one asks: “What actually works, and why?”

Axis 1: Data Curation

Real end-to-end trajectories dramatically outperform synthetic stitched data for SFT initialization. Current pipelines (e.g., ReTool) replace reasoning steps with tool invocations via template editing. This misses critical decision cues—“not only how to call a tool, but also when, why, and what to do next.”

They compare real vs. synthetic on Qwen2.5-7B and Qwen3-4B. Real data is qualitatively richer. For RL stage, high-diversity, model-aware datasets sustain exploration and prevent mode collapse.

Axis 2: Algorithm Design

Three GRPO techniques compared: loss aggregation granularity, reward shaping, clipping strategy. The key: exploration-friendly techniques matter most.

“Clip higher”—relaxing the upper clip bound in GRPO allows more exploration. Overlong reward shaping—shaped rewards guide appropriate length instead of simple penalties. Entropy management—preventing premature convergence by maintaining policy entropy.

Axis 3: Reasoning Mode

The surprising result: a deliberative strategy with fewer tool calls outperforms both frequent tool calling and verbose self-reasoning. Too many calls create dependency and overhead. Too much internal reasoning leads to hallucination accumulation. The sweet spot is deliberate, strategic tool use with concise reasoning.

Practical Impact

4B models achieve competitive results on AIME2024, AIME2025, GPQA-Diamond, LiveCodeBench-v6. They release SFT and RL datasets plus the DemyAgent-4B model.

The Meta-Lesson

Success comes from getting many small things right simultaneously. Real data, proper clipping, entropy management, deliberate reasoning—each helps individually but the combination is multiplicative. Simply plugging in GRPO does not suffice for agentic behavior to emerge.

Section 07 — Wang et al. Mar 2026

OpenClaw-RL — Next-State Signals

OpenClaw-RL (arXiv:2603.10165, Princeton + NUS, 24 pages)[7]Wang et al. “OpenClaw-RL” (arXiv:2603.10165, Mar 2026) is the most recent and ambitious paper in this section. Core observation: “Every deployed AI agent is already collecting the data it needs to improve—and discarding it.”

The Two Wastes

Evaluative signals: After each action, the next-state implicitly scores performance. A user re-query means dissatisfaction. A passing test means success. This is a natural process reward—no annotation needed. Yet systems ignore it or use it only offline.

Directive signals: Beyond scoring, next-states carry directional information. “You should have checked the file first” specifies not just what was wrong but how to fix it at the token level. This is richer than any scalar reward.

Hindsight-Guided On-Policy Distillation (OPD)

The novel method: after an action and its next-state, construct an enhanced “teacher context” including the hindsight information. Generate token-level directional advantage supervision. Key difference from standard RL: instead of just a scalar “that was bad,” OPD provides token-by-token guidance on what the output should have looked like.

Architecture

Four decoupled async components: Policy Server (SGLang), PRM Server (judge model/API), Training Engine (Megatron), Environment Server (sessions/channels/tools). The policy serves while training runs. Judging happens concurrently. Zero coordination overhead.

Unified Agent Types

Same framework for personal agents (OpenClaw chat) and general agents (terminal, GUI, SWE, tool-call). Demonstrated with: student homework, teacher grading, developer debugging. Builds on SLiMe (Megatron training), SGLang (inference), SETA (terminal RL), OSWorld (GUI RL), mini-swe-agent (SWE RL).

Note on OpenClaw Security

This paper covers the training infrastructure. The deployment security issues (exposed gateways, malicious skills, prompt injection via messaging) are documented in the OPERATE section. The tension between capability and safety runs through the entire agentic ecosystem.

Section 08 — Synthesis

Cross-Cutting Themes & Reading List

Theme 1: Scaffold → Weights Migration

Every paper documents the same story. Planning: from CoT prompting to RL-trained policies. Tool use: from ReAct to RL-optimized TIR. Memory: from static RAG to RL-controlled management (Memory-R1, MemAgent). Self-improvement: from verbal reflection to RL-driven correction. Context management: from file scaffolding to RLM code delegation (Prime Intellect).

Theme 2: Training Stability Crisis

Entropy collapse, reward hacking, mode collapse, training collapse—documented across multiple papers. ARLArena/SAMPO proposes systematic stability analysis. “Demystifying RL” prescribes algorithmic recipes. Scaling laws show efficiency saturates. This is the field’s central engineering challenge.

Theme 3: The Data Question

Two complementary findings: (1) Real end-to-end trajectories are far better than synthetic for SFT—quality matters at cold start. (2) Data reuse is effective for RL—optimization steps matter more than unique volume. Recipe: invest in quality trajectory collection, then RL with reuse.

Theme 4: Efficiency as First-Class

Agent-Omit demonstrates efficiency isn’t just operational—it’s a training objective. The shift from “maximize accuracy” to “maximize accuracy per token” signals production maturity, connecting directly to the OPERATE section’s economics.
