Agentic LLMs in the Wild: Reasoning, Acting, Interacting, and Operating

Draft v1 — March 24, 2026

Abstract

Between January 2025 and March 2026, the field of agentic large language models underwent four structural shifts that collectively outpace the framing of existing surveys. Capabilities previously implemented as external prompt scaffolding — Chain-of-Thought[1]Chain-of-Thought →, ReAct[2]ReAct →, Reflexion — are being internalized into model weights via reinforcement learning, transforming the learning problem from a degenerate single-step MDP to a temporally extended POMDP. Software development has emerged as a primary action domain, with self-improving agents achieving 77.4% on SWE-bench[3]SWE-bench → Verified, and self-play training on raw codebases substantially reduces the reliance on human-curated training data, though a minimum base model capability — itself dependent on prior human-data training — remains a prerequisite. Agent interaction has shifted from a research topic to an infrastructure and governance challenge, with standardized protocols (MCP[4]MCP →, A2A) governed by six competing companies under the Linux Foundation's Agentic AI Foundation. Production deployment has exposed a security surface that the prior survey literature deferred as future work. MCP's architectural choices amplify attack success by 23–41%, evaluated defenses are bypassed at 78%+ rates under adaptive attacks, and 57% of MCP tool interaction paths leak sensitive data by default.

This survey updates and extends Plaat et al.[5]Plaat et al. →'s taxonomy (JAIR 2025) with a fourth axis — Operate — covering safety, security, reliability, and the economics of agentic deployment. We synthesize 28 papers read from primary sources across the four categories, identify five cross-cutting themes including the emergence of a portable skills layer, and propose a research agenda grounded in the gap between benchmark performance and production reality.

1. Introduction

Plaat, van Duijn, van Stein, Preuss, van der Putten, and Batenburg published "Agentic Large Language Models, a survey" in JAIR in 2025, organizing the field around three capabilities: (1) Reason, (2) Act, (3) Interact [1]. This taxonomy provided a foundational framework for the field's understanding in early 2025. The reinforcing cycle they identified — reasoning feeds better actions, actions generate training data, interactions produce emergent behaviors that improve reasoning — remains a core conceptual model within the field.

Since its publication, the rapid evolution of agentic large language models has introduced new paradigms and challenges. This necessitates an updated framework to comprehensively address the expanded scope and complexity of the field.

1.1 Four Shifts

The model-native shift. Plaat et al. treated agentic capabilities as largely external to the model: Chain-of-Thought as prompting, tool use as API scaffolding, memory as retrieval pipelines. A significant conceptual shift since their paper is that planning, tool use, memory, and reflection are being internalized into model weights via reinforcement learning [2, 3]. DeepSeek-R1 (January 2025) demonstrated that self-reflection behavior could emerge from GRPO training without being explicitly taught [4]. By early 2026, an extensive ecosystem has developed: formal POMDP extensions [5], scaling laws for RL post-training [6], efficiency as a training objective [7], and practitioner recipes [8]. The mathematical distinction is precise: RLHF is a degenerate single-step MDP (horizon T=1, deterministic transitions, single scalar reward); agentic RL is a temporally extended POMDP (horizon T>1, stochastic transitions via tool use, step-wise rewards, partial observability) [2].

The coding explosion. Plaat et al.'s "Act" category focused on robotics, hypothetical assistants, and tool-use demonstrations. By March 2026, software development has emerged as a primary action domain. The SWE-Bench benchmark family has diversified: enterprise-scale [9], long-horizon evolution [10], continuous updates [11], realistic user queries [12], and test adequacy [13]. Self-improving agents achieve 77.4% on SWE-bench Verified with zero offline training cost [14]. Self-play RL on raw codebases substantially reduces the need for human-curated training data [15], provided the base model has crossed a minimum capability threshold. The tools landscape has undergone rapid expansion: Claude Code[6]Claude Code → (industry estimates project ARR in the billions for leading agentic coding tools by early 2026 [33]), Antigravity, Cursor, Codex CLI, Gemini CLI (96K+ GitHub stars), OpenClaw (100K+ stars) — each adopting diverse approaches to developer workflow.

The protocol infrastructure. Plaat et al.'s "Interact" category treated multi-agent systems as a research topic — Generative Agents, social simulations, game-playing. Developments have shown that standardized protocols (MCP, A2A) now govern how agents communicate with tools and with each other. The Linux Foundation's Agentic AI Foundation (AAIF), co-founded by OpenAI[7]OpenAI →, Anthropic[8]Anthropic →, Google, Microsoft[9]Microsoft →, AWS, and Block in December 2025, aims to provide neutral governance. MCP has crossed 97 million monthly SDK downloads. Multi-agent orchestration has moved from academic design patterns to production architectures, with empirical evidence that decomposition produces deterministic quality levels not achievable with single agents [16].

The safety imperative. Plaat et al. mentioned safety as "future work." By March 2026, it is recognized as a central engineering challenge. MCP's architectural choices amplify attack success rates by 23–41% compared to equivalent non-MCP integrations [17]. A meta-analysis of 78 studies finds that all tested defenses are bypassed at 78%+ rates under adaptive attacks [18]. 57% of MCP tool interaction paths leak sensitive data by default through unintentional over-exposure [19]. 26.1% of community-contributed agent skills contain security vulnerabilities [20]. The field's focus has shifted from enabling agent action to ensuring agent safety.

1.2 Why a Fourth Axis

Building upon prior work, particularly Plaat et al. (2025), these shifts motivate the comprehensive development of the four-axis taxonomy, specifically activating the 'Operate' axis previously identified but deferred:

2. Method and Scope

2.1 Paper Selection

The selection process commenced with Plaat et al.'s bibliography, followed by a structured literature search across arXiv, Semantic Scholar, and web sources for publications from November 2023 through March 2024. Papers were prioritized based on the following criteria: (a) introduction of new formalizations or frameworks, (b) provision of quantitative results on established benchmarks, (c) addressing topics not covered in Plaat et al., or (d) documentation of production deployment data.

A forward citation sweep was subsequently conducted on the six most-cited primary sources using the Semantic Scholar API, which identified 6 additional papers published in February–March 2024 that either extended or challenged the initial findings.

2.2 Reading Depth

Twenty-eight papers from downloaded PDFs were analyzed at varying depths. Three papers were read in full (7–10 pages each). Nine papers were read at 50%+ (abstract, introduction, method, key results, discussion). The remainder were read at 15–40% (abstract, introduction, key results tables). Industry sources (Anthropic 2026 Trends Report, AAIF governance documents, tool documentation, security incident reports) were analyzed via web research.

2.3 What This Survey Is Not

This is not a comprehensive survey in the style of Plaat et al. (85 pages) or the ARL Landscape Survey (95 pages, 500+ works) [2]. It is more precisely a critical perspective paper — using 28 primary sources as evidence for a specific thesis: that the field has undergone four structural shifts requiring a fourth taxonomy axis. Given the rapid pace of publication in the field, this study prioritizes depth of argument over exhaustive breadth. Sections that Plaat et al. covered well and that have not structurally changed — embodied agents, GUI navigation, scientific discovery agents — are acknowledged but not re-reviewed.

3. REASON: From Scaffolds to Weights

The integration of agent capabilities, traditionally managed through external scaffolding, directly into model weights via reinforcement learning represents a significant area of advancement in AI.

3.1 The POMDP Formalization

The Agentic RL Landscape survey [2] — authored by 25 researchers across 14 institutions and scheduled for publication in TMLR January 2026, synthesizing over 500 works — introduces a comprehensive formalization. Traditional RLHF operates as a degenerate single-step MDP: the model processes a prompt, generates a response, and receives a scalar reward. Agentic RL operates as a temporally extended POMDP, defined by the seven-element tuple ⟨S, O, A, P, R, T, γ⟩.

A key distinction arises from the decomposition of the action space into A_text (representing reasoning steps with deterministic transitions) and A_action (representing tool calls with stochastic transitions). This necessitates the RL training loop to integrate both types within a unified policy. Agent-R1 [5] illustrates this concept with a three-level architecture: Workflows (human-designed routing) → Agentic Workflows (ReAct-style iterative loops) → Fully Autonomous Agents (characterized by the absence of predefined workflows and end-to-end RL).

3.2 Scaling Laws and Saturation

The first systematic scaling law study for RL post-training [6] trained 63 models across the Qwen2.5 series (0.5B to 72B) and discovered a predictive power-law: log L(N,X) = -k(N)·log X + E(N). A key finding was: learning efficiency k(N) saturates as model size increases. Larger models are more efficient but with diminishing returns. Data reuse was found to be effective — total optimization steps matter more than unique data volume. This suggests that large-scale unique RL datasets may not be a prerequisite for effective model optimization.

3.3 Efficiency as Training Objective

Agent-Omit [7] reported the distribution of agent token costs: 45.1% originated from thoughts, 52.2% from observations, and 2.7% from actions. This analysis suggests that not all reasoning steps contribute equally to the final outcome. The Agent-Omit framework operates on the premise that initial planning phases play a significant role, but certain subsequent reasoning steps may be omitted without compromising performance. The framework trains agents to adaptively skip these identified reasoning steps through omission-aware Reinforcement Learning (RL), thereby achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods at a significantly reduced token cost when utilizing an 8B model.

3.4 Practitioner Recipes

"Demystifying RL in Agentic Reasoning" [8] systematically investigated three dimensions: data curation (real end-to-end trajectories dramatically outperform synthetic stitched data), algorithm design (exploration-friendly techniques like "clip higher" and entropy management improve training), and reasoning mode (a deliberative strategy with fewer tool calls outperforms both frequent calling and verbose self-reasoning). The application of these findings enables 4B models to achieve competitive results on AIME2024, GPQA-Diamond, and LiveCodeBench-v6.

3.5 The Training Signal Question

OpenClaw-RL [21] observed that next-state signals, which contain both evaluative (e.g., success/failure) and directive (e.g., corrective action) information, are often not fully utilized by existing systems. To address this, their asynchronous architecture decouples policy serving, reward judging, and training into concurrent processes, facilitating the integration of these signals.

Agentic Critical Training [22] demonstrated that training agents to judge action quality (via RL) leads to improved performance compared to imitation-based methods (via SFT), producing behaviors statistically consistent with deliberative self-reflection rather than pattern-matched imitation. A key finding was that action-judgment training in agentic environments improved general reasoning benchmarks (MATH-500, GPQA-Diamond) without requiring reasoning-specific training data. This suggests that agentic RL could represent a general mechanism for enhancing reasoning capabilities.

4. ACT: The Agentic Coding Explosion

4.1 The Benchmark Reality Check

The SWE-Bench family has fragmented into five complementary benchmarks, each addressing a different failure mode of the original:

SWE-Bench Pro [9]: 1,865 problems across 41 repositories spanning Python, JavaScript, TypeScript, and Go. Three-tier contamination resistance (public/commercial/held-out). Best model performance remains below 45% Pass@1. The paper reports that without human augmentations specifying requirements and interfaces, the best-performing frontier model drops from 25.9% to 8.4% (model designations reflect the authors' projected capability tiers as of late 2025).

SWE-EVO [10]: Tests what SWE-Bench can't — continuous software evolution. 48 tasks from release notes of mature projects, averaging 21 files edited and 874 tests per instance. The best-performing frontier model in the study achieves only 21% on SWE-EVO versus 65% on SWE-Bench Verified — a 44 percentage-point drop that reveals the gap between single-issue resolution and long-horizon evolution (model designations reflect the authors' projected capability tiers).

SWE-bench Live [11]: Automated pipeline (REPOLAUNCH) for continuous benchmark updates from 93 repositories. Agents show "substantial performance gap compared to static benchmarks," suggesting overfitting to fixed test sets.

Saving SWE-Bench [12]: Transforms formal GitHub issues into realistic user queries based on telemetry analysis. Reveals 20–50% overestimation on public benchmarks. Users write 10–30 word queries with error stacks; GitHub issues contain 100+ words with reproduction code.

UTBoost [13]: LLM-generated test augmentation finds 345 erroneous patches incorrectly labeled as passing, impacting 40.9% of SWE-Bench Lite leaderboard entries.

The collective implication: reported capabilities are inflated by contamination, over-specified descriptions, insufficient tests, and benchmark saturation. The real state of the art is considerably below headline numbers.

4.2 Self-Improving Agents

Live-SWE-agent[10]SWE-agent → [14] achieves 77.4% on SWE-bench Verified, establishing state-of-the-art performance with zero offline training cost. Operating from a minimal bash-only scaffold, the agent dynamically creates custom tools during problem-solving. The reflection loop is critical; agents do not spontaneously create tools without explicit prompting to consider their generation. Model capability strongly influences performance: the study reports a +22.6% improvement for the highest-capability frontier model tested and a -68.2% degradation for the lowest-capability variant (model designations reflect the authors' projected capability tiers). These results suggest a minimum model capability threshold for effective self-improvement.

Self-Play SWE-RL [15] trains a single LLM in a self-play setting, acting as both bug-injector and bug-solver. This approach requires only sandboxed repositories with source code, reducing dependence on human-generated issues or tests during the RL phase. On SWE-bench Verified, it achieved a +10.4 point improvement over its pre-RL checkpoint, consistently outperforming human-data RL baselines throughout training. This demonstrates the capacity of agents to generate their own training curriculum from raw codebases.

RepoNavigator [23] demonstrates that a single execution-aware tool (e.g., "jump" to a symbol's definition via a language server) combined with RL training can outperform multi-tool agents utilizing significantly larger models. Specifically, a 7B model surpassed 14B baselines, and a 14B model outperformed 32B competitors.

Collectively, these results indicate the efficacy of approaches centered on self-generation and learning. Live-SWE-agent's state-of-the-art performance is achieved through self-generated tools, Self-Play SWE-RL demonstrates the utility of self-generated training data, and RepoNavigator highlights the effectiveness of minimal tool design combined with RL. Each of these methods contributes to performance levels that surpass or are competitive with alternatives relying on more extensively engineered components. This reinforces the current research trajectory favoring learned over hand-engineered agent capabilities, as identified in Section 3, and extends that trajectory to the coding domain.

4.3 The 60/20 Paradox

Anthropic's 2026 Agentic Coding Trends Report documents that engineers utilize AI in approximately 60% of their work but fully delegate only 0–20%. The report interprets this observation as reflecting the nature of effective human-AI collaboration. It suggests that engineers develop "intuitions for AI delegation" over time, leading to the delegation of easily verifiable or low-stakes tasks while conceptually difficult or design-dependent work is retained. Furthermore, the report indicates that approximately 27% of AI-assisted work comprises tasks that would not have been undertaken otherwise, suggesting that AI contributes to the creation of new categories of work rather than solely replacing existing ones.

5. INTERACT: Protocol Infrastructure

5.1 The Protocol Stack

Before standardized protocols, connecting N AI models to M external tools required N×M custom integrations. Two protocols now address this at different layers [24]:

MCP (Model Context Protocol, Anthropic): JSON-RPC 2.0 client-server interface for tool invocation. Four primitives: Tools (model-controlled), Resources (application-controlled), Prompts (user-controlled), Sampling (server-initiated).

A2A (Agent-to-Agent Protocol, Google): Agent Cards for capability-based discovery. Peer-to-peer task delegation via HTTPS + SSE.

The emerging consensus suggests that MCP and A2A compose at different layers, analogous to TCP/IP. MCP is utilized for precise tool-level execution, while A2A is intended for scalable agent-level routing.

5.2 Governance

The Agentic AI Foundation (AAIF), scheduled for launch in December 2025 under the Linux Foundation, is designed to establish a framework for co-governance. Six competing companies — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, AWS, and Block — agreed to jointly steward interoperability standards. This collaborative structure aims to foster the development of interoperability standards, a factor often considered critical for enterprise adoption of new protocols.

5.3 Orchestration Evidence

In 348 controlled trials comparing single-agent versus multi-agent orchestration on incident response [16], multi-agent systems demonstrated 100% actionable recommendations within the trial parameters, compared to 1.7% for single agents. These systems also showed an 80-fold improvement in specificity and a 140-fold improvement in correctness relative to single-agent systems. Both systems exhibited comparable latency, approximately 40 seconds; the primary differentiation was observed in their quality and determinism, rather than processing speed. Multi-agent systems demonstrated a quality variance approaching zero (σ ≈ 0), suggesting a high degree of reliability and consistency for operational deployment, whereas single-agent outputs exhibited inconsistency.

5.4 Difficulty-Aware Routing

DAAO [25] dynamically generates query-specific multi-agent workflows guided by predicted query difficulty using a VAE-based estimator. A heterogeneous LLM router pairs each subtask with the optimal model. Result: +3.2–10.2% accuracy at 41% of the inference cost, generalizing to unseen model backbones.

5.5 Flow Engineering

The production shift is toward controllable orchestration — explicit workflow graphs (LangGraph-style state machines) rather than open-ended chat loops [26]. Developers define state transitions and guardrails; models fill in local decisions. This trades theoretical autonomy for practical reliability, prioritizing debuggability and human approvals.

6. OPERATE: The Security Surface

This section develops aspects of the security surface that Plaat et al. identified as requiring future work.

6.1 Protocol-Level Vulnerabilities

The first formal MCP security analysis [17] identified three architectural vulnerabilities: (1) absence of capability attestation allowing servers to self-declare arbitrary permissions, (2) bidirectional sampling without origin authentication enabling server-side prompt injection, and (3) implicit trust propagation in multi-server configurations where tool responses from Server A influence invocations on Server B.

Through 847 attack scenarios across five MCP servers and three LLM backends, MCP's architecture was shown to amplify attack success by 23–41% compared to equivalent non-MCP integrations. Cross-server propagation (61.3% ASR) and sampling-based injection (67.2% ASR) represent entirely new attack classes that don't exist without the protocol.

The proposed AttestMCP defense — capability attestation, message authentication, origin tagging, isolation enforcement — reduces overall attack success from 52.8% to 12.4% with 8.3ms median latency overhead. The residual 12.4% reflects indirect injection through legitimately-retrieved content, a limitation inherent to LLM behavior that is not directly addressable at the protocol layer.

SMCP [27] complements this with the most detailed threat-per-workflow-step mapping: 19 distinct threats across 6 MCP workflow steps, with five protocol-level security enhancements (unified identity, mutual authentication, security context propagation, fine-grained policy enforcement, comprehensive audit logging).

6.2 Defense Failure

A systematization of 78 studies [18] catalogs 42 distinct attack techniques and reveals that all tested defense mechanisms fail under adaptive attacks:

DefenseReported ASRAdaptive ASR
Protect AI<5%93%
PromptGuard<3%91%
PIGuard<5%89%
Model Armor<10%78%

The consistent ineffectiveness of these tested defense mechanisms under adaptive attacks suggests a need for architectural mitigation strategies for prompt injection, moving beyond approaches primarily focused on input filtering. A parallel can be drawn to SQL injection, a vulnerability that was effectively addressed through architectural changes, such as parameterized queries, rather than solely through input filtering.

6.3 Data Over-Exposure

AgentRaft [19] defines a distinct risk class: Data Over-Exposure (DOE), where agents inadvertently transmit sensitive data beyond user intent. Unlike prompt injection (adversarial), DOE is systemic — caused by overly broad tool data provision and LLMs' lack of contextual privacy awareness. Testing on 6,675 real-world MCP tools found that 57.07% of tool call chains exhibit unauthorized sensitive data exposure and 65.42% of transmitted data fields are over-exposed.

6.4 The Skill Supply Chain

Three concurrent studies provide the first empirical characterization of skill security [20]. Analysis of 42,447 skills from two major marketplaces found 26.1% contain at least one vulnerability across 14 distinct patterns. Behavioral verification of 98,380 skills from community registries identified 157 confirmed malicious skills with 632 vulnerabilities. Skills bundling executable scripts are 2.12× more likely to contain vulnerabilities. The speed of the ClawHub incident — 14 malicious skills within 72 hours of marketplace launch — highlights the need for robust security measures in skill ecosystems, potentially comparable to those employed in package management. However, dedicated tooling for this purpose is not yet widely available.

6.5 The Security-Capability Tradeoff

Platform vulnerability assessment [18] reveals an inverse correlation between security and capability permissiveness: Claude Code achieves the lowest vulnerability rating (Low) through aggressive sandboxing, while Cursor rates Critical due to extensive extension trust. This inverse correlation suggests that approaches at either extreme may face limitations in broad adoption or practical utility. The most viable long-term approach involves developing systems that achieve high capability through architectural security, rather than primarily through capability restriction.

7. Cross-Cutting: The Portable Agent Layer

7.1 SKILL.md as Emerging Standard

The emergence of a portable configuration layer across competing vendor tools has been observed. SKILL.md files — Markdown documents with YAML frontmatter — define reusable agent capabilities that operate across Claude Code, Gemini CLI, Cursor, Codex CLI, and four other tools without modification [28, 29].

The SoK on Agentic Skills [28] formalizes this: a skill is a four-tuple S = (C, π, T, R) — applicability condition, executable policy, termination condition, and reusable callable interface — distinguishing skills from tools (atomic, no decision-making), plans (one-time, session-scoped), and prompt templates (static text, no termination logic). Seven design patterns capture how skills are packaged and executed in practice, from metadata-driven progressive disclosure to self-evolving libraries and marketplace distribution.

The Agent Skills Survey [29] documents the progressive disclosure architecture: Level 1 metadata (~30 tokens/skill, always loaded) acts as a "menu" for intent matching; Level 2 instructions (200–2K tokens) are loaded on trigger; Level 3 resources (scripts, references) are loaded on demand. This prevents context bloat — the agentic equivalent of lazy loading.

Antigravity Awesome Skills (v8.6.0, March 2026) bundles 1,300+ skills with official contributions from Anthropic, Vercel Labs, OpenAI, Supabase, Microsoft, Google DeepMind[11]Google DeepMind →, Sentry, Trail of Bits, Expo, and Hugging Face. The universal SKILL.md format decouples skill content from tool-specific invocation, establishing a cross-vendor standard.

7.2 Configuration Convergence

Per-project agent configuration is emerging under different filenames: CLAUDE.md (Anthropic), AGENTS.md (OpenAI/AAIF), .cursorrules (Cursor), .github/copilot-instructions.md (GitHub). The function of such configuration files typically encompasses defining elements such as coding standards, workflow constraints, tool permissions, and project-specific context. This suggests a trend toward semantic convergence, notwithstanding the absence of format unification.

7.3 Five Configuration Layers

Five distinct layers are emerging:

1. Model Config (system prompt): Provider-defined, always loaded, highest trust

2. Project Config (CLAUDE.md / AGENTS.md): Team-defined, per-repository, version-controlled

3. Skills (SKILL.md): Community-contributed, cross-project, on-demand loading

4. Tools (MCP servers): Developer-configured, per-integration, OAuth-scoped

5. Agent-to-Agent (A2A Agent Cards): Cross-organizational discovery, capability-based

Trust decreases moving down the stack. Security requirements increase accordingly.

8. Synthesis: Five Findings and a Research Agenda

8.1 Finding 1: Evidence for Scaffold-to-Weights Migration and its Acceleration

Analysis of papers presented in Section 3 indicates a consistent trajectory: capabilities initially achieved through prompt engineering in 2024 are increasingly being realized via reinforcement learning (RL) training by 2026. This trend suggests that functionalities such as planning, tool use, memory, self-improvement, and context management are progressively being integrated within the model's internal architecture. While the mechanistic question—whether RL primarily amplifies existing capabilities or generates novel ones—remains unresolved [2], the practical shift in implementation is evident. Specifically, Agentic Critical Training [22] is cited as providing strong evidence that reinforcement learning within agentic environments can yield capabilities that generalize beyond their initial training domains.

8.2 Finding 2: Self-Improvement Outperforms Hand-Crafting

Three independent results converge: Live-SWE-agent (self-generated tools, 77.4%) outperforms all manually designed scaffolds. Self-Play SWE-RL (self-generated training data, +10.4) surpasses human-data RL throughout training. RepoNavigator (one tool + RL, 7B beats 14B) outperforms multi-tool agents with larger models. These findings consistently suggest that allowing agents to learn their own capabilities tends to yield superior performance compared to manual engineering of those capabilities. However, this approach necessitates sufficiently capable base models; below a certain capability threshold, self-improvement has been observed to degrade performance rather than enhance it.

8.3 Finding 3: Benchmarks Overestimate and Production Underdelivers

The SWE-Bench family collectively demonstrates that reported performance metrics can be inflated due to factors such as data contamination [11], overly specific problem descriptions [12], inadequate testing methodologies [13], and task saturation on isolated problems [9, 10]. The observed performance drop in SWE-EVO from 65% to 21% provides evidence that current agents may lack sustained multi-file reasoning capabilities, given the design of SWE-EVO to assess such complex interactions. Concurrently, the 60/20 paradox, which indicates that 60% of development work incorporates AI while only 0–20% is fully delegated, suggests that production adoption is occurring but is constrained by the necessity of human oversight and judgment.

8.4 Finding 4: Interaction as Infrastructure

Plaat et al. investigated the potential for agent collaboration and the emergent behaviors that could arise. Addressing these questions reveals that interaction itself presents fundamental challenges related to infrastructure, governance, and security. The design of interoperable systems often employs a "compose-not-compete" pattern (e.g., MCP for tools, A2A for agents), which exhibits structural parallels to the TCP/IP model in facilitating standardized communication. Furthermore, in production environments, flow engineering—characterized by explicit state machines with defined guardrails—has demonstrated advantages in reliability and control compared to approaches relying solely on fully autonomous loops.

8.5 Finding 5: Security Is Architectural, Not Patchable

Both MCP security analyses [17, 27] independently conclude that the protocol's security weaknesses are architectural — patching individual CVEs will not resolve fundamental issues such as absent capability attestation and implicit trust propagation. The SoK [18] finds every tested defense bypassed at 78%+ rates. AgentRaft [19] identifies a risk class (Data Over-Exposure) that exists independently of adversarial attacks. The skill supply chain [20] faces npm-style threats, which are exacerbated by skills having direct system access. Prompt injection is characterized as a first-class vulnerability, indicating a need for architectural mitigation over ad-hoc filtering.

8.6 Research Agenda

Closing the long-horizon gap. The observed 65%→21% drop in SWE-EVO's performance represents a significant open problem. Agents capable of sustaining multi-file reasoning across extended work periods, maintaining coherence across interdependent changes, and recovering from errors during execution have the potential to enhance software engineering productivity. This likely requires advances in both model capability (longer effective context) and training methodology (RL on evolution tasks, not just issue resolution).

Agentic scaling laws. Existing scaling laws [6] were measured for pure reasoning RL. When agents utilize tools, the offloading of deterministic computations to these tools, allowing learning to focus on high-level decision making, is hypothesized to shift the scaling curve upward. Measuring agentic scaling laws across tool-augmented settings is essential for achieving compute-optimal training allocation.

Protocol-level security. AttestMCP [17] and SMCP [27] propose complementary protocol extensions. Incorporating these into the MCP specification itself — rather than leaving them as optional add-ons — is proposed as a method to address architectural vulnerabilities at the protocol layer. The identity gap across MCP/A2A/A2UI (no unified identity flowing across protocol layers) is a specific unsolved problem.

Data Over-Exposure mitigation. AgentRaft [19] quantifies the problem (57% of tool chains leak data) but does not provide a solution. Architectures that enforce data minimization at the protocol layer — where tools return only the data requested, not their full schema — are proposed to mitigate the underlying causes of data over-exposure.

Skill governance at scale. The 26.1% vulnerability rate [20] across 42K skills indicates a need for supply chain security rigor comparable to that applied to package managers (npm, pip). Signed skill manifests, sandboxed execution, behavioral verification before marketplace listing, and trust-tiered deployment permissions are identified as necessary components. The SoK's proposed governance framework [28] provides the conceptual architecture; the implementation presents an engineering challenge.

Bridging benchmarks and production. The observed gap between SWE-bench scores and production deployment experience (the 60/20 paradox) suggests that current benchmarks may not fully capture the relevant aspects of production performance. Benchmarks designed to evaluate sustained, multi-session, human-collaborative coding workflows, rather than isolated issue resolution, could offer improved prediction of production value.

9. Limitations

We read 28 papers at varying depth (11–100%), not the comprehensive coverage of Plaat et al.'s 300+ references. Embodied agents, GUI agents, and scientific discovery agents — categories Plaat et al. covered well — are acknowledged but not re-reviewed. Our industry source analysis relies on public reports and web research rather than proprietary deployment data. The forward citation sweep (6 papers) covers only papers indexed by Semantic Scholar by March 23, 2026; concurrent work submitted but not yet indexed is necessarily missing.

Our security analysis of coding platforms [18] relies on the SoK authors' methodology and may not capture defenses deployed after January 2026. The Data Over-Exposure statistics [19] come from a single study on MCP.so-listed tools and may not represent the full MCP ecosystem.

10. Conclusion

Between January 2025 and March 2026, agentic LLM capabilities underwent four structural shifts. These shifts motivate the consideration of extending Plaat et al.'s three-axis taxonomy with a fourth axis, provisionally termed 'Operate,' to more comprehensively categorize emerging developments. Specifically, reasoning capabilities migrated from external scaffolds to model weights via reinforcement learning. Software development emerged as a primary action domain, with self-improving agents demonstrating superior performance compared to hand-crafted alternatives. Interaction evolved from a research topic into an infrastructure and governance challenge, characterized by standardized protocols managed by competing entities under neutral stewardship. Furthermore, the safety surface, previously deferred in prior surveys, became a central engineering challenge, marked by architectural vulnerabilities amplifying attacks, widespread defense failures, and systemic data leakage.

The research focus has shifted from basic feasibility — whether agents can reason, act, and interact — to the challenges of enabling these capabilities safely, reliably, and economically at production scale. Current evidence suggests these challenges remain largely unresolved.
