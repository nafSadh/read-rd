PHYSICS · PSYCHOLOGY · POETRY · 43 SOURCES

The Geometry of Feeling

Retrocausality, hope, and depression — a survey connecting the block universe, bidirectional causation, prospective psychology, and somatic experience, synthesized as source material for poetry.

March 2026 7 Dimensions 43 Sources 6 Tensions
ABSTRACT

Abstract

The block universe, retrocausality, and prospective psychology converge on a single insight: the future is not something that happens to us — it is something that shapes us now. In the geometry of spacetime, all moments coexist. Hope and depression are not reactions to the future; they are the future’s fingerprint on the present. This is not metaphor. Or rather: the metaphor and the physics point at the same structure.

This survey maps the territory across seven dimensions — the block universe, retrocausality, hope as prospection, depression as negative prospection, free will and agency, embodiment, and literary applications — drawing on 43 sources spanning physics, psychology, philosophy, somatic therapy, and fiction. The project’s purpose is not to prove that retrocausality causes hope. It is to build the conceptual scaffolding for poetry that can hold both the physics and the phenomenology without collapsing either into the other.

SECTION 01

The Block Universe

Special relativity eliminates absolute simultaneity. If two observers moving at different velocities disagree about whether events X and Y happen at the same time, there is no physical basis for preferring one judgment. The Rietdijk-Putnam argument (1966–67) formalizes the implication: if an event can be in one observer’s future and another’s past simultaneously, that event must already exist[2]IEP: Eternalism →. All times coexist. Your birth and your death are equally real. “Now” is an address, not an edge.

Sean Carroll’s crucial contribution is the entropy connection: the arrow of time is not fundamental but thermodynamic[5]Carroll: From Eternity to Here (2010) →. The second law of thermodynamics is the only law of physics that distinguishes past from future. All other fundamental equations are time-symmetric. We remember the past because it was lower-entropy. We experience flow because our brains are entropy-increasing processes. The Past Hypothesis — that the universe started in an extraordinarily low-entropy state — is a boundary condition, not a consequence of the laws.

Lee Smolin dissents: time is physically fundamental, the laws of physics themselves evolve, and the future is genuinely open[7]Smolin: Time Reborn (2013) →. Carlo Rovelli goes further than Carroll: not just “time is emergent” but “time doesn’t exist” at the fundamental level[25]Rovelli: The Order of Time (2018) →. What exists is change between events. We are the source of time.

SECTION 02

Retrocausality & Bidirectional Causation

Fundamental physics is time-symmetric. Run any equation backward and it still works. Huw Price (2012) showed that if the quantum state is real and quantum mechanics is time-symmetric, retrocausality follows necessarily[9]Price: Does Time-Symmetry Imply Retrocausality? (2012) →. Leifer and Pusey (2017) generalized this: even if the quantum state is merely epistemic, time-symmetry still entails backward causation. They constructed a temporal analog of Bell’s theorem[11]Leifer & Pusey: Bell’s Theorem in Time (2017) →.

Aharonov’s Two-State Vector Formalism characterizes the quantum state at any moment by two state vectors: one propagating forward from preparation, one propagating backward from future measurement[15]Aharonov: Two-State Vector Formalism →. The present is the intersection of past and future boundary conditions. Cramer’s Transactional Interpretation models quantum events as “handshakes” between an offer wave (forward in time) and a confirmation wave (backward in time)[14]Cramer: Transactional Interpretation (1986) →. The outcome was negotiated across time.

Price and Wharton’s Aeon essay (2016) makes this accessible through the Alice/Ecila mirror metaphor[12]Price & Wharton: Retrocausality in Aeon (2016) →. Their 2026 follow-up introduces Berkson’s bias as the mechanism behind Bell correlations[13]Price & Wharton: Berkson’s Bias (The Conversation, 2026) →. Retrocausality is becoming increasingly concrete.

SECTION 03

Hope as Retrocausal Signal

Seligman, Railton, Baumeister, and Sripada (2013) initiated a paradigm shift: the central organizing feature of human cognition is not the past but prospection — the representation of possible futures[16]Seligman et al.: Navigating Into the Future (2013) →. The brain’s default circuit — activated when subjects are told to “think of nothing” — is functionally identical to the circuit activated when subjects deliberately imagine future events. We are, at rest, prospecting. The default mode of human consciousness is future-oriented.

Memory, in this framework, is not a file drawer but a hope chest of possibilities[19]Prospective Psychology (UPenn) →. Our memories are narratives constructed for forward guidance, not accurate recordings. Emotions follow the same logic: Barrett’s constructed emotion theory shows that emotions are predictions about what’s coming, not reactions to what’s here[24]Barrett: How Emotions Are Made (2017) →. Snyder’s Hope Theory decomposes hope into pathways thinking and agency thinking — a cognitive set, not a warm feeling[20]Snyder: The Psychology of Hope (2000) →.

The Bridge

Seligman explicitly denies backward causation. But in the block universe, the future is real. “Present representations of possible future states” are representations of something that, under eternalism, actually exists. Hope is the advanced wave traveling backward from the version of yourself that made it through.

SECTION 04

Depression as Bidirectional Loop

Roepke and Seligman (2016) reframe depression: negative prospection is not a symptom but the core causal element[22]Roepke & Seligman: Depression and Prospection (2016) →. The depressed mind doesn’t fail to predict the future — it predicts the future too well, and believes every dark simulation. Beck’s cognitive triad has included the future since 1976, but clinical practice has overwhelmingly focused on past trauma[23]Beck: Cognitive Triad (1976) →.

Andy Clark’s predictive processing framework casts the brain as a prediction machine. Depression is a regime of pessimistic priors with excessively high precision weighting[23b]Clark: Surfing Uncertainty (2015) →. The predictions are so strong that positive evidence is discounted. The person is epistemically trapped — the belief system has made itself unfalsifiable. Combined with Barrett’s constructed emotion theory, the loop is self-sealing: the system predicts pain, generates somatic states consistent with pain, then interprets those states as confirming the prediction.

The Retrocausal Mirror

If hope is the advanced wave from a successful outcome, depression is the advanced wave from a bleak one. But Carroll’s compatibilism offers the escape: the block also contains the person who started therapy, who broke the loop. The intervention is already there. You can’t see it from inside the depressed slice, but it’s in the geometry.

SECTION 05

Free Will & Agency

Carroll’s compatibilism holds that free will operates at a different level of description than particle physics[28]Carroll: Free Will Is as Real as Baseball (2011) →. When choosing a shirt in the morning, “it doesn’t help me to say ‘I’m gonna do whatever my atoms want me to do.’ My atoms have no wants.” Just as the arrow of time is macroscopic while fundamental physics is time-reversible, free will is macroscopic while fundamental physics is deterministic. Neither is illusory — both are real features of an emergent description[6]Carroll: The Big Picture / Poetic Naturalism (2016) →.

Rovelli is less patient: the philosophical debate over free will and determinism is based on conceptual confusions that Spinoza resolved four centuries ago[32]Rovelli interview: Time, Free Will, Entropy →. For a writer, Carroll’s framework is powerful: it validates the vocabulary of human experience without requiring supernatural foundations. The poem about choosing doesn’t need to resolve the determinism question. The poem is the emergent-level description where choice is real.

SECTION 06

Time, Memory & Embodiment

Against the backdrop of timelessness, the body insists on time. Peter Levine’s Somatic Experiencing documents how the autonomic nervous system stores unresolved survival responses[34]Levine: Waking the Tiger (1997) →. Van der Kolk confirms at the neurological level: trauma rewires the brain and body together. Flashbacks are not memories in the narrative sense — they are re-activations of the original physiological state[36]van der Kolk: The Body Keeps the Score (2014) →. Porges’ polyvagal theory provides the mechanism: the vagus nerve mediates between social engagement, fight/flight, and freeze, based on neuroception — the body’s automatic assessment of safety[35]Porges: Polyvagal Theory (2011) →.

In block terms: trauma is a region of the block with specific physical configurations. The body, moving along its worldline, carries the thermodynamic trace. Healing — through SE, EMDR, boxing, dance — is the body’s worldline curving away from that trace. The curve was always in the block. The therapy is part of the geometry.

SECTION 07

Literary Applications

The best temporal fiction doesn’t explain the physics — it enacts it. Vonnegut’s Billy Pilgrim, unstuck in time, experiences his life non-sequentially; the Tralfamadorians respond to death with “So it goes” — not indifference but the recognition that the dead person is fine in plenty of other moments[37]Vonnegut: Slaughterhouse-Five (1969) →. Ted Chiang’s Louise Banks learns a language that requires knowing how a sentence ends before you can begin writing it — retrocausality as linguistics[38]Chiang: Story of Your Life (1998) →. Louise “chooses” to have her daughter despite foreknowing the death. But choice dissolves in simultaneous perception. Grief and joy are concurrent.

T.S. Eliot gives the poet permission: “Time present and time past / Are both perhaps present in time future”[41]Eliot: Four Quartets (1943) →. Borges anticipated many-worlds before Everett[40]Borges: The Garden of Forking Paths (1941) →. The physics and the literature converge on six structural conceits for poetry: temporal juxtaposition, bidirectional address, the observer split, gelato as measurement, the Tralfamadorian move, and Heptapod syntax.

SECTION 08

Six Tensions

T1: Is Time Fundamental or Emergent?

Smolin says time is physically real and fundamental[7]Smolin: Time Reborn →. Rovelli says time emerges from entropy and ignorance[25]Rovelli: The Order of Time →. Barbour says time doesn’t exist at all[26]Barbour: The End of Time →. Carroll bridges: “different ways of talking about the world.”

T2: Retrocausality — Real Physics or Fringe?

Price and Wharton argue it’s the most natural reading of time-symmetric QM[9]Price: Time-Symmetry → Retrocausality →. Mainstream physics treats it as a curiosity. Bell himself dismissed it — confusing it with superdeterminism. Leifer: “the onus is on us to flesh out the idea.”

T3: Prospection vs. Retrocausality — Metaphor or Mechanism?

Seligman explicitly denies backward causation: “guidance not by the future itself but by present representations.”[16]Seligman et al. (2013) → But the phenomenology maps perfectly onto retrocausal language. Is the mapping merely poetic, or does it reveal something structural?

T4: Depression — Broken Machinery or Accurate Perception?

Clinical psychology says fix the prediction machinery[22]Roepke & Seligman (2016) →. The retrocausal metaphor says the machinery might be working correctly — it’s the input (the future boundary condition) that’s the problem. The therapeutic answer must be the first. The poetic truth may be the second.

T5: Compatibilism’s Adequacy

Carroll says free will is real at the emergent level[28]Carroll: Free Will Is as Real as Baseball →. Critics say compatibilists changed the subject — the felt experience of authorship is what matters, and calling emergent patterns “free will” is semantic sleight of hand.

T6: Body Time vs. Block Time

The body keeps the score[36]van der Kolk (2014) →. But in a block universe, what is it scoring? The body doesn’t experience the block — it experiences the gradient. Trauma is a distortion of the gradient: the body gets stuck at a particular entropy configuration. Healing is the worldline curving away.

SECTION 09

Methods & Limitations

This survey synthesizes 43 sources across physics (Carroll, Rovelli, Smolin, Price, Wharton, Leifer, Aharonov, Cramer), psychology (Seligman, Roepke, Beck, Clark, Barrett, Snyder), somatic therapy (Levine, van der Kolk, Porges), philosophy (SEP, IEP), and literature (Vonnegut, Chiang, Borges, Eliot, Nolan). The project is deliberately interdisciplinary — it connects domains that rarely speak to each other because the connections are the point.

The central limitation is honest: this is not a physics paper. Retrocausality is a minority interpretation of quantum mechanics. The mapping between prospection and retrocausality is a structural analogy, not a causal claim. The value of the project is not empirical but conceptual and aesthetic — it provides vocabulary and structure for poetry about time, grief, and hope that is informed by physics without being constrained by it. The metaphor and the mechanism point at the same structure. Whether they are the same structure is a question this survey raises but cannot answer.
