# S5: Free Will, Agency & Determinism

> "My atoms have no wants. There is something that I have that is a want, and the fact that I'm made of atoms doesn't make that go away." — Sean Carroll

**Sources:** 5 (Carroll "Free Will Is as Real as Baseball" 2011, Carroll Big Think 2022, Carroll "Consciousness and Downward Causation" 2016, SEP Compatibilism, Rovelli/Borealism interview)
**Adequacy:** ✅ Adequate (minimum)

---

## 1. The Compatibilist Case

Compatibilism holds that free will is compatible with determinism because free will operates at a different level of description than particle physics. Carroll's formulation: when choosing a shirt in the morning, "it doesn't help me to say 'I'm gonna do whatever my atoms want me to do.' My atoms have no wants." The vocabulary of choice, agency, and deliberation is a higher-level emergent description of a physical system that is also describable (less usefully, at human scale) in terms of atoms and forces.

The analogy to time is direct: just as the arrow of time is macroscopic (entropy) while fundamental physics is time-reversible, free will is macroscopic (agency) while fundamental physics is deterministic. Neither the arrow of time nor free will is "illusory" — both are real features of an emergent description that is perfectly compatible with the underlying physics.

## 2. Poetic Naturalism

Carroll's broader framework: naturalism (the natural world is all there is) + poetic (there are multiple valid ways of talking about it). Any description that is (1) useful and (2) compatible with fundamental physics "deserves a place at the table." Agency, choice, meaning, purpose — all are valid descriptions at the emergent level. They don't violate physics; they describe physics at a different resolution.

This is powerful for a writer because it validates the vocabulary of human experience without requiring supernatural foundations. The poem about choosing doesn't need to resolve the determinism question. The poem IS the emergent-level description where choice is real.

## 3. Rovelli's Dismissal

Rovelli, characteristically, is less patient: "the philosophical debate over free will and determinism is based on silly conceptual confusions" that Spinoza resolved four centuries ago. Spinoza's compatibilism: we are free insofar as we act according to our own nature, even if that nature is determined. Freedom is acting from internal causes rather than external compulsion. The *source* of the action matters, not whether it was predetermined.

## 4. The Consequence Argument and Its Limits

The standard anti-compatibilist argument: if the past is fixed and the laws of nature are fixed, then the future is fixed, and we can't do otherwise. Carroll's counter: this is true at the level of atoms but irrelevant at the level of agents. We can't *compute* what the atoms will do (insufficient information, computational boundedness), so we must reason as agents making choices. The impossibility of prediction is not a limitation — it's the *reason* agency is a useful description.

## 5. What Sources Agree On

- Libertarian free will (the ability to have done otherwise, given identical conditions) does not exist
- Determinism (or near-determinism) holds at the fundamental level
- Some form of agency or choice is a valid and useful concept at the human level

## 6. Where Sources Disagree

- **Carroll vs hard determinists**: Carroll says free will is real (compatibilism). Hard determinists say compatibilism changes the subject — that "free will" should mean libertarian free will, and if that doesn't exist, the concept is bankrupt
- **Felt experience**: Critics note that most people *feel* they have libertarian free will. Compatibilism may be philosophically correct but psychologically misleading
- **Implications for moral responsibility**: If choices are determined, is punishment justified? Carroll says yes (at the emergent level). Others find this insufficient
