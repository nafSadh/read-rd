# S4: Depression as Bidirectional Loop

> The depressed mind doesn't fail to predict the future. It predicts the future too well — and believes every dark simulation.

**Sources:** 5 (Roepke & Seligman 2016, Beck Cognitive Triad, Seligman Learned Helplessness, Clark Predictive Processing, Barrett Constructed Emotion)
**Adequacy:** ✅ Adequate (minimum)

---

## 1. Negative Prospection as Core Cause

Roepke and Seligman's reframing: negative prospection is not a *symptom* of depression but its *core causal element*. Beck's cognitive triad (negative view of self, world, and future) has included the future since 1976, but clinical practice has overwhelmingly focused on past trauma and present cognition. CBT adherence protocols list 32 procedures; none explicitly target prospection.

The evidence: depressed people struggle to generate vivid positive future simulations. Their autobiographical memory is vague, especially for positive events. Memory being an input for prospection, blurred positive memories impair the construction of positive futures. Executive functioning deficits compound this — the depressed person can't override default negative projections.

## 2. Predictive Processing: The Cage of Priors

Andy Clark's framework casts the brain as a prediction machine that constantly generates top-down models and checks them against bottom-up sensory data. Depression, in this frame, is a regime of pessimistic priors with excessively high precision weighting. The predictions are so strong that positive evidence is discounted or reinterpreted. The person is epistemically trapped — they can't update beliefs because the belief system has made itself unfalsifiable.

This connects to Barrett's constructed emotion theory: if emotions are predictions about the future, depression is a system that predicts pain, generates the somatic states consistent with pain (fatigue, withdrawal, appetite loss), and then interprets those somatic states as confirming the prediction. The loop is self-sealing.

## 3. Learned Helplessness: The Historical Model

Seligman's original work (1975) showed that animals exposed to inescapable shocks later failed to escape even when escape was possible. The experience of uncontrollability generated a *expectation* of future uncontrollability — a prospective, not retrospective, phenomenon. This is the laboratory model of depression, and it's fundamentally about the future.

## 4. The Retrocausal Mirror

In block universe terms: if hope is the advanced wave from a successful outcome, depression is the advanced wave from a bleak one. The depressed person's body isn't malfunctioning — it's reading the block accurately, if the block contains the bleak outcome. The depression is the future's fingerprint on the present.

But Carroll's compatibilism offers the escape: the block also contains the person who started therapy, who broke the loop. The intervention is already there. You can't see it from inside the depressed slice, but it's in the geometry. This is where Tension T4 lands: is depression broken machinery (clinical) or accurate perception (retrocausal)? The therapeutic answer must be the first. The poetic truth may be the second.

## 5. What Sources Agree On

- Depression involves systematically negative future projections
- These projections are causal, not merely symptomatic
- The loop between prediction and behavior is self-reinforcing
- Interventions that target future thinking (not just past trauma) are promising

## 6. Where Sources Disagree

- **Roepke vs traditional CBT**: How central is prospection? Roepke says it's everything. Traditional CBT says it's one of many targets
- **Predictive processing vs folk psychology**: Clark's framework is mechanistic. It implies depression is computational, not personal — which has radically different implications for how we think about blame, responsibility, and healing
- **Clinical vs retrocausal**: The clinical view says fix the machinery. The retrocausal metaphor says the machinery might be working correctly — it's the input (the future boundary condition) that's the problem
