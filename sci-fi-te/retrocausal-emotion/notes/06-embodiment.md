# S6: Time, Memory & Embodiment

## 1. Rovelli: Time as Thermal Emergence

The Order of Time dismantles time layer by layer. Special relativity: no universal now. General relativity: time varies with gravity. Quantum gravity: time may not exist at the fundamental level. What remains is entropy — the only law that distinguishes past from future. Rovelli's "thermal time" hypothesis: time is what we experience when we have partial information about a system. A being with complete microscopic knowledge would see no time — only a static configuration. "We are the source of time."

This maps directly onto the prospection framework (S3): if time is a product of ignorance, and the brain's default mode is future-projection, then our experience of time *is* our prospection. The arrow of time and the direction of anticipation are the same phenomenon — entropy gradient perceived from within.

## 2. Barbour: Platonia

Julian Barbour's more radical position: time literally does not exist. What exists is a vast configuration space ("Platonia") containing every possible arrangement of the universe. Each "now" is a self-contained point in this space. The illusion of time arises from the structure of certain special configurations that contain records (memories, fossils, light from distant stars) that create the appearance of a history. This is eternalism taken to its logical extreme.

## 3. The Body as Timekeeper

Against this backdrop of timelessness, the body insists on time. Peter Levine's Somatic Experiencing documents how the autonomic nervous system stores unresolved survival responses. A trauma that the mind has "processed" may still be active in the body — locked in fight/flight/freeze patterns that persist years after the threat has passed. The body has its own temporal logic, one that resists narrative integration.

Van der Kolk's work confirms this at the neurological level: trauma rewires the brain and body together. Flashbacks are not "memories" in the narrative sense — they are re-activations of the original physiological state. The body is not remembering the past; it is *re-entering* it.

Porges' polyvagal theory provides the mechanism: the vagus nerve mediates between three states (social engagement, fight/flight, freeze) based on neuroception — the body's automatic assessment of safety or danger. This assessment operates below conscious awareness and on timescales faster than narrative thought. The body is assessing the present moment through a lens ground by all previous moments.

## 4. Body Time vs Block Time (Tension T6)

If all moments coexist equally in the block, why does the body carry the past forward so stubbornly? One answer: the body doesn't experience the block. It experiences the entropy gradient — the thermodynamic arrow that creates the appearance of temporal flow. Trauma is a distortion of the gradient: the body gets stuck at a particular entropy configuration and re-generates it.

In block terms: the traumatic event is a region of the block with specific physical configurations. The body, moving along its worldline, carries the thermodynamic trace of that region. Healing — through SE, EMDR, dance, boxing — is the body's worldline curving away from that trace. The curve was always in the block. The therapy is part of the geometry.

## 5. What Sources Agree On

- The experience of time is not fundamental but emergent
- The body has its own temporal processing system, partially independent of narrative cognition
- Trauma disrupts normal temporal processing
- Somatic interventions can address what cognitive approaches cannot

## 6. Where Sources Disagree

- **Rovelli vs Levine**: Rovelli says time is ignorance. Levine says the body knows things the mind doesn't. These are different kinds of "knowing" but the tension is real
- **Block perspective vs therapeutic perspective**: In the block, everything already happened. In therapy, change is the whole point. The reconciliation: "change" is itself a feature of the block — the trajectory from suffering to relief is part of the geometry
