# S2: Retrocausality & Bidirectional Causation

> The future constrains the present. Not by sending messages, but by being part of the same geometry.

**Sources:** 8 (Price, Wharton, Leifer & Pusey, Aharonov TSVF, Cramer TI, SEP, ScienceAlert, The Conversation)
**Adequacy:** ✅ Adequate

---

## 1. The Time-Symmetry Argument

Fundamental physics is time-symmetric. Run any equation backward and it still works. So why do we assume causation is one-directional? Huw Price (2012) showed that if (1) the quantum state is real and (2) quantum mechanics is time-symmetric, retrocausality is not just permitted — it follows necessarily. The key insight: in classical physics, time-symmetry coexists with forward-only causation. But quantum mechanics has a feature classical physics lacks — discreteness. Measurement outcomes are discrete (spin up or spin down, not a continuum), and this discreteness, combined with time-symmetry, implies backward influences.

Leifer & Pusey (2017) generalized Price's argument by dropping assumption (1). Even if the quantum state is merely epistemic (a description of knowledge, not reality), time-symmetry still entails retrocausality. They constructed a temporal analog of Bell's theorem, showing that quantum temporal correlations cannot be explained without either abandoning time-symmetry or accepting backward causation.

## 2. Two-State Vector Formalism (Aharonov)

Yakir Aharonov's TSVF characterizes the quantum state of a system at any moment by *two* state vectors: one propagating forward from preparation, one propagating backward from the future measurement. The present is the intersection of past and future boundary conditions. This is the most concrete retrocausal formalism in physics — it makes specific predictions and has been experimentally tested (weak measurements).

## 3. Transactional Interpretation (Cramer)

John Cramer's Transactional Interpretation models quantum events as "handshakes" between an offer wave (forward in time) and a confirmation wave (backward in time). The transaction is completed when both waves agree, producing a measurement result. No collapse needed — the outcome was negotiated across time. Kastner later extended this into a "possibilist" version where offer and confirmation waves propagate in a higher space of possibilities.

## 4. The Paris Proposal and Berkson's Bias

Price and Wharton's most accessible treatment (Aeon 2016) uses a mirror thought experiment: imagine "Ecila" who can control a photon's polarization *after* measurement. In quantum mechanics, discreteness gives her a forward control that doesn't exist classically. By time-symmetry, Alice has the same backward control. This control is real enough to count as causation but too weak to send signals.

Their latest work (2026 in The Conversation) introduces Berkson's bias — a well-known statistical selection effect — as the mechanism behind Bell correlations. This is retrocausality becoming increasingly concrete and empirically grounded.

## 5. What Sources Agree On

- Quantum mechanics has time-symmetric foundations
- Retrocausality can explain Bell correlations without action-at-a-distance
- The approach is internally consistent and makes correct predictions
- It rescues both locality and realism (the two things Bell tests threaten)

## 6. Where Sources Disagree

- **Mainstream vs retrocausalists**: Most physicists ignore or dismiss retrocausality. Leifer: "the onus is on us to flesh out the idea"
- **Bell's confusion**: Bell dismissed retrocausality, but he confused it with superdeterminism — a fundamentally different and much less palatable idea. Price has spent decades untangling this
- **Status of the program**: Is this a genuine interpretation of QM, or just "an idea for an interpretation"? The honest answer in 2026: still the latter, but gaining ground
