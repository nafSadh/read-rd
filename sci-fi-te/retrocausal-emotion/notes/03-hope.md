## 1. The Prospection Revolution

Seligman, Railton, Baumeister & Sripada's 2013 paper in Perspectives on Psychological Science initiated a paradigm shift: the dominant framework in psychology — that behavior is driven by the past — is wrong. The central organizing feature of human cognition is prospection: the representation of possible futures.

The neuroscience is striking. The brain's default circuit (activated when subjects are told to "think of nothing") is functionally identical to the circuit activated when subjects deliberately imagine future events. We are, at rest, prospecting. The default mode of human consciousness is future-oriented.

## 2. Memory as Hope Chest

The prospection framework reinterprets memory. Our memories are not accurate recordings — they are narratives constructed for forward guidance. "Memory is for doing," not for archiving. This explains why memory is so unreliable: accuracy of the past matters less than utility for the future. What matters is whether a memory helps you navigate what comes next.

Emotions follow the same logic. Barrett's constructed emotion theory shows that emotions are not reactions to present stimuli but predictions about what's coming. Fear isn't a response to danger — it's an anticipation of danger. Joy isn't a response to good news — it's the body's preparation for a good outcome. The body is an anticipation engine.

## 3. Snyder's Hope Theory

C.R. Snyder decomposed hope into two cognitive components: pathways thinking ("I can find routes to my goals") and agency thinking ("I can initiate and sustain movement along those routes"). This is structural, not emotional. Hope is a cognitive set — a way of thinking about goal-directed behavior — not a warm fuzzy feeling. High-hope individuals generate more alternative pathways when blocked and sustain effort longer.

## 4. The Bridge: Prospection as Retrocausality

Seligman explicitly denies backward causation: "guidance not by the future itself but by present, evaluative representations of possible future states." But consider: in the block universe, the future is real. The "present representations of possible future states" are representations of something that, under eternalism, actually exists. The functional difference between "the brain is modeling a future that exists" and "the future is causally influencing the present" may be smaller than it appears.

This is Tension T3: metaphor or mechanism? The phenomenology maps perfectly. The successful person is hopeful not because they predict success but (in block terms) because the success-region of the block is influencing their present worldline. The vocabulary differs; the geometry is the same.

## 5. What Sources Agree On

- Future-oriented thinking is the brain's default, not a secondary function
- Memory serves prospection more than retrospection
- Emotions are anticipatory more than reactive
- Hope has a specific cognitive structure (pathways + agency)

## 6. Where Sources Disagree

- **Seligman vs physics**: Seligman insists this is *not* backward causation. Price would say the distinction is verbal, not physical
- **Hope as cognitive set vs embodied state**: Snyder's theory is cognitive. Somatic approaches (S6) would locate hope in the body
- **Free will implications**: Seligman thinks prospection *rescues* free will. The block universe may undermine this rescue (S5)
