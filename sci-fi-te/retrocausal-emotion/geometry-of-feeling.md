# The Geometry of Feeling

> A writer's essay on block universe physics and emotional experience

**46 sources** across 7 sections · **7 tensions** · ~7,500 words
Part of [Retrocausality, Hope & Depression](https://read.sadh.app/physics/retrocausal-emotion/index.html) ·
Source material for *Second Seconds* and *Schrödinger's Gelato*

This essay argues that theoretical physics — specifically the block universe, retrocausality, and bidirectional causation — and prospective psychology converge on a single structural insight: the future shapes the present. Not metaphorically. Not as prediction. As geometry. Hope and depression are not reactions to the future. They are the future's fingerprint on the present, readable in the body, articulable in verse, and grounded in the same mathematics that describes quantum correlations across time. Drawing on 46 sources across physics, psychology, somatic neuroscience, and literature, this essay maps the territory where the equation and the elegy describe the same landscape — and identifies seven unresolved tensions that define the frontier.

---

## 1. Introduction: The Thesis

There is a moment in every physics-informed poem where the metaphor and the mathematics point at the same structure. This essay is about that moment — extended across seven dimensions of inquiry and held together by a single claim:

> **Core Thesis:** The future is not something that happens to us. It is something that shapes us now, has always shaped us, and in the geometry of spacetime, is us.

The claim emerges from two independent research programs that, remarkably, have not yet been brought into sustained conversation. On one side: the block universe of eternalism, where all times coexist with equal ontological status, and retrocausality, where the future constrains the present through the same time-symmetric equations that govern all fundamental physics. On the other: prospective psychology, where the brain's default mode is future simulation, emotions are predictions rather than reactions, and hope and depression are prospective phenomena — oriented toward what will be, not what was.

The geometry is the same. The vocabulary differs. This essay traces the convergence.

## 3. The Block Universe: All Times Coexist

The foundation is special relativity. Einstein's 1905 demonstration that the speed of light is constant for all observers eliminates absolute simultaneity. If two observers in relative motion disagree about which events are simultaneous, there is no physical basis for a universal "now." The Rietdijk-Putnam argument (1966–67) formalizes the consequence: if an event is in Observer A's future and Observer B's past, that event must exist regardless of temporal perspective. The future is real. The past is real. All events have equal ontological status.

This is eternalism — the view that the universe is a four-dimensional block in which all times coexist. Sean Carroll, Carlo Rovelli, and Brian Greene all work within this framework, though they disagree on details. Carroll emphasizes that the arrow of time is thermodynamic: the second law of thermodynamics is the *only* law of physics that distinguishes past from future. We remember the past because it was lower-entropy. We anticipate the future because entropy increases. The directionality of experience is real but emergent.

Rovelli goes further with the thermal time hypothesis: time itself emerges from our partial knowledge of the world. An omniscient being would see no time — only a static configuration. Time is what ignorance feels like from the inside. Barbour's even more radical position eliminates temporal emergence entirely: what exists is a vast configuration space ("Platonia") where each point is a complete snapshot, and the illusion of flow arises from "time capsule" configurations that contain records mimicking a past.

Against these, Lee Smolin's *Time Reborn* argues that time is fundamental, that the laws of physics evolve, and that the future is genuinely open. Smolin is a minority position but dramatically necessary for a writer, because it preserves the stakes that eternalism threatens to dissolve.

> **Writer's Position:** Hold both. The block provides the frame. Smolin provides the tension. A poem about time needs both the geometry and the stakes.

## 4. Retrocausality: The Future Constrains the Present

If the block universe provides the ontology, retrocausality provides the mechanism. Huw Price's 2012 argument demonstrates that if the quantum state is real and quantum mechanics is time-symmetric, retrocausality follows necessarily. The critical factor is discreteness: in classical physics, time-symmetry coexists with forward-only causation. But quantum measurement outcomes are discrete, and this discreteness, combined with time-symmetry, makes forward-only causation untenable.

Leifer and Pusey (2017) generalized the argument: even if the quantum state is merely epistemic, time-symmetry still entails backward influences. They constructed a temporal analog of Bell's theorem. The elegant resolution: allow retrocausality, and you can keep both locality and realism.

Yakir Aharonov's Two-State Vector Formalism makes this concrete. At any moment, a quantum system is described by two state vectors: one from the past and one from the future. The present is the intersection of past and future boundary conditions. Price and Wharton's most recent work introduces Berkson's bias as the mechanism behind Bell correlations. The mechanism is mundane. The implications are not.

> **The Crossword Metaphor:** Constraints run both directions. 17-across changes what fits at 5-down. The present is the cell where the past clue and the future clue intersect.

## 5. Hope as Advanced Wave

The prospection revolution in psychology provides the bridge. Seligman, Railton, Baumeister, and Sripada's 2013 manifesto overturned the dominant framework: the central organizing feature of human cognition is not the processing of past experience but prospection — the representation of possible futures. The brain's default mode network, activated during rest, is functionally identical to the circuit activated during deliberate future imagination. We are, at rest, modeling what comes next.

Memory, in this framework, serves prospection. Lisa Feldman Barrett's constructed emotion theory deepens this: emotions are not reactions to present stimuli but predictions about what is coming. The body is an anticipation engine.

C.R. Snyder's hope theory decomposes hope into pathways thinking (generating routes to goals) and agency thinking (initiating and sustaining movement). This is structural, not sentimental. Hope is a cognitive architecture for navigating toward the future.

Now notice the structural isomorphism with retrocausality. In the block universe, the future is real. The brain's "present representations of possible future states" are representations of something that actually exists. Is the brain modeling a future that exists, or is the existing future causally influencing the brain? Seligman explicitly denies backward causation. Price would say the distinction is verbal, not physical. For a writer, the resolution may not matter.

> **The Image:** Hope is the advanced wave traveling backward from success. The successful person is hopeful not because they predict success but because, in the geometry of the block, the success is already shaping their present.

## 6. Depression as Backward Shadow

If hope is the advanced wave from a successful outcome, depression is the advanced wave from a bleak one. Roepke and Seligman's reframing is radical: negative prospection is not a symptom of depression but its core causal element. Of the 32 procedures in CBT adherence protocols, none explicitly target prospection. The science and the practice are decades apart.

Andy Clark's predictive processing framework reveals the mechanism: depression is a regime of pessimistic priors with excessively high precision weighting. The brain trusts its own negative predictions more than positive evidence. Barrett adds the somatic layer: the system predicts pain, generates pain-consistent body states, and then interprets those body states as confirming the prediction. The loop is self-sealing.

But "self-sealing" understates it. Kube et al.'s work on biased belief updating (2020, 2023) provides the empirical teeth: depressed individuals don't merely fail to notice positive information. They *actively devalue it*. Kube calls this "cognitive immunization" — the belief system generates antibodies against its own cure. "That compliment was politeness." "That success was luck." "That connection won't last." The person is not passively trapped. They are actively constructing the trap, one defensive reappraisal at a time.

Korn, Sharot et al. (2014) demonstrate the complementary finding: healthy cognition runs on a protective positive distortion — an optimism bias in belief updating that selectively integrates good news about the future over bad. Depression is not the addition of pessimism but the subtraction of this bias. Healthy is distorted. Depressed is what undistorted looks like. The "depressive realism" literature has long hinted at this, but the belief updating data makes it precise.

Friston's free energy principle (Parr, Pezzulo & Friston, 2022) provides the deepest formalization. An agent can minimize surprise by updating its model (learning) or by acting on the world to conform to its model (niche construction). Depression is a regime where the second strategy dominates catastrophically: the agent gravitates toward environments that confirm its negative priors, achieving a local free energy minimum that is globally maladaptive. The depressed person doesn't just predict a bleak future. They *arrange* one. The loop is not merely self-sealing — it is self-*constructing*.

Seligman's original learned helplessness experiments demonstrated the prospective core: animals exposed to inescapable shocks developed the conviction that nothing they did mattered — a prediction about the *future*, not a memory of the past. The Kube/Friston synthesis shows what happens after the prediction crystallizes: it immunizes itself against correction.

### The Writer's Problem

For the poet, the cognitive immunization mechanism is the most dangerous and most generative idea in this entire survey. It connects directly to lived experience — cognitive dominance as a coping strategy, the way intelligence becomes a tool for dismantling evidence of recovery. The self-sealing loop is not an abstract clinical construct. It is the voice that says "this poem is probably bad too" after eight people said the last one worked. It is the voice that reappraises the compliment before it can land.

The retrocausal framing adds the geometry: both advanced waves exist — from the bleak future and from the healed future. Cognitive immunization is a noise generator tuned to the frequency of the healed future's signal. Therapy — SE, the heavy bag, the somatic discharge — is not fixing broken machinery. It is lowering the jamming. The positive advanced wave was always there. The work is making the antenna capable of receiving it.

The poem about depression should not be about sadness. It should be about the antibodies.

## 7. Free Will at the Intersection

The question of agency cuts across everything. Carroll's compatibilism provides the most useful framework: free will operates at a different level of description than particle physics. The vocabulary of choice and agency captures real patterns at the human level — patterns as real as temperature and pressure, both of which are also emergent descriptions of underlying physics.

Rovelli, characteristically more austere, points to Spinoza: freedom consists in acting from one's own nature rather than from external compulsion.

For the writer, the tension between the block and the slice is not a problem to solve but a space to inhabit. From inside the slice, choice is vivid, real, felt in the body. From outside the block, the worldline goes one way and was always going to. Chiang's Louise holds this tension perfectly: she "chooses" her daughter knowing the death. Choice dissolves into participation. The inhabiting is the only freedom available.

> **Writer's Position:** Write from the slice, not from the block. Inside the slice, the choice is real. The block doesn't negate the slice. It frames it. The poem happens at the intersection of freedom and geometry.

## 8. The Body as Instrument

The body is where physics becomes phenomenology. Against the backdrop of Rovelli's timelessness and Barbour's Platonia, the body insists on time. Peter Levine's Somatic Experiencing documents how the autonomic nervous system stores unresolved survival responses. Van der Kolk confirms at the neurological level: flashbacks are not memories but re-activations of the original physiological state. The body cannot distinguish "then" from "now."

Porges' polyvagal theory provides the wiring diagram: the vagus nerve mediates between social engagement, fight/flight, and freeze states based on neuroception — the body's automatic assessment of safety or danger, operating below conscious awareness.

If all moments coexist equally in the block, why does the body carry the past forward? Because the body doesn't experience the block. It experiences the entropy gradient. Trauma is a distortion of the gradient: the body gets stuck at a particular entropy configuration and re-generates it. Healing is the body's worldline curving away from the traumatic region. The curve was always in the block. The therapy is part of the geometry.

## 9. Literary Forms for Temporal Physics

Vonnegut solved the problem of writing about the block universe by making the reader experience it — non-linear chapters, tenses shifting without warning. Chiang solved simultaneous temporal perception through dual tenses — past tense for the alien encounter, future tense for the daughter's life. Borges anticipated many-worlds before Everett. Eliot compressed eternalism into verse. Nolan made the physics visceral.

The formal possibilities for confessional poetry converge on six structural conceits:

1. **Temporal Juxtaposition** — Moments without transition
2. **Bidirectional Address** — Past + future selves at once
3. **Observer Split** — Slice voice + block voice
4. **Gelato as Measurement** — Choice collapses superposition
5. **Tralfamadorian Move** — All moments co-present
6. **Heptapod Syntax** — Ending shapes beginning

Each conceit enacts a physics idea. Form IS content.

## 10. The Seven Tensions

Seven unresolved tensions define the frontier. They are not problems to solve but productive contradictions to hold:

### T1: Is time fundamental or emergent?

Smolin says fundamental. Rovelli says emergent. Barbour says it does not exist. The writer holds all four, because a poem needs both the open future and the eternal present.

### T2: Retrocausality — real physics or fringe?

Price and Wharton say it is the natural reading. Mainstream physics is skeptical. The program is gaining ground but remains a minority position.

### T3: Prospection vs retrocausality — metaphor or mechanism?

Seligman denies backward causation. Price says the distinction is verbal. The phenomenology maps perfectly.

The question is sharper than it first appears. Seligman's framework requires that the brain builds models of possible futures and is shaped by those models. Under eternalism, those futures are ontologically real. The brain's "present representations of possible future states" are representations of *actually existing regions of the block*. Whether the causal arrow runs from the real future to the present brain (Price) or from the present brain's model of the future to the present brain's behavior (Seligman) may be a distinction about the *direction of explanation*, not about the *structure being explained*.

The Kube data sharpens this further. If the depressed brain actively devalues positive information — immunizes itself against the signal from the healed future — then the mechanism is not merely "faulty prediction." It is an active process of signal rejection. In retrocausal terms: the antenna is not broken. It is deliberately mistuned. This makes the question of whether the future "really" causes anything almost secondary. The *behavior of the system* — selective reception, active filtering, immunization — is the same whether you describe it in Seligman's vocabulary or Price's.

For the writer, the resolution is pragmatic: use whichever vocabulary generates the better poem. But the structural isomorphism is not decoration. It is the reason the physics metaphors land — they describe the same geometry as the psychology, at a different resolution.

### T4: Depression as broken machinery vs boundary condition.

The clinical view says fix the prediction system. The retrocausal metaphor says the machinery might be working correctly on bad input. The therapeutic answer must be the first. The poetic truth may be the second.

### T5: Compatibilism's adequacy.

Carroll says free will is real at the emergent level. Hard determinists say he changed the subject.

### T6: Body time vs block time.

The body experiences the gradient, not the block. Healing is the worldline curving away. The curve was always in the geometry.

### T7: The Epistemological Trap — Can intelligence deepen the loop?

Kube's "cognitive immunization" implies that the self-sealing quality of depressive prospection is not a failure of intelligence but a *deployment* of it. The smarter the system, the more sophisticated its antibodies against positive disconfirmation. A person whose primary coping strategy is cognitive dominance — whose lifelong adaptive advantage is the ability to think faster, model more completely, and reframe more fluently than everyone around them — has, in depression, the most formidable jamming apparatus imaginable. Intelligence becomes the instrument of its own epistemic imprisonment.

This is not in the literature. It connects Kube's immunization mechanism to what Carroll might call a failure mode of emergence: a system that is optimally configured for one level of description (professional survival through cognitive mastery) becomes pathologically configured at another level (emotional recovery through openness to unmodeled positive evidence). The antibodies that protect the ego also attack the cure.

The poetic implication: the poem about recovery cannot be written by the smartest voice in the room. It has to be written by the voice that is *willing to be wrong about its own sadness*. Heptapod syntax — where the ending shapes the beginning — requires the writer to accept an ending they cannot yet model. The cognitive immune system has to stand down.

## 11. Conclusion: Write the Antibodies

The block universe, retrocausality, and prospective psychology converge on a single insight: the future is not something that happens to us. It is something that shapes us now.

For a writer working at the intersection of physics and confessional poetry, this convergence offers not just metaphors but structures. The poem about grief can hold two temporal perspectives simultaneously. The poem about hope can be written as an advanced wave, shaped by its ending before its beginning. The poem about the body can attend to the entropy gradient — the somatic hum that is time itself, experienced from within the geometry.

But the deepest structural conceit this survey has uncovered is not any of the six. It is the seventh, implicit one: the poem about depression must be about cognitive immunization — the antibodies the mind generates against its own recovery. "Confetti" already lives in this territory: the celebration that the speaker cannot quite let land. "Worlds" does it through scale — the cosmic frame that makes the personal loss both infinitesimal and infinite. *Schrödinger's Gelato* will do it through measurement: the flavor exists in superposition until the tongue collapses it, and the depressed speaker is the one who refuses to taste.

The physics does not replace the poetry. The poetry does not simplify the physics. Both are valid descriptions of the same reality, at different resolution. Poetic naturalism gives both a seat at the table.

The six tensions remain unresolved because they are productive. The seventh — whether intelligence deepens the trap — is the one that is personal. It is the space where *Second Seconds* lives: the journal of a mind smart enough to dismantle its own evidence of healing, learning, poem by poem, to stop.

> **The Final Image:** The future is already shaping you. Hope is the advanced wave traveling backward from the version of yourself that made it through. The cognitive immune system says: *that signal is noise*. The poem says: *what if it isn't*. Write that.
