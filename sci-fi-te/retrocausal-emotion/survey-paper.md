# The Geometry of Feeling — Survey Paper

> Retrocausality, hope, and depression — a survey connecting the block universe, bidirectional causation, prospective psychology, and somatic experience, synthesized as source material for poetry.

## Abstract

The block universe, retrocausality, and prospective psychology converge on a single insight: **the future is not something that happens to us — it is something that shapes us now**. In the geometry of spacetime, all moments coexist. Hope and depression are not reactions to the future; they are the future's fingerprint on the present. This is not metaphor. Or rather: the metaphor and the physics point at the same structure.

This survey maps the territory across seven dimensions — the block universe, retrocausality, hope as prospection, depression as negative prospection, free will and agency, embodiment, and literary applications — drawing on 46 sources spanning physics, psychology, philosophy, somatic therapy, and fiction. The project's purpose is not to prove that retrocausality causes hope. It is to build the conceptual scaffolding for poetry that can hold both the physics and the phenomenology without collapsing either into the other.

## 1. The Block Universe

Special relativity eliminates absolute simultaneity. If two observers moving at different velocities disagree about whether events X and Y happen at the same time, there is no physical basis for preferring one judgment. The Rietdijk-Putnam argument (1966–67) formalizes the implication: if an event can be in one observer's future and another's past simultaneously, that event must already exist [2]. All times coexist. Your birth and your death are equally real. "Now" is an address, not an edge.

Sean Carroll's crucial contribution is the entropy connection: the arrow of time is not fundamental but thermodynamic [5]. The second law of thermodynamics is the *only* law of physics that distinguishes past from future. All other fundamental equations are time-symmetric. We remember the past because it was lower-entropy. We experience flow because our brains are entropy-increasing processes. The Past Hypothesis — that the universe started in an extraordinarily low-entropy state — is a boundary condition, not a consequence of the laws.

Lee Smolin dissents: time is physically fundamental, the laws of physics themselves evolve, and the future is genuinely open [7]. Carlo Rovelli goes further than Carroll: not just "time is emergent" but "time doesn't exist" at the fundamental level [26]. What exists is change between events. We are the source of time.

## 2. Retrocausality & Bidirectional Causation

Fundamental physics is time-symmetric. Run any equation backward and it still works. Huw Price (2012) showed that if the quantum state is real and quantum mechanics is time-symmetric, retrocausality follows necessarily [9]. Leifer and Pusey (2017) generalized this: even if the quantum state is merely epistemic, time-symmetry still entails backward causation. They constructed a temporal analog of Bell's theorem [11].

Aharonov's Two-State Vector Formalism characterizes the quantum state at any moment by *two* state vectors: one propagating forward from preparation, one propagating backward from future measurement [15]. The present is the intersection of past and future boundary conditions. Cramer's Transactional Interpretation models quantum events as "handshakes" between an offer wave (forward in time) and a confirmation wave (backward in time) [14]. The outcome was negotiated across time.

Price and Wharton's Aeon essay (2016) makes this accessible through the Alice/Ecila mirror metaphor [12]. Their 2026 follow-up introduces Berkson's bias as the mechanism behind Bell correlations [13]. Retrocausality is becoming increasingly concrete.

## 3. Hope as Retrocausal Signal

Seligman, Railton, Baumeister, and Sripada (2013) initiated a paradigm shift: the central organizing feature of human cognition is not the past but *prospection* — the representation of possible futures [16]. The brain's default circuit — activated when subjects are told to "think of nothing" — is functionally identical to the circuit activated when subjects deliberately imagine future events. We are, at rest, prospecting. The default mode of human consciousness is future-oriented.

Memory, in this framework, is not a file drawer but a **hope chest of possibilities** [19]. Our memories are narratives constructed for forward guidance, not accurate recordings. Emotions follow the same logic: Barrett's constructed emotion theory shows that emotions are predictions about what's coming, not reactions to what's here [25]. Snyder's Hope Theory decomposes hope into pathways thinking and agency thinking — a cognitive set, not a warm feeling [20].

### The Bridge

Seligman explicitly denies backward causation. But in the block universe, the future is real. "Present representations of possible future states" are representations of something that, under eternalism, actually exists. Hope is the advanced wave traveling backward from the version of yourself that made it through.

## 4. Depression as Bidirectional Loop

Roepke and Seligman (2016) reframe depression: negative prospection is not a *symptom* but the *core causal element* [22]. The depressed mind doesn't fail to predict the future — it predicts the future too well, and believes every dark simulation. Beck's cognitive triad has included the future since 1976, but clinical practice has overwhelmingly focused on past trauma [23].

Andy Clark's predictive processing framework casts the brain as a prediction machine. Depression is a regime of pessimistic priors with excessively high precision weighting [24]. The predictions are so strong that positive evidence is discounted. Combined with Barrett's constructed emotion theory, the loop is self-sealing: the system predicts pain, generates somatic states consistent with pain, then interprets those states as confirming the prediction.

### The Cognitive Immunization Problem

Kube et al. (2020, 2023) provide the empirical specifics that Clark's framework predicts [44]. Depression is not characterized by enhanced sensitivity to negative information — it is characterized by the *inability to integrate positive information*. Depressed individuals employ what Kube calls "cognitive immunization": defensive reappraisal strategies that actively devalue novel positive evidence. The person doesn't just fail to notice good news. They notice it, then dismantle it. "That was luck." "They were being polite." "It won't last." The belief system has antibodies.

Korn, Sharot et al. (2014) demonstrate the same asymmetry from the other direction [45]. Healthy individuals show an optimism bias in belief updating — they integrate desirable information about their future more readily than undesirable information. This bias is *absent* in MDD patients, with severity correlating to the degree of absence. The critical insight: depression is not the presence of something pathological but the *absence of a protective distortion*. Healthy cognition is positively biased. Depression is what cognition looks like without the bias.

Friston's free energy principle (Parr, Pezzulo & Friston, 2022) provides the deepest formalization [46]. An agent minimizes free energy — surprise — by either updating its model of the world or by acting on the world to make it conform to its model. Depression is a regime where the second strategy dominates: the agent gravitates toward or engineers environments that confirm its negative priors, achieving a local free energy minimum that is globally catastrophic. The depressed person doesn't just predict a bleak future. They *arrange* one. The loop is not merely self-sealing — it is self-*constructing*.

### The Retrocausal Mirror

If hope is the advanced wave from a successful outcome, depression is the advanced wave from a bleak one. But the Kube/Friston synthesis adds a layer the original framing missed: the self-sealing quality of the loop means the depressed person is not merely receiving the wrong signal. They are actively *jamming* the alternative signal. The cognitive immunization is a noise generator tuned to the frequency of the healed future.

Carroll's compatibilism offers the escape: the block also contains the person who started therapy, who broke the loop. The intervention is already there. You can't see it from inside the depressed slice, but it's in the geometry. Therapy — SE, CBT, the boxing heavy bag, the somatic discharge — is not fixing broken machinery. It is *lowering the jamming*. The positive advanced wave was always there. The work is making the antenna capable of receiving it.

## 5. Free Will & Agency

Carroll's compatibilism holds that free will operates at a different level of description than particle physics [29]. When choosing a shirt in the morning, "it doesn't help me to say 'I'm gonna do whatever my atoms want me to do.' My atoms have no wants." Just as the arrow of time is macroscopic while fundamental physics is time-reversible, free will is macroscopic while fundamental physics is deterministic. Neither is illusory — both are real features of an emergent description [6].

Rovelli is less patient: the philosophical debate over free will and determinism is based on conceptual confusions that Spinoza resolved four centuries ago [33]. For a writer, Carroll's framework is powerful: it validates the vocabulary of human experience without requiring supernatural foundations. The poem about choosing doesn't need to resolve the determinism question. The poem *is* the emergent-level description where choice is real.

## 6. Time, Memory & Embodiment

Against the backdrop of timelessness, the body insists on time. Peter Levine's Somatic Experiencing documents how the autonomic nervous system stores unresolved survival responses [35]. Van der Kolk confirms at the neurological level: trauma rewires the brain and body together. Flashbacks are not memories in the narrative sense — they are re-activations of the original physiological state [37]. Porges' polyvagal theory provides the mechanism: the vagus nerve mediates between social engagement, fight/flight, and freeze, based on neuroception — the body's automatic assessment of safety [36].

In block terms: trauma is a region of the block with specific physical configurations. The body, moving along its worldline, carries the thermodynamic trace. Healing — through SE, EMDR, boxing, dance — is the body's worldline curving away from that trace. The curve was always in the block. The therapy is part of the geometry.

## 7. Literary Applications

The best temporal fiction doesn't explain the physics — it enacts it. Vonnegut's Billy Pilgrim, unstuck in time, experiences his life non-sequentially; the Tralfamadorians respond to death with "So it goes" — not indifference but the recognition that the dead person is fine in plenty of other moments [38]. Ted Chiang's Louise Banks learns a language that requires knowing how a sentence ends before you can begin writing it — retrocausality as linguistics [39]. Louise "chooses" to have her daughter despite foreknowing the death. But choice dissolves in simultaneous perception. Grief and joy are concurrent.

T.S. Eliot gives the poet permission: "Time present and time past / Are both perhaps present in time future" [42]. Borges anticipated many-worlds before Everett [41]. The physics and the literature converge on six structural conceits for poetry: temporal juxtaposition, bidirectional address, the observer split, gelato as measurement, the Tralfamadorian move, and Heptapod syntax.

## 8. Seven Tensions

### T1: Is Time Fundamental or Emergent?

Smolin says time is physically real and fundamental [7]. Rovelli says time emerges from entropy and ignorance [26]. Barbour says time doesn't exist at all [27]. Carroll bridges: "different ways of talking about the world."

### T2: Retrocausality — Real Physics or Fringe?

Price and Wharton argue it's the most natural reading of time-symmetric QM [9]. Mainstream physics treats it as a curiosity. Bell himself dismissed it — confusing it with superdeterminism. Leifer: "the onus is on us to flesh out the idea."

### T3: Prospection vs. Retrocausality — Metaphor or Mechanism?

Seligman explicitly denies backward causation: "guidance not by the future itself but by present representations" [16]. But the phenomenology maps perfectly onto retrocausal language. Is the mapping merely poetic, or does it reveal something structural?

The question is sharper than it first appears. Seligman's framework requires that the brain builds models of possible futures and is shaped by those models. Under eternalism, those futures are ontologically real — they exist with the same status as the past. The brain's "present representations of possible future states" are representations of *actually existing regions of the block*. Whether the causal arrow runs from the real future to the present brain (Price) or from the present brain's model of the future to the present brain's behavior (Seligman) may be a distinction about the *direction of explanation*, not about the *structure being explained*.

The Kube data sharpens this further. If the depressed brain actively devalues positive information — immunizes itself against the signal from the healed future — then the mechanism is not merely "faulty prediction." It is an active process of signal rejection. In retrocausal terms: the antenna is not broken. It is deliberately mistuned. This makes the question of whether the future "really" causes anything almost secondary. The *behavior of the system* — selective reception, active filtering, immunization — is the same whether you describe it in Seligman's vocabulary or Price's.

For the writer, the resolution is pragmatic: use whichever vocabulary generates the better poem. But the structural isomorphism is not decoration. It is the reason the physics metaphors land — they describe the same geometry as the psychology, at a different resolution.

### T4: Depression — Broken Machinery or Accurate Perception?

Clinical psychology says fix the prediction machinery [22]. The retrocausal metaphor says the machinery might be working correctly — it's the input (the future boundary condition) that's the problem. The therapeutic answer must be the first. The poetic truth may be the second.

### T5: Compatibilism's Adequacy

Carroll says free will is real at the emergent level [29]. Critics say compatibilists changed the subject — the felt experience of authorship is what matters, and calling emergent patterns "free will" is semantic sleight of hand.

### T6: Body Time vs. Block Time

The body keeps the score [37]. But in a block universe, what is it scoring? The body doesn't experience the block — it experiences the gradient. Trauma is a distortion of the gradient: the body gets stuck at a particular entropy configuration. Healing is the worldline curving away.

### T7: The Epistemological Trap — Can Intelligence Deepen the Loop?

Kube's "cognitive immunization" implies that the self-sealing quality of depressive prospection is not a failure of intelligence but a *deployment* of it. The smarter the system, the more sophisticated its antibodies against positive disconfirmation. A person whose primary coping strategy is cognitive dominance — whose lifelong adaptive advantage is the ability to think faster, model more completely, and reframe more fluently than everyone around them — has, in depression, the most formidable jamming apparatus imaginable. Intelligence becomes the instrument of its own epistemic imprisonment.

This is not in the literature. It connects Kube's immunization mechanism to what Carroll might call a failure mode of emergence: a system that is optimally configured for one level of description (professional survival through cognitive mastery) becomes pathologically configured at another level (emotional recovery through openness to unmodeled positive evidence). The antibodies that protect the ego also attack the cure.

The poetic implication: the poem about recovery cannot be written by the smartest voice in the room. It has to be written by the voice that is *willing to be wrong about its own sadness*. Heptapod syntax — where the ending shapes the beginning — requires the writer to accept an ending they cannot yet model. The cognitive immune system has to stand down.

## 9. Methods & Limitations

This survey synthesizes 46 sources across physics (Carroll, Rovelli, Smolin, Price, Wharton, Leifer, Aharonov, Cramer), psychology (Seligman, Roepke, Beck, Clark, Barrett, Snyder, Kube, Sharot), neuroscience (Friston, Parr, Pezzulo), somatic therapy (Levine, van der Kolk, Porges), philosophy (SEP, IEP), and literature (Vonnegut, Chiang, Borges, Eliot, Nolan). The project is deliberately interdisciplinary — it connects domains that rarely speak to each other because the connections are the point.

The central limitation is honest: **this is not a physics paper**. Retrocausality is a minority interpretation of quantum mechanics. The mapping between prospection and retrocausality is a structural analogy, not a causal claim. The value of the project is not empirical but conceptual and aesthetic — it provides vocabulary and structure for poetry about time, grief, and hope that is informed by physics without being constrained by it. The metaphor and the mechanism point at the same structure. Whether they *are* the same structure is a question this survey raises but cannot answer.
