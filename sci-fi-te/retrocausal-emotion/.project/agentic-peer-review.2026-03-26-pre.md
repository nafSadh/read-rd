# Peer Review: physics/retrocausal-emotion

**Reviewer:** Claude (agentic peer review)
**Date:** 2026-03-25

## Overall Assessment

This is a thoroughly executed paper-drive project that bridges theoretical physics, prospective psychology, somatic neuroscience, and literary craft. The 7-section structure moves cleanly from ontological foundation (block universe) through mechanism (retrocausality) to application (hope, depression, agency, embodiment) and output (literary forms). 43 sources across 7 sections, all marked adequate. Two sessions produced 17 deliverables: a literature collection, 8 deep-dive HTML pages (overview + S1-S7), 7 markdown notes, a synthesis paper in both markdown and HTML ("The Geometry of Feeling," ~6,500 words), a 26-slide deck, and a well-maintained project index.

The project's central thesis -- that physics and prospective psychology converge on the same structural insight (the future shapes the present) -- is stated clearly, defended carefully, and never overstated. The six tensions framework is the project's strongest intellectual contribution: rather than forcing consensus between retrocausal physics and clinical psychology, the project holds contradictions as productive frontier zones. The distinction between "the therapeutic answer must be the first; the poetic truth may be the second" (T4) is genuinely sophisticated.

The main weaknesses are thin source coverage in two sections (S4 and S5 at 5 sources each, both flagged as "minimum"), the absence of formal reference lists in the HTML papers, and a slight CSS inconsistency between the deep-dive accent color (blue) and the survey paper/index accent color (violet). The project also lacks any empirical or experimental discussion of retrocausality -- the closest it comes is mentioning TSVF weak measurement predictions, but no actual experimental results are cited.

**Content Quality: A-**
**Paper-Drive Compliance: A**

## Content Quality

**Intellectual depth:** The project successfully operates at the intersection of three domains that rarely communicate: quantum foundations (Price, Wharton, Aharonov), prospective psychology (Seligman, Barrett, Clark), and somatic neuroscience (Levine, Porges, van der Kolk). The synthesis is genuinely interdisciplinary rather than superficially cross-disciplinary. The isomorphism between TSVF's two-state-vector characterization and Seligman's prospection framework is identified precisely and hedged appropriately ("the vocabulary differs; the geometry may be the same").

**Physics accuracy:** The treatment of retrocausality is careful and up to date. Price's time-symmetry argument, Leifer and Pusey's generalization, Berkson's bias as mechanism for Bell correlations, and the distinction between retrocausality and superdeterminism are all correctly presented. The project correctly identifies retrocausality as a minority but growing research program rather than established physics.

**Psychology accuracy:** Roepke and Seligman's reframing of depression as faulty prospection is correctly identified as a causal claim, not merely correlational. Snyder's hope theory decomposition (pathways + agency) is accurately represented. Clark's predictive processing framework is applied appropriately to depression.

**Synthesis quality:** The synthesis paper ("The Geometry of Feeling") is the project's flagship deliverable and it works well. The 11-section arc from thesis through physics, psychology, philosophy, embodiment, and literary form is coherent and readable. The conclusion ("Write that") is earned rather than imposed. The six tensions section successfully captures genuine unresolved disagreements without false equivalence.

**Writer's application:** Every section deep-dive includes a "Writer's Takeaway" callout connecting the research to concrete poetic technique. The literary applications section (S7) provides six named structural conceits (temporal juxtaposition, bidirectional address, observer split, gelato as measurement, Tralfamadorian move, Heptapod syntax) that are specific enough to be actionable. The explicit connection to two named works (Second Seconds and Schrodinger's Gelato) grounds the project in a real creative workflow.

## Strengths

- The 7-section architecture is elegantly designed. The progression from ontology (block universe) to mechanism (retrocausality) to application (hope/depression) to philosophy (free will) to embodiment to output (literary forms) has genuine intellectual logic, not just organizational convenience.
- The tensions framework is the project's most original contribution. T3 (prospection vs retrocausality: metaphor or mechanism?) and T4 (depression as broken machinery vs boundary condition) are genuinely productive contradictions that resist easy resolution.
- Source selection is judicious. The project draws on primary sources (Price 2012, Leifer & Pusey 2017, Seligman et al. 2013) rather than secondary summaries. Accessible treatments (Price & Wharton in Aeon, Carroll's blog) are included alongside technical papers.
- The synthesis paper successfully sustains a single argument across 6,500 words without losing either rigor or readability. The prose is clear and the analogies are precise (the crossword puzzle image for bidirectional constraint is particularly effective).
- SVG diagrams in both the overview deep-dive (30 svg/diagram references) and section deep-dives provide visual anchoring for abstract concepts.
- The project is well-scoped. The "Deliberately Excluded" section (quantum computing, many-worlds, hard consciousness) shows disciplined boundary-setting that keeps the project focused.
- The continuation prompt is unusually detailed, including template system details, SVG color palette, and design consistency notes -- enabling future sessions to maintain visual coherence.
- Two-session completion with all 17 deliverables is efficient execution.

## Weaknesses

- **S4 (Depression) and S5 (Free Will) are at minimum source adequacy (5 each).** S4 in particular could benefit from additional sources: Aaron Beck's original 1979 work rather than just the 1976 triad summary, Abramson's hopelessness theory of depression, or MacLeod and Byrne's work on prospective cognition in suicidality. S5 leans heavily on Carroll (3 of 5 sources), giving a somewhat one-sided treatment of compatibilism.
- **No experimental evidence for retrocausality is cited.** The project discusses TSVF predictions and Bell correlation mechanisms but does not cite any of the actual experimental tests (e.g., delayed-choice quantum eraser experiments, Aharonov-Vaidman weak measurement results, or the Tollaksen group's experimental work). This gap weakens the claim that retrocausality is "not metaphor."
- **The geometry-of-feeling.html paper lacks a formal reference list.** The survey-paper.html has 21 footnotes with tooltips, but neither HTML paper includes a consolidated bibliography at the bottom. The markdown version has no inline citations at all.
- **Accent color inconsistency between deliverables.** The deep-dive files (s1-s7, overview) use blue as primary accent (`--accent:#2e6ab4`), while the survey paper, geometry-of-feeling.html, index page, and slide deck use violet (`--accent:#7c3aed` or `#b598ff`). This creates a visual disconnect when navigating between deliverables.
- **The synthesis paper and survey paper overlap substantially.** Both are titled variations of "The Geometry of Feeling" and cover the same 7 sections with the same thesis. The relationship between them (geometry-of-feeling.html as essay, survey-paper.html as academic paper) is not documented. A reader encountering both would be uncertain which to read.
- **The slide deck has limited SVG diagram coverage.** The changelog mentions SVG diagrams on "key slides" (entropy arrow, TSVF, self-sealing loop), but with 26 slides and only ~3 named diagrams, most content slides lack visual support.
- **No audio narration or speaker notes for the slide deck.** Some other paper-drive projects in the repo include audio support.
- **The cross-section synthesis note (00-synthesis.md) is incomplete.** The "Key Bridges" and "Images and Metaphors" sections contain placeholder language ("To be populated as sections are completed") despite all sections being complete.

## Recommendations

1. **Strengthen S4 and S5 source coverage.** S4 would benefit from Abramson, Metalsky & Alloy's hopelessness theory (1989), which directly connects prospection to depression and would strengthen the section's central argument. S5 would benefit from a non-Carroll compatibilist source (e.g., Dennett's *Freedom Evolves* or Fischer & Ravizza's reasons-responsive theory) to reduce Carroll-dependence.

2. **Add experimental retrocausality citations.** At minimum, cite Aharonov, Albert & Vaidman's 1988 weak measurement paper and one recent experimental confirmation. The Wheeler delayed-choice experiment replication by Jacques et al. (2007) would also strengthen the empirical foundation without adding excessive technical detail.

3. **Add reference lists to both HTML papers.** The `<sup class="fn">` infrastructure in survey-paper.html already anchors 21 footnotes. A consolidated `<section id="references">` with numbered entries would complete the citation apparatus. The geometry-of-feeling.html should either gain its own footnotes or include a bibliography section.

4. **Resolve the two-paper overlap.** Either (a) document the distinct purposes of geometry-of-feeling.html vs survey-paper.html in the index page, (b) merge them into a single canonical paper, or (c) differentiate them more sharply (e.g., make the survey paper more technical with formal citations, and the geometry paper more essayistic and accessible).

5. **Complete 00-synthesis.md.** The cross-section synthesis note still has placeholder text. Since all 7 sections are complete, the bridges and metaphor collection should be populated. This file is valuable as a writer's quick-reference for the creative application layer.

6. **Unify accent colors across deliverables.** Choose either blue or violet as the project accent and apply it consistently to deep-dives, papers, slides, and index. The current split creates an impression of two separate projects.

7. **Consider adding one more source to the literary section.** The current 6 sources are all canonical (Vonnegut, Chiang, Nolan, Borges, Eliot, Wharton & Price). Adding a contemporary poet who engages with physics -- such as Alice Fulton's fractal poetics, or Jorie Graham's work on entropy and time -- would strengthen the case that the project's literary conceits are not merely theoretical but have living practitioners.
