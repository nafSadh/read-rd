# S3: Dark Matter

> We can't see 27% of the universe. Forty years of looking, and the most sensitive detectors ever built have found nothing. The embarrassment of not knowing what most matter is.

**Sources:** 9 (FuturaFeed guide, SciTePress WIMP/axion papers, ScienceInsights, This Week in Science, Wikipedia MOND, Big Think MOND, Scientific American PBH, Carr et al. PBH review, JWST dark matter)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Evidence, candidates, detection efforts, alternatives, and frontier all covered.

---

## 1. The Evidence (Overwhelming)

Dark matter was first inferred in 1933 when Fritz Zwicky measured galaxy velocities in the Coma Cluster and found they were moving too fast to be held together by visible matter alone. The evidence has only grown:

**Rotation curves (1970s):** Vera Rubin and Kent Ford measured that stars in the outer parts of spiral galaxies orbit at the same speed as inner stars. Without invisible mass, outer stars should orbit slower (Keplerian decline). The flat rotation curves require a massive dark matter halo surrounding each galaxy.

**Gravitational lensing:** Massive objects bend light (Einstein, 1915). The bending around galaxy clusters is too strong to be explained by visible matter alone. Weak lensing surveys map dark matter distribution across the cosmic web.

**The Bullet Cluster (2006):** Two galaxy clusters collided. The hot gas (visible via X-rays) was slowed by friction, but the gravitational mass (mapped by lensing) sailed through. This separation of visible and total mass is direct evidence for dark matter as a substance, not a modification of gravity.

**CMB anisotropies:** The pattern of temperature fluctuations requires a component that gravitates but does not interact with photons. Without dark matter, the power spectrum peaks are wrong.

**Large-scale structure:** Galaxy surveys show filamentary structure that forms only if dark matter provides the gravitational scaffolding. Simulations without dark matter produce the wrong cosmic web.

## 2. How Much?

Planck CMB measurements: the universe is 26.8% dark matter, 4.9% ordinary matter, 68.3% dark energy. Dark matter outmasses ordinary matter roughly 5:1. In our galaxy, the Milky Way, dark matter makes up ~85% of all mass.

## 3. Particle Candidates

**WIMPs (Weakly Interacting Massive Particles):** For decades, the leading candidate. Mass 1-1000 GeV. Interact via the weak force. Should be produced in the early universe at exactly the right abundance ("WIMP miracle"). But no detection despite 40+ years of increasingly sensitive experiments.

**LUX-ZEPLIN (LZ):** 10-tonne liquid xenon detector, 1.5 km underground in South Dakota. 280 days of data through April 2024: no signal. Nearly 5x more sensitive than any previous experiment. Ruling out wide mass ranges.

**The neutrino floor:** At extreme sensitivities, astrophysical neutrinos create an irreducible background that mimics dark matter signals. We are approaching this limit. Below it, fundamentally new detector concepts are needed.

**Axions:** Ultra-light particles (10^-6 to 10^-3 eV) originally proposed to solve the strong CP problem in QCD. ADMX experiment probes axion masses around 3.3 microeV using resonant microwave cavities in strong magnetic fields. No detection yet, but mass range is being systematically explored.

**Sterile neutrinos:** Hypothetical neutrinos that interact only gravitationally. Mass ~keV. X-ray observations have produced contested claims (3.5 keV line).

**Primordial black holes:** Black holes formed in the first second after the Big Bang, not from stellar collapse. JWST observations of early universe "little red dots" and LIGO's sub-solar-mass merger candidate (Nov 2025) have renewed interest. Mass windows around 10^-12 to 10^-7 solar masses and ~1-100 solar masses remain open.

## 4. MOND: The Contrarian View

Mordehai Milgrom proposed Modified Newtonian Dynamics in 1983: below an acceleration threshold (a_0 ~ 1.2 x 10^-10 m/s^2), gravity is stronger than Newton predicts. This single parameter explains flat rotation curves without dark matter.

**Successes:**
- Predicts rotation curves from visible matter alone (no free parameters per galaxy)
- Predicted the "external field effect" -- confirmed in 150+ galaxies
- Early JWST galaxies forming faster than Lambda-CDM predicts, consistent with MOND

**Failures:**
- Cannot explain the Bullet Cluster (visible/mass separation requires real dark matter)
- Fails for galaxy cluster mass discrepancies
- Does not reproduce the CMB power spectrum
- 2024 Gaia wide binary analysis: data strongly favor Newton over MOND at sub-galactic scales

MOND remains a minority view but has passionate advocates. It may describe an emergent property of dark matter rather than replacing it.

## 5. The Detection Crisis

After four decades:
- No WIMP detected in any direct detection experiment
- No axion detected in any cavity experiment
- No supersymmetric particle at the LHC
- No definitive signal in indirect detection (gamma rays, positrons)
- The neutrino floor approaches

This is not a failure of technology -- detectors have improved by factors of 10,000. It may mean WIMPs at the most-searched masses simply do not exist, and dark matter is something more exotic (axions at different masses, PBHs, or a particle we have not imagined).

## 6. What Sources Agree On

1. The gravitational evidence for dark matter is overwhelming and consistent across scales.
2. Dark matter is ~5x more abundant than ordinary matter.
3. WIMPs in the most-searched mass range are increasingly constrained.
4. MOND cannot replace dark matter entirely (Bullet Cluster, CMB).
5. The identity of dark matter is one of the most important unsolved problems in physics.

## 7. Where Sources Disagree

1. **Leading candidate:** WIMPs (fading), axions (rising), PBHs (speculative), or something entirely new?
2. **MOND's role:** Falsified alternative, or clue to dark matter's behavior?
3. **PBHs as dark matter:** JWST and LIGO hints are suggestive but far from conclusive.
4. **Timeline:** Will we identify dark matter in a decade, or is it a century-scale problem?
