# S4: Dark Energy & The Fate of the Universe

> 68% of the universe is... what? The cosmological constant Einstein called his biggest blunder turned out to be right -- and now even that might not be the full story.

## 1. The 1998 Surprise

Two rival teams set out to measure how quickly the universe's expansion was decelerating (as everyone expected gravity would dictate):

- **Supernova Cosmology Project** (Saul Perlmutter, Berkeley)
- **High-Z Supernova Search Team** (Brian Schmidt & Adam Riess)

Both used Type Ia supernovae as standard candles. The supernovae were fainter than expected -- meaning more distant -- meaning the expansion was not decelerating. It was accelerating.

This was the most shocking discovery in cosmology since Hubble. Three researchers shared the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics.

## 2. Einstein's Biggest Blunder (That Was Right)

In 1917, Einstein added a "cosmological constant" (Lambda) to his field equations to keep the universe static. When Hubble discovered expansion in 1929, Einstein reportedly called Lambda his "biggest blunder" and abandoned it.

In 1998, it was resurrected. The accelerating expansion can be described by Lambda: a constant energy density pervading all of space, pushing space apart. The irony: Einstein's biggest blunder turned out to describe 68% of the universe.

But Lambda has its own problem: quantum field theory predicts a vacuum energy density ~10^120 times larger than observed. This "cosmological constant problem" is often called the worst prediction in the history of physics.

## 3. What Is Dark Energy?

Nobody knows. Possibilities:

**Cosmological constant (Lambda):** A fixed property of spacetime itself. Constant through time and space. The simplest explanation.

**Quintessence:** A dynamic scalar field that evolves over time. The energy density changes, potentially explaining why dark energy "turned on" relatively recently in cosmic history.

**Phantom energy:** Dark energy that strengthens over time (w < -1). Leads to the Big Rip.

**Modified gravity:** Perhaps general relativity is wrong on cosmological scales. Dark energy is not a substance but a failure of our gravity theory.

## 4. The DESI Revolution (2024-2025)

The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument mapped 6+ million galaxies, measuring baryon acoustic oscillations across cosmic time. Combined with CMB and supernova data, the results suggest:

- Dark energy may not be constant: preference for time-varying w(z) at 2.8-4.2 sigma.
- The equation of state parameter w may cross -1 (the "phantom divide"), which the cosmological constant cannot do.
- Not yet at 5-sigma discovery threshold, but the most significant hint of new physics since 1998.

The Dark Energy Survey's final supernova analysis (~1500 SNe over 5 years) provides the strongest supernova constraints on expansion ever, also hinting at time variation.

If confirmed, this would require abandoning the simplest Lambda-CDM model and finding a new theoretical framework.

## 5. The Fate of the Universe

The nature of dark energy determines how the universe ends:

**Big Freeze (Heat Death):** If dark energy is a constant, the universe expands forever. Stars exhaust their fuel. Black holes evaporate via Hawking radiation. The universe approaches thermodynamic equilibrium at ~0 K. Timeline: last stars die in ~10^14 years; last black holes evaporate in ~10^100 years. Most probable outcome given current data.

**Big Rip:** If dark energy strengthens over time (phantom energy), expansion accelerates without limit. First galaxy clusters are torn apart, then galaxies, then solar systems, then planets, then atoms. Timeline depends on w: possibly in ~22 billion years.

**Big Crunch:** If dark energy weakens or reverses, gravity could re-dominate and the universe collapses back to a singularity. The time-reverse of the Big Bang. Currently disfavored.

**Big Bounce:** Cyclic cosmology -- the universe oscillates between Big Bangs and Big Crunches. Each cycle may reset physical constants. Related to conformal cyclic cosmology (Roger Penrose).

## 6. The Arrow of Time

The second law of thermodynamics gives time a direction: entropy always increases. On cosmic scales, this means the universe evolves from the extraordinarily low-entropy state of the Big Bang toward the maximum-entropy state of heat death.

Why was the Big Bang's entropy so low? This "past hypothesis" is one of the deepest unsolved problems in physics. Roger Penrose's Weyl Curvature Hypothesis and Sean Carroll's baby-universe proposals are among the attempts to explain it.

## 7. What Sources Agree On

1. The universe's expansion is accelerating (beyond any reasonable doubt).
2. Dark energy accounts for ~68% of the universe's mass-energy.
3. The cosmological constant is the simplest explanation and consistent with most data.
4. The Big Freeze is the most probable fate given current evidence.
5. DESI's hints of evolving dark energy are the most important recent development.

## 8. Where Sources Disagree

1. **Is dark energy constant?** DESI says probably not (2.8-4.2 sigma). Conservative view says wait for 5-sigma.
2. **Cosmological constant problem:** Is the 10^120 discrepancy a real problem or a misapplication of QFT?
3. **Fate:** Big Freeze vs Big Rip depends entirely on dark energy's equation of state.
4. **Arrow of time:** Why was the Big Bang low-entropy? No consensus.
