# S1: Scales of the Universe

> From the Planck length to the observable universe: 61 orders of magnitude, and the physics changes at every level.

**Sources:** 7 (Wikipedia cosmic distance ladder, Astronomy.com, Terry Tao/3blue1brown, Wikipedia observable universe, Wikipedia galaxy filaments, NASA large-scale structures, Planck scale review)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Good coverage across measurement techniques, scale hierarchy, and cosmic structure.

---

## 1. The Hierarchy of Scale

The universe spans from the Planck length (1.6 x 10^-35 m) to the observable universe diameter (8.8 x 10^26 m) -- roughly 61 orders of magnitude. At each scale, different physics dominates:

- **Planck scale** (10^-35 m): Quantum gravity regime. Spacetime may be discrete. LQG predicts spin networks; string theory predicts vibrating strings in 10-11 dimensions.
- **Subatomic** (10^-15 m): Quantum chromodynamics. Quarks bound by gluons into protons and neutrons.
- **Atomic** (10^-10 m): Quantum mechanics. Electron orbitals, chemistry.
- **Human scale** (10^0 m): Classical mechanics. Newton's laws suffice.
- **Planetary** (10^7 m): Newtonian gravity with relativistic corrections (GPS requires GR).
- **Stellar** (10^9 m): Nuclear physics (fusion), hydrostatic equilibrium, stellar evolution.
- **Galactic** (10^20 m): Gravitational dynamics, dark matter halos, spiral arm formation.
- **Cosmological** (10^26 m): General relativity, dark energy, cosmic expansion.

The "powers of ten" perspective (Eames, 1977) remains one of the most powerful pedagogical tools in science -- each factor of ten reveals new phenomena.

## 2. The Cosmic Distance Ladder

No single method can measure all distances in the universe. The cosmic distance ladder uses a succession of techniques, each calibrated by the previous:

**Rung 1: Parallax** (< ~30,000 ly). Earth's orbit provides a baseline of 2 AU. The ESA Gaia mission (2013-2025) measured parallaxes for 1.8 billion stars with sub-milliarcsecond precision, anchoring the entire ladder to 1% accuracy.

**Rung 2: Cepheid Variables** (< ~100 Mly). Henrietta Leavitt's 1912 period-luminosity relation. Longer period = brighter star. JWST has observed Cepheids in distant galaxies, validating calibrations.

**Rung 3: Type Ia Supernovae** (< ~10 Bly). Thermonuclear detonation of white dwarfs at the Chandrasekhar limit (~1.4 solar masses). Nearly uniform peak brightness makes them excellent standard candles. These are the tools that discovered accelerating expansion.

**Rung 4: Baryon Acoustic Oscillations** (cosmological). Sound waves frozen into the distribution of matter at recombination (380,000 years). Provide a "standard ruler" of ~490 Mly. DESI measures these across cosmic time.

**Rung 5: Standard Sirens** (gravitational waves). LIGO's detection of the neutron star merger GW170817 (2017) provided an independent distance measurement via gravitational wave amplitude. No electromagnetic calibration needed.

## 3. The Observable Universe

The observable universe has a diameter of about 93 billion light-years (28.5 Gpc), despite the universe being only 13.8 billion years old. This apparent paradox arises because space itself has been expanding during the time light has been traveling. Objects that emitted light 13.8 billion years ago are now ~46 billion light-years away.

Key numbers:
- **Diameter:** 93 billion light-years
- **Estimated galaxies:** ~2 trillion (Conselice et al. 2016, revised downward from Hubble Deep Field extrapolations)
- **Estimated stars:** ~10^24 (1 septillion)
- **Observable matter:** ~10^53 kg (10^80 baryons)
- **Age:** 13.799 +/- 0.021 Gyr

## 4. Large-Scale Structure: The Cosmic Web

At the largest scales, matter is arranged in a web-like structure:

- **Filaments:** Threadlike structures of galaxies hundreds of millions of light-years long. The cosmic web's backbone.
- **Walls:** Sheet-like concentrations of galaxies. The Sloan Great Wall stretches 1.37 billion light-years.
- **Superclusters:** Gravitationally bound collections of galaxy clusters. The Laniakea Supercluster (our home) spans 520 Mly.
- **Voids:** Vast empty regions 100-300 Mly across, containing very few galaxies.
- **Largest structures:** The Hercules-Corona Borealis Great Wall at ~10 billion light-years is the largest known structure.

Dark matter provides the scaffolding: galaxies form along dark matter filaments, detected via gravitational lensing.

## 5. The Planck Scale and Quantum Gravity

At the smallest conceivable scale, the Planck length (sqrt(hbar * G / c^3) = 1.6 x 10^-35 m), quantum mechanics and gravity become equally important. Below this, our current theories cannot make predictions.

Leading approaches:
- **String theory:** Strings vibrating in 10-11 dimensions. Predicts extra dimensions, supersymmetric particles (none found). Landscape of 10^500 vacua.
- **Loop quantum gravity:** Space itself is quantized into discrete "atoms" (spin networks). Predicts a minimum volume. No extra dimensions needed.
- **Asymptotic safety:** Gravity becomes well-behaved at high energies. Fewer radical assumptions.

Empirical tests are extremely difficult -- the Planck length is 10^20 times smaller than a proton. However, gravitational wave detectors and CMB observations may probe quantum gravity effects indirectly.

## 6. Why Scale Matters

Scale determines which forces dominate: the strong force at 10^-15 m, electromagnetism at atomic-molecular scales, gravity at planetary-cosmic scales. Quantum effects wash out at large scales (decoherence). Relativistic effects are negligible at low speeds and weak gravity.

This is why a "theory of everything" is so elusive -- the theories that work spectacularly at their respective scales are mathematically incompatible with each other. The universe does not care about our categories.

## 7. What Sources Agree On

1. The distance ladder is well-calibrated thanks to Gaia and JWST.
2. The observable universe is finite but unbounded -- we see a sphere, not an edge.
3. Large-scale structure is filamentary, with dark matter as scaffolding.
4. Quantum gravity effects are expected at the Planck scale but remain undetectable.
5. The hierarchy of scales is both the universe's organizing principle and physics's greatest challenge.

## 8. Where Sources Disagree

1. **Hubble tension:** Does the distance ladder give the "right" H0, or is there a systematic error in Cepheid calibrations?
2. **Quantum gravity approach:** String theory vs LQG vs asymptotic safety -- no experimental evidence favors any.
3. **Observable vs total universe:** Is the universe finite or infinite? Measurements are consistent with flat (infinite) but cannot rule out very large curvature (finite).
