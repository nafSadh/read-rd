# S7: Frontiers

## 1. James Webb Space Telescope

JWST, launched December 2021, has become the most productive space telescope ever built. Its 6.5-meter gold-coated mirror observes in infrared, seeing through dust and back to the universe's first galaxies.

**Key revelations (2023-2026):**
- Galaxies at z > 10 (< 500 Myr after Big Bang) that are larger and more mature than expected. Some appear to have formed stars impossibly quickly under standard Lambda-CDM.
- Supermassive black holes at z > 10 that are too massive to have grown from stellar-mass seeds in the available time. Suggests either super-Eddington accretion, direct collapse black holes, or primordial black hole seeds.
- "Little red dots" at high redshift: compact red sources that may be heavily dust-obscured active galactic nuclei or primordial black holes.
- Atmospheric characterization of dozens of exoplanets, including detection of CO2, H2O, and even signs of atmospheric loss in real time (WASP-121b).

JWST's biggest impact may be what it challenges: the too-early, too-massive galaxies and black holes stress-test Lambda-CDM at its edges.

## 2. Gravitational Wave Astronomy

LIGO's first detection (2015) opened an entirely new observational window. By November 2025, the LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA collaboration had detected 250+ gravitational wave events in the fourth observing run (O4).

**Milestones:**
- Black hole-black hole mergers (most common)
- Neutron star-neutron star mergers (GW170817: first multi-messenger event, independently measured H0)
- Neutron star-black hole mergers
- GW250114: unusual black hole merger suggesting exotic formation
- Sub-solar-mass merger candidate (Nov 2025): if confirmed, evidence for primordial black holes

**Upcoming:**
- O5 (IR1): planned Sep-Oct 2026 with improved sensitivity
- LISA (ESA, 2037): space-based detector for supermassive BH mergers
- Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer: next-generation ground detectors, 2030s

Gravitational waves detect what light cannot: black holes, mergers in dust, the very early universe.

## 3. Neutrino Astronomy

IceCube, a cubic-kilometer detector buried in Antarctic ice, has established neutrino astronomy as a new field.

**Key results:**
- First identification of a cosmic neutrino source: the blazar TXS 0506+056 (2018)
- NGC 1068 (Seyfert galaxy): most significant neutrino source
- Milky Way galactic neutrino emission detected
- 7 astrophysical tau neutrino candidates (2024) -- significant because all three neutrino flavors now detected from cosmic sources
- First low-energy (<100 TeV) cosmic neutrinos in the southern sky

**IceCube-Gen2:** 8 km^3 detector (8x current volume), 10x detection rate. Installation beginning 2025/2026 season.

Neutrinos travel straight through matter, providing unobstructed views of violent cosmic environments.

## 4. The Hubble Tension

The most significant crisis in modern cosmology. Two methods of measuring the universe's expansion rate (Hubble constant H0) give incompatible results:

- **Local measurement** (Cepheids + Type Ia SNe): H0 = 73.04 +/- 1.04 km/s/Mpc (Riess et al. 2022)
- **Early universe** (Planck CMB): H0 = 67.4 +/- 0.5 km/s/Mpc

The discrepancy is ~5 sigma -- well beyond what can be attributed to random error. JWST has validated the local Cepheid calibrations, making systematic error less likely.

**Proposed resolutions:**
- Early dark energy: an additional dark energy component active only in the first ~100,000 years
- Modified gravity at cosmological scales
- Primordial magnetic fields affecting recombination and CMB-derived H0
- Unknown systematic errors in one or both measurements
- New particle physics (extra neutrino species, decaying dark matter)

No resolution has gained consensus. The "Tensions in Cosmology 2025" conference at Nature Astronomy highlighted the depth of the problem.

## 5. The Vera Rubin Observatory

The Vera C. Rubin Observatory in Chile began its 10-year Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST) in early 2026 -- the most ambitious astronomical survey ever attempted.

**Specifications:**
- 8.4-meter mirror, 3.2 gigapixel camera (largest ever built for astronomy)
- Southern sky imaged every few nights in 6 filters
- 20 billion galaxies catalogued
- First light: June 2025. First alerts: February 2026.

**Science goals:**
- Dark matter mapping via weak gravitational lensing at unprecedented resolution
- Dark energy constraints via billions of galaxy distances
- Transient discovery: supernovae, kilonovae, asteroids
- Solar system: potentially 5 million new asteroid discoveries

Combined with DESI's spectroscopic measurements, Rubin will provide the most precise dark energy equation of state measurements ever.

## 6. DESI and Evolving Dark Energy

DESI's 2024-2025 results represent the most significant hint of new physics in cosmology since 1998. The 2.8-4.2 sigma preference for time-varying dark energy challenges the simplest Lambda-CDM model.

Key questions for the next few years:
- Will DESI Year 5+ data reach 5 sigma?
- Will Rubin Observatory's independent measurements confirm?
- What theoretical framework replaces Lambda if dark energy evolves?
- Can quintessence, phantom energy, or modified gravity be distinguished?

## 7. What We Might Know by 2050

- Whether the Hubble tension requires new physics (JWST + Rubin + DESI combined)
- Whether dark energy is constant or evolving (DESI + Rubin combined)
- Whether primordial gravitational waves exist (LiteBIRD + CMB-S4)
- 10x more gravitational wave events (Einstein Telescope, Cosmic Explorer)
- Detailed dark matter maps of the cosmic web (Rubin)
- Whether primordial black holes constitute a significant dark matter fraction
- Whether quantum gravity has any observable consequences
- Still probably not: what dark matter is, a theory of everything, or alien contact
