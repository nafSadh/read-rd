Freud’s Tendentious Jokes

In Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious (1905), Freud drew a crucial distinction. Innocent jokes are funny on their own terms. Tendentious jokes serve a purpose: they disguise aggression or desire in socially acceptable packaging[1]Freud: Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious (1905) →. The joke provides “forepleasure” — aesthetic enjoyment that bribes the listener into accepting the forbidden thought.

Innocent Jokes

Funny on their own terms — wordplay, absurdity, clever construction. No hidden agenda.

Tendentious Jokes

Serve hostile or obscene purposes. Disguise aggression/desire in socially acceptable packaging.

02

Gallows Humor as Defense

Freud’s 1928 essay “Humour” contains his most profound psychological observation. The condemned man led to the gallows on Monday who says “Well, this is a good beginning to the week” is performing something extraordinary: the super-ego comforts the ego, refusing to let reality impose suffering[2]Freud: “Humour” (1928) →.

Freud’s Highest Defense

Humor is not denial. It is a refusal to be diminished — the healthiest of all defense mechanisms. The mind asserts its invulnerability against a world that could destroy it.

The Willinger Intelligence Study

Willinger et al. (2017) tested 156 participants on dark humor cartoon appreciation, measuring IQ, mood, and aggressiveness[5]Willinger et al.: Cognitive and Emotional Demands of Black Humour (2017) →. Three groups emerged: the highest appreciation group had the highest IQ and lowest aggression. The most aggressive group appreciated dark humor the least.

The “Too Soon” Problem

McGraw’s BVT explains “too soon” precisely: temporal distance hasn’t yet made the violation benign[6]McGraw & Warren: Moral Violation and Distance (2010–2016) →. Geographic distance matters too — 9/11 jokes were acceptable sooner outside New York. Social distance: out-group suffering can be joked about sooner. Hypothetical distance: fiction becomes funny faster than reality.

  • Temporal — Tragedy + time = comedy
  • Geographic — Distance reduces felt severity
  • Social — In-group pain stays raw longer
  • Hypothetical — Fiction is funnier than reality
06

The Overton Window of Comedy

The line between funny and offensive is not fixed — it moves with cultural context, audience, and relationship. “Punching up” (mocking the powerful) is broadly accepted; “punching down” (mocking the vulnerable) is increasingly contested. Social media accelerates context collapse — jokes that work in small groups fail when broadcast.

Small Group

High trust, shared context, known audience. More transgression tolerated. “You had to be there.”

Broadcast

Low trust, no shared context, unknown audience. Narrower Overton window. Context collapse risk.

07

Key Tensions & Ethics

Dark humor lives at the intersection of several unresolved tensions: healthy coping versus normalization of harm, individual resilience versus social sensitivity, the comedian’s right to transgress versus the audience’s right not to be targeted. These tensions cannot be resolved — they can only be navigated.
