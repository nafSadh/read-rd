# S5: Cultural Comedy — Notes

## National Comedy Styles

### British Humor
- Dry wit: understatement, irony, deadpan delivery
- Self-deprecation as default mode — mocking oneself is expected
- Class consciousness as comedy engine — the gap between pretension and reality
- Absurdism (Monty Python, Eddie Izzard) as distinctly British
- "Keeping a straight face" valued — the humor is in the tension between content and delivery

### American Comedy
- More direct, broader, often physical
- Observational humor (Seinfeld, Carlin) as dominant mode
- Self-deprecation used selectively, not as default
- Stand-up as cultural institution — solo performer as truth-teller
- Greater comfort with vulgarity/explicitness than British comedy

### Japanese Manzai
- Duo format: boke (funny man) + tsukkomi (straight man)
- 1000+ year tradition rooted in classical performance
- Quick-exchange wordplay — language itself as comic mechanism
- Audience as "ratified overhearers" rather than addressed recipients (vs American direct address)
- Emphasis on the relationship between performers, not solo virtuosity

### Indian Comedy
- Mimicry traditions — imitation as humor
- Bollywood comedy blends physical, verbal, and musical
- Growing English-language stand-up scene (metro cities)
- Comedy of social observation: caste, class, regional differences
- Self-deprecating humor about Indian identity increasingly popular

### German "Non-Humor" (and its myth)
- Stereotype: "Germans have no sense of humor"
- Reality: German humor exists but tends toward Schadenfreude (pleasure in others' misfortune)
- Wordplay-heavy, often untranslatable
- Cabaret tradition: political satire as serious art form

## Cross-Cultural Translation Problems

### What Translates
- Physical comedy (slapstick, pratfalls) — most universal
- Situational humor — recognizable human situations
- Status reversal — powerful person humiliated (near-universal appeal)
- Surprise and incongruity — the basic mechanism works everywhere

### What Doesn't Translate
- Puns and wordplay — depend on phonological overlap unique to each language
- Cultural references — require shared context that doesn't cross borders
- Self-deprecation norms — charming in Britain, weird in some cultures
- Taboo boundaries — what's transgressive varies enormously
- Timing — comic rhythm differs by language and culture

### The Untranslatability Problem
- Some jokes simply cannot cross language barriers
- Humor localization (adapting) vs humor translation (converting)
- Film subtitle humor: translators must choose between accuracy and funniness
- Memes may be the first truly global humor form — visual + minimal text

## Internet Memes as Collective Humor

### Shifman's Framework
- Internet memes: digital content units with common characteristics, created with awareness of each other, circulated/imitated/transformed by many users
- Memes as participatory culture — humor as collective creation, not individual authorship
- Memes reveal what a culture finds funny at a given moment
- The speed of meme evolution mirrors the speed of cultural change

### What Memes Tell Us
- Collective humor is template-based: the format is shared, the content is personalized
- Humor as social currency: sharing memes signals group membership
- The lifecycle of a meme mirrors the lifecycle of a joke: fresh → familiar → stale → ironic
- Meta-humor (memes about memes) is uniquely digital

## Self-Deprecating vs Other-Deprecating Norms
- British/Japanese/Australian: self-deprecation as social leveling, expected
- American: self-deprecation used strategically, not as default
- Some cultures: self-deprecation seen as weakness, not humor
- The direction of the joke (self vs other) carries different social weight in different cultures
- Universal: the power dynamic between joker and target matters everywhere

## Key Insight
Humor is universal but jokes are not. Every culture has humor; no culture has the same jokes. The underlying mechanisms (incongruity, violation, surprise) are shared. The specific triggers, targets, and styles are culturally determined. Studying cross-cultural humor reveals what's deep (the mechanism) and what's surface (the content).
