# S3: Comedy Structure & Craft — Notes

## Fundamental Structures

### Setup-Punchline
- The atom of comedy. Setup creates expectation; punchline subverts it.
- Setup = premise (the world as the audience assumes it to be)
- Punchline = reinterpretation (the world as the comedian reveals it to be)
- The gap between assumption and revelation is the humor

### The Rule of Three
- Establish, reinforce, surprise (Tony Allen's formulation)
- Two items create a pattern; the third breaks it
- Three is the minimum needed: one doesn't establish, two doesn't confirm, three subverts
- Latin: "omne trium perfectum" — everything in threes is perfect
- Works at every scale: within a joke, within a bit, within a set

### Callbacks
- Return to material from earlier in the set, in a new context
- Rewards the audience for paying attention
- Creates continuity and layering
- The audience feels clever for recognizing the reference
- Most effective when unexpected and recontextualized

### Misdirection
- Lead the audience's expectations toward one interpretation, then deliver another
- The comedian controls the audience's prediction engine
- Successful misdirection requires the setup to be plausible on its own terms
- The punchline reframes the setup retroactively

## Timing

### Jack Benny's Pause
- The most famous moment in comedy timing: "Your money or your life!" [long silence] "I'm thinking it over!"
- The pause lets the audience complete the joke before the comedian speaks
- Benny built his entire career around what he didn't say
- TIME magazine called him "the grand master of comic timing" and compared him to Pinter and Beckett
- The silence creates the joke; the words merely confirm it

### Timing Principles
- Too fast: audience can't process the incongruity
- Too slow: audience figures it out and the surprise is lost
- Perfect timing: the punchline arrives at the exact moment of maximum cognitive tension
- Comedy is "tension and release" — timing controls both

## Genre Differences

### Stand-Up
- Solo performer, direct audience address, real-time feedback
- The "tight five": five minutes of polished material, gateway to professional comedy
- Working the room: adjusting material based on audience response
- Requires vulnerability — the comedian is alone

### Sketch
- Written, rehearsed, ensemble
- Premise-based: "what if [absurd premise] were true?"
- Can sustain longer than a joke but must escalate or transform
- Written rooms: collaborative development

### Improv
- Unscripted, collaborative, audience-derived
- "Yes, and..." — accept and build on what your partner offers
- Failure is built into the form — some bits die, that's part of the show
- Teaches the mechanics of comedy in real-time

### Sitcom / TV Comedy
- Written rooms with multiple writers
- Character-based humor that builds over episodes/seasons
- Running gags, callbacks across episodes
- Studio audience or laugh track as social laughter cue

## Why Explaining a Joke Kills It
This is actually a profound cognitive insight:
- Humor requires the audience to perform the incongruity resolution themselves
- The reward (dopamine hit) comes from the audience's own cognitive leap
- When you explain the joke, you perform the resolution for them
- They get the information but not the reward
- It's like telling someone the answer to a puzzle they were about to solve
- This is evidence for the cognitive-evolutionary theory: the "ha-ha" IS the reward for the resolution

## The Tight Five
- Five minutes of material, every line tested and refined
- Standard audition format for comedy clubs and festivals
- Represents months or years of development
- Each joke in the tight five has been performed hundreds of times
- The audience experiences spontaneity; the comedian experiences precision
