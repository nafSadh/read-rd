# S1: Theories of Humor — Notes

## The Three Classical Theories

### Incongruity Theory (Kant, Schopenhauer, Kierkegaard)
- Kant (1790): humor arises from "the sudden transformation of a strained expectation into nothing"
- Schopenhauer (1818): laughter results from "the sudden perception of the incongruity between a concept and the real objects"
- Modern form: humor = perception of incongruity + resolution (or failed resolution, in absurdist humor)
- Strongest for: puns, wordplay, logical jokes, absurdism
- Weakest for: tickling, slapstick, social laughter without cognitive content

### Superiority Theory (Hobbes, Plato, Aristotle)
- Hobbes (1651): laughter is "sudden glory arising from some sudden conception of some eminency in ourselves, by comparison with the infirmity of others"
- Plato: the laughable is a form of self-ignorance — we laugh at the pretentious
- Aristotle: comedy represents people as worse than they are
- Strongest for: mockery, satire, schadenfreude, roasts
- Weakest for: puns, absurdism, self-deprecating humor (which reverses the power dynamic)

### Relief Theory (Freud, Spencer)
- Spencer (1860): laughter discharges "nervous energy"
- Freud (1905): jokes allow expression of forbidden impulses (aggression, sexuality) by disguising them in a socially acceptable form
- Freud's joke-work parallels dream-work: condensation, displacement, representation
- Strongest for: dirty jokes, hostile humor, humor about taboo topics
- Weakest for: puns, innocent wordplay, observational humor with no taboo element

## Modern Theories

### Benign Violation Theory (McGraw & Warren, 2010)
- Something is humorous when it: (1) is a violation, (2) is benign, and (3) both appraisals occur simultaneously
- Violations: threats to physical well-being, identity, beliefs, norms
- Benign because: alternative interpretation exists, committed person is seen as harmless, norm is seen as illegitimate, psychologically distant
- Distance is key moderator: temporal, spatial, social, hypothetical
- "Too soon" = violation not yet benign. "Too late" = violation no longer salient
- Strongest unified theory to date, but struggles with pure wordplay and silliness

### Cognitive-Evolutionary Theory (Hurley, Dennett & Adams, 2011)
- "Inside Jokes": humor evolved as reward for epistemic vigilance
- The mind constantly generates assumptions/predictions. Some are wrong.
- Humor is the reward for catching these errors — the "ha-ha" rewards cognitive debugging
- Explains the ha-ha / a-ha connection: both involve resolving incongruity
- Most sophisticated descendant of incongruity theory
- Critics: overly cognitive, doesn't explain social/emotional aspects

### Play Theory (Morreall)
- Humor requires freedom from practical concern — a "play frame"
- We can only find things funny when we're not threatened
- Explains why the same event can be terrifying in one context and hilarious in another
- The frame, not the content, determines whether something is funny

## Why No Single Theory Works
Each theory captures a real aspect of humor:
- Incongruity: the cognitive mechanism
- Superiority: the social dynamic
- Relief: the emotional function
- Benign violation: the appraisal process
- Cognitive-evolutionary: the adaptive purpose
- Play: the contextual prerequisite

A joke can simultaneously be incongruous, make us feel superior, release tension, violate a norm benignly, debug a false assumption, and occur within a play frame. The theories aren't wrong — they're partial.
