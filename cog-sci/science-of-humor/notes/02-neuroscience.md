# S2: The Neuroscience of Laughter — Notes

## How the Brain Processes Jokes

### The Two-Stage Model
1. **Setup processing** (left hemisphere dominant): Language comprehension, expectation building, prediction generation. Left inferior frontal gyrus, temporal cortex active.
2. **Punchline processing** (bilateral + right hemisphere): Incongruity detection (temporo-parietal junction), resolution attempt (prefrontal cortex), reward if resolved (ventral striatum, nucleus accumbens).

### Mobbs et al. (2003) — The Landmark Study
- First high-field (3T) fMRI study of humor appreciation
- Showed humor modulates mesolimbic dopaminergic reward system
- Key regions: nucleus accumbens, ventral striatum, VTA
- Funnier = stronger BOLD signal in reward regions
- Same circuit activated by food, sex, drugs — humor is literally rewarding

### Four-Stage Model (Wattendorf et al., 2023)
1. Setup encoding
2. Incongruity detection
3. Resolution
4. Appreciation (reward)
- Critical finding: comprehension (dorsal striatum) and appreciation (ventral striatum) are dissociable
- You can understand a joke without finding it funny, and vice versa
- Different neural substrates for "I get it" vs "that's funny"

## Laughter Neuroscience

### Duchenne vs Non-Duchenne Laughter
- **Duchenne**: Genuine — involves both zygomatic major (mouth) and orbicularis oculi (eyes, "crow's feet"). Named after French neurologist Guillaume Duchenne.
- **Non-Duchenne**: Social/voluntary — mouth only, no eye crinkle. Controllable.
- Different neural pathways: involuntary (brainstem, basal ganglia) vs voluntary (motor cortex)
- Humans can distinguish them intuitively, even cross-culturally

### Contagious Laughter
- Provine (1992): hearing laughter alone is sufficient to trigger laughter
- Mirror neuron system mediates: seeing/hearing laughter activates premotor cortex as if laughing
- TV laugh tracks exploit this: they work even when we know they're fake
- Contagion stronger for in-group members
- Scott et al. (2014): social context modulates laughter perception

### Provine's Key Findings
- People laugh 30x more in social situations than alone
- The speaker laughs 46% more than the audience
- Laughter punctuates speech at clause boundaries, not randomly
- Laughter is primarily a social signal, not a response to humor
- Most conversational laughter follows non-humorous statements

## Neurochemistry of Laughter
- Releases dopamine (reward, pleasure)
- Releases endorphins (natural opioids — pain relief, bonding)
- Releases serotonin (mood regulation)
- Decreases cortisol (stress hormone) — 32-37% reduction in meta-analysis
- Increases natural killer cell activity (immune function)
- Activates parasympathetic nervous system (calming)

## Gelotology
- Study of humor and laughter and their effects on the body
- From Greek gelos (laughter)
- Established as research field mid-20th century
- Bridges neuroscience, psychology, physiology, and medicine
- William Fry (Stanford) was a pioneer

## Key Insight
The neuroscience reveals something the theories miss: humor is not primarily about cognition. The brain processes jokes through reward circuits normally associated with survival needs. Evolution made humor feel as good as food. That's not an accident — it's a clue about how important humor was (and is) to human fitness.
