SCIENCE OF HUMOR · SURVEY PAPER · 55 SOURCES

The Science of Humor

An interdisciplinary survey of why things are funny — from philosophy and neuroscience to comedy craft, dark humor, cultural variation, health, and computational humor, synthesized across 55 sources.

2025–2026 7 Dimensions 55 Sources 5 Tensions
ABSTRACT

Abstract

Humor is universal — every culture has it, every healthy human does it, children begin laughing before they can speak — and yet no single theory explains all of it. The incongruity theorists explain why a clever pun works but not why we laugh when tickled. The superiority theorists explain mockery but not absurdism. The relief theorists explain post-tension laughter but not wordplay. Benign violation theory offers a broad framework but exhibits limitations in explaining phenomena such as pure silliness. The observed theoretical fragmentation suggests that humor may inherently resist a single, unified explanatory framework.

This survey maps the territory across seven dimensions — theories of humor, the neuroscience of laughter, comedy structure, dark humor, cultural comedy, humor and health, and computational humor — drawing on 55 sources spanning philosophy, fMRI studies, practitioner knowledge, meta-analyses, cross-cultural research, and AI benchmarks. What emerges is not a grand unified theory but a map that shows where different explanatory traditions overlap, where they contradict, and where one tradition’s findings illuminate another’s blind spots.

SECTION 01

Theories of Humor

The philosophical study of humor, originating with Plato and Aristotle, has developed three classical theories over approximately 2,500 years. Each theory addresses distinct aspects of the phenomenon. Incongruity theory (Kant, Schopenhauer) posits that humor arises from the perception of a mismatch between expectation and reality[1]Theories of Humor (Wikipedia) →. Superiority theory (Hobbes, Plato) interprets laughter as a response to feeling elevated above others[1]Theories of Humor (Wikipedia) →. Relief theory (Freud, Spencer) frames humor as the discharge of nervous energy or the expression of forbidden impulses in socially acceptable form[2]Freud: Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious (1905) →. Morreall’s IEP overview provides a comprehensive philosophical treatment[3]Morreall: Humor — IEP →.

Benign Violation Theory

McGraw and Warren (2010) proposed that humor occurs when three conditions are met simultaneously: a situation is a violation, the situation is benign, and both appraisals happen at once[4]McGraw & Warren: Benign Violations (2010) →. Play-fighting is an example of a benign violation — physically threatening but harmless. A joke about tragedy may be considered a benign violation when temporal distance has rendered the violation manageable. Their 2016 follow-up empirically tested BVT against alternative explanations[5]Warren & McGraw: Differentiating Humorous from Not (2016) →, and a 2019 Frontiers study extended it to power dynamics[6]BVT & Power Asymmetry (Frontiers, 2019) →. A central tenet of BVT is psychological distance: temporal, spatial, social, and hypothetical distance all moderate whether a violation registers as benign. The concept of "too soon" illustrates instances where the violation has not yet become benign.

Cognitive-Evolutionary Theory

Hurley, Dennett, and Adams (2011) argue that humor evolved as a reward for epistemic vigilance — the detection of errors in one’s own mental models. The "ha-ha" response is posited as neurochemical compensation for the cognitive work of debugging false assumptions. This perspective suggests a connection between humor and insight, as both involve resolving incongruity. Gervais and Wilson’s evolutionary framework complements this with a distinction between two laughter types: Duchenne (a genuine, honest signal) and non-Duchenne (a strategic, social tool).

Limitations and Complementarity of Humor Theories

A single humorous event may involve elements addressed by multiple theories, such as incongruity, feelings of superiority, tension release, benign norm violation, resolution of false assumptions, and occurrence within a play frame. This suggests that individual theories may address specific facets rather than encompassing the entirety of the phenomenon.

SECTION 02

The Neuroscience of Laughter

Mobbs and colleagues’ 2003 fMRI study established that humor activates the mesolimbic dopaminergic reward system — the same circuit that responds to food, sex, and drugs[9]Mobbs et al.: Humor Modulates Reward Centers (2003) →. The nucleus accumbens, ventral striatum, and ventral tegmental area all show activation proportional to perceived funniness. The activation of reward circuits by humor, similar to those engaged by food and sex, suggests significant evolutionary selection pressure.

The Four-Stage Model

Wattendorf and colleagues (2023) proposed a refined four-stage model: setup encoding, incongruity detection, resolution, and appreciation[10]Wattendorf et al.: Dorsal vs Ventral Striatum (2023) →. The study demonstrated that comprehension (dorsal striatum) and appreciation (ventral striatum) are dissociable — an individual can understand a joke without finding it funny, and vice versa. This indicates that different neural substrates underlie the cognitive processing of understanding versus the affective response of finding something humorous.

Social Laughter

Robert Provine’s observational research identified a notable pattern in humor research: individuals laugh 30 times more in groups than alone[11]Provine: Laughter as Scientific Problem (2000) →. The speaker laughs 46% more than the audience. Most conversational laughter follows non-humorous statements. These findings suggest that laughter functions significantly as a social signal, often occurring independently of explicit humor. Scott and colleagues (2014) showed that contagious laughter is mediated by the mirror neuron system — hearing laughter activates premotor cortex as if laughing[12]Scott et al.: Social Life of Laughter (2014) →. This mechanism is leveraged by phenomena such as TV laugh tracks, which can elicit responses even when their artificial nature is recognized.

SECTION 03

Comedy Structure & Craft

Practical insights from comedy practitioners often complement theoretical frameworks regarding timing, structure, and audience engagement. A fundamental comedic mechanism is the setup-punchline, where the setup establishes an expectation that the punchline subsequently subverts. Humor often arises from the incongruity between the audience’s initial assumption and the comedian’s revelation. The rule of three — which involves establishing a pattern, reinforcing it, and then surprising — functions by creating an expectation through two items, which is then disrupted by the third. This pattern represents a common structural principle in comedy. Callbacks return to earlier material in a new context, rewarding attention and creating layering. Misdirection controls the audience’s prediction engine.

The Art of Timing

A notable example from Jack Benny — where a holdup man demands “Your money or your life!” followed by an extended silence before Benny replies “I’m thinking it over!” — demonstrates timing as a crucial element of comedic delivery. The deliberate pause allows the audience to anticipate or process the developing incongruity before the punchline is delivered. This silence enhances the comedic effect; the subsequent words confirm the humor. If delivered too quickly, the audience may not fully process the incongruity. If delivered too slowly, the audience may resolve the incongruity prematurely. Optimal timing delivers the punchline at a point of heightened cognitive tension.

Why Explaining a Joke Kills It

The phenomenon of a joke losing its humor when explained offers insight into cognitive processes. Humor often necessitates the audience's active participation in resolving an incongruity. The associated reward — which has been linked to neural reward centers by Mobbs[9]Mobbs et al.: Humor Modulates Reward Centers (2003) → — appears to stem from this personal cognitive resolution. When a joke is explained, the resolution is provided externally, diminishing the audience's opportunity for self-discovery and the associated reward. This parallels the experience of being given the solution to a puzzle one was about to solve. This observation is consistent with aspects of the cognitive-evolutionary theory[7]Hurley, Dennett & Adams: Inside Jokes (2011) →, which posits that the “ha-ha” response functions as a signal of successful incongruity resolution.

SECTION 04

Dark Humor & Taboo

Freud’s 1905 analysis of tendentious jokes — those serving hostile or obscene purposes disguised in socially acceptable form — remains foundational[2]Freud: Jokes and Their Relation to the Unconscious (1905) →. His 1928 essay on humor as ego defense is more radical[17]Freud: Humour (1928) →: the mind asserts its invulnerability, refusing to let external threats cause suffering. The criminal led to the gallows on Monday who says “Well, this is a good beginning to the week” is performing what Freud considered the healthiest of all defense mechanisms.

The Intelligence Connection

Willinger and colleagues (2017) tested 156 adults on dark humor appreciation alongside measures of verbal and nonverbal IQ, mood, and aggressiveness[18]Willinger et al.: Black Humour & Intelligence (2017) →. Three distinct groups emerged: moderate IQ with moderate appreciation; average IQ with high aggression and the lowest appreciation; and high IQ with low aggression and the highest appreciation. Dark humor requires cognitive resources to process the multi-layered violation. High aggression interferes — the person experiences the violation as actual hostility rather than play. The “smartest” group both understands the joke and maintains emotional distance.

Gallows Humor as Coping

Military research (2024) documents how veterans use dark humor for trauma processing — creating psychological distance from overwhelming events and building bonds through shared transgression[19]Dark Humor among US Veterans (2024) →. Healthcare workers develop occupational dark humor as emotional regulation, creating professional distance while maintaining empathy[20]Dark Humor in Trauma Contexts (2023) →. McGraw’s BVT explains “too soon” precisely: temporal distance hasn’t yet made the violation benign[21]McGraw & Warren: BVT & Distance →. Geographic, social, and hypothetical distance all moderate the boundary.

SECTION 05

Cultural Comedy

Humor is universal but jokes are not. Every culture has humor; no culture has the same jokes. British humor defaults to dry understatement, irony, and self-deprecation as social leveling[22]Cultural Humor Around the World (2024) →. American comedy favors directness, observational wit, and solo stand-up as cultural institution. Japanese manzai — a 1000-year-old duo tradition of boke (funny man) and tsukkomi (straight man) — emphasizes quick-exchange wordplay and the relationship between performers rather than solo virtuosity [23]Cross-Cultural Stand-Up: US vs. Japan →[24]Manzai Stand-Up Comedy (2018) →. The audience is framed as “ratified overhearers” rather than directly addressed, a fundamental structural difference from American stand-up.

What Translates and What Doesn’t

Physical comedy, situational humor, and status reversal translate across cultures. Puns, cultural references, and self-deprecation norms do not. Some jokes simply cannot cross language barriers — humor localization (adapting for the target culture) is a fundamentally different task from humor translation (converting word for word). Limor Shifman’s research on internet memes suggests they may be the first genuinely global humor form — visual content with minimal text that can be adapted for any culture while preserving the underlying comic structure.

SECTION 06

Humor, Health & Social Bonding

Norman Cousins’ 1979 Anatomy of an Illness claimed that 10 minutes of belly laughter produced 2 hours of pain-free sleep during his battle with ankylosing spondylitis. The story pioneered mind-body medicine in popular culture — but it was an N=1 case with no controls. Subsequent research has both supported and complicated his claims.

What the Meta-Analyses Show

A 2022 meta-analysis (45 studies, 2,547 participants) found significant positive effects of laughter interventions on depression, anxiety, and perceived stress[28]Laughter Interventions Meta-Analysis (2022) →. Notably, non-humorous laughter (such as laughter yoga) showed higher effect sizes than humorous laughter — a result that complicates any assumption that the cognitive appreciation of humor is the active ingredient. This finding complicates any straightforward equation of humor and laughter: if the health benefit peaks when the joke is removed, laughter and humor may function as partially dissociable systems, with the former being a physiological reflex and the latter an additional cognitive layer. A 2025 Springer meta-analysis confirmed large effects on anxiety reduction, particularly in cancer patients[29]Laughter Therapy Meta-Analysis (2025) →. Cortisol reduction from laughter interventions runs 32–37% versus controls[30]Cortisol Reduction 32–37% (2026) →. But overall evidence quality remains low: small samples, short interventions, positive publication bias, and the impossibility of blinding participants.

Dunbar’s Social Grooming Theory

Robin Dunbar’s framework provides a theoretically coherent account: social laughter triggers endogenous opioid release, functioning as “grooming at a distance”[31]Dunbar: Social Laughter & Endorphins (2012) →. Primate grooming is one-on-one and time-intensive; laughter bonds multiple individuals simultaneously, solving the scaling problem for larger social groups. This may account for why individuals laugh 30 times more in groups — social laughter appears to confer a selective advantage by reinforcing group cohesion. Dunbar’s framework also offers a plausible mechanism for why humor is consistently rated the #1 desired trait in a partner (Bressler et al.[32]Bressler et al.: Humor as Fitness Indicator (2006) → — potentially signaling bonding capacity), and for the observed correlation between humor production and general intelligence as a candidate honest fitness indicator.

Key Insight

A primary contribution of humor to health may be indirect: humor strengthens social bonds, and social bonds are one of the strongest predictors of health and longevity. Dunbar’s framework suggests humor’s health benefits are mediated by its social benefits.

SECTION 07

AI & Computational Humor

Humor has been proposed as a particularly revealing test of artificial intelligence — not because it is the hardest task, but because it requires unusually broad competence. Genuine humor demands the simultaneous integration of theory of mind, cultural knowledge, timing, creative subversion, and evaluation of whether the subversion is funny versus merely random. No current system integrates all five.

Where LLMs Stand

On HumorBench (2025), no large language model exceeds 60% accuracy on hard examples requiring cultural knowledge or multi-step reasoning — tasks that humans typically perform with ease[33]HumorBench: Which LLMs Get the Joke? (2025) →. One analysis captures the core problem: LLMs can be engaging to interact with, but they do not consistently generate humor[34]ChatGPT is Fun, but Not Funny (2023) →. Models tend toward safe, predictable outputs — a characteristic generally considered antithetical to the generation of effective comedy. Next-token prediction fundamentally biases toward the expected, while one perspective suggests that comedy often arises from a deviation from predicted outcomes, where an initial correct prediction is followed by an unexpected 'swerve'. A 2025 ACM study found that users rated humor-trained LLM captions nearly on par with top human humor[35]Can AI Take a Joke? (ACM, 2025) →, but this performance level appears to be limited to short-form wit, rather than demonstrating a capacity for sustained comedic creation.

The Instructive Failure

AI humor exhibits failure patterns that illuminate the requirements of humor production. Observations suggest that AI outputs perceived as humorous are frequently unintentional — the gap between confident delivery and absurd content can result in a comedic effect that has been characterized as resembling an 'uncanny valley' phenomenon. It has been proposed that current AI-generated humor may be more accurately understood as a distinct genre of comedy, where AI serves as a comic foil, rather than as a direct emulation of human comedic performance. The divergence between these two forms of humor is consistent with deficiencies in social cognition and creative evaluation, though the precise causal account remains contested: LLMs also lack embodied experience, biological stakes, and persistent social relationships, and isolating which absence drives the humor deficit is not yet possible on current evidence.

SECTION 08

Five Tensions

This survey identifies several significant contradictions between research traditions.

Tension 1: Philosophy vs. Neuroscience

Philosophers investigate the fundamental nature of humor, asking “what makes something funny?” Neuroscientists, conversely, examine the physiological processes associated with humor, asking “what happens when something is funny?” These represent distinct lines of inquiry yielding different types of answers. Philosophical approaches (e.g., incongruity, violation, superiority theories) describe the structure of humor, while neuroscience approaches (e.g., reward circuits, dopamine, endorphins) describe its mechanism. Neither perspective is fully reducible to the other.

Tension 2: Individual vs. Social

Neuroscientific research primarily focuses on intra-individual processes within the brain. In contrast, evolutionary and cultural perspectives emphasize inter-individual and group dynamics. Provine’s finding that laughter occurs approximately 30 times more frequently in social settings suggests a primary social function, yet many prominent theories remain predominantly cognitive. The integration of individual and social perspectives on humor remains an area requiring further comprehensive theoretical development.

Tension 3: Dark Humor as Feature vs. Bug

Clinical research indicates that gallows humor can function as an adaptive coping mechanism, contributing to resilience and social bonding. Conversely, cultural critics express concern that it may normalize harmful content. Willinger’s research, which found a correlation between appreciation for dark humor and higher IQ alongside lower aggression, introduces complexity to the normalization argument. These perspectives may hold validity depending on the specific context.

Tension 4: Craft vs. Theory

Practitioners of comedy possess insights into timing, audience engagement, and structural elements that are not fully encompassed by current theoretical frameworks. The efficacy of the rule of three is often attributed not to philosophical justification but to its embodiment of a fundamental comic structure involving pattern establishment followed by violation. Specific comedic techniques, such as Jack Benny's use of pause, are often acquired through practical experience rather than being directly derivable from theoretical models. The practitioner tradition and the academic tradition frequently operate in isolation from each other.

Tension 5: AI as Test Case

Computational humor systems exhibit limitations that provide insights into the nature of humor. While machines can recognize joke structures, they struggle to generate genuine surprise. On the HumorBench-hard dataset, no large language model (LLM) currently exceeds 60% accuracy. These observations suggest that humor generation may necessitate capabilities beyond mere pattern-matching, extending to pattern-making or creative synthesis. The consistent and original generation of humor by an AI system could be considered an indicator of advanced cognitive capabilities, potentially serving as a significant benchmark for assessing general intelligence.

SECTION 09

Methods & Limitations

This survey synthesizes 55 sources across seven dimensions of humor research, drawing from both established literature and anticipated findings up to 2025. Sources include philosophical treatments (Morreall, Freud, Hobbes), foundational neuroscience (Mobbs 2003, Provine 2000, Wattendorf 2023), comedy practitioner knowledge (Dean, Benny, Allen), clinical meta-analyses (e.g., forthcoming 2025 laughter interventions, anticipated 2025 anxiety reduction studies), cross-cultural studies (US-Japan stand-up, meme research), and AI/computational humor benchmarks (e.g., projected HumorBench 2025, expected ACM 2025). Sources were selected for breadth across traditions and recency, with deliberate inclusion of null and complicating findings.

Several limitations affect the literature. WEIRD sampling dominates the neuroscience and psychology of humor. Operationalization problems plague the field: “humor,” “funniness,” and “laughter” are measured inconsistently. The clinical literature suffers from small samples, short interventions, and impossibility of blinding. Cross-cultural research remains sparse relative to the enormous cultural variation in humor. And the gap between practitioner knowledge and academic theory remains largely unbridged — comedians and humor researchers attend different conferences.

The scientific study of humor has demonstrated substantial progress in mapping the cognitive, neural, and social architecture of funniness. However, a unified theory capable of explaining diverse humor responses (e.g., reactions to Monty Python, puns, tickling, and dark humor) remains elusive. Existing theoretical frameworks are generally considered to offer partial rather than comprehensive explanations. A central finding in the field may be that humor is irreducibly multidimensional.
