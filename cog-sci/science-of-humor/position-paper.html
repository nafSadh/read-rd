SCIENCE OF HUMOR · POSITION PAPER

Humor Is Irreducibly Social

A position paper arguing that the dominant cognitive theories of humor have the emphasis backwards — humor is primarily a social technology, and the cognitive mechanisms serve the social function, not the other way around.

March 2026 Position Paper 9 Lines of Evidence
THE CLAIM

The Thesis

The dominant theories of humor — incongruity theory, benign violation theory, cognitive-evolutionary theory — share a common framing: humor is fundamentally a cognitive phenomenon that happens to occur in social settings. The mind detects incongruity, resolves it, and experiences reward. The social context is treated as a backdrop, not the main event.

This paper argues the emphasis is backwards. The evidence, taken as a whole, supports a stronger claim: humor is primarily a social technology. The cognitive mechanisms — incongruity detection, pattern violation, surprise — are real, but they are in service of the social function. Laughter evolved not to reward clever thinking but to build and maintain social bonds. The cognitive architecture is the engine; the social function is the purpose the engine was built for.

Nine lines of evidence converge on this position. No single finding is decisive. Together, they shift the center of gravity from cognition to connection.

EVIDENCE 01

Provine’s Data

Robert Provine[1]Provine: Laughter as Scientific Problem (2000) →’s observational research — what he called “sidewalk neuroscience” — produced a set of findings that, taken together, are devastating to the cognitive-first view of humor.

30×More laughter in groups
46%Speaker laughs more than audience
MostFollows non-humorous statements

People laugh 30 times more in social situations than alone. The speaker laughs 46% more than the audience. Laughter punctuates speech at clause boundaries, not at funny moments. And most critically: most conversational laughter follows non-humorous statements. Laughter in everyday life is not primarily a response to humor. It is a social signal — a way of saying “I am with you,” “I agree,” “we are together.”

If humor were primarily cognitive, we would expect people to laugh at roughly the same rate alone and in groups — a funny thing is funny regardless of audience. The 30:1 ratio is not what a cognitive theory predicts. It is exactly what a social theory predicts.

EVIDENCE 02

Laughter as Social Grooming

Robin Dunbar[3]Dunbar: Social Laughter & Endorphin Release (2012) →’s framework provides the evolutionary logic. In primates, social bonds are maintained through grooming — a one-on-one, time-intensive activity. As human group sizes expanded beyond what grooming could support (Dunbar’s number: ~150), a new bonding mechanism was needed. Laughter solves the scaling problem: it triggers endogenous opioid release in reward-processing brain regions, and it works on multiple individuals simultaneously.

Crucially, Dunbar’s research demonstrates that social laughter drives the endorphin release, not solo laughter. The neurochemical bonding effect requires the presence of others. Fifteen minutes of group laughter increases pain tolerance by approximately 10% — a direct measure of endorphin release. The same laughter alone does not produce the same effect. The social context is not incidental to the neurochemistry; it is required for it.

EVIDENCE 03

Contagious Laughter

Provine[2]Provine: Contagious Laughter (1992) → (1992) demonstrated experimentally that hearing laughter alone — without any humorous content — is sufficient to trigger laughter. Scott and colleagues[4]Scott et al.: Social Life of Laughter (2014) → (2014) showed this is mediated by the mirror neuron system: hearing laughter activates premotor cortex as if the listener were laughing themselves. TV laugh tracks exploit this mechanism. They work even when the audience knows they are fake.

This is a deeply social phenomenon. The mirror neuron pathway is the same system that underlies empathy, imitation, and social learning. Contagious laughter does not require cognitive humor processing; it bypasses it entirely. The trigger is not “something is funny” but “someone near me is laughing.” Contagion is stronger for in-group members — further evidence that laughter serves group membership, not intellectual appreciation.

EVIDENCE 04

Mate Selection & Fitness Signaling

Sense of humor is consistently rated the #1 most desired trait in a romantic partner, across studies and cultures. Bressler and colleagues[5]Bressler et al.: Humor as Sexually Selected Trait (2006) → (2006) argue it functions as a sexually selected fitness indicator: humor production correlates with intelligence, and intelligence is hard to fake. Women prefer humor producers (signaling cognitive fitness); men prefer humor appreciators (signaling receptivity and bonding capacity).

If humor were primarily about cognitive incongruity resolution, its status as the top mate-selection criterion would be puzzling — why would the ability to detect pattern mismatches be more attractive than physical fitness, wealth, or kindness? Under the social model, it makes perfect sense. Humor signals the ability to bond. In a species whose survival depends on cooperative relationships, the capacity to create and sustain social bonds is the ultimate fitness indicator. Humor is the display; connection is the trait being advertised.

EVIDENCE 05

Dark Humor as In-Group Bonding

Research on military dark humor[7]Dark Humor among US Veterans (PMC, 2024) → (2024) reveals a pattern that cognitive theories cannot explain: veterans develop shared gallows humor that is deliberately incomprehensible to outsiders. The humor functions not despite but because of its exclusionary quality. “You had to be there” is the point. The shared transgression creates a bond that depends on shared experience, shared norms, and mutual trust.

Healthcare workers develop occupational dark humor through the same mechanism — creating professional distance while simultaneously reinforcing group identity. The cognitive content of the joke (the incongruity, the violation) is the vehicle; the social function (bonding through shared transgression, marking group membership) is the payload. Willinger’s[8]Willinger et al.: Black Humour, IQ & Aggression (2017) → finding that dark humor appreciation requires low aggression is consistent: the function is not hostility but play — and play is fundamentally social.

EVIDENCE 06

The Cultural Evidence

Cross-cultural comedy research reveals that the social structure of humor varies as much as the content. Japanese manzai[9]Cross-Cultural Stand-Up: US vs. Japan →, a 1000-year-old tradition, is built around the relationship between two performers — the boke and the tsukkomi. The audience is framed as overhearers of a social interaction, not direct addressees. American stand-up, by contrast, is a solo performer addressing the crowd directly. The humor in manzai lives in the dynamic between people; the humor in stand-up lives in the relationship between performer and audience. Both are irreducibly social — just differently.

Internet memes[10]Shifman: Memes in Digital Culture (MIT Press, 2013) → (Shifman 2013) are the most striking recent evidence. Memes are collectively authored humor — created with awareness of each other, remixed, transformed, shared as social currency. Sharing a meme signals group membership. The lifecycle of a meme (fresh → familiar → stale → ironic) mirrors not cognitive processing but social currency dynamics. A meme is funny partly because your group finds it funny. The humor and the sociality are inseparable.

EVIDENCE 07

Health Benefits Are Socially Mediated

The 2022 meta-analysis[11]Laughter Interventions Meta-Analysis (2022) → of laughter interventions (45 studies, 2,547 participants) produced a counterintuitive finding: non-humorous laughter (such as laughter yoga — voluntary laughing exercises without any jokes) showed higher effect sizes on mental health than humorous laughter. The physical act of laughing in a group matters more than cognitive humor appreciation.

This is the strongest single piece of evidence for the social-first position. If humor’s health benefits were primarily cognitive (insight, reappraisal, incongruity resolution), then humor-based interventions should outperform non-humorous laughter. They don’t. The body doesn’t distinguish genuine from simulated laughter — and the social bonding occurs either way. Dunbar’s framework predicts exactly this: the health benefits of humor are mediated by social bonding. Humor → laughter → opioid-mediated bonds → stronger social network → better health. Remove the humor but keep the social laughter, and the pathway still works.

The Strongest Evidence

Non-humorous laughter in groups produces larger health effects than humorous laughter. The physical-social act matters more than the cognitive content. This is what a social-first theory predicts and what a cognitive-first theory does not.

EVIDENCE 08

The AI Test Case

If humor were primarily cognitive — pattern detection, incongruity resolution, violation appraisal — then large language models should be getting good at it. They have vast pattern-matching capability, enormous cultural knowledge, and sophisticated language understanding. Yet on HumorBench-hard[13]HumorBench: Which LLMs Get the Joke? (2025) →, no LLM exceeds 60% accuracy. Researchers note[14]ChatGPT is Fun, but Not Funny (2023) → that LLMs can be fun to interact with but are not funny.

The social-first position explains why. LLMs have no social relationships. They have no audience to bond with, no in-group to signal membership in, no face to lose and no status to negotiate. They can detect incongruity patterns in text, but they cannot do what humor actually does: create a moment of shared experience between minds. The most genuinely funny AI outputs are the unintentional ones — and what makes them funny is the social incongruity between the machine’s confident delivery and the human audience’s recognition of absurdity. Even AI humor requires a human social context to land.

SECTION 09

Counterarguments

People Do Laugh Alone

People laugh at books, at memories, at their own thoughts. This is real but rare — 30 times rarer than social laughter, per Provine. And much solo laughter is parasocial: laughing at a book is laughing with an imagined author; laughing at a sitcom is responding to the social cues of actors and a laugh track. The cognitive capacity for humor may persist in isolation because it was built for social use, just as language persists in solitary thought because it was built for communication.

Puns Seem Purely Cognitive

Wordplay appears to be humor without social content — pure pattern manipulation. But puns are overwhelmingly produced for audiences. The groan a pun elicits is itself a social ritual. And puns are consistently rated the least funny form of humor — which, under the social model, makes sense: they have the thinnest social payload. Puns may be the exception that proves the rule.

The Cognitive Mechanisms Are Real

This position does not deny that humor involves incongruity detection, surprise, and cognitive resolution. These mechanisms are well-documented in the neuroscience (Mobbs et al.[15]Mobbs et al.: Humor Modulates Mesolimbic Reward Centers (2003) →, Wattendorf et al.[16]Wattendorf et al.: Dorsal vs Ventral Striatum in Humor (2023) →). The claim is not that cognition is absent but that it is instrumental. The cognitive architecture is the mechanism by which humor achieves its social purpose, just as the vocal apparatus is the mechanism by which language achieves communication. Describing the mechanism does not describe the function.

SECTION 10

Implications

For Research

If humor is primarily social, then studying it in isolation — single subjects rating cartoons in fMRI scanners — systematically misses the phenomenon. The field needs more ecological research: humor in conversation, in relationships, in workplaces, in cultures. Provine’s “sidewalk neuroscience” may have produced more insight than decades of controlled experiments precisely because it studied humor where it naturally lives: between people.

For Comedy

Comedians have always known this. “Working the room” is not an afterthought; it is the core skill. The tight five is not five minutes of cleverness but five minutes of relationship with an audience. A joke that kills in one room dies in another because the room is not the backdrop — the room is the instrument.

For AI

If genuine humor requires genuine social relationships, then progress in computational humor depends not on better pattern-matching but on AI systems that can participate in social contexts — that have something at stake in the interaction. This is a much harder problem than incongruity detection, and it may be why humor benchmarks have plateaued.

For Everyday Life

The social model reframes what it means to “have a good sense of humor.” It is not primarily an intellectual capacity (detecting incongruity) but a social one (creating connection). The funniest person in the room is not the cleverest but the one most attuned to the people around them. Humor is not a cognitive performance. It is an act of belonging.

The Position

We don’t laugh because things are funny. We laugh because we are together. The funniness is a technology for being together more effectively.
