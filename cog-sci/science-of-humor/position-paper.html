SCIENCE OF HUMOR · POSITION PAPER

Humor Is Irreducibly Social

A position paper arguing that the dominant cognitive theories of humor have the emphasis backwards — humor is primarily a social technology, and the cognitive mechanisms serve the social function, not the other way around.

March 2025 Position Paper 9 Lines of Evidence
THE CLAIM

The Thesis

Dominant theories of humor — incongruity theory, benign violation theory, and cognitive-evolutionary theory — primarily frame humor as a cognitive phenomenon. These perspectives often describe the mind's detection and resolution of incongruity as the core mechanism leading to reward. While acknowledging social settings, the primary explanatory focus within these frameworks tends to reside in individual cognitive processes.

This paper proposes a re-evaluation of this emphasis. The presented evidence supports the argument that humor is primarily a social technology. While cognitive mechanisms such as incongruity detection, pattern violation, and surprise are acknowledged, this paper contends they function in service of social objectives. It is hypothesized that laughter may have conferred a selective advantage primarily by facilitating social bonding, rather than solely by rewarding cognitive processing. In this view, cognitive architecture serves as the mechanism, while social function constitutes the primary evolutionary driver.

Nine lines of evidence are presented, which, while individually not decisive, collectively suggest a reorientation of focus from purely cognitive explanations to those emphasizing social function.

EVIDENCE 01

Provine’s Data

Robert Provine[1]Provine: Laughter as Scientific Problem (2000) →’s observational research, which he termed “sidewalk neuroscience,” provides empirical data that complicates the cognitive-first view.

Observations indicate that individuals laugh approximately 30 times more in social situations compared to when alone. Speakers were observed to laugh 46% more frequently than their audience. Laughter was found to punctuate speech predominantly at clause boundaries rather than at moments perceived as humorous. A significant finding was that most conversational laughter followed non-humorous statements. Based on these observations, Provine concluded that laughter in everyday life functions predominantly as a social signal, rather than primarily as a response to humor, serving communicative roles such as affirming presence, agreement, or group cohesion.

If humor were understood as a purely cognitive phenomenon, independent of social context, one might hypothesize that individuals would exhibit similar laughter rates whether alone or in groups. However, the observed 30:1 ratio of social to solitary laughter does not align with such a hypothesis. Conversely, this ratio is consistent with predictions derived from social theories of laughter.

EVIDENCE 02

Laughter as Social Grooming

Robin Dunbar[3]Dunbar: Social Laughter & Endorphin Release (2012) →’s framework provides the evolutionary logic. In primates, social bonds are maintained through grooming — a one-on-one, time-intensive activity. As human group sizes expanded beyond what grooming could support (Dunbar’s number: ~150), a new bonding mechanism was needed. Laughter solves the scaling problem: it triggers endogenous opioid release in reward-processing brain regions, and it works on multiple individuals simultaneously.

Dunbar’s research demonstrates that social laughter is associated with endorphin release, whereas solo laughter is not. Fifteen minutes of group laughter increases pain tolerance by approximately 10% — a direct measure of endorphin release. The same laughter alone does not produce the same effect. Evidence suggests the neurochemical response is significantly modulated by, and perhaps dependent upon, social context.

EVIDENCE 03

Contagious Laughter

Provine[2]Provine: Contagious Laughter (1992) → (1992) demonstrated experimentally that hearing laughter alone — without any humorous content — is sufficient to trigger laughter. Scott and colleagues[4]Scott et al.: Social Life of Laughter (2014) → (2014) showed this is mediated by the mirror neuron system: hearing laughter activates premotor cortex as if the listener were laughing themselves. TV laugh tracks exploit this mechanism.

The involvement of the mirror neuron pathway, which underlies empathy, imitation, and social learning, indicates the social nature of this phenomenon. Contagious laughter does not require cognitive humor processing; it bypasses it entirely. The trigger is not “something is funny” but “someone near me is laughing.” This suggests that contagious laughter primarily functions in group affiliation, independent of cognitive humor processing.

EVIDENCE 04

Mate Selection & Fitness Signaling

A sense of humor is consistently rated as a highly desired trait in a romantic partner across various studies and cultures. Bressler and colleagues[5]Bressler et al.: Humor as Sexually Selected Trait (2006) → (2006) propose that humor functions as a sexually selected fitness indicator, noting a correlation between humor production and intelligence, a trait difficult to feign. Their research suggests women tend to prefer humor producers, indicating a preference for cognitive fitness, while men often prefer humor appreciators, which may signal receptivity and bonding capacity.

The high valuation of humor as a mate-selection criterion suggests it may signal traits more complex than simple pattern recognition. An alternative or complementary perspective holds that humor primarily signals the ability to form and maintain social bonds. In species where cooperative relationships are vital for survival, the capacity for social connection represents a significant fitness indicator. From this viewpoint, humor serves as a display, advertising the underlying trait of social bonding capacity.

EVIDENCE 05

Dark Humor as In-Group Bonding

Research on military dark humor[7]Dark Humor among US Veterans (PMC, 2024) → (2024) identifies a pattern where veterans develop shared gallows humor that is deliberately incomprehensible to outsiders, a phenomenon whose social dimensions may extend beyond the scope of purely cognitive explanations. This humor functions not despite but because of its exclusionary quality. The necessity of shared presence — the “had-to-be-there” phenomenon — encapsulates its essence. The shared engagement with such humor fosters a bond contingent on shared experience, shared norms, and mutual trust.

Healthcare workers also develop occupational dark humor, exhibiting a similar social dynamic of creating professional distance while simultaneously reinforcing group identity within high-stress environments. In this context, the cognitive content of the joke (e.g., incongruity, violation) serves as the mechanism, while its primary social function (e.g., bonding through shared transgression, marking group membership) constitutes the outcome. Willinger et al.'s[8]Willinger et al.: Black Humour, IQ & Aggression (2017) → finding that appreciation for dark humor correlates with lower aggression levels is consistent with an interpretation that its function is often rooted in social play rather than overt hostility, given that play is a fundamentally social activity.

EVIDENCE 06

The Cultural Evidence

Cross-cultural comedy research reveals that the social structure of humor varies as much as the content. Japanese manzai[9]Cross-Cultural Stand-Up: US vs. Japan →, a 1000-year-old tradition, is built around the relationship between two performers — the boke and the tsukkomi. The audience is framed as overhearers of a social interaction, not direct addressees. American stand-up, by contrast, is a solo performer addressing the crowd directly. The humor in manzai lives in the dynamic between people; the humor in stand-up lives in the relationship between performer and audience. Both are irreducibly social — just differently.

Internet memes[10]Shifman: Memes in Digital Culture (MIT Press, 2013) → (Shifman 2013) represent a significant recent form of evidence. Memes are *collectively authored humor* — created with awareness of each other, remixed, transformed, and shared as social currency. Sharing a meme signals group membership. The lifecycle of a meme (fresh → familiar → stale → ironic) reflects *social currency dynamics*. A meme's humor is partly derived from its reception within a social group. The humor and the sociality are inseparable.

EVIDENCE 07

Health Benefits Are Socially Mediated

The 2022 meta-analysis[11]Laughter Interventions Meta-Analysis (2022) → of laughter interventions (45 studies, 2,547 participants) indicated a finding that warrants consideration: non-humorous laughter (such as laughter yoga — voluntary laughing exercises without any jokes) showed higher effect sizes on mental health than humorous laughter. This suggests that the physical act of laughing in a group may have a greater impact than cognitive humor appreciation.

This finding is relevant to the social-first position, though it requires careful interpretation. If humor’s health benefits were primarily cognitive (e.g., insight, reappraisal, incongruity resolution), then humor-based interventions would be expected to outperform non-humorous laughter. This was not observed. One reading of this result is that it demonstrates humor and social laughter are partially dissociable systems — the social act of laughing together may carry the primary physiological load, with humor serving as a reliable trigger for, rather than the source of, that act. The physiological response to laughter does not differentiate between genuine and simulated laughter, and social bonding appears to occur irrespective of the laughter’s origin. Dunbar’s framework provides a theoretical basis for this observation, positing that the health benefits of laughter are mediated by social bonding. The proposed sequence is: Humor → laughter → opioid-mediated bonds → stronger social network → improved health. The pathway remains operational even when humor is absent, provided social laughter is present. This interpretation supports the claim that humor functions as a social technology precisely because the social laughter it produces — not the cognitive resolution it entails — generates the bonding effect.

Key Evidence

Non-humorous laughter in groups has been observed to produce larger health effects than humorous laughter. This finding is consistent with a social-first account in which humor is one reliable means of eliciting shared laughter, but the bonding mechanism resides in the social act itself rather than in cognitive humor appreciation.

EVIDENCE 08

The AI Test Case

If humor's primary mechanisms were limited to cognitive processes such as pattern detection, incongruity resolution, and violation appraisal, then large language models might be expected to demonstrate proficiency in generating it. These models possess extensive pattern-matching capabilities, broad cultural knowledge, and advanced language understanding. Yet on HumorBench-hard[13]HumorBench: Which LLMs Get the Joke? (2025) →, no LLM exceeds 60% accuracy. Researchers note[14]ChatGPT is Fun, but Not Funny (2023) → that LLMs can be engaging to interact with but are not consistently humorous.

The social-first position offers one explanation for this observation. From this perspective, LLMs lack social relationships, an audience for bonding, an in-group for signaling membership, and the capacity for managing social standing. It should be noted that LLMs also lack embodied experience and biological mortality, among other features that may bear on humor production; attributing their limitations specifically to absent social relationships remains a hypothesis rather than a demonstrated causal account. Nevertheless, proponents of the social-first view argue that even if LLMs could perfectly model incongruity and violation, they could not fulfill humor’s proposed primary social function: to create a moment of shared experience between individuals. Consistent with this, AI outputs perceived as humorous often appear to arise from unintentional incongruities, and the humor is frequently located in the social dynamic between the machine’s confident delivery and the human audience’s recognition of absurdity — suggesting that even AI-adjacent humor often depends on a human social context for its reception.

SECTION 09

Counterarguments

People Do Laugh Alone

People laugh at books, at memories, at their own thoughts. This is real but rare — 30 times rarer than social laughter, per Provine. And much solo laughter is parasocial: laughing at a book is laughing with an imagined author; laughing at a sitcom is responding to the social cues of actors and a laugh track. The cognitive capacity for humor may persist in isolation because it was built for social use, just as language persists in solitary thought because it was built for communication.

Puns Seem Purely Cognitive

Wordplay, such as puns, may appear to represent humor primarily driven by cognitive pattern manipulation, seemingly devoid of social content. However, puns are predominantly produced for audiences, and the groan they often elicit can be understood as a social ritual. Furthermore, puns are consistently rated as a less humorous form of communication. This observation aligns with the social model, which posits that their comparatively limited social function contributes to this perception. Consequently, the characteristics of puns, rather than contradicting the social model, may serve to illustrate its principles by demonstrating how a reduced social component correlates with diminished perceived humor.

The Cognitive Mechanisms Are Real

This position does not deny that humor involves incongruity detection, surprise, and cognitive resolution. These mechanisms are well-documented in the neuroscience (Mobbs et al.[15]Mobbs et al.: Humor Modulates Mesolimbic Reward Centers (2003) →, Wattendorf et al.[16]Wattendorf et al.: Dorsal vs Ventral Striatum in Humor (2023) →). The claim is not that cognition is absent but that it is instrumental. The cognitive mechanisms provide the necessary framework by which humor achieves its social function, just as the vocal apparatus is the mechanism by which language achieves communication. Describing the mechanism does not describe the function.

SECTION 10

Implications

For Research

If humor is primarily social, then studying it in isolation — single subjects rating cartoons in fMRI scanners — systematically misses the phenomenon. The field needs more ecological research: humor in conversation, in relationships, in workplaces, in cultures. Provine’s “sidewalk neuroscience” may have produced more insight than decades of controlled experiments precisely because it studied humor where it naturally lives: between people.

For Comedy

Practitioners of comedy have long recognized this dynamic. Audience engagement is not an afterthought; it is the core skill. A standard performance set is not five minutes of cleverness but five minutes of relationship with an audience. A joke that elicits a high response in one context fails in another because the audience is not the backdrop — the audience is the instrument.

For AI

If genuine humor requires genuine social relationships, then progress in computational humor depends not on better pattern-matching but on AI systems that can participate in social contexts — that have something at stake in the interaction. This is a much harder problem than incongruity detection, and it may be why humor benchmarks have plateaued.

For Everyday Life

The social model reframes what it means to “have a good sense of humor.” It is not primarily an intellectual capacity (detecting incongruity) but a social one (creating connection). This suggests that humor may be as much a function of social attunement as it is of intellectual agility. Humor is not a cognitive performance. It is an act of belonging.

The Position

People do not laugh primarily because things are funny. They laugh because they are together. The funniness is a technology for being together more effectively.
