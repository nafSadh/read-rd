# Methodology — Attachment Theory: Useful Rather Than True

## Research Question
What does attachment theory actually tell us, how strong is the evidence, and where does the pop psychology version diverge from the science? This project treats attachment theory as a *contested framework* — not settled science, not pseudoscience — and surveys it from both the proponent and critic perspectives.

## Scope & Dimensions
This survey covers 7 axes:
1. **Historical origins** — Bowlby, Ainsworth, ethological roots, political context
2. **The taxonomy** — Four styles, dimensional vs. categorical debate
3. **Adult extension** — Hazan & Shaver, ECR, stability/change research
4. **Neuroscience** — Polyvagal theory, somatic storage, neuroception
5. **The critique** — Temperament confounds, WEIRD bias, stability problem, pop psychology distortion
6. **Healing pathways** — Earned secure attachment, EMDR, somatic experiencing
7. **Personal application** — When the framework helps, when it hurts

Deliberately excluded: clinical attachment disorders in children (DSM-5 RAD/DSED), attachment-based parenting interventions, attachment in non-romantic adult relationships (workplace, friendships).

## Source Strategy
- Web search for academic reviews, meta-analyses (PMC, PubMed), and critical journalism
- Priority: peer-reviewed longitudinal studies (Fraley, Roisman), foundational texts (Bowlby, Ainsworth), and named critics (Kagan, Harris, Keller, Mesman)
- Pop psychology sources (Attached, Instagram) cited only as objects of critique, not as evidence
- Polyvagal theory sources include both Porges and his critics (Neuhuber & Berthoud)

## Reading & Synthesis Approach
Discourse survey: what are the competing claims, where do sources agree, where do they contradict?
