SECTION 12

Historical Origins

From Bowlby's ethological revolution to Ainsworth's 20-minute laboratory test
I
S1 · THE SSP3

The Strange Situation Procedure

Bowlby (1950s): Children separated from caregivers show protest → despair → detachment. Not Freudian drive theory — evolutionary biology.

Ainsworth (1978): 20-minute lab protocol. Infant separated from caregiver, reunited. Three patterns from reunion behavior: secure (B), avoidant (A), anxious-resistant (C).

Main & Solomon (1986): Added disorganized (D) — contradictory or fearful behavior, often linked to abusive caregiving.

The Political Context

Bowlby's 1951 WHO report was used to argue against working mothers. The science was real; the policy extrapolation was ideological.

Sources: Bretherton 1992, BPS 2020, Vicedo 2022

SECTION 24

Taxonomy & Measurement

Four clean types — or two messy dimensions?
II
S2 · DIMENSIONS5

Categories vs. Dimensions

Fraley's taxometric analysis: attachment is better described as two continuous dimensions — anxiety and avoidance — than as four discrete types.

Three instruments: SSP (infant behavior), AAI (narrative coherence), ECR (self-report). Problem: AAI and ECR correlate weakly — the convergence failure.

The four "types" are convenient labels for regions of continuous space — not natural kinds.

Why It Matters

"What's your attachment style?" assumes a discoverable, stable type. The science says: two dimensions, weak stability, context-dependent expression.

SECTION 36

Adult Attachment & Stability

r ≈ .30 — real but far from deterministic
III
S3 · STABILITY7

"Stable Instability"

Fraley (2002): Stability r≈.30 over 10 years in childhood. In adults, r≈.50. Statistically significant — but r≈.30 explains ~9% of variance.

Fraley et al. (2021), N=4,000+: 50% of life events triggered immediate attachment shifts; 25% produced lasting change. "Stable instability."

The pursue-withdraw cycle (Simpson & Rholes): avoidant partners distance under stress, anxious partners escalate. Real pattern — but activated by context, not running constantly.

The gap between science and folk theory Science: childhood explains ~9% of adult attachment variation
Pop psych: "your childhood determines your love life"

Diathesis-stress model: attachment = latent vulnerability, not fixed trait

SECTION 4–58

Neuroscience & The Critique

Polyvagal theory's promise and its scientific problems. The field's own reckoning.
IV
S4 · POLYVAGAL9

Porges's Three-Tier Hierarchy

Polyvagal theory: ventral vagal (social engagement / safety) → sympathetic (fight-flight / danger) → dorsal vagal (shutdown / life threat).

"Neuroception": the nervous system's unconscious safety evaluation. Secure attachment = consistent safety detection in caregiver's face and voice.

The critique: Neuhuber & Berthoud (2022) — phylogenetic hierarchy is inaccurate (lungfish have myelinated vagal fibers). Grossman — vagal tone as social engagement index is unreliable.

The Verdict

Clinically useful metaphor. Scientifically contested neurobiology. The hierarchy works in therapy rooms — but the evolutionary claims don't hold in labs.

S5 · CRITIQUE10

The Field's Own Reckoning

Thompson et al. (2022): Nine fundamental questions posed to the field. Roisman & Groh called it an "era of exhaustion." Mesman identified confirmation bias. Keller called core concepts "fuzzy."

Kagan: The SSP measures temperamental reactivity, not relationship quality. A "secure" infant may simply be low-reactive.

Cultural WEIRD bias: 52% avoidant in Northern Germany, 0% in Japan. "Secure" = Western independence norms, not a biological universal.

The horoscope comparison (UnHerd 2023): Attachment styles feel true because they're vague enough to fit anyone, categorical enough to feel definitive, explanatory enough to satisfy the need for narrative.

The Pop Pipeline

Observation → taxonomy → extension → simplification → identity labels → TikTok diagnostics. At each step, nuance lost, certainty gained.

SECTION 611

Earned Security

The most useful part of attachment theory is also the least categorical.
VI
S6 · HEALING12

Three Pathways to Earned Security

Narrative pathway (Main): Adults who narrate difficult childhoods coherently — without minimizing or being overwhelmed — score secure on the AAI. Security is earned through integration.

Somatic pathway: EMDR (18 RCTs, small-to-moderate effects for PTSD). Somatic Experiencing (Brom 2017: Cohen's d = 0.94–1.26).

Relational pathway: EFT improves 70% of couples. Corrective emotional experiences across all contexts — not just romantic.

The Hope

If attachment theory's greatest contribution is earned security — people can change through coherent self-understanding — then the most useful version is the least categorical.

Synthesis

Six tensions. One honest framing.
∑
SYNTHESIS · TENSIONS14

Six Unresolved Tensions

01 — Attachment vs. temperament: Does the SSP measure relationship quality or inborn reactivity?

02 — Style stability: Weak prototype (r≈.30) vs. fully context-dependent.

03 — Categories vs. dimensions: Four pop types vs. two continuous dimensions.

04 — Cultural universality: 52% avoidant in Germany, 0% in Japan. Universal biology or Western norms?

05 — Polyvagal validity: Clinically useful hierarchy vs. phylogenetically inaccurate.

06 — Mother as determinant: Maternal sensitivity vs. peers, genes, culture, class.

The tensions are the most valuable findings — where the field honestly disagrees with itself.

CONCLUSION15

Useful Rather Than True

Attachment theory identifies real patterns: relational anxiety, avoidance, the pursue-withdraw cycle. It provides a language. It motivates effective therapies.

But the framework — four fixed types, infant determinism, cultural universality, maternal primacy — does not withstand the evidence.

The Most Honest Version

The most useful version of attachment theory is the least categorical one — earned security, dimensional thinking, context-dependence, and the humility to say "this is a lens, not a law."
