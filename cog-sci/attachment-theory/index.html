Attachment Theory: Useful Rather Than True

A critical survey of what attachment theory actually tells us, how strong the evidence is, and where the pop psychology version diverges from the science.

Attachment theory occupies a peculiar position in contemporary culture: more trusted than MBTI, less rigorous than the Big Five, and amplified by a social media ecosystem that strips away every caveat the science requires. This project surveys attachment theory across 7 dimensions and 40+ sources — from Bowlby’s ethological origins through Ainsworth’s Strange Situation, the adult extension, polyvagal neuroscience, the critique literature, and healing pathways.

The central finding: attachment theory identifies real patterns (relational anxiety, avoidance, the pursue-withdraw cycle) but packages them in a framework (four fixed types, infant determinism, cultural universality) that does not withstand scrutiny. The pop psychology version strips away every caveat the science requires. The most honest framing: useful rather than true.

Key Tensions

  • 01 Attachment vs. temperament: Does the SSP measure relationship quality or inborn reactivity?
  • 02 Style stability: Prototype model (stable factor with fluctuations) vs. fully context-dependent (no enduring prototype)
  • 03 Categories vs. dimensions: Pop psychology uses 4 types; taxometric research finds continuous dimensions
  • 04 Cultural universality: 52% avoidant in Germany, 0% in Japan — is “secure” universal or Western?
  • 05 Polyvagal validity: Clinically useful hierarchy vs. phylogenetically inaccurate claims
  • 06 Mother as determinant: Classic AT says maternal sensitivity drives attachment; Harris, Kagan say peers, genes, culture matter more
