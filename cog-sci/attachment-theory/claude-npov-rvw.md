# NPOV Review — Attachment Theory

## Files Reviewed
- `.project/methodology.md`
- `notes/00-synthesis.md`
- `literature-collection.md` (40 sources)
- `survey-paper.html` (full text, 8 sections + references)
- `survey-slides.html` (structure only)

## Issues Found

### POV (Neutral Point of View)

1. **Title framing is a conclusion, not a question.** "Useful Rather Than True" is the paper's thesis stated as its title. The methodology describes treating attachment theory as a "contested framework," which is appropriately neutral, but the title resolves the contest before the reader enters. The paper acknowledges this in the limitations ("The 'useful rather than true' framing is itself a position"), which is good self-awareness, but the framing still colors every section.

2. **Critique section given deliberate over-representation.** The methodology states this is intentional ("to counterbalance the field's confirmation bias"), which is a defensible editorial choice. However, it means the paper is not neutral -- it is corrective. This is acknowledged but should be kept in mind: the paper reads as a critique of attachment theory more than a balanced survey.

3. **Pop psychology is treated as a straw target.** The paper frequently sets up the "TikTok version" and then dismantles it. This is valid as cultural criticism, but it means the paper's strongest arguments are against a version of the theory that no serious researcher holds. The scientific version of attachment theory (dimensional, probabilistic, context-dependent) is harder to critique and gets less direct engagement.

### RIGOR

1. **Sources are adequate but secondary-heavy.** Ainsworth (1978) and Hazan & Shaver (1987) are accessed via secondary sources. The paper acknowledges this in the limitations. For a survey paper this is acceptable, but the literature collection flags the gaps.

2. **r-squared interpretation is correct and well-presented.** The callout "r~.30 explains roughly 9% of the variance" is accurate and is one of the paper's strongest analytical moments.

3. **Cross-cultural data is presented accurately.** The Northern German (52% avoidant) and Japanese (0% avoidant) figures match the standard citations.

4. **Polyvagal section is balanced.** Both Porges's claims and the neuroscience critiques are presented. The paper could note more explicitly that PVT is peripheral to attachment theory proper -- Porges's framework is an add-on, not a core component.

### BIAS

1. **Anti-categorical bias throughout.** The paper consistently treats categorical/typological thinking as inferior to dimensional thinking. This is the current scientific consensus, so it is not factually wrong, but it is presented as obvious rather than argued. Readers sympathetic to categorical models (or who find them clinically useful) may feel the paper dismisses rather than engages.

2. **Healing section is uncritical of EMDR/SE.** The paper notes EMDR mechanism debate but presents Brom et al.'s SE effect sizes (d=0.94-1.26) without noting this is a single RCT with a relatively small sample. The healing section reads as more credulous than the critique section -- an asymmetry.

### TONE

1. **Rhetorical voice is confident and opinionated.** Phrases like "devastating" (for cross-cultural studies), "should give pause," and "the most pointed comparison" carry editorial judgment. This is effective writing but not neutral writing.

2. **The "horoscope comparison" is presented approvingly.** UnHerd's horoscope analogy is quoted and developed rather than merely reported. A more neutral treatment would present the comparison and then the rebuttals (Vrticka's) with equal weight.

## Fixes Applied

None. This is a review-only pass. The paper's editorial stance is clearly intentional and acknowledged in its limitations section. The issues above are characteristics of the paper's approach, not errors.

## Remaining Concerns

- The paper would benefit from a stronger steelman of modern attachment research (dimensional, probabilistic, context-sensitive) before critiquing the popular version.
- The healing section's evidence assessment is less rigorous than the critique section's, creating an internal asymmetry.
- Some readers may read the title as dismissive of a framework that, by the paper's own account, "identifies real patterns" and "motivates effective therapies."

### Issues Found and Fixed

#### 1. Second-Person POV in Scholarly Sections

The most pervasive issue was second-person address ("you/your") in sections that should maintain scholarly register. Several sections used direct address to the reader in what are analytical, survey-style pages.

- **s4-neuro.dd.html** (4 fixes): Changed "Your body decides" to "The body decides" in the PVT summary. Changed "You cannot will yourself into genuine social engagement" to third person. Changed "Your body has already decided" to "The body has already decided" in neuroception section. Changed "You can know intellectually that your partner is trustworthy" to third person in neuroception callout.

- **s2-taxonomy.dd.html** (4 fixes): Changed "What This Means for You" heading to "What This Means in Practice" and rewrote paragraph in third person. Changed "You are a point in a continuous two-dimensional space" to "each person occupies a point." Rewrote "Practical Guidance" paragraph from imperative/second person to third person. Fixed callout "your position in the space" to "any individual's position."

- **s5-critique.dd.html** (7 fixes): Changed "What This Means for You" heading to "Practical Implications" and rewrote in third person. Changed "When you identify as an introvert" to "When a person identifies." Changed "When you diagnose your ex" to "When someone diagnoses an ex." Changed "you are one of four types" / "you have one attachment style" to third person. Changed "your childhood set your style" / "your mother" to "childhood set the style" / "the mother." Changed "the attachment style you discover" to "the attachment style discovered." Changed "your partner is avoidant, your mother made you anxious" to third person.

- **s3-adult.dd.html** (3 fixes): Changed "If you take only one thing" to "If one takes only one thing." Changed "your attachment style was set in infancy" to "attachment style was set in infancy." Changed "Where do you tend to fall" to "Where does a person tend to fall."

- **overview.dd.html** (2 fixes): Changed "whether you interpret it" to "whether the person interprets it" in the earned security summary. Changed "where your attachment needs are met ... when you pursue ... when you shut down" to passive/third person.

- **s6-healing.dd.html** (3 fixes): Changed "when you pursue ... when you shut down" to passive voice in relational pathway summary. Changed "regardless of where you started" to "regardless of starting point." Changed "when you expect abandonment ... when you expect rejection ... your attachment expectations" to passive/third person.

**Not fixed (intentionally second-person):** s7-personal.dd.html (the entire "Personal Application" section) and overview.dd.html Section 07 ("The Personal Lens") -- these sections are explicitly addressed to the reader and second person is their deliberate register. Similarly, s6-healing's detailed therapeutic guidance sections (narrative prescription, body awareness, daily practice) retain second person as appropriate for applied/prescriptive content.

#### 2. Scholastic Rigor: Imprecise Statistical Language

- **s5-critique.dd.html** (1 fix): Changed "knowing a child's attachment classification at age one allows prediction of adult attachment classification with about 9% accuracy above chance" to "explains about 9% of the variance in adult attachment." The original phrasing conflated explained variance with predictive accuracy, which are different statistical concepts. The correct formulation (r-squared = .09 = 9% of variance explained) was already used correctly elsewhere in the same project.

### Issues Noted but Not Fixed

1. **Title framing ("Useful Rather Than True")**: Remains a conclusion stated as thesis. Pass 1 noted this; it is an intentional editorial choice acknowledged in the limitations section.

2. **Editorial tone words**: "devastating" (s5-critique, describing cross-cultural evidence), "simple but devastating" (s5-critique, describing Kagan's argument) remain. These are editorial flourishes in what is an opinionated survey, and the paper's voice is consistently confident throughout. Changing isolated instances would create tonal inconsistency.

3. **Healing section evidence asymmetry**: The healing section (s6) still presents SE effect sizes (d=0.94-1.26) prominently from a single RCT. The summary and detail sections do note "evidence base still small" and "few RCTs," so the caveat is present, but the presentation is less skeptical than the critique section's treatment of comparable evidence.

4. **"We/our" in scholarly sections**: s2-taxonomy uses "What We Know" / "What We Do Not Know" headings with first-person plural prose. This is standard academic register (the scholarly "we") and does not constitute POV injection.

5. **survey-paper.html**: Two instances of "your" remain but both are in quoted pop-psychology phrases ("your attachment style," "Your childhood determines your love life") used as referenced claims, not direct reader address. These are appropriate.

### Summary

Pass 2 found and fixed **24 instances** of second-person POV in scholarly sections across 6 files, plus 1 imprecise statistical claim. The content is otherwise logically consistent, well-cited for a survey paper, and maintains appropriate hedging on contested claims. The major structural characteristics noted in Pass 1 (thesis-as-title, corrective framing, anti-categorical bias, pop-psychology straw target) remain as intentional editorial choices.
