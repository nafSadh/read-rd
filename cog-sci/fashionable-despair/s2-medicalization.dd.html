01

Before the Checklist

Before DSM-III (1980), depression lived in a psychoanalytic landscape. Conditions were understood through the analyst-patient relationship, not symptom checklists. Terms like “neurosis” and “reactive depression” carried relational meaning — they described a person’s situation, not a disease entity.

The 19th century had its own pharmaceutical shortcuts (patent medicines, tonics promising relief from “tired nerves”), and new conditions for the worried well — neurasthenia, the neuroses. But the conceptual framework remained person-centered rather than symptom-centered.

02

The Symptom Revolution

DSM-III (1980) was the pivot. Hundreds of new or reconceptualized disorders appeared. The manual shifted from etiological (why you’re suffering) to descriptive (what symptoms you show). Depression became a checklist: 5 of 9 symptoms for 2 weeks. No requirement to assess context or cause.

Horwitz & Wakefield’s foundational critique: DSM abandoned “validity for the sake of reliability.” It can reliably identify who has the symptoms but cannot validly distinguish between genuine disorder and normal sadness after loss.

04

Depression as Narrative

Regina Smardon (2008): “narrative is the primary vehicle through which antidepressant consumers negotiate their sense of identity and reality.” The DSM itself “tends to commodify illness.” The patient becomes a consumer; the treatment becomes a product; the diagnosis becomes a story you tell about yourself.

The 1990s memoir wave — Styron, Kaysen, Wurtzel, Jamison — established depression as a literary genre. These are genuine accounts of genuine suffering, but they also made the condition claimable as narrative identity.

05

Diagnosis as Rhetoric

A crucial argument from the rhetoric of health literature: “mental-illness diagnosis, as a rhetorical act and a speech act, shifts a problem from social conditions of precarity and inequity to personal conditions of pathology.”

When you diagnose an individual with depression, you locate the problem inside them. The social conditions that may have caused the suffering — poverty, discrimination, isolation — become invisible. This is the privatization of suffering.

06

The Loss of Sadness

Horwitz & Wakefield (2007): “while depressive disorder certainly exists and can be a devastating condition, the apparent epidemic in fact reflects the way the psychiatric profession has reclassified normal human sadness as largely an abnormal experience.”

The “false positive” problem: overestimates of prevalence inflate the apparent epidemic, drive overprescription, and create a cultural context where claiming depression becomes increasingly easy and socially available.

The Fatal Flaw

DSM criteria diagnose symptoms without context. The same 5 symptoms after a death, a divorce, a job loss, and for no reason all yield the same diagnosis.

The One Exception

Bereavement was the DSM’s only contextual exclusion. DSM-5 (2013) removed even that, turning normal grief into potential MDD.

The False Positives

Over-inclusive criteria inflate prevalence, which normalizes the diagnosis, which reduces stigma, which increases claiming — a feedback loop.

The Real Cost

When everyone is depressed, the genuinely ill disappear into the crowd. Resources spread thin. The concept loses clinical utility.

07

The Pill-Selling Machine

Allen Frances (DSM-IV chair): “Drug companies benefited greatly from the DSM-III approach. The best way to sell pills is to promote disease-mongering.” Pharma marketing targeted psychiatrists, then primary care, then the public directly via DTC advertising (legal since 1997 in the US).

The result: “a pill-popping society” where medication use has “skyrocketed” driven by “billion-dollar marketing budgets” — great for shareholders, often inappropriate for patients.

08

Cure vs. Capture

The medicalization arc contains a paradox that mirrors the project’s central tension. Broader diagnostic criteria mean more people get help. But they also mean more people get unnecessary help, or worse, get help that defines their identity around a condition they may not have.

Cure

Millions of genuinely depressed people found relief through SSRIs and the destigmatization that came with diagnosis.

Capture

Millions of normally sad people were captured by diagnostic criteria that couldn’t distinguish their sadness from disorder.

Identity

Diagnosis became narrative, then identity. “I am depressed” shifted from description to definition.

Industry

Every expansion of diagnostic criteria created a larger market for pharmaceuticals, therapy platforms, and wellness products.

Section 01 — Details

Before the Checklist

The story of how depression became fashionable in its modern form begins not with Tumblr or even Prozac but with a conceptual revolution in psychiatry. Before DSM-III, the dominant framework for understanding mental suffering was psychoanalytic. Depression was not a discrete disease entity to be diagnosed via symptom checklist — it was a manifestation of unconscious conflict, unresolved loss, or developmental disruption. The clinician’s job was not to match symptoms to criteria but to understand the person.

This framework had serious problems. It was unreliable (two clinicians could see the same patient and reach wildly different diagnoses), unfalsifiable (psychoanalytic interpretations resist empirical testing), and often interminable (treatment could last decades without measurable progress). But it had one virtue that DSM-III would sacrifice: it insisted on context. “Reactive depression” — sadness caused by identifiable life events — was a legitimate category, distinct from “endogenous depression” that arose without obvious cause. The framework could, at least in principle, distinguish between being sad for a reason and being depressed without one.

The Pre-Pharmaceutical Moment

The Psychology Today account traces the therapeutic revolution to the 1950s, when a host of existing compounds were discovered to have beneficial psychotropic properties. First antipsychotics, then anxiolytics, sedatives, stimulants (Ritalin), and antidepressants entered the market. Miltown (1955) for anxiety, Librium (1960), and Valium (1963) became the first psychiatric blockbusters. But looking further back, the 19th century already had “various elixirs and tonics (patent medicines) promising relief for maladies like anxiety and fatigue and offering a new lease on life.” And new conditions were being defined for the worried well: neurasthenia (“tired nerves”), the neuroses.

The pattern is old: every era produces both genuine treatments for genuine suffering and expansions of the treatable population to include people who are not sick but uncomfortable. The medicalization of discomfort is not a DSM invention. It is a recurring feature of therapeutic cultures, and its current iteration began well before 1980.

The Recurring Pattern

Every era produces both genuine treatments for genuine suffering and expansions of the treatable population to include people who are not sick but uncomfortable. The medicalization of discomfort predates the DSM by centuries.

Section 02 — Details

The Symptom Revolution

The 1980 publication of DSM-III was a deliberate break with psychoanalytic tradition. Its architects — principally Robert Spitzer — wanted psychiatry to be scientific, which meant reliable: two clinicians should reach the same diagnosis for the same patient. The solution was symptom-based criteria that could be applied mechanically, without requiring the clinician to infer unconscious dynamics or assess context.

For depression, the criteria crystallized into what clinicians still use (with modifications) today: five or more of nine symptoms, present for at least two weeks, causing clinically significant distress or impairment. The symptoms include depressed mood, diminished interest, weight change, sleep disturbance, psychomotor change, fatigue, worthlessness/guilt, concentration difficulty, and suicidal ideation. Check the boxes, count to five, diagnose.

The Validity Problem

Horwitz and Wakefield’s central argument is that this approach sacrificed validity for reliability. The criteria can reliably identify who has the symptoms — clinicians will agree on the count — but cannot validly distinguish between a person with a genuine depressive disorder (a dysfunction in the brain’s mood regulation) and a person experiencing intense but normal sadness in response to devastating life events. A woman who loses her husband, her job, and her home in the same month and then experiences five of nine symptoms for two weeks meets the criteria for Major Depressive Disorder. By the DSM’s own general definition of mental disorder — which specifies that symptoms should not be “an expectable response to a stressor” — she should be excluded. But the individual diagnostic criteria do not incorporate this exclusion.

The one exception was bereavement. DSM-III and DSM-IV included a “bereavement exclusion” that prevented diagnosing MDD in someone whose symptoms began within two months of a loved one’s death. This was the manual’s sole acknowledgment that context matters. DSM-5 (2013) removed even this exemption, at Allen Frances’s vocal objection, “transforming normal grief into major depressive disorder.”

The Fatal Trade

DSM-III traded validity for reliability. The diagnosis became consistent across clinicians but lost the ability to distinguish disorder from normal sadness. When the bereavement exclusion fell in DSM-5, the last contextual safeguard disappeared.

Section 03 — Details

The Prozac Moment

If DSM-III provided the diagnostic infrastructure, Prozac provided the pill. Launched in 1987, fluoxetine (brand name Prozac) was not the first antidepressant — tricyclics and MAOIs had been available for decades — but it was the first antidepressant that felt modern. Cleaner side-effect profile, simpler dosing, and a name that sounded like progress rather than pathology. Within years, it became the most widely prescribed antidepressant in history.

Peter Kramer’s Listening to Prozac (1993) introduced a concept that would prove fateful: “cosmetic pharmacology.” The rhetoric of health analysis notes that Kramer’s book was “laden with the value of the hyperthymic (optimistic, charismatic, confident) personality” and was “praised for illuminating questions of mood and identity, and blamed for ushering in an era of ‘cosmetic pharmacology’ — and for making Prozac an object at the center of promiscuous prescription.” The idea that medication could make you not just well but better than well implied that the natural human baseline was somehow deficient — that normal was a condition to be improved upon, not accepted.

The Memoir Wave

Elizabeth Wurtzel’s Prozac Nation: Young and Depressed in America (1994) did something Kramer’s medical text could not: it turned depression into first-person literary content. The Washington Post, revisiting the book 30 years later, notes that Wurtzel “changed the way we think about depression and mental health, for better and worse.” She joined a wave: William Styron’s Darkness Visible (1990), Susanna Kaysen’s Girl, Interrupted (1993), Kay Redfield Jamison’s An Unquiet Mind (1995).

These are genuine accounts of genuine suffering — Wurtzel’s honesty about her lowest moments was, for many readers, literally lifesaving. But the literary phenomenon they constituted also established something new: depression as a publishing category, depression as a confessional mode, depression as a way to make your pain legible and commercially viable. The line from Wurtzel to the sad girl TikTok is not direct, but it is traceable: both involve making private suffering public, both require the suffering to be interesting as well as authentic, and both operate within a market that rewards compelling performances of pain.

Cosmetic Pharmacology

Kramer’s concept implied that normal mood is a deficiency to be corrected. If medication can make you “better than well,” then being merely well is already a form of illness. This logic makes everyone a potential patient.

Section 04 — Details

Depression as Narrative

Regina Smardon’s 2008 study in Health introduces a framework essential to understanding how medicalization enables fashionable depression: “narrative is the primary vehicle through which antidepressant consumers negotiate their sense of identity and reality.” The diagnosis is not just a clinical label — it is a story template. “I have depression” is a narrative with a beginning (onset), a middle (symptoms), and an implied ending (treatment). It organizes experience into a legible shape.

Smardon notes that the DSM itself “tends to commodify illness” — the diagnostic manual, by reducing complex suffering to checklists, converts lived experience into standardized products. The patient becomes a consumer of treatments; the treatment becomes a product to be marketed; the diagnosis becomes a brand to be adopted. This is not conspiracy — it is the natural consequence of applying market logic to subjective experience.

The Identity Shift

The crucial shift is from “I feel depressed” (a description of current state) to “I have depression” (a claim about what you are) to “I am depressed” (an identity). Each step narrows the gap between condition and self. The Prozac-era memoirs were early instances of this identity fusion: Wurtzel did not merely describe her depression; she built her public persona around it. The book’s very title — Prozac Nation — claims not just a personal condition but a national one.

This is the bridge to everything that follows. When depression becomes narrative identity, it creates the preconditions for S3’s sad girl aesthetic (sadness as content format), S4’s celebrity disclosure economy (vulnerability as brand strategy), and S5’s therapy-speak culture (diagnosis as social language). The medicalization arc did not directly cause any of these phenomena, but it created the conceptual infrastructure that made them possible.

The Three Steps

“I feel depressed” (state) → “I have depression” (condition) → “I am depressed” (identity). Each step narrows the gap between diagnosis and self until they are indistinguishable.

Section 05 — Details

Diagnosis as Rhetoric

The UF Press analysis of Kramer’s Listening to Prozac makes an argument that sits at the center of this project: “mental-illness diagnosis, as a rhetorical act and a speech act, shifts a problem from social conditions of precarity and inequity, for example, to personal conditions of pathology.” This is not a critique of psychiatry specifically — it is a critique of any framework that locates social problems inside individuals.

Consider the implications. A woman working two jobs, unable to afford childcare, sleeping four hours a night, who develops five of nine depressive symptoms, receives a diagnosis of Major Depressive Disorder. The diagnosis locates the problem in her brain chemistry. It offers a pill. What it does not do is address the conditions that produced the symptoms: wage stagnation, absent social safety net, structural inequality. The diagnosis privatizes her suffering — it becomes her problem, her disorder, her treatment to pursue. The social conditions disappear behind the clinical label.

The Link to S5 and S6

This privatization of suffering is exactly what Bandinelli and Pinzón Arteaga will critique in S5 as the “therapeutic turn” — the broader cultural trend of using therapeutic frameworks to “individualise suffering and neutralise symbolic contradictions.” And it is exactly the mechanism that produces the disparities documented in S6: if depression is individual, then the racial and economic structures that produce disproportionate suffering in minority communities become invisible. The diagnosis treats the symptom and hides the cause.

The Rhetorical Move

Diagnosis relocates suffering from the social to the personal. Poverty becomes depression. Discrimination becomes anxiety. Exploitation becomes burnout. The structural cause disappears behind the clinical label.

Section 06 — Details

The Loss of Sadness

Allan Horwitz and Jerome Wakefield’s The Loss of Sadness (Oxford, 2007) is the most comprehensive argument for why the depression epidemic is partly an artifact of diagnostic inflation. Their thesis: “while depressive disorder certainly exists and can be a devastating condition warranting medical attention, the apparent epidemic in fact reflects the way the psychiatric profession has understood and reclassified normal human sadness as largely an abnormal experience.”

The book traces how the DSM’s symptom-based criteria produce “false positives” — people who meet the diagnostic criteria but whose symptoms are normal responses to identifiable life circumstances. The Psychiatric Times review calls it a work that future historians will rely on to understand “why psychiatric diagnosis abandoned validity for the sake of reliability in the years surrounding the millennium.”

The Medicalization Feedback Loop

The false positive problem creates a self-reinforcing cycle relevant to this project. Over-inclusive diagnostic criteria capture more people. More captured people inflate prevalence estimates. Higher prevalence normalizes the diagnosis. Reduced stigma makes the diagnosis easier to claim. More people claiming the diagnosis confirms the prevalence. Each step is individually reasonable; the cumulative effect is an apparent epidemic that is partly real and partly constructed.

This connects directly to S1’s “vocabulary hypothesis”: when “melancholy” (rich, contextual, philosophical) was replaced by “depression” (clinical, flat, context-free), the experiential vocabulary was lost. But the DSM’s symptom-based definition of depression is even flatter than the word itself suggests — it is a checklist, not a concept. And a checklist is inherently context-free, which is why it cannot distinguish grief from disorder.

The Solution That Was Rejected

Horwitz and Wakefield proposed a straightforward fix: expand the bereavement exclusion to cover other major life stressors. If the DSM already acknowledged that grief after death should not be diagnosed as MDD, why not extend the same logic to grief after divorce, job loss, or serious illness? The proposal was rejected for DSM-5 — and the bereavement exclusion itself was removed. Frances: DSM-5 would “considerably increase medicalization and may turn our current diagnostic inflation into hyperinflation.”

Section 07 — Details

The Pill-Selling Machine

Allen Frances, who chaired the DSM-IV task force, is the most authoritative insider critic of what followed DSM-III. His account: “Drug companies benefited greatly from the DSM-III approach, particularly since 1987 when Prozac established the template for promoting blockbuster psychiatric drugs. Pharma realized that the best way to sell pills is to promote disease-mongering.”

The marketing strategy was systematic: target psychiatrists first (through funded research, conferences, continuing education), then primary care physicians (who write the majority of antidepressant prescriptions), and, since 1997, the general public directly through television and print advertising. The misleading core message: “mental disorders are underdiagnosed, easy to diagnose due to chemical imbalances in the brain and best treated with a pill.”

The Numbers

The results are measurable. Something like one in six adult women takes an antidepressant. On some college campuses, up to 25% of students without a prescription report taking stimulants. Even preschoolers, as young as 2 or 3, are being treated with stimulants, antipsychotics, and other medications for disruptive behaviors. Frances: “Use of medication has skyrocketed as a result of these billion-dollar marketing budgets, turning us into a pill-popping society. This increase in drug use is great for Pharma shareholders and executives, but often inappropriate for clients and terribly costly to the economy.”

The Fashionability Mechanism

The pharmaceutical industry’s contribution to fashionable depression is indirect but powerful. By normalizing antidepressant use through massive advertising, pharma normalized the diagnosis that antidepressants treat. If Prozac is a household name, depression must be a household condition. If depression is a household condition, having it is no longer stigmatized. If it’s no longer stigmatized, it can become identity. The industry did not set out to make depression fashionable — it set out to sell pills — but expanding the addressable market had the side effect of making the diagnosis culturally available in ways that enabled everything from the memoir wave to the sad girl aesthetic.

The Side Effect

Pharma didn’t make depression fashionable on purpose. It made depression normal on purpose — to sell pills. But normalization is the precondition for fashionability, and the cultural consequences followed.

Section 08 — Details

Cure vs. Capture

The medicalization arc contains the project’s central paradox in its purest form. Every expansion of diagnostic criteria captures more people. Some of those people genuinely needed help and would not have received it under narrower criteria. Others were experiencing normal human sadness and were misidentified as disordered. You cannot expand access without expanding capture, and you cannot restrict capture without restricting access. This is not a problem to be solved but a tension to be managed.

The Cure Case

Millions of people with genuine depressive disorders found relief through SSRIs and the therapeutic infrastructure that medicalization built. The destigmatization of depression — which depended on its normalization — saved lives. People who would have suffered in silence sought treatment because the culture told them it was acceptable to do so. The NAMI testimonial about Prozac Nation captures this: a teenager found in Wurtzel’s memoir “a glimpse of the light at the end of the tunnel” at a moment when she had “trouble understanding that my anguish was not permanent.”

The Capture Case

The false positive problem documented by Horwitz and Wakefield means that many people diagnosed with MDD were experiencing normal sadness. Their diagnosis: (a) gave them a medical identity they did not need, (b) exposed them to pharmaceutical side effects, (c) privatized suffering that had social causes, and (d) diluted the diagnosis for people with genuine disorders. Frances’s worry about DSM-5 captures the scale: “overdiagnosis transforms normal grief into major depressive disorder, normal temper tantrums into disruptive mood dysregulation disorder, normal forgetfulness of old age into minor neurocognitive disorder.”

The Connection to Fashionable Depression

Fashionable depression is, in one sense, the cultural expression of diagnostic inflation. When the diagnostic criteria are broad enough to capture normal sadness, the diagnosis becomes available as identity to a much larger population — including people whose adoption of the label is performative or aspirational rather than clinical. The medicalization arc did not create the desire to be interestingly sad (that, as S1 shows, is 2,400 years old), but it provided the institutional and linguistic infrastructure that made the contemporary version possible.

The Infrastructure

Medicalization provided the diagnostic language, the pharmaceutical normalization, and the memoir tradition that made fashionable depression possible. Aristotle provided the desire. The DSM provided the means.
