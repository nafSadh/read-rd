Fashionable Despair

The Aestheticization of Depression from Ficino to TikTok

nafsadh · March 2026 · read.sadh.app/cog-sci/fashionable-despair

Abstract. This paper surveys the aestheticization of depression across seven dimensions: its historical roots in Renaissance melancholy, the medicalization arc from DSM-III through Prozac Nation, the social media sad girl aesthetic, vulnerability as celebrity currency, therapy-speak and the commodification of mental health language, racial and gender inequities in whose sadness becomes culturally visible, and the emerging backlash against therapeutic culture. Drawing on 43 sources across academic research, journalism, and cultural criticism, the survey identifies seven structural features of fashionable despair: a recurring template (Ficino’s cycle of reframing, fashion, market, and backlash), a diagnostic ratchet (categories that only expand), a recovery paradox (identity-suffering fusion that makes getting better threatening), an attention inequality (visibility inversely correlated with treatment need), a privatization engine (structural suffering translated into individual pathology), an undecidable (destigmatization and romanticization as structurally identical from outside), and an emerging post-fashionable framework. The central finding is that the mechanisms driving destigmatization and romanticization are the same mechanisms, operating on the same platforms, and may not be separable.
INTRODUCTION

The Shape of the Problem

In 1489, Marsilio Ficino performed one of intellectual history’s most consequential reframings: melancholy, long classified as a disease of black bile, became the mark of Saturn’s children — philosophers, poets, contemplatives.[1]Wikipedia: Melancholia → Sadness was no longer something that happened to you. It was something you were. Five centuries later, a TikTok hashtag carries 17.6 million posts under “depressed girl aesthetic.”[12]The Batt, "Depression Isn't Aesthetic" (2024) → The content has changed; the mechanism has not.

This paper surveys the discourse of fashionable despair — the cultural phenomenon in which depression is aestheticized, branded, commodified, and contested. It is not a clinical survey of depression itself, nor a technical review of treatment efficacy. It is a survey of what people are saying about how depression became fashionable, who benefits from that fashion, and what it costs.

The survey covers seven dimensions: the historical genealogy of melancholy as cultural capital (Section 1), the medicalization arc that transformed sadness into a diagnostic category (Section 2), the social media aesthetics that turned that category into visual grammar (Section 3), the economy of celebrity vulnerability (Section 4), the migration of therapy language into everyday speech (Section 5), the racial and gender inequities in whose suffering becomes visible (Section 6), and the emerging backlash against the entire discourse (Section 7). A synthesis section identifies seven structural features that operate across all dimensions.

SECTION 1

Historical Melancholy

The idea that sadness confers depth has a precise origin. Zimmermann traces it as a culture-bound syndrome specific to European intellectual history: Hippocrates’ humoral theory classified melancholy as disease, but Aristotle asked why “all those who have become eminent” seemed touched by it.[2]Zimmermann, "History of Melancholy" (1995) → Ficino formalized the link in De Vita Libri Tres, connecting Saturn to intellectual greatness and transforming melancholy from vice to virtue.[1]Wikipedia: Melancholia →

The template ran through the English Elizabethans (where “fashionable melancholy” became a literary cult), through Burton’s encyclopedic Anatomy of Melancholy (1621), and reached full expression in Romanticism. Frink argues that the English Romantics used melancholy not merely as aesthetic posture but as political protest against Enlightenment rationalism — to suffer was to reject the prevailing order.[3]Frink, "Romantic Melancholy" (2016) → Pfau identifies the philosophical depth: melancholy as “excess of knowledge exceeding representation,” a cognitive surplus that could not be contained by rational forms.[4]Pfau on Byron, "Romantic Melancholy" (2013) →

Two features of the historical template persist into the present. First, the social hierarchy of suffering: melancholy was always a claim of the educated classes, and Zimmermann documents that it functioned as a European-specific cultural construct, not a human universal.[2]Zimmermann, "History of Melancholy" (1995) → Second, the gendered dimension: Frink shows that women’s melancholy was interpreted differently from men’s, with female suffering read as pathology where male suffering was read as genius.[3]Frink, "Romantic Melancholy" (2016) → Both patterns recur across every subsequent era this survey examines.

SECTION 2

The Medicalization Arc

The transition from melancholy to depression was not merely terminological but structural. Allen Frances, who chaired DSM-IV, provides the clearest insider critique: DSM-III (1980) introduced symptom checklists that severed the link between suffering and context, and DSM-5 (2013) removed the bereavement exclusion — the last clause acknowledging that intense sadness might be proportionate.[5]Frances, "DSM & Medicalization" (2022) → Each revision expanded diagnostic boundaries; none contracted them.

Psychology Today traces the cultural arc: the 1950s therapeutic revolution, DSM-III’s introduction of hundreds of new disorders, and by the 2020s, one in six adult women on antidepressants.[6]Psychology Today, "Prozac Culture" (2024) → Horwitz and Wakefield argue that the depression epidemic substantially reflects reclassification of normal sadness as pathology.[7]Before and After Prozac, Springer (2021) →

The Prozac era produced a literary genre. Elizabeth Wurtzel’s Prozac Nation (1994), followed by Styron’s Darkness Visible, Kaysen’s Girl, Interrupted, and Jamison’s An Unquiet Mind, established the memoir of mental illness as literary form.[9]Washington Post, "Prozac Nation at 30" (2024) → Rhetoric of depression scholarship shows how Kramer’s Listening to Prozac introduced “cosmetic pharmacology” — the idea that medication could optimize personality, shifting problems from social conditions to personal pathology.[10]Rhetoric of Depression, UF Press (2023) → Smardon documents how the DSM itself “commodifies illness” — the checklist format makes self-diagnosis possible and intuitive.[8]Smardon, "Stigma & Commodification" (2008) →

SECTION 3

Social Media Aesthetics

The sad girl aesthetic migrated from Tumblr’s stream-of-consciousness culture to TikTok’s calculated visual grammar in roughly a decade. The Daily Cal documents how the aesthetic is “dominated by white and attractive women,” with pressure to conform to specific items and media.[11]Daily Cal, "Social Media's Sad Girl" (2022) → The Batt reports 17.6 million TikTok posts under “depressed girl aesthetic,” marking the transformation from expression to genre.[12]The Batt, "Depression Isn't Aesthetic" (2024) →

The aesthetic has intellectual origins. Audrey Wollen’s “Sad Girl Theory” (2014) proposed female sadness as a form of political resistance, drawing on feminist traditions of reclaiming suffering as agency.[13]Watch Us Rise, "Sad Girl Aesthetic" (2023) → But the theory’s migration from art criticism to social media stripped it of its political content. Lucy Dacus objects to the “classification and commodification and perpetual expectation of women’s pain.”[14]M-A Chronicle, "Facade of the Sad Girl" (2025) → Harvard’s Amber Levis identifies the racial dimension: the Sad Girl “reeks of complacency” and “represents a very white kind of beauty.”[13]Watch Us Rise, "Sad Girl Aesthetic" (2023) →

The recovery paradox emerges most visibly here. A Trill Mag writer captures the bind: the fear that getting better means losing beauty, losing the aesthetic identity that makes you legible to your community.[15]Trill Mag, "#Sadgirl and My Mental Health" (2026) → The Polytechnic Pawprint describes the result: the sad girl “makes us see her sadness in all its glory without any real appeal for change.”[16]Polytechnic Pawprint, "Takeover of Sad Girl" → Phase Learning documents how algorithmic reinforcement creates feedback loops that discourage help-seeking.[17]Phase Learning, "Romanticizing Mental Illness" (2024) →

SECTION 4

Vulnerability as Currency

Celebrity mental health disclosure operates as both stigma reduction and brand strategy, and the two functions are difficult to separate. Gronholm and Thornicroft identify the “subtyping” paradox: audiences may exempt the disclosing celebrity from stigma while paradoxically increasing stigma for ordinary people — treating the famous person as brave exception rather than representative case.[18]Gronholm & Thornicroft, PMC (2022) →

The empirical evidence for positive effects is real. Calhoun and Gold document the “Diana effect” — Princess Diana’s bulimia disclosure doubled treatment-seeking rates.[19]Calhoun & Gold, Academic Psychiatry (2020) → But Thelandersson shows how disclosure functions simultaneously as “profitable vulnerability” and strategic branding, with Demi Lovato and Selena Gomez health narratives serving both personal processing and career rehabilitation.[20]Thelandersson, Celebrity Mental Health (Springer) →

Shekhar adds a crucial finding: Black men’s mental health disclosure operates through different infrastructures (YouTube rather than TikTok, long-form rather than aesthetic, community-oriented rather than individually branded), with Lil Wayne’s suicide disclosure generating engagement organized around Ubuntu philosophy and collective care.[21]Shekhar, "Digital Permission Structures" (2025) → Positive comments received 2.4x engagement, suggesting these alternative platforms support a different economy of vulnerability — one less oriented toward individual branding and more toward communal processing.

SECTION 5

Therapy-Speak & Commodification

Galloway documents how therapy-speak “left the couch and conquered social media,” with terms like “boundaries,” “triggers,” “gaslighting,” and “trauma response” now structuring everyday language.[22]Galloway, "The Cult of Therapy" (2025) → The migration is quantified: 83% of TikTok mental health videos contain misleading information.[22]Galloway, "The Cult of Therapy" (2025) → Recent PMC research finds the vocabulary susceptible to weaponization for “epistemic injustice” and “affective injustice” — using therapeutic language to delegitimize another person’s emotional experience.[24]PMC, "Unmasking Therapy-Speak" (2025) →

The economic dimension is stark. BetterHelp spent $900 million on marketing in 2023, against a user base where 70% of surveyed users reported poor experience and 30% became less likely to pursue therapy afterward.[23]First Session, "BetterHelp Risk" (2025) → This is the comfort industry as market — optimized for customer acquisition, not clinical outcomes.

Bandinelli and Pinzón Arteaga identify the political function: therapy-speak individualizes suffering and “neutralizes symbolic contradictions.”[25]Bandinelli & Pinzón, Mad in America (2025) → Poverty becomes “burnout.” Discrimination becomes “anxiety.” Exploitation becomes “boundary issues.” The vocabulary feels empowering but the diagnosis locates the problem in the individual, not the system. WBUR On Point provides the generational data: 72% of Gen Z women say mental health challenges are an important part of their identity, compared with 27% of Boomer men.[26]WBUR On Point, "Therapy Culture" (2025) →

SECTION 6

Who Gets to Be Sad

Fashionable despair is not equally distributed. DBSA reports treatment disparities that mirror the attention inequality: 48% of white Americans who need mental health treatment receive it, compared with 31% of Black Americans, 22% of Asian Americans, and only one-third of African Americans overall.[28]DBSA, "Disparities in Mental Health" (2021) → LGBTQ individuals face 2.5 times the depression risk.[28]DBSA, "Disparities in Mental Health" (2021) →

Ward and Mengesha find that African American men are the most reticent to use mental health services (30% willing, versus 51% of non-African American males).[29]Ward & Mengesha, PMC (2019) → The Lancet’s 2025 meta-analysis of 1.3 million people across 421 studies documents increasing depression among Black populations after 2020, driven by both COVID and the aftermath of George Floyd’s murder.[30]Lancet, "Depression Among Black Individuals" (2025) →

Assari provides the crucial intersectional finding: the effects of race, gender, and socioeconomic status on depression are multiplicative, not additive. High income is protective for white women but becomes a risk factor for African American men.[31]Assari, PMC (2017) → Mereish, examining 42 intersectional groups, finds past-year depression prevalence ranging from 3.4% to 31.4%.[32]Mereish, PMC (2023) → These findings demonstrate that the individual diagnostic frame is empirically inadequate for populations whose depression is driven by structural conditions.

SECTION 7

The Backlash

Freya India’s June 2025 essay, “Nobody Has a Personality Anymore,” is the most articulate expression of the emerging backlash. Her central claim: therapy-speak has not merely changed vocabulary but altered the structure of selfhood.[35]Freya India, "Nobody Has a Personality" (2025) → The piece went viral, reprinted in The Free Press, discussed on WBUR On Point, debated across Substack. The supporting data: 72% of Gen Z women say mental health challenges are an important part of their identity.[26]WBUR On Point, "Therapy Culture" (2025) →

The overdiagnosis critique has multiple strands. Frances argues DSM-5 creates “diagnostic hyperinflation.”[5]Frances, "DSM & Medicalization" (2022) → Horwitz and Wakefield contend the depression epidemic reflects reclassification of normal sadness.[7]Before and After Prozac, Springer (2021) → In an interview with Theos Think Tank, India identifies the algorithmic dimension: the center is pushed toward extremes in both directions, and holding a balanced position (“we’ve over-diagnosed, but some people genuinely suffer”) generates less engagement than either pole.[36]Theos Think Tank, Freya India Interview (2025) →

The backlash has its own backlash. Article Club’s discussion surfaces the counter-argument: India’s critique can be read as “privilege talking” — it is easier to dismiss diagnosis when you don’t need one. One commenter: “people are trying to connect and apologize and give themselves permission to exist.”[37]Article Club, Discussion #502 (2025) → The counter-backlash also notes that religious and conservative communities still under-diagnose, and that the backlash risks providing intellectual cover for re-stigmatization.

SYNTHESIS

Seven Structural Features

Across all seven dimensions, the survey identifies seven structural features of fashionable despair that operate across eras, platforms, and populations:

1. Ficino’s Template

A recurring four-phase cycle — reframe, fashion, market, backlash — running since 1489 and accelerating from centuries to months.[1]Wikipedia: Melancholia → Each cycle deposits institutional residue (DSM categories, literary traditions, identity infrastructure) that makes the next cycle’s terrain different. The template is a ratchet disguised as a pendulum.

2. The Diagnostic Ratchet

Diagnostic categories only expand. Each expansion creates constituencies (patients, clinicians, pharmaceutical companies, advocacy organizations) that resist contraction.[5]Frances, "DSM & Medicalization" (2022) → The cultural ratchet (TikTok self-diagnosis using clinical checklists) operates faster than the institutional ratchet (DSM revisions) but depends on it for legitimacy.[8]Smardon, "Stigma & Commodification" (2008) →

3. The Recovery Paradox

When suffering becomes identity, recovery threatens the self. The mechanism operates across eras: Romantic genius,[3]Frink, "Romantic Melancholy" (2016) → memoir authenticity,[9]Washington Post, "Prozac Nation at 30" (2024) → platform brand.[20]Thelandersson, Celebrity Mental Health (Springer) → The serialization problem — ongoing content requires ongoing suffering — is the platform-era intensifier.[15]Trill Mag, "#Sadgirl and My Mental Health" (2026) →

4. The Attention Inequality

Visibility is inversely correlated with treatment need. The sad girl aesthetic serves populations with relatively high treatment access (48% of white Americans receive care) while those with the least access (22% of Asian Americans, 31% of Black Americans) remain structurally invisible in the discourse.[28]DBSA, "Disparities in Mental Health" (2021) →

5. The Privatization Engine

Therapy-speak translates structural suffering into individual pathology, neutralizing political force.[25]Bandinelli & Pinzón, Mad in America (2025) → The $900M BetterHelp marketing budget is the economic expression of this logic — maximizing customer acquisition through broad diagnostic categories, identity-based framing, and individual rather than structural explanations.[23]First Session, "BetterHelp Risk" (2025) →

6. The Undecidable

Destigmatization and romanticization are structurally identical from outside. Authenticity, intent, and outcome tests all fail to cleanly separate them.[18]Gronholm & Thornicroft, PMC (2022) → The same mechanism that reduces stigma (open discussion) also enables commodification (marketable identity). The distinction may not be stable enough to hold.

7. The Post-Fashionable Framework

The evidence across all seven sections converges on structural interventions: reconnect diagnosis to context,[7]Before and After Prozac, Springer (2021) → redistribute attention toward underserved populations,[21]Shekhar, "Digital Permission Structures" (2025) → deprivatize suffering by supplementing therapeutic with collective political action,[25]Bandinelli & Pinzón, Mad in America (2025) → and decouple identity from diagnosis by creating pathways to selfhood that don’t depend on diagnostic categories.[35]Freya India, "Nobody Has a Personality" (2025) →

METHODS & LIMITATIONS

Scope and Constraints

This is a discourse survey, not a clinical or epidemiological review. It maps what is being said about the aestheticization of depression, not the disease itself. The 43 sources span academic research (PMC, Lancet, Springer, SAGE), cultural criticism (Substack, journalism), and institutional reports (DBSA, APA, Pew). Sources were collected via web search across all seven dimensions, prioritizing published research, institutional data, and substantive cultural criticism.

Key limitations: the survey is anglophone and primarily US-focused. Non-Western frameworks for depression (which may not map onto the Western aesthetic/medical binary) are excluded. Clinical pharmacology is excluded. The survey cannot determine net effects — whether fashionable despair increases or decreases total suffering — because the evidence is genuinely mixed and the mechanisms are non-canceling. The backlash section (S7) is thinner on empirical data than other sections, reflecting the recency of the discourse.

The survey is authored by a single researcher using AI-assisted research tools, which introduces both efficiency gains (broader literature coverage) and risks (potential for synthesis errors in rapidly reviewed sources). All claims are attributed to specific sources, and the literature collection is publicly available.
