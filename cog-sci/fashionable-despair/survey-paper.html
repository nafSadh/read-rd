Fashionable Despair

The Aestheticization of Depression from Ficino to TikTok

nafsadh · March 2026 · read:rd · read.sadh.app/cog-sci/fashionable-despair

Abstract. This paper surveys the aestheticization of depression across seven dimensions: its historical roots in Renaissance melancholy, the medicalization arc from DSM-III through Prozac Nation, the social media sad girl aesthetic, vulnerability as celebrity currency, therapy-speak and the commodification of mental health language, racial and gender inequities in whose sadness becomes culturally visible, and the emerging backlash against therapeutic culture. Drawing on 43 sources across academic research, journalism, and cultural criticism, the survey identifies seven recurring conceptual patterns observed across the literature on fashionable despair: a recurring template (Ficino’s cycle of reframing, fashion, market, and backlash), a diagnostic ratchet (categories that only expand), a recovery paradox (identity-suffering fusion that makes getting better threatening), an attention inequality (visibility inversely correlated with treatment need), a privatization engine (structural suffering translated into individual pathology), an undecidable (destigmatization and romanticization as structurally identical from outside), and an emerging post-fashionable framework. The literature suggests that the mechanisms driving destigmatization and romanticization may be structurally inseparable, operating on the same platforms through the same social logics — though this remains a point of contention in the discourse.
INTRODUCTION

The Shape of the Problem

In 1489, Marsilio Ficino published a reframing later widely cited in European thought: melancholy, long classified as a disease of black bile, became the mark of Saturn’s children — philosophers, poets, contemplatives.[1]Wikipedia: Melancholia → This reframing shifted melancholy from an external affliction to an intrinsic aspect of identity. Five centuries later, a TikTok hashtag carries 17.6 million posts under “depressed girl aesthetic.”[12]The Batt, "Depression Isn't Aesthetic" (2024) → This contemporary phenomenon invites an examination of potential parallels in how distress becomes integrated into self-perception, despite significant shifts in cultural context and expression.

This paper surveys the discourse of fashionable despair — the cultural phenomenon in which depression is aestheticized, branded, commodified, and contested. It is not a clinical survey of depression itself, nor a technical review of treatment efficacy. It is a survey of what people are saying about how depression became fashionable, the social actors involved in this discourse and the documented psychological and social consequences.

The survey covers seven dimensions: the historical genealogy of melancholy as cultural capital (Section 1), the medicalization arc that transformed sadness into a diagnostic category (Section 2), the social media aesthetics that turned that category into visual grammar (Section 3), the economy of celebrity vulnerability (Section 4), the migration of therapy language into everyday speech (Section 5), the racial and gender inequities in whose suffering becomes visible (Section 6), and the emerging backlash against the entire discourse (Section 7). A synthesis section identifies seven structural features that operate across all dimensions.

SECTION 1

Historical Melancholy

The idea that sadness confers depth has a precise origin. Zimmermann traces it as a culture-bound syndrome specific to European intellectual history: Hippocrates’ humoral theory classified melancholy as disease, but Aristotle asked why “all those who have become eminent” seemed touched by it.[2]Zimmermann, "History of Melancholy" (1995) → Ficino formalized the link in De Vita Libri Tres, connecting Saturn to intellectual greatness and transforming melancholy from vice to virtue.[1]Wikipedia: Melancholia →

The template ran through the English Elizabethans (where “fashionable melancholy” became what scholars have characterized as a literary cult), through Burton’s encyclopedic Anatomy of Melancholy (1621), and reached full expression in Romanticism. Frink argues that the English Romantics used melancholy not merely as aesthetic posture but as political protest against Enlightenment rationalism — to suffer was to reject the prevailing order.[3]Frink, "Romantic Melancholy" (2016) → Pfau identifies the philosophical depth: melancholy as “excess of knowledge exceeding representation,” a cognitive surplus that could not be contained by rational forms.[4]Pfau on Byron, "Romantic Melancholy" (2013) →

Two features of the historical template persist into the present. First, the social hierarchy of suffering: melancholy was always a claim of the educated classes, and Zimmermann documents that it functioned as a European-specific cultural construct, not a human universal.[2]Zimmermann, "History of Melancholy" (1995) → Second, the gendered dimension: Frink argues that women’s melancholy was interpreted differently from men’s, with female suffering read as pathology where male suffering was read as genius.[3]Frink, "Romantic Melancholy" (2016) → Both patterns recur across every subsequent era this survey examines.

SECTION 2

The Medicalization Arc

The literature describes the transition from melancholy to depression as structural rather than merely terminological. Allen Frances, who chaired DSM-IV, provides a prominent insider critique: in Frances’s account, DSM-III (1980) introduced symptom checklists that weakened the link between suffering and context, and DSM-5 (2013) removed the bereavement exclusion — the last clause acknowledging that intense sadness might be proportionate.[5]Frances, "DSM & Medicalization" (2022) → Across major revisions, diagnostic boundaries expanded on balance; Frances and others note that while some criteria were tightened in specific domains (such as the consolidation of Asperger’s into ASD), the net direction was toward broader inclusion rather than contraction.

Psychology Today traces the cultural arc: the 1950s therapeutic revolution, DSM-III’s introduction of hundreds of new disorders, and by the 2020s, one in six adult women on antidepressants.[6]Psychology Today, "Prozac Culture" (2024) → Horwitz and Wakefield argue that the depression epidemic substantially reflects reclassification of normal sadness as pathology.[7]Before and After Prozac, Springer (2021) →

The Prozac era produced a literary genre. Elizabeth Wurtzel’s Prozac Nation (1994), followed by Styron’s Darkness Visible, Kaysen’s Girl, Interrupted, and Jamison’s An Unquiet Mind, established the memoir of mental illness as literary form.[9]Washington Post, "Prozac Nation at 30" (2024) → Rhetoric of depression scholarship shows how Kramer’s Listening to Prozac introduced “cosmetic pharmacology” — the idea that medication could optimize personality, shifting problems from social conditions to personal pathology.[10]Rhetoric of Depression, UF Press (2023) → Smardon documents how the DSM itself “commodifies illness” — the checklist format makes self-diagnosis possible and intuitive.[8]Smardon, "Stigma & Commodification" (2008) →

SECTION 3

Social Media Aesthetics

The sad girl aesthetic migrated from Tumblr’s stream-of-consciousness culture to TikTok’s calculated visual grammar in roughly a decade. The Daily Cal documents how the aesthetic is “dominated by white and attractive women,” with pressure to conform to specific items and media.[11]Daily Cal, "Social Media's Sad Girl" (2022) → The Batt reports 17.6 million TikTok posts under “depressed girl aesthetic,” a phenomenon that has been characterized as a transformation from expression to genre.[12]The Batt, "Depression Isn't Aesthetic" (2024) →

The aesthetic has intellectual origins. Audrey Wollen’s “Sad Girl Theory” (2014) proposed female sadness as a form of political resistance, drawing on feminist traditions of reclaiming suffering as agency.[13]Watch Us Rise, "Sad Girl Aesthetic" (2023) → However, its subsequent adoption within social media contexts has been critiqued for diminishing its original political content. Lucy Dacus objects to the “classification and commodification and perpetual expectation of women’s pain.”[14]M-A Chronicle, "Facade of the Sad Girl" (2025) → Harvard’s Amber Levis identifies the racial dimension: the Sad Girl “reeks of complacency” and “represents a very white kind of beauty.”[13]Watch Us Rise, "Sad Girl Aesthetic" (2023) →

The recovery paradox is particularly evident in this domain. A Trill Mag writer articulates the paradox: the fear that improvement may entail the loss of an aesthetic identity perceived as integral to community legibility.[15]Trill Mag, "#Sadgirl and My Mental Health" (2026, forthcoming) → The Polytechnic Pawprint describes the result: the sad girl “makes us see her sadness in all its glory without any real appeal for change.”[16]Polytechnic Pawprint, "Takeover of Sad Girl" → Phase Learning documents how algorithmic reinforcement creates feedback loops that discourage help-seeking.[17]Phase Learning, "Romanticizing Mental Illness" (2024) →

SECTION 4

Vulnerability as Currency

Celebrity mental health disclosure operates as both stigma reduction and brand strategy, and the two functions are difficult to separate. Gronholm and Thornicroft identify the “subtyping” paradox: audiences may exempt the disclosing celebrity from stigma while paradoxically increasing stigma for ordinary people — treating the famous person as brave exception rather than representative case.[18]Gronholm & Thornicroft, PMC (2022) →

Empirical evidence supports positive effects. Calhoun and Gold report the “Diana effect” — an approximate doubling of treatment-seeking rates following Princess Diana’s bulimia disclosure.[19]Calhoun & Gold, Academic Psychiatry (2020) → But Thelandersson shows how disclosure functions simultaneously as “profitable vulnerability” and strategic branding, with Demi Lovato and Selena Gomez health narratives serving both personal processing and career rehabilitation.[20]Thelandersson, Celebrity Mental Health (Springer) →

Shekhar's research also indicates: Black men’s mental health disclosure operates through different infrastructures (YouTube rather than TikTok, long-form rather than aesthetic, community-oriented rather than individually branded), with Lil Wayne’s suicide disclosure generating engagement organized around Ubuntu philosophy and collective care.[21]Shekhar, "Digital Permission Structures" (2025) → Positive comments received 2.4x engagement, suggesting these alternative platforms support a different economy of vulnerability — one less oriented toward individual branding and more toward communal processing.

SECTION 5

Therapy-Speak & Commodification

Galloway documents the widespread adoption of therapy-speak, noting its transition from clinical settings to social media, where terms such as “boundaries,” “triggers,” “gaslighting,” and “trauma response” have become common in everyday discourse.[22]Galloway, "The Cult of Therapy" (2025) → The proliferation of this language is accompanied by concerns regarding its accuracy; Galloway reports that a 2022 study found 83% of TikTok mental health videos contained misleading information (primary source not independently verified by this survey).[22]Galloway, "The Cult of Therapy" (2025) → Furthermore, recent PMC research indicates that this vocabulary is susceptible to weaponization, contributing to “epistemic injustice” and “affective injustice” by enabling the delegitimization of another person’s emotional experience through therapeutic terminology.[24]PMC, "Unmasking Therapy-Speak" (2025) →

The economic landscape of digital mental health services reveals significant investment in marketing. For instance, BetterHelp allocated $900 million to marketing in 2023 (First Session is a commercial therapy-finder platform and reports these figures with a competitive interest in the sector). According to the same survey, 70% of users reported a poor experience, and 30% subsequently became less inclined to pursue therapy. These figures, pending independent corroboration, are consistent with a market orientation that prioritizes customer acquisition; observers have raised questions about the emphasis on clinical outcomes within this sector.[23]First Session, "BetterHelp Risk" (2025) →

Bandinelli and Pinzón Arteaga identify a political function of therapy-speak, asserting that it individualizes suffering and “neutralizes symbolic contradictions.”[25]Bandinelli & Pinzón, Mad in America (2025) → This framework reframes systemic issues, for example, by reinterpreting poverty as “burnout,” discrimination as “anxiety,” and exploitation as “boundary issues.” The authors contend that while this vocabulary may appear to offer agency, its diagnostic application tends to locate problems within the individual rather than addressing systemic factors. This individualization of mental health challenges may contribute to shifts in identity formation, as evidenced by WBUR On Point data indicating that 72% of Gen Z women consider mental health challenges an important part of their identity, compared with 27% of Boomer men.[26]WBUR On Point, "Therapy Culture" (2025) →

SECTION 6

Disparities in the Experience of Depression

The prevalence and treatment of mental health conditions are not uniformly distributed across populations. The DBSA reports treatment disparities that are consistent with broader inequalities in mental health care access: in some DBSA breakdowns, 48% of white Americans who need mental health treatment receive it, compared with 31% of Black Americans and 22% of Asian Americans, while separate figures cite roughly one-third of African Americans overall receiving care.[28]DBSA, "Disparities in Mental Health" (2021) → Furthermore, per DBSA, LGBTQ individuals face approximately 2.5 times the depression risk compared to cisgender heterosexual peers.[28]DBSA, "Disparities in Mental Health" (2021) →

Ward and Mengesha find that African American men are the most reticent to use mental health services (30% willing, versus 51% of non-African American males).[29]Ward & Mengesha, PMC (2019) → The Lancet’s 2025 meta-analysis of 1.3 million people across 421 studies documents increasing depression among Black populations after 2020, driven by both COVID and the aftermath of George Floyd’s murder.[30]Lancet, "Depression Among Black Individuals" (2025) →

Assari's research indicates that the effects of race, gender, and socioeconomic status on depression are multiplicative, not additive. High income is protective for white women but becomes a risk factor for African American men.[31]Assari, PMC (2017) → Mereish, examining 42 intersectional groups, finds past-year depression prevalence ranging from 3.4% to 31.4%.[32]Mereish, PMC (2023) → The cumulative evidence suggests that an individual diagnostic frame may be insufficient for fully addressing depression in populations where structural conditions are significant contributing factors.

SECTION 7

The Backlash

Freya India’s June 2025 essay, “Nobody Has a Personality Anymore,” is a prominent expression of the emerging critique. Her central claim posits that therapy-speak has not merely changed vocabulary but has fundamentally altered the structure of selfhood — a claim the essay does not empirically demonstrate but that drew substantial engagement.[35]Freya India, "Nobody Has a Personality" (2025) → The essay gained widespread attention, being reprinted in The Free Press, discussed on WBUR On Point, and debated across Substack. Observations consistent with this perspective include data indicating that 72% of Gen Z women report mental health challenges as an important component of their identity.[26]WBUR On Point, "Therapy Culture" (2025) →

The overdiagnosis critique has multiple strands. Frances argues DSM-5 creates “diagnostic hyperinflation.”[5]Frances, "DSM & Medicalization" (2022) → Horwitz and Wakefield contend the depression epidemic reflects reclassification of normal sadness.[7]Before and After Prozac, Springer (2021) → In an interview with Theos Think Tank, India identifies the algorithmic dimension: the center is pushed toward extremes in both directions, and holding a balanced position (“we’ve over-diagnosed, but some people genuinely suffer”) generates less engagement than either pole.[36]Theos Think Tank, Freya India Interview (2025) →

The critique of therapy-speak has, in turn, generated counter-arguments. A discussion hosted by Article Club highlighted the perspective that India’s critique might be perceived as overlooking the experiences of those who benefit from diagnosis, suggesting that the ease of dismissing diagnosis may be contingent on not requiring one. An alternative interpretation offered by a commenter is that individuals utilize therapy-speak to foster connection, express apologies, and validate their existence.[37]Article Club, Discussion #502 (2025) → Furthermore, concerns have been raised that the focus on overdiagnosis might inadvertently detract from persistent issues of under-diagnosis in certain demographics, such as religious and conservative communities. Critics also express apprehension that the discourse surrounding the backlash could contribute to the re-stigmatization of mental health conditions.

SYNTHESIS

Seven Recurring Themes

The following seven conceptual patterns emerge from the surveyed literature across eras, platforms, and populations. They are presented as recurring themes observed in the sources, not as theoretical claims independent of those sources:

1. Ficino’s Template

A recurring four-phase cycle—reframe, fashion, market, backlash—has been identified, with historical observations suggesting its presence since 1489 and an acceleration from centuries to months in its progression.[1]Wikipedia: Melancholia → Each cycle contributes to the accumulation of institutional residue (e.g., DSM categories, literary traditions, identity infrastructure), which subsequently alters the conditions for successive cycles. This template exhibits characteristics of a unidirectional progression despite appearing cyclical.

2. The Diagnostic Ratchet

Across the DSM revisions examined in the literature, diagnostic categories expanded on balance. Each expansion tends to create constituencies (patients, clinicians, pharmaceutical companies, advocacy organizations) that resist contraction.[5]Frances, "DSM & Medicalization" (2022) → The cultural ratchet (TikTok self-diagnosis using clinical checklists) operates faster than the institutional ratchet (DSM revisions) but depends on it for legitimacy.[8]Smardon, "Stigma & Commodification" (2008) →

3. The Recovery Paradox

The integration of suffering into personal identity can present challenges to the process of recovery. This mechanism has been observed across various eras, exemplified by concepts such as Romantic genius,[3]Frink, "Romantic Melancholy" (2016) → memoir authenticity,[9]Washington Post, "Prozac Nation at 30" (2024) → and platform brand.[20]Thelandersson, Celebrity Mental Health (Springer) → The requirement for ongoing content, which often necessitates ongoing narratives of suffering, is identified as an intensifier in the platform era.[15]Trill Mag, "#Sadgirl and My Mental Health" (2026, forthcoming) →

4. The Attention Inequality

Visibility is inversely correlated with treatment need. The sad girl aesthetic serves populations with relatively high treatment access (48% of white Americans receive care) while those with the least access (22% of Asian Americans, 31% of Black Americans) remain structurally invisible in the discourse.[28]DBSA, "Disparities in Mental Health" (2021) →

5. The Privatization Engine

The discourse of 'therapy-speak' has been observed to reframe structural issues as individual pathologies, a process that may diminish collective political engagement.[25]Bandinelli & Pinzón, Mad in America (2025) → The substantial marketing expenditures, such as the reported $900M BetterHelp budget, exemplify an economic strategy that prioritizes customer acquisition through broad diagnostic categories, identity-based framing, and individual rather than structural explanations.[23]First Session, "BetterHelp Risk" (2025) →

6. The Undecidable

From an external perspective, destigmatization and romanticization exhibit structural similarities. Attempts to differentiate them based on authenticity, intent, or outcome have not consistently yielded clear distinctions.[18]Gronholm & Thornicroft, PMC (2022) → The mechanism facilitating stigma reduction (e.g., open discussion) also contributes to commodification (e.g., marketable identity). Consequently, the stability of this distinction appears challenging to maintain.

7. The Post-Fashionable Framework

The analysis across all seven sections suggests a focus on structural interventions: recontextualizing diagnosis,[7]Before and After Prozac, Springer (2021) → reallocating attention toward underserved populations,[21]Shekhar, "Digital Permission Structures" (2025) → addressing suffering through collective political action in addition to therapeutic approaches,[25]Bandinelli & Pinzón, Mad in America (2025) → and fostering pathways to selfhood that are not contingent on diagnostic categories.[35]Freya India, "Nobody Has a Personality" (2025) →

METHODS & LIMITATIONS

Scope and Constraints

This is a discourse survey, not a clinical or epidemiological review. It maps what is being said about the aestheticization of depression, not the disease itself. The 43 sources span academic research (PMC, Lancet, Springer, SAGE), cultural criticism (Substack, journalism), and institutional reports (DBSA, APA, Pew). Sources were collected via searches of Google Scholar, PubMed/PMC, and general web search across all seven dimensions, using primary search strings including "melancholy aestheticization," "depression medicalization DSM," "sad girl aesthetic TikTok," "celebrity mental health disclosure," "therapy-speak commodification," "racial disparities depression treatment," and "therapeutic culture backlash." Selection prioritized peer-reviewed research, institutional data, and substantive cultural criticism; sources were excluded if they lacked identifiable authorship or institutional affiliation.

Key limitations: the survey is anglophone and primarily US-focused. Non-Western frameworks for depression (which may not map onto the Western aesthetic/medical binary) are excluded. Clinical pharmacology is excluded. The survey cannot determine net effects — whether fashionable despair increases or decreases total suffering — because the evidence is genuinely mixed and the mechanisms are non-canceling. The backlash section (S7) is thinner on empirical data than other sections, reflecting the recency of the discourse.

The survey is authored by a single researcher using AI-assisted research tools, which introduces both efficiency gains (broader literature coverage) and risks (potential for synthesis errors in rapidly reviewed sources). All claims are attributed to specific sources, and the literature collection is publicly available.
