Fashionable Despair

The aestheticization, commodification, and normalization of depression — from Renaissance melancholy to TikTok sad girls.

Depression has been fashionable before. Ficino transformed melancholy from humoral curse into mark of genius in the 15th century. The Romantics made suffering a prerequisite for art. What’s new is the scale, the speed, and the marketplace. Social media turned sadness into an aesthetic with 16 billion views. Celebrity disclosure became a branding strategy. Therapy language escaped the clinic and colonized everyday speech. And somewhere in the noise, the people who actually suffer became harder to see.

This project surveys 7 dimensions of fashionable despair across 46+ sources — from Aristotle’s claim that all great minds are touched by melancholy, through the Prozac revolution, the sad girl pipeline, the vulnerability economy, and the emerging backlash. The central tension: destigmatization and romanticization look identical from the outside, and telling them apart matters.

Emerging Tensions

  • 01 Destigmatization vs. romanticization: They look identical from the outside. How do you tell “it’s okay to not be okay” from “not being okay is interesting”?
  • 02 Awareness vs. identity fusion: 72% of Gen Z women say mental health challenges define them — is that hard-won self-knowledge or diagnostic capture?
  • 03 Access vs. commodification: BetterHelp reaches millions but 70% report poor experience. Is scaled therapy better than no therapy?
  • 04 Celebrity disclosure: Reduces stigma through social learning but triggers “subtyping” that exempts celebrities from the stigma ordinary people face.
  • 05 The aesthetics of permission: The sad girl aesthetic is overwhelmingly white, thin, and beautiful. Whose sadness counts as fashionable?
  • 06 Historical rhyme: Ficino’s melancholy-as-genius served the same social function as today’s depression-as-depth. Is anything actually new?
