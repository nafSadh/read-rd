FASHIONABLE DESPAIR · POSITION PAPER

The Missing Third Position

The problem is not that being depressed is fashionable. The problem is that a culture with no template for ordinary suffering can only toggle between hiding pain and performing it.

nafsadh March 2026 7 Arguments · 38 Sources read.sadh.app
THE CLAIM

Suffering as Ordinary

Lifetime depression diagnoses in the United States rose from 19.6% to 29.0% between 2015 and 2023 (Gallup, n = 5,167).[1]Gallup, “Depression Rates Reach New Highs” (2023) → Forty percent of U.S. high school students report persistent sadness or hopelessness (CDC YRBS, 2023).[2]CDC YRBS 2023 Results → Meanwhile, 72% of Gen Z women say mental health challenges are an important part of their identity (Skeptic Research Center, n = 3,014).[3]Skeptic Research Center (2022), n = 3,014 American adults, DASS-21 validated →

This paper argues that these numbers reflect something deeper than a diagnostic trend. They are the signature of a culture that has no framework for suffering that is neither stigmatized nor aestheticized. No template exists for suffering that is simply ordinary — neither hidden nor displayed, neither shameful nor interesting. The debate over whether sadness is “genuine or performed” is itself the symptom.

The claim that depression is fashionable is true but uninteresting. Melancholy has been fashionable since Ficino reframed it as the mark of genius in 1489.[4]Zimmermann, “History of Melancholy” (1995) → What matters is what the current version does that previous versions did not, who it serves, and what structural absence makes it inevitable. Seven arguments converge on a single conclusion: the culture needs a third position.

ARGUMENT 01

The Culture Can Only Toggle

The destigmatization movement succeeded. But the only available alternative to hiding suffering turned out to be displaying it. The culture moved from position 1 to position 2 without ever constructing a position 3.

The evidence for this binary is quantitative. The Skeptic Research Center survey (n = 3,014, DASS-21 validated) found 72% of Gen Z women agree that mental health challenges are an important part of their identity, versus 27% of Boomer men.[3]Skeptic Research Center (2022), n = 3,014 American adults, DASS-21 validated → A Harmony Healthcare IT survey (n = 1,010 Gen Z Americans) found 46% have received a formal diagnosis and another 37% believe they have an undiagnosed condition — meaning 83% of Gen Z see themselves through a clinical lens.[5]Harmony Healthcare IT, “State of Gen Z Mental Health” (2025), n = 1,010 → Blue Shield of California reports 94% of Gen Z youth experience regular mental health challenges.[6]Blue Shield of California poll (Sept 2025) → This is not simply increased openness. Identity-level fusion with a diagnostic category is position 2 operating at full intensity.

Meanwhile, position 1 persists, sorted by demographics. African American men remain the demographic most reluctant to seek mental health services: 30% willing, versus 51% of non-African American males (Ward & Mengesha, PMC).[7]Ward & Mengesha, PMC (2019) → SAMHSA’s National Survey on Drug Use and Health (2021–2023, pooled) confirms that white and multiracial adults receive mental health services at significantly higher rates than Black, Hispanic, or Asian adults.[8]SAMHSA NSDUH 2021–2023 Race/Ethnicity Companion Report → The two positions coexist. They are demographically sorted.

ARGUMENT 02

Not New, But Different

The template is ancient. Zimmermann traces melancholy as fashionable pose to Hippocrates; Ficino formalized the link between Saturn and intellectual greatness in 1489.[4]Zimmermann, “History of Melancholy” (1995) → Frink documents the Romantics using melancholy as protest against Enlightenment rationalism.[9]Frink, “Romantic Melancholy” (2016) → Wurtzel’s Prozac Nation and the 1990s memoir wave made depression a literary genre.[10]Washington Post, “Prozac Nation at 30” (2024) → The cycle runs: reframe, fashion, market, backlash.

So what is genuinely different now? One mechanism: identity fusion at algorithmic scale. Ficino made melancholy a pose. The Romantics made it an aesthetic. Prozac made it a diagnosis. TikTok made it a self — and the algorithm locks you in.

The empirical evidence for algorithmic lock-in is specific. Meta’s own internal research (leaked 2021) found that 32% of teen girls said Instagram made them feel worse about their bodies, and 13.5% said it worsened suicidal thoughts.[11]2021 Facebook Files leak; originally Wall Street Journal → A Frontiers in Psychology study (2025) found that test accounts simulating 13-year-olds were exposed to feeds centered on depressive content in under one hour, with half of recommended videos focusing on depressive themes after just 15–20 minutes.[12]Frontiers in Psychology, algorithm rabbit hole study (2025) → A meta-analysis of 17 RCTs (n = 5,624) found that restricting social media use improved well-being with effect size d = 0.17 (95% CI 0.08–0.27), and 5 of 7 studies measuring depression found significant reduction.[13]JMIR, Meta-analysis of 17 social media restriction RCTs (2024–2025), n = 5,624 → Haidt and Twenge document a 134% rise in depression among U.S. college undergraduates between the early 2010s and 2020, replicated across Anglosphere countries.[14]Haidt & Twenge, “The Anxious Generation” data (2024) →

Previous eras had performance. They did not have a closed loop between performance, identity, community, and a machine optimized to keep the loop running.

ARGUMENT 03

The Recovery Paradox

When suffering becomes identity, recovery threatens the self. Byron could stop performing melancholy and still be Byron. A teenager whose social identity, feed, community, and self-concept are organized around #sadgirl cannot stop being sad without an identity crisis that is itself a mental health event.

The self-diagnosis pipeline is now quantified. 30% of Gen Z self-diagnose mental illness based on social media content (Scimatic, 2024).[15]Scimatic, Self-diagnosis prevalence study (2024) → Among the 100 most popular ADHD TikTok videos (~500 million combined views), 52% were classified as misleading; non-healthcare providers produced misleading content at 55.1% versus 27.3% for providers (Yeung et al., Canadian Journal of Psychiatry, p < 0.001).[16]Yeung, Ng & Abi-Jaoude, Canadian J. of Psychiatry (2022), 100 top ADHD TikToks → A Harvard Petrie-Flom review found 84% of TikTok mental health videos deceptive, with only 9% of creators possessing relevant qualifications.[17]Harvard Petrie-Flom, “Dr. TikTok” (2025) → 17.6 million TikTok posts mark the transformation from expression to genre.[18]The Batt, “Depression Isn’t Aesthetic” (2024) →

Phase Learning documents how algorithmic reinforcement creates feedback loops that discourage help-seeking.[19]Phase Learning, “Romanticizing Mental Illness” (2024) → A Current Psychology study (2026) confirmed the association between mental health TikTok consumption and both self-reported symptoms and perceived prevalence.[20]Current Psychology, TikTok MH content & symptom self-report (2026) → The serialization problem — ongoing content requires ongoing suffering — is the platform-era intensifier that previous cultural cycles lacked.

THE BIND

The fear is not that getting better is hard. The fear is that getting better means becoming invisible — losing the aesthetic identity that makes you legible to your community.

ARGUMENT 04

A Regressive Redistribution

Fashionable depression is not a universal cultural shift. It is a class-specific cultural shift that serves the populations least in need while leaving the populations most in need exactly where they were.

DBSA reports that 48% of white Americans who need care receive it, compared with 31% of Black Americans and 22% of Asian Americans; LGBTQ individuals face 2.5 times the depression risk.[21]DBSA, “Disparities in Mental Health” (2021) → SAMHSA’s pooled NSDUH data (2021–2023) confirm that white and multiracial adults receive services at significantly higher rates than Black, Hispanic, or Asian adults.[8]SAMHSA NSDUH 2021–2023 Race/Ethnicity Companion Report → The sad girl aesthetic, meanwhile, is dominated by white, thin, conventionally attractive women.[22]Daily Cal, “Social Media’s Sad Girl” (2022) →

The Lancet’s 2025 meta-analysis (1.3 million people, 421 studies) documents increasing depression among Black populations after 2020, driven by both COVID and the aftermath of George Floyd’s murder.[23]Lancet meta-analysis (2025), 1.3M people, 421 studies → Assari’s intersectional analysis shows the effects are multiplicative, not additive: high income is protective for white women but becomes a risk factor for African American men.[24]Assari, PMC (2017) → Mereish, examining 42 intersectional groups, finds past-year depression prevalence ranging from 3.4% to 31.4%.[25]Mereish, PMC (2023) → CDC YRBS data show 53% of female high school students report persistent sadness, with LGBQ+ students at 2–3× the rate of heterosexual peers.[2]CDC YRBS 2023 Results →

The awareness economy concentrates cultural attention where structural need is lowest. The populations with the most reason to suffer are stuck in position 1. The populations with the least reason have moved to position 2. Neither has access to position 3.

ARGUMENT 05

The Privatization Trap

Therapy-speak individualizes suffering and neutralizes its political force. Bandinelli and Pinzón Arteaga argue that the therapeutic frame translates structural problems into personal pathology: poverty becomes “burnout,” discrimination becomes “anxiety,” exploitation becomes “boundary issues.”[26]Bandinelli & Pinzón, Mad in America (2025) → The vocabulary feels empowering, but the diagnosis locates the problem in the individual, not the system.

The economic infrastructure is now enormous. The online therapy market reached $4.39 billion in 2025, projected to hit $14.10 billion by 2034.[27]GlobeNewsWire, Online therapy market report (2025) → BetterHelp alone generated $1.03 billion in 2024 revenue with 5 million+ users.[28]TapTwice Digital, BetterHelp statistics (2024) → But outcomes are poor: BetterHelp spent $900 million on marketing in 2023; 70% of surveyed users reported poor experience and 30% became less likely to pursue therapy afterward.[29]First Session, “BetterHelp Risk” (2025) → This is the comfort industry optimized for customer acquisition, not clinical outcomes.

PMC research documents therapy language being weaponized for epistemic injustice — using therapeutic vocabulary to delegitimize another person’s emotional experience.[30]PMC, “Unmasking Therapy-Speak” (2025) → Cosmetic pharmacology, which Kramer identified in Listening to Prozac, was the early form[31]Rhetoric of Depression, UF Press (2023) → — but the social media version is more powerful because it does not even require medication. It requires only a hashtag.

ARGUMENT 06

The Backlash Cannot Save Us

The backlash against fashionable depression is largely correct on diagnosis. The overdiagnosis data is substantial: a Johns Hopkins study (Mojtabai, 2013) found that roughly 1 in 4 depression diagnoses in U.S. primary care are false positives — patients who do not meet criteria on structured clinical interview.[32]Mojtabai, Johns Hopkins, depression overdiagnosis (2013) → Canadian primary care data show misdiagnosis rates of 65.9% for major depressive disorder and 71.0% for generalized anxiety disorder.[33]PMC, Canadian primary care misdiagnosis rates → Frances’s critique of DSM-5 diagnostic inflation is supported by the numbers: U.S. ADHD diagnoses rose 67% from 6.1% to 10.2% between 1997 and 2016, with an additional 15.2% increase in adult ADHD from 2020 to 2023.[34]CDC ADHD data; Psychiatric Research and Clinical Practice (2024) → Gallup’s survey data show lifetime depression diagnoses rising from 19.6% to 29.0% between 2015 and 2023, with women’s rates rising at nearly twice the rate of men’s.[1]Gallup, “Depression Rates Reach New Highs” (2023), n = 5,167 →

But the backlash is wrong on prescription. It risks pushing the culture back to position 1 (suffering is shameful, hide it) rather than forward to position 3 (suffering is ordinary, live with it). The counter-backlash names the risk correctly: India’s critique can be read as privilege talking — it is easier to dismiss diagnosis when you do not need one. Article Club discussion surfaces the defense that people are trying to give themselves permission to exist.[35]Article Club, Discussion #502 (2025) → The Gronholm and Thornicroft “subtyping” paradox applies here too: exempting the critic from the need for diagnosis may paradoxically increase stigma for those who need it most.[36]Gronholm & Thornicroft, PMC (2022) →

THE RISK

Any backlash that cannot articulate position 3 — suffering as ordinary — will default to re-imposing position 1. The pendulum is not a solution. A new equilibrium is.

ARGUMENT 07

The Third Position

Position 3 holds that suffering is an unremarkable feature of being alive. It requires neither concealment nor content. It is not interesting or shameful. It is simply there — like weather, or age, or the fact that Tuesday follows Monday.

What would position 3 look like in practice? The evidence suggests three structural requirements:

RECONNECT DIAGNOSIS TO CONTEXT

The false positive rate in primary care depression diagnosis (~25%)[32]Mojtabai, Johns Hopkins, depression overdiagnosis (2013) → and the DSM-5’s removal of the bereavement exclusion[37]Frances, “DSM & Medicalization” (2022) → imply that diagnosis should be contextualized — not eliminated, but connected to the conditions that produce suffering. A person who is sad because their life is structurally bad does not need a diagnostic label. They need structural change. Position 3 makes this distinction possible without requiring either that suffering be hidden or that it be interesting.

REDISTRIBUTE ATTENTION

Shekhar’s research on digital permission structures offers a model: Black men’s mental health disclosure on YouTube operates through collective care rather than individual branding, with positive comments receiving 2.4× engagement.[38]Shekhar, “Digital Permission Structures” (2025), 2.4× positive engagement → The attention economy is not inevitable. Alternative platforms and community structures can support a different economy of vulnerability — one oriented toward mutual care rather than mutual display. The 48%–22% treatment gap[21]DBSA, “Disparities in Mental Health” (2021) → is not a knowledge problem. It is an attention problem.

DECOUPLE IDENTITY FROM DIAGNOSIS

The 72%-to-27% gap between Gen Z women and Boomer men who see mental health as core identity[3]Skeptic Research Center (2022), n = 3,014 American adults → is not evidence of generational weakness. It is evidence that the culture currently offers young women no pathway to selfhood that does not route through diagnostic categories. The 46% formal diagnosis rate among Gen Z[5]Harmony Healthcare IT (2025), n = 1,010 → may reflect genuine illness. But the additional 37% who believe they have an undiagnosed condition — bringing the total to 83% who see themselves through a clinical lens — suggests that the diagnostic frame has become the default vocabulary for ordinary human difficulty. Position 3 creates an alternative: you can suffer and have a personality. You can be sad and that not be the most interesting thing about you.

ONE SENTENCE

The problem is not that being depressed is fashionable; the problem is that a culture with no template for ordinary suffering can only toggle between hiding pain and performing it.
