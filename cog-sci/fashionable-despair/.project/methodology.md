# Methodology — Fashionable Despair

## Research Question
How did depression transform from stigma to social currency — and what are the consequences for discourse, treatment, and people who actually suffer? We trace the arc from Renaissance "fashionable melancholy" through Romantic genius-as-suffering, 20th-century medicalization, and the current moment where depression functions simultaneously as aesthetic, identity marker, content strategy, and commodity.

## Scope & Dimensions
Seven sections covering the phenomenon across historical, medical, cultural, and social axes:

1. **S1: Historical Melancholy** — The long tradition of melancholy as cultural capital, from Aristotle through Burton's *Anatomy* to Romantic-era genius worship
2. **S2: The Medicalization Arc** — DSM evolution, Prozac Nation, the transformation of depression from experience to diagnosis to identity
3. **S3: Social Media Aesthetics** — Sad girl aesthetic, depression memes, the Tumblr → TikTok pipeline, melancholy as brand
4. **S4: Vulnerability as Currency** — Celebrity disclosure economy, mental health as content, the performance of suffering
5. **S5: Therapy-Speak & Commodification** — BetterHelp culture, pathologizing normal emotions, therapy language in everyday life
6. **S6: Who Gets to Be Sad** — Class, race, gender dimensions — who is "fashionably" depressed vs who just suffers
7. **S7: The Backlash** — Freya India, Abigail Shrier, anti-therapy-speak movement, overdiagnosis critique

**Deliberately excluded:** Clinical treatment efficacy, pharmacology deep-dives, specific DSM criteria debates, non-Western depression frameworks (could be a future project).

## Source Strategy
- **Primary:** Academic papers (PMC, JSTOR, Springer), major journalism (New Yorker, Washington Post, Psychology Today), Substack essays (Freya India, Scott Galloway)
- **Secondary:** Cultural criticism, student journalism (for generational voice), book reviews
- **Historical:** Wikipedia for timelines, academic dissertations for Romantic-era analysis
- **Data:** Pew/Skeptic Research Center surveys on Gen Z mental health identity, CDC data on depression prevalence

## Reading & Synthesis Approach
Discourse survey — mapping what people are saying across disciplines (cultural criticism, psychiatry, sociology, media studies). Not a clinical or technical survey.

## Limitations

**Anglophone and US-centric.** The survey covers English-language sources almost exclusively. Non-Western frameworks for depression — which may not map onto the Western aesthetic/medical binary — are entirely absent. A parallel survey of, say, East Asian or South Asian depression discourse would likely reveal a fundamentally different landscape, where the "fashionable" dimension may not apply at all, or may apply differently.

**Source ecosystem bias.** The literature collection is weighted toward cultural criticism and journalism (Substack, student journalism, magazines) rather than peer-reviewed empirical work. This reflects the nature of the topic — the aestheticization of depression is a cultural phenomenon better documented in cultural criticism than in clinical journals. But it means the empirical anchors (DBSA treatment data, Lancet meta-analysis, Assari's intersectional findings) carry disproportionate weight relative to their number.

**S7 thinness.** The Backlash section has fewer sources (5) than other sections, and is dominated by a single voice (Freya India). This reflects the recency of the backlash discourse — the critique is still emerging. A survey conducted in 2027 would likely find substantially more material.

**Net effect unknown.** The survey cannot determine whether fashionable despair increases or decreases total suffering. The Diana effect (doubled treatment-seeking) and the BetterHelp effect (30% less likely to try therapy) are both empirically supported, operate simultaneously, and do not cancel. The survey maps the structure of the discourse, not the arithmetic of its consequences.

**Single researcher, AI-assisted.** All literature search, reading, and synthesis was performed by one researcher using Claude as research assistant. This introduces both efficiency gains (broader coverage in less time) and risks (potential for synthesis errors in rapidly reviewed sources, possible blind spots from single-perspective curation).

## Reproducibility

The entire project is publicly accessible at `read.sadh.app/cog-sci/fashionable-despair/`. The literature collection (`literature-collection.md`) lists every source with URL, key claims, and date. Any reader can verify the sourcing by following the links. The methodology, changelog, and TODO are all public.

To replicate or extend:
1. Start with the literature collection as a baseline
2. Search for new sources in undercovered areas (S7 backlash, non-Western frameworks, empirical outcome data)
3. The deep-dive format (read-aid template) is reusable for additional sections
4. The survey paper's Wikipedia-style citation system is documented in the paper-drive skill

## What Broke

**Nothing catastrophic.** This was a clean build. Minor issues:

- **Source adequacy for S7** was borderline (5 sources). In a longer project, a dedicated search session for backlash material would have been warranted. The section works but is thinner than S1–S3.
- **Lit collection gaps noted but not filled:** Ficino primary text, Kristeva on melancholy, Waldman New Yorker piece, Shrier *Bad Therapy*, Lukianoff & Haidt — all flagged as gaps in the TODO but not pursued due to session time constraints. These would strengthen the survey if added.
- **The overview was built efficiently** because all 7 sections were completed first (anti-pattern #21 correctly avoided).
