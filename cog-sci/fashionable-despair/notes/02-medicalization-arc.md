# S2: The Medicalization Arc

> The DSM didn't just classify depression. It created a language that made depression claimable as identity.

**Sources:** 8 (Psychology Today/Prozac Culture, Springer/Before and After Prozac, Smardon/SAGE, Frances/Professional Counselor, WashPost/Prozac Nation 30 Years, UF Press/Rhetoric of Depression, Horwitz & Wakefield/Loss of Sadness, Wikipedia/Prozac Nation)
**Adequacy:** Adequate

## 1. The Pre-DSM Landscape: Melancholy to Neurosis

Before DSM-III (1980), depression existed in a therapeutic landscape inherited from psychoanalysis. Conditions were understood through the lens of the analyst-patient relationship, not symptom checklists. Terms like "neurosis," "neurasthenia," and "reactive depression" carried relational and contextual meaning — they described a person's situation, not a disease entity. The 19th century had its own pharmaceutical shortcuts (patent medicines, elixirs, tonics), but the conceptual framework remained person-centered rather than symptom-centered.

## 2. DSM-III and the Symptom Revolution (1980)

The publication of DSM-III in 1980 was the pivot. Hundreds of new or reconceptualized disorders appeared. The manual shifted from etiological (why you're suffering) to descriptive (what symptoms you show). Psychology Today: "various forms of antisocial and deviant behavior are redefined as psychiatric illnesses in need of treatment." The diagnostic criteria for depression became symptom-based — you need 5 of 9 symptoms for 2 weeks — without requiring clinicians to assess context or cause.

Horwitz & Wakefield's devastating critique: DSM abandoned "validity for the sake of reliability." The manual can reliably identify who has the symptoms but cannot validly distinguish between genuine disorder and "normal sadness" in response to loss. The one exception was bereavement — if you were grieving a death, your symptoms could be contextually excluded. But no other context (job loss, divorce, serious illness, poverty) received the same exemption.

Allen Frances (who chaired DSM-IV): DSM-5 made it worse — even the bereavement exclusion was removed, "transforming normal grief into major depressive disorder."

## 3. The Prozac Moment (1987–2000)

Prozac launched in 1987 and became the template for blockbuster psychiatric drugs. Peter Kramer's *Listening to Prozac* (1993) coined "cosmetic pharmacology" — the idea that medication could make you "better than well." Elizabeth Wurtzel's *Prozac Nation* (1994) turned depression-as-identity into a bestselling memoir.

Psychology Today: "Prozac culture is a therapeutic ethos of guiding beliefs and interventions. It is built on psychoactive medications and an ever-expanding medicalization of everyday life." One in six adult women takes an antidepressant. On some college campuses, 25% of students without a prescription take stimulants. Even preschoolers (age 2-3) receive psychiatric medication.

The 1990s memoir wave: Styron's *Darkness Visible* (1990), Kaysen's *Girl, Interrupted* (1993), Jamison's *An Unquiet Mind* (1995). These are genuine accounts of genuine suffering — but they also established depression-as-narrative, making the condition claimable as literary identity.

## 4. The Smardon Paradox: Narrative and Identity

Regina Smardon (2008): "narrative is the primary vehicle through which antidepressant consumers negotiate their sense of identity and reality." The DSM itself "tends to commodify illness." The patient becomes a consumer; the treatment becomes a product; the diagnosis becomes a story you tell about yourself. This is the medicalization of identity itself — not just the body or the mind, but the narrative self.

## 5. Depression as Rhetoric

The UF Press piece on Kramer's *Listening to Prozac* makes a crucial argument: "mental-illness diagnosis, as a rhetorical act and a speech act, shifts a problem from social conditions of precarity and inequity to personal conditions of pathology." When you diagnose an individual with depression, you locate the problem inside them. The social conditions that may have caused the suffering — poverty, discrimination, isolation, exploitation — become invisible. This is the privatization of suffering that S5 (therapy-speak) will explore further.

## 6. The Loss of Sadness

Horwitz & Wakefield (2007): "while depressive disorder certainly exists and can be a devastating condition warranting medical attention, the apparent epidemic in fact reflects the way the psychiatric profession has understood and reclassified normal human sadness as largely an abnormal experience." The DSM's symptom-based criteria cannot distinguish between a person with genuine clinical depression and a person experiencing intense but normal sadness after a major life event.

The "false positive" problem: overestimates of depression prevalence inflate the apparent epidemic, drive overprescription, and — crucially for this project — create a cultural context in which claiming depression becomes increasingly easy and socially available. If the diagnostic criteria are too broad, more people meet them; if more people meet them, depression becomes more normal; if depression becomes more normal, the stigma reduces; if the stigma reduces, more people claim the diagnosis. This is the medicalization feedback loop.

## 7. Drug Company Amplification

Frances: "The best way to sell pills is to promote disease-mongering." Pharma marketing targeted psychiatrists first, then primary care, then (since 1997 DTC advertising) the public directly. The result: "a pill-popping society" where use of medication has "skyrocketed" driven by "billion-dollar marketing budgets." This is commodification not of sadness but of its treatment — which creates economic incentives for expanding the definition of who counts as depressed.

## 8. What Sources Agree On
- DSM-III's symptom-based approach was a necessary correction to psychoanalytic vagueness but produced massive overdiagnosis
- Prozac (1987) and its successors created a pharmaceutical infrastructure that medicalized everyday distress
- The memoir wave of the 1990s established depression-as-narrative-identity
- Diagnosis functions as rhetoric: locating social problems inside individuals
- The bereavement exclusion was the DSM's one acknowledgment that context matters — and DSM-5 removed even that

## 9. Where Sources Disagree
- Whether overdiagnosis is the main problem (Horwitz & Wakefield, Frances) or undertreatment of genuine cases (APA mainstream)
- Whether Prozac was a net good (genuine relief for millions) or a net harm (normalizing medication for normal sadness)
- Whether the memoir tradition (Wurtzel, Styron) helped destigmatize depression or helped turn it into a literary brand
