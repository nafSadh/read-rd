# S3: Social Media Aesthetics

> The sad girl is not a person. She is a content format.

**Sources:** 10 (Daily Cal, Lifebonder, The Batt, Phase Learning, Trill Mag, Polytechnic Pawprint, Watch Us Rise, M-A Chronicle, Nylon/Wollen interview, Harvard Crimson, Dazed x2, INC Longform, i-D)
**Adequacy:** Strong — upgraded from 8 to 10 with Wollen material

---

## 1. Genealogy: Tumblr to TikTok

The sad girl aesthetic has a clear platform lineage. On Tumblr (2010–2016), it emerged organically: teenagers posting stream-of-consciousness thoughts, Lana Del Rey lyrics, black-and-white photos with sad captions. Key visual motifs: cigarettes, drugs, runny mascara, vintage filters, rainy weather, serene landscapes. Mina Le (cited in The Batt): the early phase "felt more like coincidence than a calculated attempt to aestheticize our own identities."

The shift happens when it migrates to TikTok and Instagram. On Tumblr, you posted into a void. On TikTok, you perform for an algorithm. The Batt: "depressed girl aesthetic" is its own auto-fill on TikTok — over 17.6 million posts. The Carolinian (UNC Greensboro): #sadgirl has over 16.1 billion views on TikTok; #Tumblr has 6.8 billion views.

Key transformation: from expression to production. On Tumblr, being sad was something you documented. On TikTok, being sad is something you produce — with editing, music selection, caption writing, and aesthetic curation. The content pipeline requires not just feeling sad but performing sadness in the correct visual register.

## 2. The Visual Grammar

The sad girl aesthetic has specific, documented visual rules:

- **Color**: Greyscale or muted pastels; the coquette micro-trend (bows, pastels, sad expression)
- **Music**: Lana Del Rey ("Pretty When You Cry," "Sad Girl"), Billie Eilish, Fiona Apple, Lorde, boygenius
- **Literary references**: Sylvia Plath, Oscar Wilde, Donna Tartt
- **Film**: Sofia Coppola (The Virgin Suicides, Marie Antoinette), Skins (Effy Stonem, Cassie Ainsworth), Euphoria (Rue)
- **Beauty**: "Crying girl makeup" — blush-heavy, mimicking post-cry glow. Bella Hadid's crying selfies as trend catalyst
- **Products**: Spotify's "sad girl starter pack" (1.5M likes); "Sad Girl Winter" candle; sad girl Etsy merch

The M-A Chronicle (Celeste Zucker): "When turned into an aesthetic — a beautiful, visualized style — the Sad Girl becomes an intentional performance of pain, not a reality of mental health issues."

## 3. Audrey Wollen and Sad Girl Theory

The most intellectually ambitious framing comes from Audrey Wollen (CalArts, now CUNY PhD), who coined "Sad Girl Theory" in 2014. Core claim: female sadness should be "witnessed and re-historicized as an act of resistance, of political protest." Girls being sad has been "categorized as this act of passivity, and therefore, discounted from the history of activism."

Wollen's lineage: Judy Garland, Sylvia Plath, Ana Mendieta, Lana Del Rey, Frida Kahlo, Virginia Woolf. She uses Instagram deliberately — "I'm having a conversation about Eve Kosofsky Sedgwick with a 14-year-old girl in Kansas."

KEY TENSION: Wollen's theory treats sadness as agency. The aesthetic that borrowed her language treats it as commodity. She eventually deleted her Instagram. The INC Longform piece traces how commercial institutions stripped Wollen's irony and self-awareness, turning activism into "postfeminism" — "when a message of empowerment is commercialized, it is suggested that confidence can be bought with the right products."

Harvard Crimson (Amber Levis): "Simply put, the Sad Girl reeks of complacency... rather than challenging the systems that oppress her, the Sad Girl beautifies her suffering." This is the exact opposite of Wollen's intention.

## 4. The Whiteness Problem

Multiple sources converge on this: the sad girl aesthetic is overwhelmingly white, thin, and conventionally beautiful.

Daily Cal: "The aesthetic is overwhelmingly dominated by people who are white and attractive, which can make girls who don't fit that category feel even more alienated, or like they can't be sad in the 'good' way."

Harvard Crimson (Levis): The Sad Girl "represents a very white kind of beauty."

Watch Us Rise: The aesthetic has a "heroin chic beauty standard."

This connects directly to S1's finding (fashionable melancholy was always class-filtered) and to S6 (Who Gets to Be Sad).

## 5. The Algorithmic Feedback Loop

The Trill Mag first-person account is the most honest source on what the algorithm does to actual depressed people. The author describes how TikTok's algorithm picked up on her depression and "inundated" her with sad girl content. Initially comforting, then dangerous:

"What if I get better and I lose my beauty? What if I become boring? I've certainly been afraid of this. If my aesthetic is of the #sadgirl, then what aesthetic will I have if I'm no longer sad?"

This is the recovery paradox: when depression becomes identity/aesthetic, recovery threatens not just your condition but your self-concept and social position. The algorithm reinforces this by feeding you more of what you engage with, creating a loop where consuming sadness content generates more sadness content in your feed.

## 6. The Gender Asymmetry

Lucy Dacus (boygenius): "Sadness can be meaningful, but I got a bone to pick with the 'sad girl indie' genre, not the music that gets labeled as that, but the classification and commodification and perpetual expectation of women's pain."

M-A Chronicle: "Sad music by men is portrayed as cathartic, intentional vulnerability, while sad music by women is trivialized and subject to romanticization."

Wollen herself: "We get stuck in a catch 22: if you act 'strong,' you're a bitch; if you act emotional you're weak and pathetic."

## 7. The CDC Numbers

Behind the aesthetic, the numbers are real. CDC data: 57% of teenage girls reported feeling persistently sad or hopeless in 2021, up from 36% in 2011. UCLA Psychology Professor Francis Steen: "If you're feeling sad, maybe it's comforting to some degree to see that you're not alone... But there's a limit to that."

The question the aesthetic raises but cannot answer: Is the aesthetic a response to real suffering (destigmatization) or a contributor to it (romanticization)? The honest answer is almost certainly both, simultaneously.

## 8. What Sources Agree On
- The Tumblr → TikTok pipeline transformed sad girl culture from expression to production
- The aesthetic is overwhelmingly white, thin, and conventionally attractive
- Audrey Wollen's intellectual framing was stripped of nuance by commercial adoption
- The algorithm creates a feedback loop between consuming and producing sadness content
- There is a genuine epidemic of teen girl sadness underneath the aesthetic

## 9. Where Sources Disagree
- Whether Wollen's Sad Girl Theory was always vulnerable to co-optation or whether the internet stripped it of context
- Whether the aesthetic helps (community, visibility) or harms (romanticization, identity fusion)
- Whether Plath/Del Rey/Coppola bear responsibility for the aesthetic or are merely conscripted into it posthumously
- Whether the sad girl aesthetic is feminist (reclaiming female pain) or anti-feminist (beautifying the conditions that produce it)
