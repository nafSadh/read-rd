# S1: Historical Melancholy

> The idea that sadness is interesting — and that interesting people are sad — is not a TikTok invention. It is at least 2,400 years old.

**Sources:** 7 (Wikipedia Melancholia, Zimmermann, Frink thesis, Springer Melancholy Experience, Academia.edu Keats/Byron, Babbitt/Tradition Restored, One Alkaline Life)
**Adequacy:** Adequate

## 1. The Humoral Foundation: Aristotle's Fateful Question

Melancholy begins as pathology — an excess of "black bile" (μέλαινα χολή) in the Hippocratic system. Symptoms: persistent fears, despondency, poor appetite, sleeplessness, irritability. Six of nine map directly onto modern DSM criteria for major depression. But then Aristotle (or pseudo-Aristotle) asks a question that changes everything: "Why is it that all men who have become outstanding in philosophy, statesmanship, poetry, or the arts are melancholic?" (Problemata XXX.1). This single sentence creates a 2,400-year feedback loop: if genius requires suffering, then suffering becomes evidence of genius.

## 2. Ficino's Alchemy: Vice to Virtue

The medieval period treats melancholy as sin (tristitia in Pope Gregory I's vice list) or demonic possession. Marsilio Ficino (1433–1499) reverses this entirely. Drawing on Neoplatonism, Ficino "transformed what had hitherto been regarded as the most calamitous of all the humours into the mark of genius" (Roy Strong, 1964). This is the critical pivot: melancholy goes from something that happens TO you to something that signals WHO you are. By the late 16th century, "the attitudes of melancholy soon became an indispensable adjunct to all those with artistic or intellectual pretentions."

## 3. Burton and the First Literature of Fashionable Sadness

Robert Burton's *The Anatomy of Melancholy* (1621) is both medical treatise and literary monument — 900+ pages that treat melancholy as the universal condition of being human. It is, in effect, the first attempt to make depression interesting at book length. Edward Young's *Night-Thoughts* (1742–45) carries this into verse, hugely popular across several languages. Dürer's *Melencolia I* (1514) turns the concept visual: melancholy as the state of waiting for inspiration, not as affliction. These works don't just describe melancholy — they perform it, making the pose available for imitation.

## 4. Romantic Melancholy: The Genius Cult at Full Power

The Romantic period represents peak fashionable melancholy. Key moves:
- Keats's "Ode on Melancholy" (1820): Don't flee sadness — go TO it, dwell in it, find beauty in its transience. Melancholy "dwells with Beauty — Beauty that must die."
- Byron: Ironic detachment masking deep emotional undercurrents. Don Juan juxtaposes melancholic imagery with humor. Byron's public persona IS the melancholic genius.
- Goethe's *Sorrows of Young Werther* (1774): Triggered a wave of copycat suicides across Europe. The first viral sad boy content.
- Baudelaire's "spleen": Distinguished forms of dejection, deconstructing melancholia into refined categories of suffering.

Irving Babbitt (1919): "No movement has perhaps been so prolific of melancholy as emotional romanticism. To follow it from Rousseau down to the present day is to run through the whole gamut of gloom." The Romantic poet writes for "the relief of his heart" — he is "like the pelican who rends and lacerates his own flesh to provide nourishment for his young."

Thomas Pfau: Melancholy stems from "an excess of knowledge that exceeds representation, leading to feelings of futility." This epistemic framing — depression as seeing TOO clearly — is the Romantic version of what Sadh's retrocausality project calls depression being "structurally correct."

## 5. The Class Dimension: Who Got to Be Melancholic

This is not a universal experience. Fashionable melancholy was explicitly for the elite. 18th-century writers noted that "the well-to-do, the sedentary, and the studious were even more liable to be placed" in the melancholic category. Matthew Bell argues the condition is "fundamentally associated with a Western understanding of self-consciousness." If you were a laborer, you were tired. If you were a poet, you were melancholic. The class filter on whose sadness counts as interesting predates TikTok by centuries.

## 6. The End of Fashionable Melancholy — And Its Ghost

Philippe Pinel (late 18th–early 19th c.) breaks with both humoral doctrine and the Renaissance dual character of melancholy. He locates it only among mental alienation — stripping away the genius association. Esquirol explicitly refuses to attribute "that particular superiority of talent" to melancholy. This is the medicalization turn: melancholy stops being interesting and becomes illness. BUT — as the Springer volume notes — "over the years, varied analogies, metaphors, images and ideas amassed around this notion of melancholy, and those suffering from it had powerful means to speak and write about their experiences." When melancholy was discarded as a diagnostic entity in the 20th century, "this rich language, unfortunately, also disappeared from the public eye."

The loss of that language may be part of why it keeps being reinvented.

## 7. What Sources Agree On
- Melancholy has been aestheticized for at least 2,400 years
- The Ficino pivot (vice → genius) is the foundational move
- The Romantic period is the peak of fashionable sadness in Western culture
- Fashionable melancholy was always a class/gender phenomenon — available primarily to educated white men
- The medicalization of melancholy in the 19th century killed the fashionable version — temporarily

## 8. Where Sources Disagree
- Whether the Romantic experience of melancholy is philosophically distinct from modern depression (Keats scholars say yes; clinical researchers say no meaningful difference)
- Whether Ficino's move was liberating (freeing genius from shame) or destructive (glamorizing suffering) — the same tension that haunts today's destigmatization debate
- Whether "fashionable melancholy" is a continuous tradition or multiple independent reinventions
