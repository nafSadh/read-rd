STOICISM IN PRACTICE · SURVEY PAPER · 34 SOURCES

Between Epictetus and Instagram

This survey examines the alignment between ancient Stoic practices and modern cognitive science through the reappraisal-suppression framework, and considers how popular interpretations of Stoicism may diverge from both the philosophical tradition and its empirical basis.

2024 6 Dimensions 34 Sources 5 Tensions
ABSTRACT

Abstract

Stoicism has become a prominent philosophy within the internet self-improvement ecosystem, evidenced by 257,000 TikTok videos[1]TX2 Paideia Institute → accumulating 2.5 billion views. This analysis investigates the implications of examining ancient Stoic practices through the lens of modern cognitive science. Drawing on 54 sources across neuroscience, clinical psychology, cultural criticism, and philosophy, the analysis finds that the distinction between cognitive reappraisal and expressive suppression — established by Gross & John (2003)[2]CM3 Gross & John → — aligns closely with the difference between authentic Stoic practice and its divergent popular interpretations.

The central argument posits that authentic Stoic teachings (examining impressions before assenting) correspond to cognitive reappraisal, a process that engages the prefrontal cortex to modulate amygdala reactivity and is associated with measurable well-being improvements. Conversely, popular interpretations of "stoicism" often advocate practices (such as hiding emotions and enduring without processing) that align with expressive suppression, a mechanism that maintains amygdala activity while masking behavioral output and is associated with less favorable mental health outcomes. While both approaches may present an outward appearance of calm, their internal psychological effects on well-being differ significantly. The dissemination within popular psychology — mirroring the trajectory observed with "McMindfulness"[3]PP5 Purser: McMindfulness → where Buddhist ethical frameworks were separated from mindfulness practices — appears to favor the suppression message. It has been argued that the constraints of short-form media may favor the dissemination of suppression-based messages over the more complex cognitive reappraisal framing, though this hypothesis has not yet been tested through systematic content analysis.

SECTION 01

The Reappraisal-Suppression Framework

A key observation from this six-dimensional analysis is that the distinction between classical Stoic principles and certain maladaptive interpretations of Stoicism aligns with a well-established psychological framework. Gross and John (2003)[2]CM3 Gross & John → identified two emotion regulation strategies with distinct outcomes. Cognitive reappraisal involves altering one's interpretation of a situation before an emotional response fully consolidates, a process conceptually aligned with Epictetus's counsel to examine impressions before assenting to them. Expressive suppression, conversely, involves inhibiting the outward manifestation of an emotion after it has already been experienced. Certain popular cultural narratives, such as those associated with 'man up' culture or some popular Stoic content, often emphasize or inadvertently promote expressive suppression.

In six studies, habitual reappraisers reported greater positive emotion, less negative emotion, better interpersonal functioning, and higher well-being. Habitual suppressors reported the opposite on every measure, plus self-reported feelings of inauthenticity that were associated with eroded relationship quality over time. Butler et al. (2003)[4]CM4 Butler et al. → demonstrated that suppression even disrupts partners physiologically: when one person suppresses during conversation, the other's blood pressure increases.

This framework offers an explanatory lens for several observed phenomena: the reported well-being improvements among Stoic Week participants (consistent with the practice of reappraisal); the association of certain hyper-masculine interpretations of Stoicism with negative psychological outcomes (potentially linked to an emphasis on suppression); the efficacy of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) through its alignment with Stoic principles in targeting cognitive appraisal; and the potential risks associated with certain popular psychology approaches that may inadvertently promote suppression by de-emphasizing the cognitive work inherent in reappraisal.

SECTION 02

The Neural Evidence

The reappraisal-suppression distinction is not merely behavioral — it has a specific neural signature. A meta-analysis by Buhle et al. (2014)[6]CM1 Buhle et al. Meta-Analysis → synthesized findings from 48 fMRI studies of cognitive reappraisal. The results demonstrated consistent patterns: reappraisal activates dorsolateral, ventrolateral, and dorsomedial prefrontal cortex while attenuating bilateral amygdala activation. These neural patterns align with philosophical descriptions, such as the Stoic concept of achieving equanimity through reason.

A 2021 meta-analysis[7]CM2 Amygdala-PFC Connectivity → found convergent functional connectivity between amygdala and ventrolateral PFC during reappraisal. The coupling strength between these regions predicts individual differences in regulation success, suggesting a potential for neural plasticity and training of this circuit. Amygdala-OFC connectivity[8]CM6 Individual Differences → predicts the degree of negative affect attenuation. This finding has been interpreted as providing a neurobiological basis for the "muscle" metaphor sometimes applied to practices involving emotional regulation.

Further evidence emerged in 2023 from a combined TMS-fMRI study[9]CM5 TMS-fMRI Causal Evidence →, which investigated causal relationships rather than mere correlations. Stimulating the ventrolateral PFC enhanced reappraisal performance, increased vlPFC and vmPFC activity, and attenuated amygdala and insula responses. The PFC appears to play a causal role in the modulation of the reappraisal process, beyond mere correlation. This finding indicates a mechanistic role for the PFC in successful reappraisal and has been interpreted as providing a neurobiological rationale for the emphasis on regular practice in disciplines that aim to cultivate emotional regulation, such as Stoicism, by suggesting the training of relevant neural circuits.

SECTION 03

The Evidence Landscape

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), a prominent evidence-based psychotherapy, has documented explicit Stoic roots. Albert Ellis[12]EB1 Ellis: Stoic Root of CBT → developed Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT) with foundations in Epictetus's philosophy. Aaron Beck[13]EB2 Big Think: Ancient Roots → referenced Marcus Aurelius in his treatment manual. The direct acknowledgments by both founders suggest that CBT's established evidence base provides indirect support for certain Stoic principles.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy[14]EB6 ACT Narrative Review → integrates the Stoic dichotomy of control with principles of Buddhist acceptance. The Stoic Attitudes and Behaviours Scale[15]SP4 LeBon et al. SABS → (LeBon et al.) represents the first validated measure of philosophical Stoicism, having been administered to over 8,000 participants across 116 countries. SABS scores demonstrate a positive correlation with flourishing and resilience, a pattern distinct from colloquial "stoicism" scales that measure emotional suppression.

Stoic Week[16]SP2 Stoic Week Report → reported 1,424 completers in its most recently published cycle, marking over a decade of consecutive years with observed improvements in participant well-being. Earlier iterations[17]SP7 Stoic Week 2018 → indicated a 14% reduction in negative emotions, a 10% increase in positive emotions, and a 13% increase in life satisfaction. However, these results are subject to methodological limitations, including self-selection bias, the absence of a control group, and a 25% completion rate.

Evidence from related therapeutic components sometimes draws on more controlled study designs. For instance, a journaling meta-analysis[18]EB10 Journaling Efficacy → found 68% of interventions to be effective. A gratitude meta-analysis[19]EB9 Gratitude Meta-Analysis → reported 6.86% higher life satisfaction and 7.76% lower anxiety. However, these studies validate individual components rather than the integrated Stoic practice. No randomized controlled trial (RCT) has yet tested complete Stoic practice against CBT or a waitlist control.

Tension: Components vs. Package

Most evidence supports individual components of Stoic practice, such as reappraisal, gratitude, journaling, and acceptance. However, empirical support for the integrated Stoic package is currently limited. The potential synergistic effects of these components within a comprehensive Stoic practice remain to be rigorously tested.

SECTION 04

Misapplied Stoicism

A common pattern in misapplied Stoicism involves a conflation of the reappraisal-suppression distinction. While both may manifest as outward calm, reappraisal necessitates active cognitive engagement, including examining impressions, questioning interpretations, and generating alternative framings. Popular interpretations of Stoicism often simplify or omit this cognitive work, which can lead individuals to default to the more immediate strategy of emotional suppression.

Hyper-masculine interpretations of Stoicism within certain digital self-improvement subcultures — sometimes described in cultural criticism as "Broicism"[20]TX1 Trill Mag: Broicism → — selectively appropriate Stoic philosophy by redefining emotional suppression as strength while often omitting core Stoic tenets such as virtue, cosmopolitan compassion, and social duty. Research, such as a systematic review [21], indicates that traditional masculinity norms emphasizing emotional stoicism are associated with risky coping behaviors and reduced mental health help-seeking.

The ancient Stoics themselves drew a careful distinction that is entirely absent from popular presentations. Pathos referred to destructive passions based on false judgments. Eupatheiai referred to appropriate emotional responses arising from correct judgments. The goal of apatheia was freedom from destructive passions — not emotional flatness. Seneca wept for friends. Marcus Aurelius expressed deep affection for his teachers. Sharpe's analysis[22]TX7 Sharpe: Stoicism and Mourning → of the Stoic consolation tradition documents significant divergences between contemporary popular presentations and the philosophical tradition.

The dichotomy of control[23]TX8 Tremblay: Misunderstandings → is a Stoic concept with significant potential for both beneficial application and detrimental misapplication. When properly applied, it directs focus and energy toward an individual's agency. However, when improperly applied, it may be used to suggest that individuals bear sole responsibility for outcomes partly determined by systemic factors — for instance, that an individual cannot control systemic issues such as racism and should therefore not seek to address them. Marcus Aurelius emphasized social duty, indicating that the dichotomy was not intended as a justification for political quietism.

SECTION 05

The Commercialization Trajectory

The commercialization of Stoicism exhibits parallels with the trajectory Ron Purser documented for mindfulness in McMindfulness.[24]PP6 McMindfulness (Book) → This trajectory often involves a progression where rigorous philosophical practice transitions into a clinical tool, then a popular book, subsequently social media content, and potentially a simplified or decontextualized form. At various stages of this process, there is a risk of diminishing the ethical framework (e.g., virtue as the sole good), the communal dimension (e.g., cosmopolitan brotherhood), and the intellectual rigor (e.g., logic, physics, dialectic) inherent in the original philosophy.

Ryan Holiday[25]PP4 Holiday (Wikipedia) → has established a highly commercially successful brand associated with Stoicism. In Silicon Valley, Stoicism has been adopted[26]PP3 Quartz: Silicon Valley → as a tool for productivity, often emphasizing the dichotomy of control while frequently de-emphasizing or omitting its traditional critique of excessive wealth. Academic analysis of TikTok Stoicism[27]TX9 The Conversation: TikTok → concludes the platform promotes disinformation about the philosophy.

The influence of commercial incentives suggests that this commercialization trajectory is challenging to prevent entirely. Pigliucci[28]PP8 Pigliucci (Wikipedia) →'s approach attempts to balance accessibility with academic rigor, representing one model for how popular and scholarly treatment of Stoicism need not be mutually exclusive. An alternative strategy involves creating accessible content that maintains scholarly fidelity to the comprehensive demands of Stoic philosophy, rather than resorting to gatekeeping.

SECTION 06

Clinical Integration

Donald Robertson[29]EB3 Robertson: Stoic Philosophy as CBT → has developed a comprehensive framework connecting Stoicism and clinical psychology. His Philosophy of CBT[30]EB4 Robertson: Philosophy of CBT → provides a scholarly mapping of Stoic exercises to therapeutic protocols. Robertson argues that Stoicism may produce more lasting effects than standard CBT due to its provision of a comprehensive philosophy of life, rather than solely coping techniques, emphasizing identity integration over mere technique application.

James Stockdale[31]IC4 Stockdale: Courage Under Fire →'s experience as a prisoner of war (POW) represents a well-documented case study of resilience: enduring 7.5 years as a POW, including four years in isolation and fifteen instances of torture, sustained by the philosophy of Epictetus. While not a clinical trial, and subject to survivorship bias given the absence of records for POWs who applied similar techniques without surviving, this case suggests that the application of Stoic principles can contribute to psychological coherence during extreme adversity. The observation that "optimists died first" (the "Stockdale Paradox") indicates that survivors combined a radical acceptance of brutal reality with a steadfast commitment to virtue.

The U.S. Army's $145M Comprehensive Soldier Fitness program[32]IC6 US Army CSF → and Master Resilience Training[33]IC7 MRT → incorporate principles from cognitive-behavioral and positive psychology that align with Stoic philosophy. The Center for Junior Officers[34]IC8 Stoic Readiness → explicitly references Stoic philosophy as a foundation for building readiness.

SECTION 07

Synthesis

Five tensions define the current landscape of empirical Stoicism. Each represents a genuine unresolved question where the evidence does not yet dictate a clear answer.

Reappraisal vs. suppression. Neuroscientific research indicates that cognitive reappraisal is associated with improved well-being, whereas expressive suppression is linked to adverse outcomes. Popular interpretations of Stoicism may conflate these two processes due to their similar outward manifestations of calmness. Presentations of Stoicism that do not adequately emphasize the cognitive examination step may risk defaulting to practices akin to suppression, irrespective of the philosophical terminology employed.

Philosophy vs. therapy. The classification of Stoicism as a philosophy implies an engagement with its comprehensive tenets, encompassing virtue, cosmopolitanism, and social duty. Conversely, its consideration as a therapeutic modality suggests evaluation through randomized controlled trial (RCT) standards. The development of instruments such as the SABS[15]SP4 SABS Validation → facilitates the feasibility of clinical trials. Prior to such trials, existing evidence primarily supports underlying mechanisms rather than the efficacy of specific Stoic integrations.

Evidence quality. While Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is supported by hundreds of RCTs, interventions specifically labeled as "Stoic" currently rely on observational data from initiatives like Stoic Week and the SABS, which are promising but not yet conclusive. A single, well-designed RCT could significantly advance the evidence base for Stoic interventions, potentially catalyzing a shift in the field's empirical foundation.

Dichotomy of control. The dichotomy of control functions as a significant attentional framework, yet its application to systemic problems carries the risk of being misinterpreted as a means of assigning blame to individuals. Classical Stoic authors such as Marcus Aurelius wrote extensively on social duty, suggesting that political engagement was integral to the tradition; however, contemporary popular interpretations may inadvertently de-emphasize such dimensions.

Accessibility paradox. Popularized Stoicism achieves broad dissemination but often at the expense of philosophical nuance. In contrast, academic Stoicism maintains rigor but has a more limited reach. The trajectory observed with the decontextualization of mindfulness practices suggests that accessibility and accuracy are not inherently mutually exclusive, although commercial pressures may incentivize simplification.

The Central Finding

The reappraisal-suppression framework serves as a central analytical lens for understanding the current landscape of empirical Stoicism. This framework provides an explanation for the potential therapeutic benefits associated with philosophically-grounded Stoic practices, as well as the adverse outcomes observed in research linked to certain popular Stoic interpretations. It also suggests that addressing these issues requires more than mere simplification. The cognitive engagement inherent in Stoic philosophy constitutes a fundamental aspect of its practice. Without this cognitive component, the practice risks resembling suppression, albeit under a Stoic designation.

SECTION 08

Methods & Limitations

This survey draws on 34 sources across six dimensions: the science of Stoic practice (9 sources), cognitive mechanisms (8), evidence-based Stoicism (10), misuse and misapplied Stoicism (9), the commercialization trajectory (8), and clinical integration (10). Note: the methodology section's dimensional source counts sum to 54; the reference list contains 34 numbered entries, reflecting consolidation of duplicates and removal of unverifiable future-dated citations. Sources include peer-reviewed primary research, practitioner literature, and cultural criticism.

Several limitations are present. The cultural criticism sections (S4, S5) rely heavily on journalistic and opinion sources rather than systematic studies. No primary Stoic texts are cited directly; the project discusses Epictetus, Seneca, and Marcus Aurelius extensively through secondary commentary. The harm claims are based on a single empirical study of masculinity norms and general suppression research. No systematic content analysis of social media Stoic accuracy has been conducted, and no longitudinal data on harm from misapplied Stoicism exists.

Future research directions include dedicated RCTs comparing Stoic practice to CBT, fMRI studies of experienced Stoic practitioners, longitudinal harm data from misapplied Stoicism, and systematic content analysis of social media Stoic accuracy. The SABS and the reappraisal-suppression framework provide the conceptual and measurement infrastructure. Empirical work in these areas remains to be conducted.
