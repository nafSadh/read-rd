# Notes — S1: The Science of Stoic Practice

## Key Facts
- Stoic Week: 13th year in 2025, 1,424 completers from 3,650 signups
- 2017-2018 data: 14% reduction in negative emotions, 10% increase in positive emotions, 13% increase in life satisfaction — after just one week
- SABS (Stoic Attitudes and Behaviours Scale): first scientifically validated measure of philosophical Stoicism, published in Cognitive Therapy and Research (LeBon et al. 2025)
- SABS: 8,000+ participants across 116 countries, 7-factor 40-item solution
- SABS scores positively correlated with flourishing, resilience, positive affect; negatively with anger and negative emotions
- Key distinction: SABS measures *philosophical* Stoicism, not colloquial "being stoic" (which correlates with *worse* outcomes)
- Frontiers 2025 paper on "Stoicism, mindfulness, and the brain" — empirical foundations of second-order desires
- SMRT (Stoic Mindfulness and Resilience Training) course collects longer-term outcome data

## Specific Practices Studied
1. **Morning reflection** (prosoche) — intention-setting, what is "up to me" today
2. **Evening reflection** (Seneca's nightly review) — what went well, what could improve
3. **Negative visualization** (premeditatio malorum) — imagining loss to increase appreciation
4. **View from above** (perspective exercise) — zooming out to reduce attachment
5. **Dichotomy of control** (applied throughout day) — sorting controllable from uncontrollable

## Limitations
- Stoic Week is self-selected, no control group, online-only, one-week duration
- Most "evidence" for Stoicism is actually evidence for CBT/reappraisal/gratitude — practices that overlap with but are not identical to Stoicism
- SABS is newly validated — needs replication and longitudinal follow-up
- Negative visualization: can backfire if done excessively (rumination risk)
- No RCTs specifically testing "Stoic practice package" vs active control
