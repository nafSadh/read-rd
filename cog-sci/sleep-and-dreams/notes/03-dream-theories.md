# S3: Dream Theories — Section Notes

## Core Argument
Dream science has at least six competing theoretical frameworks, none clearly dominant. The field has moved from Freud's psychological theories through Hobson's neurological reductionism to modern attempts at synthesis through predictive processing. The hard problem of dream consciousness — why subjective experience exists during sleep at all — remains unanswered.

## Major Theories

### 1. Freud's Wish Fulfillment (1900)
- Dreams express repressed unconscious wishes in disguised form
- Manifest content (what you remember) vs. latent content (hidden meaning)
- Dream-work: condensation, displacement, symbolization
- **Status**: Debunked as scientific theory, but historically foundational
- The concept of dreams as meaningful psychological events (not random noise) has been vindicated

### 2. Activation-Synthesis (Hobson & McCarley, 1977)
- Random PGO (ponto-geniculo-occipital) spikes activate during REM
- Forebrain tries to synthesize coherent narrative from random input
- Dream bizarreness = result of random activation, not disguised meaning
- **Evolution**: AIM model added input-output gating and chemical modulation dimensions
- **Status**: Influential but too reductionist — doesn't explain emotional coherence of dreams

### 3. Threat Simulation Theory (Revonsuo, 2000)
- Dreams evolved to simulate threatening scenarios
- Rehearsal in safe environment improves real-world threat response
- Predicts: dreams should be negatively biased (they are — ~65% negative emotions)
- **Status**: Explains negativity bias but not why many dreams are mundane or positive

### 4. Continual-Activation Theory (Zhang, 2005)
- Dreams result from brain maintaining neural pathways during sleep
- Memory consolidation requires periodic activation
- Dreams are the conscious experience of this maintenance process

### 5. Default Mode Network Theory
- DMN active during both mind-wandering (wake) and dreaming (REM)
- Dreams may be the sleeping version of spontaneous thought
- Explains why dream content often resembles daydreaming themes

### 6. Predictive Processing (Hobson & Friston)
- Brain constantly generates predictions about sensory input
- During REM, sensory input is minimal — brain relies entirely on internal predictions
- Dreams = "hallucinations" produced by the prediction machinery running without input correction
- May explain why dreams feel real despite being bizarre
- **ARRM model (2025)**: Unifies as Associative-Rehearsal & Reframing — dreams as structured adaptive process

## The Hard Problem of Dream Consciousness
- Why is there subjective experience during sleep at all?
- Dreams are emotionally vivid but logically incoherent — different mode of consciousness
- Prefrontal cortex (executive function, logic) is deactivated during REM
- This explains lack of critical thinking in dreams — you accept bizarre events as normal
- Lucid dreaming (S4) is the exception: prefrontal reactivation enables metacognition

## Open Questions
- Do dreams serve a function, or are they an epiphenomenon of sleep processes?
- Why do some dreams incorporate recent memories (day residue) while others don't?
- Why is emotional content preserved while logical consistency is not?
- Can dream content be reliably decoded from brain activity? (Early fMRI work suggests yes, partially)
