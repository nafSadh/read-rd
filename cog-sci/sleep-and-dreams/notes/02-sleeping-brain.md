# S2: The Sleeping Brain — Section Notes

## Core Argument
The sleeping brain is far from inactive. Three major processes happen during sleep: memory consolidation via hippocampal replay, metabolic waste clearance via the glymphatic system, and synaptic recalibration via the homeostasis hypothesis. Each represents a different level of analysis — circuits, fluid dynamics, and synapses.

## Memory Consolidation
- **Hippocampal replay**: During NREM sleep, the hippocampus replays experiences from the day at compressed timescales
- **Sleep spindles**: 12-16 Hz bursts during Stage 2 NREM, serve as "file transfer" mechanism from hippocampus to neocortex
- **Sharp-wave ripples**: Brief high-frequency oscillations in hippocampus, coordinated with spindles and slow waves
- **Two-stage model**: Encoding during wake (hippocampus) → consolidation during sleep (neocortex)

## The Glymphatic System
- Discovered by Maiken Nedergaard (University of Rochester), named by analogy with lymphatic system
- Brain-wide clearance pathway using CSF flowing through perivascular channels
- Interstitial space expands ~60% during sleep, facilitating waste removal
- Clears amyloid-beta (Alzheimer's protein) and tau
- **2024 breakthrough**: Norepinephrine oscillations every ~50 seconds during NREM drive vascular pulsations that pump CSF (Cell, 2024)
- First human demonstration: 5 surgery patients with MRI (2024-2025)
- Zolpidem (Ambien) suppresses these oscillations — meaning sedation ≠ real sleep for waste clearance

## Controversy
- Mechanism specifics still debated — some researchers question whether perivascular flow is the primary clearance route
- Human evidence limited (5 surgical patients)
- "Glymphatic" framing may oversimplify a more complex set of clearance mechanisms
- The Transmitter (2025): new methods reignite controversy

## Synaptic Homeostasis Hypothesis (Tononi & Cirelli)
- Waking strengthens synapses (learning creates new connections)
- This is energetically costly and eventually saturates
- During NREM sleep, slow waves globally downscale synaptic strength
- "Fittest" circuits (repeatedly strengthened, well-integrated) survive the pruning
- Analogy: sleep is to synapses what pruning is to plants
- Prediction: synaptic strength should be higher at end of waking day than at start

## REM Paralysis
- During REM, the brainstem actively paralyzes voluntary muscles (atonia)
- Prevents acting out dreams — failure of this mechanism = REM behavior disorder
- Glycine and GABA inhibit motor neurons in spinal cord
- Only eye muscles and diaphragm are spared (enabling eye-signal communication in lucid dreaming)
