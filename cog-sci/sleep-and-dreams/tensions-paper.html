SLEEP & DREAMS · TENSIONS PAPER · 6 CONTRADICTIONS

Six Tensions

Where credible sources, examining the same evidence, reach opposing conclusions about the science of sleep and dreams. These contradictions define the frontier of the field.

March 2026 12 Sources 6 Tensions
INTRODUCTION

Why Tensions Matter

Sleep science is characterized by a diversity of findings and theoretical perspectives, leading to ongoing debates regarding fundamental aspects of the field. Humans spend approximately one-third of their lives in a state whose fundamental purpose remains contested. Dreams fill nocturnal experience with subjective phenomena whose function — if any — is debated across at least six competing theoretical frameworks. Even practical questions, such as individual sleep requirements, the effects of screen exposure on sleep, and the clinical utility of pharmacological interventions, receive varied answers from equally credible researchers.

This paper identifies six tensions within the Sleep & Dreams survey. These are characterized as unresolved disagreements stemming not from insufficient data, but from fundamentally different interpretive frameworks applied to existing evidence.

TENSION 01

The Core Function Problem

THE PARADOX
Does sleep have one purpose or many?

The 2012 discovery of the glymphatic system significantly influenced subsequent research directions, offering a candidate unifying explanation: that the primary function of sleep is metabolic waste clearance from the brain. The 2024 Cell paper identifying the norepinephrine-driven waste pump provided significant evidence for the waste-clearance hypothesis. Yet synaptic homeostasis, memory consolidation, immune restoration, and energy conservation all occur during sleep too — and none reduces to waste clearance. The question of whether sleep has a single core function or is irreducibly multi-purpose remains a central conceptual problem in the field.

Single core function

Glymphatic clearance as the unifying explanation. NE oscillations drive CSF flow. 60% interstitial expansion. Amyloid-beta clearance links sleep to Alzheimer’s prevention. Nedergaard (2012), Cell (2024).

vs

Multi-purpose state

Synaptic homeostasis (Tononi & Cirelli). Memory consolidation (hippocampal replay). Immune function. Energy conservation. Emotional regulation. No single theory accounts for all observations.

What Would Resolution Look Like?

If glymphatic clearance is truly the core function, we should be able to show that animals with more efficient clearance mechanisms sleep less, and that the other functions of sleep are secondary consequences of the clearance process. If sleep is irreducibly multi-functional, then no single manipulation — enhancing waste clearance, supplementing memory consolidation, boosting immune function — should be able to substitute for sleep itself. The mathematical modeling approach[1]PLOS ONE: Evolution of Sleep in Periodic World (2018) → suggests sleep is evolutionarily inevitable in periodic environments regardless of any specific function, which may mean the question is malformed: sleep may have been selected for different reasons in different lineages.

A synthesis of the current evidence suggests that glymphatic clearance is a prominent candidate for a core function, but the evidence that it is the sole core function remains circumstantial. The controversy documented by The Transmitter[2]The Transmitter: Glymphatic Controversy (2025) → — with new imaging methods challenging some of Nedergaard’s original claims — shows that even the mechanism specifics remain in flux.

TENSION 02

Why We Dream

THE PARADOX
Are dreams adaptive or epiphenomenal?

Revonsuo proposes that dreams evolved to rehearse threats. Hobson suggests they are neural noise that the brain attempts to narrativize. The predictive processing framework posits them as unconstrained hallucinations. The ARRM model proposes they unify memory abstraction and emotional integration. Six competing theories exist, each with supporting evidence, none clearly dominant. After 125 years of scientific dream research, consensus remains elusive regarding the functional significance of dreams.

Adaptive function

Threat simulation (Revonsuo, 2000): 65% negative dream emotions. Memory consolidation via replay. Emotional regulation through REM processing. ARRM (2025) unification model. Continuity with waking concerns.

vs

Epiphenomenon

Activation-synthesis (Hobson, 1977): random PGO spikes narrativized by forebrain. Antidepressants suppress REM without obvious cost. Dolphins show minimal REM. Many dreams are mundane, not threat-related.

The Deeper Puzzle

A significant factor contributing to the complexity of this tension is the hard problem of dream consciousness. Even if the functional role of dreams were resolved, the question of why subjective experience occurs during sleep would persist. A brain capable of consolidating memory and processing emotions could presumably do so without generating the vivid phenomenal experiences termed dreams.[3]PMC: On Dreams and Motivation (2017) →

The 2025 ARRM model[4]PhilArchive: Cognitive Dream Theory — ARRM (2025) → represents a comprehensive attempt at resolution, proposing that each prior theory captures one component of a unified process: associative activation (Hobson), rehearsal (Revonsuo), and reframing (Freud, predictive processing). Whether this synthesis withstands empirical testing remains to be seen, but its scope reflects the field’s growing recognition that no single framework fully accounts for dream phenomena.

TENSION 03

Individual Sleep Requirements

THE PARADOX
Universal 8 hours or genuine individual variation?

Walker and major health organizations recommend 7-9 hours for adults, with warnings that chronic restriction produces cumulative damage. Yet cross-species data shows elephants thriving on under 2 hours. Natural short sleepers carry genetic variants enabling full function on 4-6 hours. Some recovery sleep studies show rapid restoration of cognitive function, questioning whether sleep debt accumulates as linearly as claimed.

Universal requirement

8-hour recommendation (Walker, major guidelines). Cumulative sleep debt. 24h awake = 0.1% BAC. Cognitive deficits accelerate with chronic restriction. Randy Gardner’s 264-hour experiment.

vs

Individual variation

Elephants: <2h. Short-sleeper genes (DEC2). Rapid cognitive recovery after extended deprivation. Some cultures practice biphasic sleep. Drosophila species vary enormously.

The Orthosomnia Paradox

This tension has a practical consequence that is itself paradoxical. Critics argue that Walker’s (2017) presentation of sleep deprivation risks may overstate the current evidence base, and that framing chronic sleep restriction as universally catastrophic has produced unintended effects. One documented consequence is orthosomnia: anxiety about sleep quality that itself disrupts sleep. People who obsessively track their sleep scores on wearable devices may sleep worse precisely because they are trying so hard to sleep well.[5]Sleep Foundation: 2024 Sleep Industry Projections →

The evidence for genuine interspecies variation is strong: a 2024 Nature study[6]Nature: Divergent Evolution of Sleep in Drosophila (2024) → showed dramatic sleep variation even among closely related Drosophila species. Whether this variation extends to meaningful within-human differences — beyond rare short-sleeper mutations — remains unclear. While a general sleep duration of 7-9 hours is widely recommended for most adults, the precision of this recommendation in accounting for individual physiological variability is an area requiring further empirical investigation.

TENSION 04

Lucid Dreaming: Evidence and Institutional Status

THE PARADOX
Legitimate neuroscience or still institutionally marginal?

LaBerge’s eye-signal paradigm has been replicated across multiple studies. Brain imaging confirms a distinct neural signature, characterized by prefrontal reactivation and 40Hz gamma activity. Randomized controlled trials have reported PTSD resolution rates of up to 85%. Despite this accumulating evidence, lucid dreaming research remains institutionally marginal, characterized by small sample sizes, limited funding, and a persistent association with non-scientific movements, which has contributed to its marginalization within institutional frameworks.

Legitimate science

Eye-signal paradigm replicated in multiple international studies. Distinct EEG signature identified. 40Hz tACS induction reported at 77% (Voss 2014). Positive outcomes in PTSD RCTs. A history of therapeutic application for nightmares spanning over three decades.

vs

Still marginal

Small clinical sample sizes. Not universally inducible or learnable. Long-term effects on sleep architecture remain uninvestigated. tACS induction requires independent replication. Potential for selection bias in participant recruitment. Persistent cultural association with non-scientific movements.

A Field in Transition

The tension in this domain primarily concerns the sociological gap between the accumulating empirical evidence for lucid dreaming as a neurological phenomenon and the field’s institutional recognition. The growing body of research, encompassing peer-reviewed studies, objective EEG verification, and demonstrated clinical applications across healthy and clinical populations, suggests a potential inflection point for the field. A future comprehensive review, such as the 2026 meta-analysis[8]MedicalXpress: Lucid Dreaming for Mental Health (2026) →, could further consolidate this evidence. However, whether this evidence base is sufficient to attract the funding and institutional support required for large-scale clinical translation remains an open question.

TENSION 05

Insomnia: CBT-I vs. Pharmacological Intervention

THE PARADOX
First-line treatment with limited patient access

CBT-I is recommended as first-line therapy for chronic insomnia by every major medical organization. It produces 70-80% improvement rates with effects lasting years. Yet most insomnia patients still receive pharmacological prescriptions. The 2024 Cell finding that zolpidem suppresses glymphatic clearance further exacerbates this concern: pharmacological interventions may induce unconsciousness without providing the restorative benefits associated with natural sleep.

CBT-I superiority

70-80% success rate. Lasting effects. Every guideline recommends it first-line. Digital CBT-I showing comparable efficacy. No side effects. No rebound insomnia.

vs

Real-world barriers

Provider shortage. 8 sessions vs 5-minute prescription. Insurance coverage variable. Patient compliance challenging. Pharmacology is faster, easier, available now.

An Implementation Gap, Not an Evidence Gap

This tension is notable for the consensus on the evidence, as there is broad agreement that CBT-I demonstrates greater efficacy than medication for chronic insomnia.[9]AASM: Top Sleep Research 2025 → The tension is between what the evidence says and what the healthcare system does. Digital CBT-I programs represent a significant potential solution, with culturally tailored internet-delivered versions demonstrating considerable potential for reaching underserved populations. However, the disparity between evidence and clinical practice persists as a prominent challenge within medicine.

The zolpidem finding from the 2024 Cell paper[10]Cell: NE-Mediated Slow Vasomotion (2024) → raises additional concerns: if sleeping pills suppress glymphatic clearance, pharmacological sleep may fail to deliver the restorative benefits of natural sleep. Critics argue that this mechanistic finding, pending longitudinal clinical confirmation, elevates the CBT-I access gap from a quality-of-care issue to a potential patient safety concern.

TENSION 06

Blue Light: Impact and Confounds

THE PARADOX
Observed effects and their overall significance

Blue light (460-480nm) suppresses melatonin via well-documented mechanisms. Some studies have observed sleep onset delays of up to 90 minutes following evening screen exposure.[11]PMC: Influence of Blue Light on Sleep (2022) → A significant proportion of students report poor sleep associated with evening device use, though estimates vary across studies. Yet blue-light-blocking glasses show mixed results in meta-analyses. Daytime blue light exposure has been associated with improved alertness and mood. Attributing modern sleep disruption solely to blue light may not fully encompass the complexity of interacting variables.

Observed effect

Melatonin suppression well-documented. 460-480nm wavelength most potent. Dose-response: >4h device use predicts worse outcomes. Majority of surveyed students report sleep disruption linked to device use (estimates vary). Sleep onset delay observed up to 90 minutes in some studies.

vs

Significance debated

Blue-light glasses: mixed meta-analysis results. Content engagement may contribute more than light wavelength. Daytime blue light is beneficial. Book-reading before bed also delays sleep onset. Effect size may be small.

Blue Light as a Singular Factor

Blue light functions as an accessible public health narrative because it offers a discrete, addressable mechanism. However, evidence indicates that the relationship between screens and sleep involves factors beyond wavelength alone.[11]PMC: Influence of Blue Light on Sleep (2022) → Content engagement (e.g., social media use, email checking, high-arousal digital content) may contribute to sleep disruption to a greater extent than photon exposure alone. Cognitive arousal from digital interaction, emotional stimulation from content, and social notification pressure are plausible confounds that have not been consistently accounted for in numerous blue-light studies.

The relevance of blue light is not negated by these considerations. The melatonin suppression mechanism is established and well-studied.[12]PMC: Blue Light and Sleep Disruption in Adolescents (2024) → This suggests that while the popular framing — that blue light is a primary cause of sleep disruption — may identify a contributing factor, its overall impact might be overstated. Therefore, simple technological interventions (e.g., night mode, blue-light glasses) may not fully address the multifaceted nature of sleep disruption.

META-TENSION

The Irreducible Complexity

All six tensions converge on a single meta-observation: sleep resists simple explanation. It is not one thing. It does not have one function. It cannot be fixed by one intervention. It cannot be replaced by one drug. The field’s deepest tensions arise from attempts to impose singular narratives on a phenomenon that is fundamentally multi-dimensional.

This observation suggests that the complexity may be inherent to the phenomenon itself, rather than a deficiency in scientific understanding. Sleep evolved early, it is universal, and it has become integrated into multiple biological systems over hundreds of millions of years. Expecting a single explanation for why we sleep may be like expecting a single explanation for why we eat — the answer involves nutrition, energy, pleasure, social bonding, and cultural identity, none of which reduces to the others.

The Pipeline Problem

Sleep science encounters challenges in the dissemination of findings: rigorous research is often simplified during popularization, subsequently modified in commercial applications, and then integrated into public understanding, sometimes leading to heightened concerns. Contested claims about sleep deprivation severity can transition from scientific discourse to intensive engagement with wearable devices, potentially contributing to conditions like orthosomnia. A significant translational challenge for the field involves ensuring that future discoveries are communicated to the public with an appropriate representation of their inherent uncertainties.

These six tensions are not merely issues to be resolved but rather constitute fundamental characteristics of a field investigating a complex biological phenomenon. Each represents an unresolved disagreement between credible sources examining the same evidence. A comprehensive characterization of sleep science in 2026 necessitates acknowledging its inherent complexity, rather than relying on any singular claim about sleep. It is the irreducible complexity of a phenomenon that occurs every night but remains incompletely understood.
