# Methodology — Borrowed Grief: Dream Incorporation & the Subconscious Voice

## Research Question
How does the subconscious incorporate another person's emotional vocabulary as its own during dreams — and what does the moment of lucid recognition ("that's hers, not mine") reveal about the boundary between self and other in grief?

## Scope & Dimensions
Three dimensions, deliberately tight for a mini-project:

1. **S1: Incorporation** — How waking-life memory fragments (especially other people's words, phrases, emotional expressions) get woven into dream content. Continuity hypothesis, day-residue vs. remote memory, emotional intensity as incorporation driver.
2. **S2: Grief Dreams & Continuing Bonds** — Dreams of the ex-partner after separation/divorce as a form of continuing bond. Comforting vs. distressing grief dreams. The ex's voice, words, or emotional expressions appearing in dreams. Cartwright's work on divorce dreams and depression recovery.
3. **S3: Lucid Metacognition as Boundary-Setting** — The lucid recognition that borrowed speech belongs to someone else. Choosing to wake up as emotional regulation. The hybrid consciousness state of lucid dreaming and its role in grief processing.

**Deliberately excluded:** Bilingual dreaming / code-switching (language selection is not the core phenomenon here), sleep physiology beyond what's needed for context, nightmare disorder/PTSD-specific literature.

## Source Strategy
- Web search for peer-reviewed papers, clinical studies, and researcher profiles
- Cross-reference with existing sleep-and-dreams project literature (57 sources)
- Key researchers to track: Joshua Black (grief dreams), Rosalind Cartwright (divorce dreams), Deirdre Barrett (dream speech), Michael Schredl (continuity hypothesis)

## Reading & Synthesis Approach
Discourse survey — what researchers are currently saying — with an anonymized dream narrative as framing case study.

## Deliverables Plan
1. Three section deep-dives (S1–S3)
2. One survey paper synthesizing across sections
