# Literature Collection — Borrowed Grief: Dream Incorporation & the Subconscious Voice

## S1: Incorporation — How Memory Fragments Enter Dreams

| ID | Source | Key Data Points | Date |
|----|--------|----------------|------|
| I1 | [PLOS ONE: Memory Sources of Dreams — Content-Matching Paradigm](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0185262) | 83.8% of dreams related to waking life experiences (WLEs); avg 1.8 incorporations per dream; emotional intensity rated 5.65/10; day-residue effect confirmed | 2017 |
| I2 | [SLEEP Advances: Memory Updating in Dreams](https://academic.oup.com/sleepadvances/article/5/1/zpae096/7927647) | 39% of dreams incorporated multiple past episodes; certainty of memory-dream link correlated with life importance (r=.19); emotional valence unrelated to confidence of association | 2024 |
| I3 | [eLife: How the Brain Constructs Dreams](https://elifesciences.org/articles/58874) | ~50% of dreams contain elements from specific waking experiences; dreams rarely replicate whole memories — fragments intermingle across recent and remote sources; hippocampus central to dream construction | 2020 |
| I4 | [Scientific American: Dreaming and Memory (Lewis)](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/what-is-dreaming-and-what-does-it-tell-us-about-memory-excerpt/) | Day residues in 65-70% of single dream reports; dream-lag effect (incorporation dips days 2-5, peaks again days 6-7); ~2% of dreams replay complete episodic memories | 2013/2024 |
| I5 | [PMC: Functional Role of Dreaming in Emotional Processes](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6428732/) | REM theta oscillations linked to emotional re-processing; frontal theta correlates with number of waking-life references in dreams; emotional events incorporated regardless of positive/negative valence | 2019 |
| I6 | [Nature: Incorporation of Visuo-Olfactory Episodic Memory into Dreams](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-019-51497-y) | 86% of learning-related dreams occurred on first night after encoding; dream reports with learning incorporation were significantly longer (227 vs 90 words) | 2019 |
| I7 | [Nature: We Dream to Forget — Evidence of Active Role](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-58170-z) | Dream-recallers showed emotional memory trade-off and reduced reactivity; more positive dreams led to greater reduction in next-day reactivity; dreaming facilitates "successful forgetting" | 2024 |
| I8 | [ResearchGate: Inner Speech in Dreaming — A Dialogic Perspective](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/263923793_Inner_Speech_in_Dreaming_A_Dialogic_Perspective) | Inner speech has important function in dream formation; verbal thoughts may be transformed into images (literal or metaphorical) or expressed as explicit speech; dream is product of interplay between inner speech and imagery | 2013 |
| I9 | [Wikipedia: Dream Speech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dream_speech) | Kraepelin: dream speech parallels schizophrenic speech disorder; grammar preserved but meaning suffers; Barrett: poetry fares better than prose in dreams due to rhythm/rhyme preservation; Broca's area active, Wernicke's compromised | — |
| I10 | [Paller et al.: Memory, Sleep, Dreams, and Consciousness](https://faculty.wcas.northwestern.edu/paller/NSS2025.pdf) | Memory Theory of Consciousness (MToC): same system generates conscious experience and explicit memory; brain doesn't need sensory input to produce realistic experiences; dreams use same processing as waking reality | 2025 |

**Gaps:** Foulkes (1985) cognitive-psychological analysis of dreaming, Malinowski & Horton (2015) metaphor and hyperassociativity paper, Hartmann (2000) focused cognitive processes hypothesis

## S2: Grief Dreams & Continuing Bonds

| ID | Source | Key Data Points | Date |
|----|--------|----------------|------|
| G1 | [Black, Belicki, Ralph (2024): Dreams and Waking Experiences of the Deceased](https://www.psypost.org/most-bereaved-people-dream-of-or-sense-the-deceased-study-finds-and-the-two-may-be-linked/) | 73.5% of partner mourners dreamed of deceased within past month; 50.7% reported waking experiences (seeing, hearing voice, feeling touch); those who dream of deceased more likely to have waking experiences too | 2024 |
| G2 | [Black, Belicki, Piro, Hughes (2020): Comforting vs Distressing Dreams of Deceased](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0030222820903850) | Three proposed functions: processing trauma, maintaining continuing bond, regulating emotion; 216 participants who lost romantic partner; support found for all three functions | 2020 |
| G3 | [PMC: Dream Content in Complicated Grief](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3929213/) | CG dreams had more familiar characters including deceased (in women), fewer social interactions and emotions vs. norms; dreaming of deceased may be compensatory — providing aspects previously fulfilled by deceased | — |
| G4 | [Dr. Leslie Ellis: Dreams of Bereavement](https://drleslieellis.com/how-your-dreams-help-you-grieve-2/) | Black's findings: 86% of bereaved spouses dream of partner within first year; 92% of those dreams are positive; attachment styles predict who dreams of deceased; desire for continuing bonds is a factor | 2024 |
| G5 | [Tandfonline: Impact of Continuing Bonds — Systematic Review](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/07481187.2023.2223593) | 79 studies reviewed; externalized bonds more associated with distress than internalized; dreaming and yearning associated with more intense separation distress vs. sense of presence; three themes: comfort/distress, ongoing bonds/identity, uncertainty/spirituality | 2023 |
| G6 | [Wikipedia: Continuing Bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuing_bonds) | Klass, Silverman, Nickman (1996): maintaining bond with deceased is normal, not pathological; challenged Freud's "grief work" model; bereaved experience deceased through memories, imagined dialogues, dreams, sensory experiences | — |
| G7 | [EurekAlert: Cartwright — Dreams of Divorced Depressed Patients](https://www.eurekalert.org/news-releases/860322) | When dreams of ex-spouse become less frequent and less negative, depression remits; when ex-spouse seen casually/distantly in dreams, recovery begins; emotionally-rich dreams predict faster adjustment | — |
| G8 | [Schredl (2003): Continuity Between Waking and Dreaming](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1053810002000727) | Continuity hypothesis: waking experiences reflected in dreams; divorce, stress, personality all affect dream content; emotional involvement modulates incorporation rate; highly focused cognitive processes rare in dreams | 2003 |
| G9 | [Selterman & Drigotas (2009): Attachment Styles and Dream Content](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0030222820903850) | Attachment styles predict emotional content, stress, and conflict in dreams of romantic partners; referenced in Black et al. | 2009 |
| G10 | [IDEALS Illinois: Long-Term Stability of Attachment After Separation](https://www.ideals.illinois.edu/items/126801/bitstreams/414370/data.pdf) | "I have dreams about this person" as attachment measure; lingering non-zero attachment bond compels attachment behaviors toward former partners after dissolution; Bowlby: acute grief subsides but bond never fully extinguished | — |

**Gaps:** Cartwright (1984) primary paper "Broken Dreams", Cartwright (1996) "Dreams and the Adaptation to Divorce" chapter, Field & Filanosky (2010) continuing bonds and adjustment
