Religion Through the Lens of Science

A literature survey examining religion from the perspectives of cognitive science, psychology, developmental science, moral psychology, and neuroscience — what the empirical evidence actually shows about why humans believe, how belief affects them, and what happens in the brain during religious experience.

Religion is cognitively natural. The human mind — through agency detection, Theory of Mind, teleological reasoning, and dualism intuition — naturally produces religious ideas without explicit instruction. But “cognitively natural” does not settle the truth question: Barrett, a devout Christian, argues God designed minds to detect him; Dennett, a committed atheist, argues the same evidence shows religion is a cognitive illusion. The science is compatible with both interpretations.

This survey maps the landscape across 6 dimensions and 52+ sources — cognitive science (Boyer, Barrett, Atran), psychology of belief (Willard & Norenzayan, Kahneman), mental health (Pargament, scrupulosity, religious trauma), moral psychology (Haidt, 2024 meta-analysis), developmental psychology (Kelemen, deconversion), and neuroscience (Newberg, Johns Hopkins psilocybin program). Each section addresses what the evidence actually shows, with explicit attention to methodological limitations.

Six Tensions

The most valuable findings in any survey are the contradictions — places where credible evidence points in opposite directions. These six tensions define the scientific study of religion in 2026.
  • T1 HADD vs. evidence — CSR’s most popular theory (Hyperactive Agency Detection Device) has no empirical support after ~30 years. The field’s flagship mechanism may need replacement. [S1]
  • T2 Religion helps vs. hurts — Positive religious coping predicts resilience. Negative religious coping, scrupulosity, and religious trauma cause real harm. The variable is how you are religious, not whether. [S3]
  • T3 Moral self-report vs. behavior — Religious people say they are more moral (r=.15). They barely behave more morally (r=.06). The gap may be self-enhancement bias. [S4]
  • T4 Born believers vs. Gen Z exodus — If religion is cognitively natural (Kelemen, Barrett), why is the most educated generation the least religious? Cognitive naturalness explains prevalence, not cultural trajectory. [S5]
  • T5 Neural correlates vs. meaning — Every experience has neural correlates. Prayer activates the prefrontal cortex; this explains nothing about prayer’s meaning. Neuroscience maps the biology without adjudicating the truth. [S6]
  • T6 Byproduct vs. adaptation — Is religion a cognitive accident or an evolved function? Recent scholarship suggests both: cognitive byproduct origins, cultural adaptive elaboration. A false dichotomy. [S1]
