# NPOV & Rigor Review — Religion Through the Lens of Cognitive Science & Psychology

**Date:** 2026-04-20
**Reviewer:** Claude (npov-cog2-r2)
**Files reviewed:** `survey-paper.html`

## Summary
A 52-source survey of the cognitive science and psychology of religion across six dimensions (CSR, belief psychology, mental health, moral behavior, developmental trajectories, neuroscience) with a "six tensions" section that explicitly maps disagreements. The paper achieves balanced NPOV on the believer/atheist axis — Barrett's theistic reading and Dennett's atheistic reading are presented as compatible with the same evidence. No anti-religious or pro-religious framing was identified in the exposition. The biggest concerns are (1) a handful of residual authorial-voice flourishes in opening/closing sentences, (2) overstated-superlative claims presented without sufficient hedging ("The hard problem remains," "religion binds and blinds" repeated as shorthand), and (3) several "2025/2026 forthcoming" citations load-bearing for strong claims — the methods section flags this but in-body citations do not.

## POV Issues
- `survey-paper.html#abstract` — "**Religion is cognitively natural**." — Opening-sentence bolded declarative in paper voice. The claim is defensible (it IS the CSR consensus the paper surveys) but stating it as the paper's own banner sentence sits in narrator voice rather than attributed voice. **Fix:** "CSR research converges on the claim that religion is cognitively natural..." or "The dominant CSR account holds that..."
- `survey-paper.html#abstract` — "But 'cognitively natural' does not mean 'true' — and it does not mean 'false.'" — first-person-adjacent rhetorical voice ("does not mean"). Not a POV violation per se, but authorial-voice mode. Mild.
- `survey-paper.html#abstract` — "The present analysis maps the territory across six dimensions..." — Appropriate scholarly self-reference ("the present analysis"). No issue.
- `survey-paper.html#methods` — "In summary, the cognitive science and psychology of religion has made genuine progress in understanding the cognitive architecture that facilitates religious belief, while remaining far from a complete account of why specific individuals hold specific beliefs in specific contexts." — "genuine progress" is mildly editorializing but defensible as a summary claim. **Fix:** "has made substantial progress" or "has advanced understanding."
- No first-person "I/we," no second-person "you/your" in exposition. No pro-religious or anti-religious framing identified.

## Logical Consistency Issues
- `survey-paper.html#csr` — The section opens "religion emerges from cognitive mechanisms that evolved for entirely non-religious purposes" (standard byproduct framing), then later Tension 6 ("Byproduct vs. Adaptation") concludes "cognitive byproduct origins followed by cultural adaptive elaboration." These are compatible, but the opening ("entirely non-religious purposes") is stronger than the later synthesis allows. **Fix:** soften opening to "cognitive mechanisms that did not originally evolve for religious purposes."
- `survey-paper.html#csr` — "Barrett's Hyperactive Agency Detection Device (HADD) emerged as a widely cited mechanism within CSR" then immediately cites Lisdorf finding "a lack of conclusive empirical evidence supporting HADD over nearly 30 years, noting multiple null findings" — the paper presents HADD as both standard and unsupported. This is the intended point and is handled in the tension section; no contradiction, but new readers encounter "widely cited" before "empirically unsupported," which may be confusing. Acceptable.
- `survey-paper.html#mental-health` vs. `#mental-health` callout — "Kenneth Pargament's foundational framework (1997) suggests that the pertinent inquiry is not whether religion generally benefits mental health, but rather: *which kind of religious coping?*" — later the callout states "positive associations attributed to 'religion' in mental health contexts may be more specifically linked to engagement in contemplative practices." These are two different claims (coping style is the variable / practice is the variable), and the paper presents each as the de-confounding explanation. Not a contradiction but some tension between the two framings. **Fix:** explicitly note that practice-based and coping-style accounts are complementary, not competing.
- `survey-paper.html#morality` — The paragraph reports r=.13/r=.06 and "Religiosity accounts for less than 2% of variance in actual prosocial behavior," then the Tension section (Tension 3) adds "Most secular societies (Scandinavia, Japan) are among the world's most peaceful and cooperative." This last addition steps beyond the meta-analysis into cultural comparison, and the cited Kelly & Kramer meta-analysis does not support the "Scandinavia/Japan" claim. **Fix:** separate the meta-analytic finding from the cultural-comparative observation, and either source the latter or drop it.

## Scholastic Rigor Issues
- `survey-paper.html#csr` — "The evolutionary rationale is as follows: in ancestral environments, the cost of a false positive (detecting a predator that isn't there) was trivial compared to a false negative (failing to detect one that is)." — This is the standard HADD rationale attributed to Barrett elsewhere, but in this sentence the paper presents it as stipulation. **Fix:** "Barrett's evolutionary rationale..." or "In the HADD account,..."
- `survey-paper.html#csr` — "Recent scholarship suggests both byproduct and adaptation may be partially correct — cognitive byproduct origins, cultural adaptive elaboration." — "Recent scholarship suggests" is unsourced. **Fix:** cite the specific work (Shults et al. or similar) or hedge more explicitly.
- `survey-paper.html#belief` — "atheists scored 18.7% higher on composite analytic thinking measures across a dataset of over 50,000 participants in 16 countries" — Very specific number (18.7%) on a very large sample with no footnote marker at this sentence. The footnote on the preceding sentence points to Pennycook (CRT), but Pennycook et al. 2012 is not a 50,000-participant, 16-country dataset — that sounds like a different meta-analysis or cross-national study. **Fix:** verify which study the 18.7%/50,000/16-country figures refer to and cite it directly; or separate the Pennycook citation from the larger cross-national claim.
- `survey-paper.html#mental-health` — "Scrupulosity — religious OCD — affects up to 33% of OCD patients, with approximately 5% presenting it as their primary symptom." — "up to 33%" is a common estimate range in clinical literature; hedging is appropriate but could be tighter. **Fix:** "an estimated 5–33% of OCD patients, depending on sample and measurement." (More accurate range per IOCDF and systematic reviews.)
- `survey-paper.html#mental-health` — "Studies observing secular meditators and contemplative monks have shown identical default mode network signatures..." — "identical" is a strong superlative for fMRI-derived network signatures, which generally show *similar* patterns but with statistical differences. **Fix:** "similar" or "largely overlapping."
- `survey-paper.html#morality` — "Haidt's summary: 'religion binds and blinds.'" — The direct quote is attributed, but the framework is then presented without the "and blinds" caveat being examined (e.g., the "blinding" claim is itself contested in the sources). Acceptable as a direct-quote summary, but the caveat could be noted.
- `survey-paper.html#morality` — "Most secular societies (Scandinavia, Japan) are among the world's most peaceful and cooperative." — Uncited generalization. The "world's most peaceful" claim typically rests on Global Peace Index or similar; naming "cooperative" is additional and unsourced. **Fix:** cite the index or hedge ("Scandinavia and Japan score highly on indices of peacefulness...").
- `survey-paper.html#development` — "74% of young adult religious exiters left before age 17, with 26% leaving before their teenage years." — Figure cited to American Survey Center. The reference is present and recent (2024); acceptable. Minor: "their teenage years" — earlier than age 13 — may surprise readers, worth a sentence of context (sample description).
- `survey-paper.html#neuroscience` — "Prayer activates the prefrontal cortex (focused intention), while mystical states — experiences of unity, boundary dissolution, timelessness — are associated with decreased activity in the superior parietal lobe..." — Presented as fact. This is Newberg's SPECT finding; his work is contested (sample sizes, blinding limits). **Fix:** "Newberg reports that prayer activates..." or "In Newberg's SPECT imaging work, prayer activates..."
- `survey-paper.html#neuroscience` — "67% of participants rated their sessions among the top five most meaningful experiences of their lives" — Correctly cited to Griffiths et al. 2006; the paper adds "an assessment that persisted at 14-month follow-up" without a second citation. **Fix:** confirm follow-up citation or add "per follow-up reports from the same research group."
- `survey-paper.html#neuroscience` — "The Hopkins program now operates from a dedicated $55M research center." — Fact claim without footnote. **Fix:** cite the Hopkins Psychedelic Consciousness Research Center founding grant (2019) or remove the dollar figure.
- `survey-paper.html#neuroscience` — "However, hyperreligiosity is rare and not typical for TLE; historical retrospective diagnoses (Dostoevsky, Paul of Tarsus) are methodologically unreliable." — Appropriate skeptical hedging; acceptable. The historical-diagnosis caveat is a common scholarly point.
- `survey-paper.html#neuroscience` — "Persinger's 'God Helmet' — which posited that weak magnetic fields could induce a 'sensed presence' — failed double-blind replication." — Correct summary. No citation, but this is established; acceptable.
- `survey-paper.html#tensions` — Tension 2 cites Pargament with **malformed HTML** — uses curly quotes (`”`) inside `href=` attribute, breaking the link. This is a technical bug, not a rigor issue, but worth flagging for the author.
- `survey-paper.html#tensions` — Tension 3 cites "pubmed.ncbi.nlm.gov" (missing ".nih") — broken URL; technical bug.
- `survey-paper.html#tensions` — Tension 4 has curly-quote href attributes (broken links).
- `survey-paper.html#references` — ref-49 dated 2026 (per methods note, flagged as pre-print/early access). Acceptable given the caveat is explicit.

## Tone Issues
- `survey-paper.html#abstract` — "Children are promiscuous teleologists by age five. Adults under cognitive load revert to purpose-based reasoning. The infrastructure for belief precedes belief itself." — The three short declaratives have a rhetorical punch not typical of neutral exposition. "The infrastructure for belief precedes belief itself" is an author-coined aphorism. **Fix:** merge into a single attributed sentence — "Kelemen's studies indicate that children attribute purpose to natural phenomena by age five; adults under cognitive load show similar reversion to purpose-based reasoning."
- `survey-paper.html#csr` — "A tree that listens. A person who walks through walls." — Rhetorical fragment list; evocative but editorial flourish. Acceptable in survey but borderline. **Fix:** "Examples include concepts such as a tree that listens or a person who walks through walls."
- `survey-paper.html#csr` — "These concepts are posited to occupy an optimal point for cognitive processing, being sufficiently surprising to be memorable while remaining familiar enough for processing." — Hedged well; "optimal" is a loaded word but appropriately attributed ("posited to"). Acceptable.
- `survey-paper.html#abstract` — "What emerges is not a single verdict on religion — helpful or harmful, true or false — but an account of a cognitive system whose outputs depend critically on coping style, cultural context, developmental stage, and individual variation." — Closing-paragraph authorial summary; "critically" is mildly editorial. **Fix:** "heavily" or "substantially."
- `survey-paper.html#mental-health` — "Sufferers experience intrusive blasphemous thoughts, excessive religious rituals performed as compulsions, and paralyzing doubt about their spiritual state." — "Paralyzing doubt" is evocative/clinical-evocative. Common in clinical literature, but "paralyzing" leans descriptive-editorial. **Fix:** "severe" or "highly distressing."
- `survey-paper.html#mental-health` — "religious trauma syndrome (RTS) describes the psychological damage from authoritarian religion" — "psychological damage from authoritarian religion" is causal. RTS is not a universally recognized DSM category and the framing could read as taking a position. **Fix:** "describes the psychological distress some individuals experience following participation in high-control religious contexts..."
- `survey-paper.html#morality` — "Haidt's summary: 'religion binds and blinds.'" — Direct quote. Acceptable.
- `survey-paper.html#development` callout — "The Gen Z Paradox" heading — "paradox" is a strong framing; the phenomenon (secular growth despite cognitive naturalness) is better described as a "puzzle" or "open question." **Fix:** "The Gen Z Puzzle" or similar.
- `survey-paper.html#neuroscience` — "Newberg's position is explicitly non-reductive: the brain mediates spiritual experience; it does not explain it away." — Second clause ("does not explain it away") is Newberg's framing, but the survey presents it as paper voice. **Fix:** "Newberg frames his work as explicitly non-reductive..."
- `survey-paper.html#tensions` Tension 5 — "The hard problem remains — why does parietal deactivation *feel like* unity?" — Invocation of Chalmers' "hard problem" in a non-Chalmers context; evocative rhetorical gesture. **Fix:** "A distinct explanatory gap remains" or attribute the "hard problem" phrase to Chalmers' framing if that's the intended reference.
- `survey-paper.html#tensions` Tension 2 — "Research on the meditation exception suggests that practice, not belief, accounts for some of the more consistent associations with positive outcomes." — "The meditation exception" is an author-coined label. Acceptable shorthand but flag as paper-internal.

## Priority Fixes
1. **Broken hrefs in Tensions section** (`#tensions` Tension 2, 3, 4) — curly quotes inside `href=` break the links and `pubmed.ncbi.nlm.gov` typo. Technical but visible. Must fix before circulation.
2. **Unsourced cross-national analytic-atheism figure** (`#belief`, "50,000 participants in 16 countries, 18.7% higher") — This looks like a specific meta-analysis separate from Pennycook. Verify the primary source and cite it directly.
3. **Unsourced Scandinavia/Japan claim** (`#tensions` Tension 3, "Most secular societies... among the world's most peaceful and cooperative") — add citation or drop. The claim is contested in the comparative-religion literature and needs support.
4. **Newberg findings presented as fact** (`#neuroscience`, prayer/PFC, mystical/parietal) — attribute to Newberg's SPECT work and note methodological caveats already mentioned elsewhere in the section.
5. **Opening-sentence narrator-voice claims** (`#abstract` "Religion is cognitively natural." / "The infrastructure for belief precedes belief itself.") — reframe as attributed consensus ("The CSR consensus holds that..."). These are the most visible places the paper's narrator-voice leaks past NPOV.

## Overall Assessment
**B+** — This is the most NPOV-successful of the three projects. The believer/atheist axis is handled with notable balance (Barrett/Dennett on same evidence, tensions explicitly foregrounded). No anti-religious or pro-religious framing was identified. Scholastic rigor is generally strong with meta-analyses cited (Kelly & Kramer, Shariff, Abu-Raiya & Pargament, Garssen). Remaining issues cluster in three areas: (1) a handful of opening/closing narrator-voice declaratives that should be attributed, (2) a small set of uncited statistics that look like they come from specific studies but lack pinpoint citation, and (3) a technical HTML issue with broken links in the Tensions section that the review flagged but cannot fix per read-only constraint. No advocacy language, no editorial flourishes of the "remarkable/stunning/fascinating" variety, no second-person or first-person authorial voice.
