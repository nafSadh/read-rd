## Issues Found

### POV (Point of View)

1. **The project is impressively neutral on religion's truth value.** The abstract states: "'cognitively natural' does not mean 'true' -- and it does not mean 'false.'" Barrett (devout Christian) and Dennett (committed atheist) are presented as drawing opposite conclusions from the same evidence, with neither position endorsed. This is exemplary NPOV handling of a topic where most writers take sides. **Status: No issue.**

2. **S2 (Belief) analytic atheism framing is well-hedged.** The CRT/religiosity correlation is reported with appropriate caveats: varies across cultures, disappears in some countries, predicts questioning local norms rather than atheism specifically, "many highly analytical people are deeply religious." The survey paper follows through on these caveats in its body text. **Status: No issue.**

3. **S4 (Morality) framing slightly favors the "religion doesn't help" conclusion.** The meta-analysis finding (r = .06 behavioral) is prominently featured, while the self-report finding (r = .15) receives less interpretive weight. The three competing interpretations for the self-report gap are listed, but the framing ("Religious people *believe* they are more moral but barely *behave* more morally") implies that the behavioral measure is the "real" one. This is a defensible interpretive choice but not the only one -- the lab-behavioral gap may reflect measurement limitations rather than religious self-deception. **Severity: Minor.**

4. **S3 (Mental Health) negative coping receives slightly more vivid treatment.** Scrupulosity and religious trauma are described with specific clinical detail (33% of OCD patients, Scripture misuse, victim-blaming). Positive coping is described more abstractly ("benevolent reappraisal," "community support"). The asymmetry in specificity could leave a reader with the impression that religion's harms are better documented than its benefits, when in fact the positive coping literature is larger. **Severity: Minor.**

### RIGOR

1. **Effect sizes consistently reported.** The project reports r values, sample sizes, and confidence qualifiers throughout. This is unusually rigorous for a survey project. The synthesis note's summary ("we know less than textbooks suggest but more than skeptics admit") is an honest epistemic assessment.

2. **HADD critique is well-sourced.** The claim that HADD has "no compelling empirical support after ~30 years" is attributed to Lisdorf's review and the 2025 alternative study. This is a strong claim but supported by the cited literature.

3. **Publication bias flagged appropriately.** The mental health section and morality section both flag publication bias as a concern for positive findings. This is good methodological practice.

4. **The "Six Tensions" framework is an original analytical contribution.** It is not claimed to emerge from the literature but rather to be the reviewer's synthesis. This is transparent.

### BIAS

1. **No pro- or anti-religious bias detected in the survey paper.** The paper treats religion as a natural phenomenon to be studied empirically, without either defending or attacking it. Positive findings (community support, suicide protection, meaning-making) and negative findings (scrupulosity, religious trauma, parochial prosociality) receive roughly equal analytical attention.

2. **WEIRD sampling bias acknowledged.** The methodology and synthesis both note that most research is from Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic samples. The project does not overclaim generalizability.

3. **Neuroscience section avoids reductionism.** The treatment of neurotheology explicitly states that "neural correlates do not explain meaning" and that brain imaging cannot adjudicate truth claims about religious experience. Newberg's non-reductive approach is presented faithfully. The God Helmet's failure is reported as a scientific result, not as a victory for either side.

4. **Gen Z deconversion treated descriptively.** The 34% unaffiliated statistic and the "born believers vs. Gen Z exodus" tension are presented as demographic facts to be explained, not as either progress or decline. The note that "the religious impulse may be detaching from organized religion, not disappearing" is appropriately hedged.

### TONE

1. **Academic register maintained throughout.** The survey paper uses measured, source-attributed prose. There are no dismissive or celebratory passages about religion.

2. **Section notes are more compressed but still neutral.** The bullet-point format in notes 01-06 is efficient without being tendentious.

3. **One tonal edge: "the depression epidemic" (S3 notes).** The phrase "overestimates of depression prevalence inflate the apparent epidemic" could be read as implying there is no real increase in depression. But this is in the religion-psychology project's notes cross-referencing the fashionable-despair project, not in the religion-psychology deliverables themselves. **Severity: Minimal -- wrong project context.**

## Fixes Applied

None. No changes were made to the files. The project's NPOV handling is strong across all deliverables.

## Remaining Concerns

1. **S4 morality framing** could add one sentence acknowledging that behavioral measures in laboratory settings may underestimate real-world religious prosociality (volunteering, charitable giving outside the lab). The three competing interpretations are listed but the narrative voice slightly favors interpretation #2 (self-enhancement bias).

2. **S3 positive coping specificity.** Adding one or two concrete examples of positive religious coping with the same clinical specificity given to scrupulosity (e.g., a specific study showing prayer reduces cortisol in hospice patients, or a finding about religious community buffering suicide risk with effect size) would balance the section's vivid treatment of religious harm.

3. **Psilocybin section is enthusiastic.** The Johns Hopkins findings are reported accurately but the 67% top-5 life experience statistic, the $55M research center, and the personality change findings are presented without equal space for criticisms of the psilocybin research program (e.g., expectancy effects, self-selection, difficulties with blinding in psychedelic research). **Severity: Minor.**

## Overall Assessment

This is the strongest NPOV performance of the three projects reviewed. The topic (religion) is one where bias is most difficult to avoid, and the project navigates it with consistent discipline. The "Six Tensions" framework is an effective structural device that surfaces disagreements rather than smoothing them over. The Barrett/Dennett pairing in the abstract is a particularly effective NPOV signal: same evidence, opposite conclusions, both presented respectfully. The project neither defends nor attacks religion, and the data-driven approach (effect sizes, meta-analyses, null findings given prominence) provides natural NPOV guardrails.

**NPOV Grade: A-** -- exemplary neutrality on the core topic, minor asymmetries in specificity between positive and negative findings, psilocybin enthusiasm.

### Issues Found and Fixed

#### 1. Second-Person POV Injection (s4-morality.dd.html)
**Severity: Minor. Fixed.**
The religious priming section used second person: "If you have internalized the norm... If you haven't, the prime is inert." Rephrased to third person ("For someone who has internalized...").

#### 2. Editorial Framing of Self-Report Gap (survey-paper.html, overview.dd.html)
**Severity: Moderate. Fixed.**
Three locations presented the morality self-report/behavioral gap with language that presupposed the self-enhancement interpretation:
- survey-paper.html S4 body: "Religious people *believe* they are more moral but barely *behave* more morally" -- replaced with neutral framing acknowledging competing explanations.
- survey-paper.html S4 SVG diagram annotation: same editorializing text -- replaced with "Self-report and behavioral measures diverge -- the interpretation remains contested."
- overview.dd.html S4 summary: "Religious people believe they are more moral. The behavioral evidence is far less clear." -- replaced with balanced three-interpretation framing.

#### 3. Overstatement: "Definitive Answer" (survey-paper.html)
**Severity: Minor. Fixed.**
S4 opened with "The definitive answer comes from Kelly and Kramer's 2024 meta-analysis." No single meta-analysis provides a definitive answer. Changed to "The most comprehensive evidence comes from."

#### 4. Psilocybin Enthusiasm Without Methodological Caution (s6-neuroscience.dd.html, survey-paper.html)
**Severity: Moderate. Fixed.**
Flagged in Pass 1 but not addressed. The psilocybin timeline and findings were entirely positive with no mention of well-known methodological limitations. Added:
- s6-neuroscience.dd.html: new "Methodological Cautions" box noting blinding difficulties, self-selection bias, small samples, and limited generalizability.
- survey-paper.html: added a sentence noting blinding challenges, self-selection, and small sample sizes after the psilocybin findings.

#### 5. Advocacy Label: "The Autonomy Advantage" (s4-morality.dd.html)
**Severity: Minor. Fixed.**
The secular morality section included a bullet labeled "The autonomy advantage" that framed secular moral reasoning as superior without acknowledging religious traditions' capacity for moral development. Relabeled to "Flexibility on novel issues" and added a balancing clause.

#### 6. First-Person Plural "We" in Third-Person Prose (overview.dd.html, s1-csr.dd.html, s2-belief.dd.html)
**Severity: Minor. Fixed.**
Several passages used "We intuit," "We project," "We apply" in content that otherwise maintains a third-person academic register. Rephrased to participial constructions ("Humans naturally separate mind from body, intuiting that...") for consistency. Note: the survey-paper.html uses "we know less than textbooks suggest" -- this is retained as it functions as an authorial epistemic summary, not a POV injection.

### Issues Reviewed and Left Unchanged

#### 1. S3 Positive/Negative Coping Specificity Asymmetry
Pass 1 flagged that negative coping (scrupulosity, trauma) receives more vivid clinical detail than positive coping. On second review, the asymmetry is real but not severe enough to warrant rewriting: both sides receive substantive treatment, and the positive coping section does include specific mechanisms (benevolent reappraisal, community support, suicide protection). The detail-level gap is modest.

#### 2. "We know less than textbooks suggest but more than skeptics admit"
This phrase appears in both the survey-paper abstract and methods section. It uses first-person plural but functions as an authorial epistemic claim -- a standard convention in academic survey writing. Retained.

#### 3. S3 Mental Health: "The Meditation Exception" Framing
The callout implies meditation benefits are belief-independent, which could be read as diminishing religion's contribution. However, this is an empirical finding (secular and religious meditators show identical neural signatures) reported accurately. The framing question ("Which practices are beneficial, and can they be accessed without the religious framework?") is a legitimate research question, not advocacy. Retained.

#### 4. Logical Consistency
No contradictions found. Claims track their evidence appropriately across files. Effect sizes cited in .dd.html files match those in survey-paper.html. The "Six Tensions" framework in the survey paper accurately represents the disagreements documented in the section-level files.

### Overall Assessment

Pass 2 addressed the three remaining concerns from Pass 1 (S4 morality framing, S3 specificity asymmetry review, psilocybin enthusiasm) plus identified and fixed new issues (second-person POV, overstatement, advocacy labeling, first-person plural inconsistency). The project's NPOV handling was already strong; these fixes bring the morality and neuroscience sections into line with the neutrality standard set by the rest of the project.

**NPOV Grade (Post-Pass 2): A** -- all significant asymmetries corrected, no remaining advocacy language, consistent third-person register, methodological cautions added where previously missing.
