# S1 Notes — Cognitive Science of Religion

## Core Claim
Religious belief is a natural byproduct of ordinary cognitive mechanisms (agency detection, Theory of Mind, minimally counterintuitive concepts) that evolved for non-religious purposes.

## Key Findings
- **HADD (Barrett):** Humans over-detect agents from minimal cues. Evolutionary logic: false positives (seeing predators when absent) are cheap; false negatives are fatal. Applied to invisible agents → gods.
- **MCI Concepts (Boyer):** Religious ideas violate exactly one ontological expectation while preserving others. Memorable and culturally transmittable. A tree that listens. A person who walks through walls.
- **Theory of Mind:** Transforms detected agents into full supernatural persons with beliefs, desires, and intentions. Makes gods into moral agents who care about human behavior.
- **Byproduct vs. Adaptation:** Standard Model says byproduct. Adaptationist alternatives cite group cohesion, costly signaling. Recent work suggests both may be partially correct.

## Critical Problems
- HADD has NO empirical support after ~30 years (2025 review). Multiple null findings. Alternative: effortful mental state reasoning, not automatic detection.
- MCI memorability ≠ believability. Many MCI concepts are remembered without being believed (talking animals, invisible friends).
- Enactivist critique (2024): CSR's underlying cognitivist model (modular mind) is itself contested in cognitive science.

## Key Researchers
- Pascal Boyer (Religion Explained, 2001) — MCI concepts
- Justin Barrett (HADD, Born Believers) — devout Christian doing CSR
- Scott Atran (In Gods We Trust, 2002) — evolutionary landscape
- Deborah Kelemen — promiscuous teleology, intuitive theism
