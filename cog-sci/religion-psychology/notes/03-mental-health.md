# S3 Notes — Religion & Mental Health

## Core Finding
Religion is not uniformly helpful or harmful. The critical variable is coping style (Pargament), not religiosity per se.

## Positive Religious Coping
- Benevolent reappraisal of suffering
- Community support ("casserole effect")
- Collaborative coping with God as partner
- Sacred meaning-making
- Suicide protection (community, prohibition, meaning)
- Effect sizes: small to moderate (r = .10–.20); publication bias concern

## Negative Religious Coping & Harm
- **Punishing God reappraisal:** Suffering as divine punishment → existential threat
- **Sacred loss:** Losing something invested with sacred meaning → uniquely intense grief
- **Scrupulosity:** Religious OCD; ~33% of OCD patients show symptoms; 5% primary. CBT+ERP treatment. Most difficult OCD subtype. Cross-cultural (Kashmir 2025 study confirms).
- **Religious trauma syndrome (Winell):** Authoritarian religion → anxiety, depression, sexual dysfunction. Mechanisms: indoctrination, coercive control, spiritual abuse, shunning.
- **Spiritual abuse (2025 Hong Kong study):** Scripture misuse for coercive control; victim-blaming through religious frameworks

## The Meditation Exception
- Most consistent positive findings come from contemplative practices
- Secular meditators and contemplative monks show SAME DMN signatures
- Benefits appear practice-dependent, not belief-dependent
- Implication: "religion helps" may really mean "contemplative practice helps"

## Methodological Problems
- Publication bias (positive findings overrepresented)
- Confounding (social networks, health behaviors, SES)
- Self-selection (healthier people may attend services more)
- WEIRD samples, measurement inconsistency, positive framing bias
