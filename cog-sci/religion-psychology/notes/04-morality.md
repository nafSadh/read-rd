# S4 Notes — Moral Psychology & Religion

## Central Finding
2024 meta-analysis (Kelly & Kramer): 701 effects, 811,663 participants, ~60 years of research.
- r = .13 overall (religiosity → prosociality)
- r = .15 self-report
- r = .06 behavioral
- Religiosity accounts for <2% of variance in prosocial behavior

## The Self-Report Gap
Three competing interpretations:
1. Lab measures miss real-world religious prosociality (volunteering, charity)
2. Religious self-enhancement bias inflates self-reported virtue
3. Both religiosity and self-report share common cause in favorable self-presentation

## Haidt's Moral Foundations Theory
- Five (later six) foundations: Care, Fairness, Loyalty, Authority, Sanctity (+Liberty)
- Secular liberals weight Care/Fairness; religious conservatives weight all five
- Religion doesn't make people universally moral — it makes them moral in a group-binding sense
- "Religion binds and blinds"

## Religious Priming
- Meta-analysis (93 studies): robust priming effects on prosociality
- BUT only for religious participants; non-religious unaffected
- 2025 Bayesian analysis: NO significant priming effect. Supports absence of effect.
- Publication bias likely inflated earlier estimates

## Parochial Prosociality
- Religious generosity favors in-group consistently
- God priming enhances cooperation with fellow believers, not outsiders
- Self-report: "I am universally generous." Behavior: generosity flows within religious networks.
- "Sunday effect" may be social monitoring, not genuine moral transformation

## Supernatural Monitoring (Norenzayan)
- Belief in omniscient morally-concerned God → reduced cheating
- "Big Gods" enabled scaling cooperation beyond kin groups
- Modern secular institutions may substitute for divine monitoring

## Secular Morality
- Behavioral measures show minimal religion/secular difference (r = .06)
- Most secular societies (Scandinavia, Japan) are among most peaceful/cooperative
- Secular moral philosophy provides robust frameworks without supernatural foundations
