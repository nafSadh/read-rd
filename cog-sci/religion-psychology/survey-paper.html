COGNITIVE SCIENCE & PSYCHOLOGY · SURVEY PAPER · 52 SOURCES

Religion Through the Lens of Cognitive Science & Psychology

A survey of why humans believe — cognitive architecture, belief formation, mental health, moral behavior, developmental trajectories, and the neuroscience of spiritual experience, synthesized across 52 sources.

March 2026 6 Dimensions 52 Sources 6 Tensions
ABSTRACT

Abstract

Religion is cognitively natural. The human mind, through its ordinary architecture — agency detection, Theory of Mind, teleological reasoning, dualism intuition — naturally produces religious ideas without explicit instruction. This is the consensus finding of the cognitive science of religion (CSR), supported by developmental psychology, cross-cultural research, and neuroimaging. Children are promiscuous teleologists by age five. Adults under cognitive load revert to purpose-based reasoning. The infrastructure for belief precedes belief itself.

But “cognitively natural” does not mean “true” — and it does not mean “false.” Justin Barrett[2]Barrett: HADD & Why Would Anyone Believe in God? (2004) →, a devout Christian, argues that God designed minds to detect him. Daniel Dennett, a committed atheist, argues the same evidence shows religion is a cognitive illusion. The science is compatible with both interpretations. The present analysis maps the territory across six dimensions — cognitive foundations, belief psychology, mental health, moral behavior, developmental trajectories, and neuroscience — drawing on 52 sources spanning foundational texts, meta-analyses, and recent research. The current review highlights areas of tension, where credible researchers, examining the same evidence, reach opposing conclusions.

What emerges is not a single verdict on religion — helpful or harmful, true or false — but an account of a cognitive system whose outputs depend critically on coping style, cultural context, developmental stage, and individual variation.

SECTION 01

The Cognitive Science of Religion

The cognitive science of religion (CSR) addresses the question of why humans, across every known culture and throughout recorded history, generate religious ideas. The field’s answer, developed over three decades, posits that religion emerges from cognitive mechanisms that evolved for entirely non-religious purposes. Pascal Boyer[1]Boyer: Religion Explained (2001) →’s foundational Religion Explained (2001) proposed that religious concepts succeed because they are minimally counterintuitive (MCI) — they violate exactly one ontological expectation while preserving others. A tree that listens. A person who walks through walls. These concepts are posited to occupy an optimal point for cognitive processing, being sufficiently surprising to be memorable while remaining familiar enough for processing.

The Standard Model

Barrett’s Hyperactive Agency Detection Device (HADD) emerged as a widely cited mechanism within CSR. The evolutionary rationale is as follows: in ancestral environments, the cost of a false positive (detecting a predator that isn’t there) was trivial compared to a false negative (failing to detect one that is). Humans over-detect agents from minimal cues — rustling bushes, ambiguous shadows. Applied to invisible agents, this generates gods. Scott Atran[3]Atran: In Gods We Trust (2002) →’s In Gods We Trust (2002) complemented this with an evolutionary landscape model: religion occupies a space carved by modular cognition, not designed for worship but inadvertently hospitable to it. Theory of Mind then transforms detected agents into full supernatural persons with beliefs, desires, and moral concerns — making gods into agents who care about human behavior.

HADD Under Pressure

Challenges have been raised regarding the empirical basis of a central mechanism within CSR. Lisdorf[4]Lisdorf: What’s HIDD’n in the HADD? (2007) →’s review identified a lack of conclusive empirical evidence supporting HADD over nearly 30 years, noting multiple null findings. A 2025 study[5]Belief as Explanation — Alternative to HADD (2025) → proposes an alternative: supernatural agents arise not from automatic, low-level agency detection but from effortful mental state reasoning applied to explain puzzling phenomena. This inverts HADD’s core claim — religious cognition may be cognitively effortful, not automatic.

A separate challenge comes from LeRon Shults and colleagues[8]LeRon Shults et al.: Embodied CSR (2024) → (2024), who argue that CSR’s entire theoretical foundation — modular, representational cognitivism — is contested within cognitive science itself. Their enactivist reconstruction frames religious cognition as embodied practice, not mental module output. Meanwhile, Powell and Clarke[7]Powell & Clarke: Byproduct Hypothesis Critique (2012) → note that the byproduct hypothesis may be unfalsifiable: any religious behavior can be reinterpreted as a byproduct of something. Recent scholarship suggests both byproduct and adaptation may be partially correct — cognitive byproduct origins, cultural adaptive elaboration.

Key Insight

A prominent theory within CSR (HADD) has been found to lack conclusive empirical support over approximately 30 years, suggesting this central mechanism may rest on untested assumptions. While the field's foundational theoretical framework is also subject to debate, research continues to investigate the cognitive underpinnings of religious phenomena, exploring both automatic and effortful pathways for their emergence.

SECTION 02

The Psychology of Belief

If CSR explains why religion is cognitively possible, the psychology of belief explains how specific cognitive biases channel the mind toward religious conclusions. Willard and Norenzayan (2013) developed a path model which identified four cognitive biases — mentalizing, dualism, teleological thinking, and anthropomorphism — that independently predict religious belief. Dualism mediates the link between mentalizing and belief: the intuition that mind and body are separate entities makes supernatural agents (disembodied minds) seem plausible. The model was replicated cross-culturally[11]Willard, Cingl & Norenzayan: Czech/Slovak Replication (2020) → in Czech and Slovak populations, though the relative strength of each pathway varied by culture.

Dual-Process Theory and Analytic Atheism

Religious belief appears to be primarily a System 1 (fast, intuitive) product. Pennycook and colleagues[12]Pennycook et al.: CRT and Religious Belief (2012) → found that performance on the Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) inversely correlates with religiosity: atheists score 18.7% higher on composite analytic thinking measures across a dataset of over 50,000 participants in 16 countries. System 2 (slow, analytical) thinking can override intuitive religious conclusions — but rarely does in everyday cognition, because the cognitive defaults are strong and the motivation to override them is weak.

The analytic-atheism correlation[14]Lindeman & Aarnio: Cognitive Styles and Religion (2021) → varies dramatically across cultures; in some countries it disappears entirely. Analytical thinking predicts questioning local norms, not atheism specifically — in a secular society, the same cognitive style might produce religious exploration. Many highly analytical people are deeply religious. The cognitive bias model explains variance in populations, not determination in individuals. Furthermore, the bias framework does not fully account for experiential dimensions such as mystical states, conversion experiences, and contemplative depth.

Teleological Thinking

Deborah Kelemen[35]Kelemen: Intuitive Theists (2004) →’s research shows that children are “promiscuous teleologists” — they attribute purpose to everything, from mountains (“for climbing”) to rain (“so flowers can grow”). This design stance persists into adulthood. Banerjee and Bloom[13]Banerjee & Bloom: Teleological Reasoning (2014) → demonstrated that under cognitive load, even atheists endorse teleological statements about life events. Scientists under time pressure revert to purpose-based reasoning. These findings suggest that teleological explanations, such as those found in creationism, align with intuitive cognitive processes, whereas evolutionary explanations may require overriding these natural cognitive tendencies. This has implications for pedagogical approaches in science education.

SECTION 03

Religion & Mental Health

Kenneth Pargament’s foundational framework (1997) suggests that the pertinent inquiry is not whether religion generally benefits mental health, but rather: which kind of religious coping? Positive religious coping — characterized by benevolent reappraisal of suffering, collaborative coping with a divine entity, sacred meaning-making, and community support — consistently predicts resilience. Conversely, negative religious coping — involving the perception of suffering as divine punishment, feelings of abandonment by a divine entity, or experiences of sacred loss — consistently predicts distress. This indicates that the influential variable is not religiosity itself, but the specific manner in which an individual engages with their religion.

This framework was validated under pressure during the COVID-19 pandemic. Abu-Raiya and Pargament[19]Abu-Raiya & Pargament: COVID-19 Critical Review (2024) →’s 2024 critical review confirmed that positive coping predicted resilience while negative coping predicted distress, even in the extreme conditions of a global pandemic. Garssen and colleagues[20]Garssen et al.: Spirituality, Religiousness, Mental Health (2021) →’ large review reports small-to-moderate positive associations between religion and lower depression/anxiety — but flags publication bias as a serious concern, with positive findings systematically overrepresented.

Adverse Manifestations: Scrupulosity and Religious Trauma

Scrupulosity[21]IOCDF: Scrupulosity (2023) → — religious OCD — affects up to 33% of OCD patients, with approximately 5% presenting it as their primary symptom. Sufferers experience intrusive blasphemous thoughts, excessive religious rituals performed as compulsions, and paralyzing doubt about their spiritual state. Systematic reviews[22]Scrupulosity Psychotherapies Systematic Review (2024) → identify it as one of the most difficult OCD subtypes to treat, with CBT and exposure-response prevention (ERP) as the primary intervention. A 2025 study from Kashmir[23]Scrupulosity in Kashmir (2025) → confirms the cross-cultural validity of the construct: recurrent religious doubts and excessive prayer as compulsion appear across religious traditions.

Religious trauma syndrome[24]Religious Trauma Syndrome (2024) → (RTS) describes the psychological damage from authoritarian religion: anxiety, depression, sexual dysfunction, identity disruption. The mechanisms are specific — indoctrination, coercive control, spiritual abuse, community shunning after departure. A 2025 Hong Kong study[25]Spiritual Abuse — Hong Kong (2025) → documents instances where Scripture is employed as a tool of coercive control, with victim-blaming channeled through religious frameworks and communities contributing to the retraumatization of survivors.

Contemplative Practices and Mental Health

Research indicates that many consistent positive findings regarding mental health outcomes are associated with contemplative practices. Studies observing secular meditators and contemplative monks have shown identical default mode network signatures, suggesting a common neurobiological basis for the effects of these practices. This evidence leads to the hypothesis that observed benefits may be primarily practice-dependent rather than belief-dependent. Consequently, it has been proposed that some positive associations attributed to "religion" in mental health contexts might be more specifically linked to the engagement in contemplative practices.

SECTION 04

Moral Psychology & Religion

The relationship between religiosity and moral behavior is a subject of ongoing debate. A large-scale 2024 meta-analysis by Kelly and Kramer[28]Kelly & Kramer: Religiosity Predicts Prosociality Meta-Analysis (2024) →’s 2024 meta-analysis — the largest ever conducted on religiosity and prosociality: 701 effects, 237 samples, 811,663 participants, spanning approximately 60 years of research. The overall correlation is r = .13. When disaggregated by measurement method, self-report yields r = .15 while behavioral measures yield r = .06. Religiosity accounts for less than 2% of variance in actual prosocial behavior. The discrepancy between self-reported and behavioral prosociality remains a significant area of investigation, with competing explanations ranging from self-enhancement bias to limitations of laboratory behavioral measures.

The Self-Report Gap

Three competing interpretations exist for the self-report/behavioral discrepancy. First, laboratory measures may miss real-world religious prosociality like volunteering and charitable giving. Second, religious self-enhancement bias may inflate self-reported virtue. Third, both religiosity and favorable self-presentation may share a common cause in social desirability. Behavioral evidence indicates that, while self-reports may suggest universal generosity, observed behavior demonstrates generosity flowing primarily within religious networks.

Haidt’s Moral Foundations

Jonathan Haidt[27]Haidt: Moral Psychology and the Misunderstanding of Religion (2007) →’s Moral Foundations Theory provides the most influential framework for understanding religion’s moral function. Secular liberals weight two foundations — Care and Fairness. Religious conservatives weight all five (later six): Care, Fairness, Loyalty, Authority, Sanctity, and Liberty. Religiosity is associated with group-binding moral foundations rather than universal prosociality. Haidt’s summary: “religion binds and blinds.”

Religious Priming — A Collapse

An earlier meta-analysis by Shariff and colleagues[29]Shariff et al.: Religious Priming Meta-Analysis (2016) → (93 studies, 11,653 participants) found robust priming effects: exposure to religious concepts enhanced prosocial behavior, but only among already-religious participants. However, a 2025 Bayesian analysis by Chhabra and colleagues[30]Chhabra et al.: Bayesian Analysis of Religious Priming (2025) → found no significant priming effect, with results supporting the absence of an effect rather than merely failing to detect one. Publication bias likely inflated earlier estimates. Studies on in-group vs. out-group[32]Religious Priming: Ingroup vs. Outgroup (2016) → prosocial behavior confirm the parochial pattern: God priming enhanced cooperation with fellow believers but showed weaker effects for outgroup members.

SECTION 05

Developmental Psychology of Religion

Kelemen’s research on promiscuous teleology establishes the developmental baseline: children aged 3–5 spontaneously attribute purpose to natural phenomena (“rocks are pointy so animals can scratch themselves”). This design stance never fully disappears; it merely goes underground, re-emerging under cognitive load, time pressure, or during existential crisis. Barrett’s “born believers” thesis builds on this: the developmental trajectory moves from agency detection (ages 0–2) through Theory of Mind (2–4), teleology (3–5), and dualism intuition (4–6) to differentiated God concepts (5–7). The cognitive infrastructure for religion is in place before any formal religious teaching.

Children’s God Concepts

Cross-cultural research by Lane and colleagues[37]Lane et al.: Cross-Cultural God Concepts (2017) → reveals that children attribute supernatural psychological properties to God (omniscience, knowing hidden things) before supernatural physical properties (invisibility, walking through walls), regardless of cultural or religious background. Saide and colleagues[36]Saide et al.: Parents’ and Children’s God Concepts (2022) → (2022) found that parent-child God concepts correspond but are moderated by children’s executive functioning: children don’t passively absorb theology but actively construct it using their available cognitive tools. God is not just a big person to young children — even the youngest distinguish God from humans on psychological dimensions.

Deconversion

The American Survey Center[42]American Survey Center: Gen Z & the Future of Faith (2024) →’s data shows that 74% of young adult religious exiters left before age 17, with 26% leaving before their teenage years. The reasons cluster around irrelevance, science conflict, moral disagreement with the institution, and institutional failure. Hardy and Taylor[38]Hardy & Taylor: Deconversion in Adolescence (2024) →’s 2024 literature review describes a multi-stage process: pre-exit strain, a turning point, anomie (the disorienting void left by the lost framework), and reconstruction of a non-religious identity.

Bleidorn and colleagues[39]Bleidorn et al.: Personality Change Before/After Deconversion (2024) → (2024) found that personality changes precede deconversion, not just follow it. Openness increases before the break, suggesting that deconversion is not a single event but the culmination of a personality shift already underway. After deconversion, emotional stability drops temporarily before recovering. A 2025 study of Polish Catholic adolescents[40]Polish Adolescent Deconversion (2025) → highlights peer community integration as a driver: deconversion is partly about finding belonging elsewhere. The simultaneous loss of a meaning system, social network, family approval, and daily routine can produce a grief response that mimics bereavement.

The Gen Z Paradox

The observation that highly educated generations exhibit lower rates of religious affiliation presents a paradox when considering the cognitive naturalness of religion. Cognitive naturalness explains prevalence across cultures but not the cultural conditions under which the pull weakens. The decline in religious affiliation does not necessarily equate to a disappearance of spiritual impulses; instead, it may reflect a detachment from organized religion.

SECTION 06

The Neuroscience of Religious Experience

Andrew Newberg[43]Newberg: Neurotheology — Practical Applications (2025) →’s neurotheology program, which has spanned two decades of SPECT imaging across various religious traditions, reports findings regarding brain activity during spiritual practice. Prayer activates the prefrontal cortex (focused intention), while mystical states — experiences of unity, boundary dissolution, timelessness — are associated with decreased activity in the superior parietal lobe, the region associated with the processing of self-other boundaries. Newberg[44]Newberg: Neurotheology (Columbia UP, 2016) →’s position is explicitly non-reductive: the brain mediates spiritual experience; it does not explain it away.

The “God Spot” Debate: Current Understanding

Early neuroscience of religion sought to identify a single brain region responsible for religious experience. However, this search has largely concluded, as current evidence points toward a more distributed model. The 2025 Frontiers review[52]Frontiers: Neuroscience of Religion Review (2025) → confirms a distributed network model: religious experience involves the prefrontal cortex (intentionality), temporal lobes (narrative and meaning), parietal cortex (self-boundary), the default mode network (self-reference), and the Theory of Mind network (attributing intentions to God). Different religious activities activate different networks. There is no “God spot.”

Psilocybin and Mystical Experience

The Johns Hopkins psilocybin program has yielded significant findings in the neuroscience of religion. Griffiths and colleagues[45]Griffiths et al.: Psilocybin Occasions Mystical Experiences (2006) →’ landmark 2006 study showed that psilocybin reliably produces mystical experiences: 67% of participants rated their sessions among the top five most meaningful experiences of their lives, an assessment that persisted at 14-month follow-up. Follow-up work[46]Griffiths et al.: Psilocybin + Meditation (2018) → (2018) found that psilocybin combined with meditation and spiritual practice produced enduring prosocial attitude changes and increased personality Openness. Yaden and colleagues[47]Yaden et al.: Beyond Mystical Experience (2024) → (2024) extended the framework beyond mystical experience to include emotional breakthrough, insight, and connectedness as clinically relevant effects.

The neural mechanism involves suppression of the claustrum (associated with ego dissolution) and disruption of the default mode network. For cancer patients, psilocybin produced substantial, lasting decreases in depression and anxiety. The Hopkins program now operates from a dedicated $55M research center. Methodological caution is warranted: blinding is difficult in psychedelic trials (participants typically know they received an active substance), volunteers are self-selected and positively predisposed, and sample sizes remain small by clinical standards.

Temporal Lobe Epilepsy and the God Helmet

Temporal lobe epilepsy[48]Hyperreligiosity in TLE (2015) → (TLE) has long been associated with religious experience, with 0.4–3.1% of partial epilepsy patients reporting ictal religious experiences, predominantly from the right temporal lobe. A 2026 review[49]Neural Pathways of Faith — Epilepsy (2026) → examines hyperreligiosity in epilepsy, documenting sudden convictions and ecstatic states. However, hyperreligiosity is rare and not typical for TLE; historical retrospective diagnoses (Dostoevsky, Paul of Tarsus) are methodologically unreliable.

Persinger’s “God Helmet” — which posited that weak magnetic fields could induce a “sensed presence” — failed double-blind replication. The observed effects were attributed to suggestion rather than magnetic stimulation. The experiment's findings have not been consistently replicated, leading to its reevaluation within the field.

Meditation and the Default Mode Network

Brewer and colleagues[50]Brewer et al.: Meditation & DMN (PNAS, 2011) → (2011, PNAS) demonstrated that experienced meditators show reduced default mode network activity across all meditation types, with increased connectivity between the DMN and executive control networks. Long-term practitioners show these differences even at rest — a state-to-trait transition. A key finding is that secular and religious meditators show the same neural signatures. Benefits are practice-dependent, not belief-dependent.

SECTION 07

Six Tensions

This survey identifies significant insights in areas where credible researchers, examining similar evidence, reach divergent conclusions, rather than solely in areas of consensus. These tensions indicate the inherent complexity of the domain.

Tension 1: HADD vs. Empirical Evidence

The theory that religion arises from an automatic, hypersensitive agency detection device, a prominent theory in CSR, lacks robust empirical support after approximately 30 years of research. Multiple null findings exist. The 2025 alternative proposes effortful mental state reasoning, not automatic detection. Yet the broader claim that religion is cognitively natural survives through other pathways (teleology, dualism, Theory of Mind). The empirical support for this widely cited mechanism appears less robust than for other proposed pathways.

Tension 2: Religion Helps vs. Religion Hurts

Pargament’s framework offers a resolution: the variable is coping style, not religiosity itself. Positive religious coping predicts resilience; negative religious coping predicts distress. Scrupulosity and religious trauma are real clinical presentations that existing “religion is good for you” reviews tend to underrepresent. The meditation exception suggests that practice, not belief, is associated with the most consistent positive findings.

Tension 3: Religion Makes People Moral vs. The Data

The 2024 meta-analysis challenges the popular claim: r = .13 overall, r = .06 behavioral. Religious prosociality is parochial — it favors the in-group. The self-report gap suggests social desirability bias rather than an indication of moral superiority. Most secular societies (Scandinavia, Japan) are among the world’s most peaceful and cooperative.

Tension 4: Born Believers vs. Gen Z Exodus

The observation that highly educated generations exhibit lower rates of religious affiliation presents a tension with the hypothesis of religion's cognitive naturalness. 34% of Gen Z are unaffiliated. Cognitive naturalness explains cross-cultural prevalence but not the conditions under which this influence weakens. The religious impulse may be detaching from organized religion rather than disappearing — “no religion” does not equal “no spirituality.”

Tension 5: Neural Correlates vs. Meaning

Every experience has neural correlates. Finding that prayer activates the prefrontal cortex does not address the subjective meaning of prayer. Newberg is explicit: neuroscience can map the biology of spiritual experience without adjudicating its truth. The hard problem remains — why does parietal deactivation feel like unity? The direction problem (does belief cause neural activity or vice versa?) is unresolved. The elucidation of subjective meaning is generally considered to lie outside neuroscience’s methodological reach.

Tension 6: Byproduct vs. Adaptation

Is religion a cognitive accident or an evolved function? Boyer and Atran propose the byproduct hypothesis. Haidt emphasizes a group-binding adaptive function. Powell and Clarke note the byproduct hypothesis may be unfalsifiable. Recent scholarship suggests the debate is a false dichotomy: cognitive byproduct origins followed by cultural adaptive elaboration. Religion may have originated as a cognitive byproduct and been retained due to its functional utility.

SECTION 08

Methods & Limitations

This survey synthesizes 52 sources across six dimensions of the cognitive science and psychology of religion. Sources include foundational texts (Boyer 2001, Barrett 2004, Atran 2002, Pargament 1997), landmark meta-analyses (Kelly & Kramer 2024, Shariff et al. 2016), recent empirical studies (2024–2025 publications in neuroscience, developmental psychology, and cross-cultural research), and field reviews from frontiers journals. Sources were selected for empirical rigor, citation impact, and recency, with deliberate inclusion of critical and null findings.

Methodological Caveats

Several structural limitations affect the literature as a whole. WEIRD sampling remains pervasive: the majority of studies draw from Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic populations, limiting generalizability. Publication bias systematically inflates positive findings, particularly in the religion-mental health literature. Measurement inconsistency presents a significant challenge within the field — “religiosity,” “spirituality,” and “prosociality” are operationalized differently across studies, making direct comparisons unreliable. Effect sizes are typically small: even the meta-analytic religion-prosociality correlation of r = .13 accounts for under 2% of variance.

The challenges associated with operationalization are particularly salient. The construct of "religion" encompasses a diverse range of phenomena. For instance, a contemplative Quaker, a Pentecostal faith healer, a nominal Catholic who attends Mass at Christmas, and a scrupulosity-afflicted OCD patient may all be categorized as “religious” by standard measures. Averaging across such diverse populations may yield findings that lack specificity or generalizability to particular subgroups. While frameworks such as Pargament’s coping style distinction and Haidt’s moral foundations framework represent attempts to add necessary granularity, the field as a whole continues to grapple with fundamental questions regarding the precise nature of the constructs being measured.

In summary, the cognitive science and psychology of religion has made genuine progress in understanding the cognitive architecture that facilitates religious belief, while remaining far from a complete account of why specific individuals hold specific beliefs in specific contexts.
