# Methodology — Religion Through the Lens of Cognitive Science & Psychology

## Research Question
Why are humans religious, and what does the empirical evidence actually show about religion's cognitive origins, psychological effects, moral consequences, and neural substrates? This project surveys religion not as theology but as a natural phenomenon amenable to scientific investigation — examining what cognitive science, psychology, developmental science, and neuroscience have discovered (and failed to discover) about religious belief and experience.

## Scope & Dimensions
This survey covers 6 axes:
1. **Cognitive Science of Religion (CSR)** — Why humans are naturally religious: HADD, Theory of Mind, minimally counterintuitive concepts, byproduct vs. adaptation debate (Boyer, Barrett, Atran)
2. **Psychology of Belief** — Cognitive biases supporting religious thinking: pattern recognition, teleological thinking, dualism intuition, dual-process theory applied to belief (Kahneman meets theology)
3. **Religion & Mental Health** — Empirical evidence on religious coping (positive vs. negative), prayer/meditation neuroscience, Pargament's framework, scrupulosity and religious OCD, when religion helps vs. hurts
4. **Moral Psychology & Religion** — Does religion make people moral? Haidt's moral foundations theory, prosocial behavior and religious priming, the Sunday effect, in-group vs. out-group morality, secular vs. religious moral reasoning
5. **Developmental Psychology of Religion** — How children develop god concepts, Kelemen's "intuitive theism," religious identity formation, deconversion psychology, identity disruption and reconstruction
6. **Neuroscience of Religious Experience** — Newberg's neurotheology, the "God spot" debate, psilocybin and mystical experience (Johns Hopkins research), temporal lobe epilepsy and religious visions, what brain imaging tells us (and doesn't)

Deliberately excluded: theological arguments for/against God's existence, comparative religion, sociology of religion (Durkheim, Weber), economics of religion, political science of religion.

## Source Strategy
- Web search for academic reviews, meta-analyses (PMC, PubMed, PsycNET), and critical journalism
- Priority: peer-reviewed studies, meta-analyses, and foundational texts (Boyer, Barrett, Atran, Pargament, Haidt, Kelemen, Newberg, Griffiths)
- Pop psychology and apologetics sources cited only as objects of analysis, not as evidence
- Both sides of contested questions represented (e.g., byproduct vs. adaptation, religion helps vs. hurts)

## Reading & Synthesis Approach
Discourse survey: what are the competing empirical claims, where do sources agree, where do they contradict? Special attention to the gap between laboratory findings and real-world complexity.
