# Cross-Section Synthesis — Atrophy & Decisions

> Built incrementally as sections are completed.

## Core Thesis

Most decisions across the adult lifespan are not freely chosen from an open field of possibilities. They are constrained — and often effectively determined — by what cognitive capacities, skills, relationships, motivations, and identity structures have *not yet* atrophied. We choose from what remains.

## Convergences Across Sections

1. **The "use it or lose it" principle appears at every level**: cognitive (habit automaticity, decision fatigue), skills (decay curves), social (relationship maintenance), motivational (apathy after learned helplessness), identity (possible selves collapse), biological (synaptic pruning, gray matter loss). The same mechanism — degradation through disuse — operates across all scales.

2. **Default emergence as the signature of atrophy**: When a capacity degrades, the remaining capacities fill in. Habits form when deliberation atrophies (S1). Skill repertoire shrinks to well-practiced domains (S2). Social options narrow to surviving relationships (S3). Motivation consolidates around remaining sources of meaning (S4). Identity foreclosure uses past commitments as rails (S5). The brain recruits surviving networks to compensate for lost ones (S6).

3. **Time perception as the master variable**: Carstensen's SST shows that perceived future time horizon shapes goal structure (S3). As time horizons shrink — whether from age, illness, or circumstance — knowledge-seeking goals yield to emotional-meaning goals, which means deliberate expansion yields to protective consolidation. This maps onto identity foreclosure (S5), reduced motivational investment (S4), and preference for familiar cognitive defaults (S1).

4. **Path dependency accelerates all forms of atrophy**: Skills not practiced fall below competence thresholds (S2). Relationships not maintained decay (S3). Possible selves not enacted become unavailable (S5). Every act of not-choosing speeds the narrowing.

## Key Tensions

1. **Atrophy as constraint vs. atrophy as governance**: Is atrophy merely one factor shaping decisions, or does it *determine* them? The strong thesis (position paper) argues governance; the weaker thesis argues constraint.

2. **Adaptive vs. maladaptive atrophy**: SST predicts that social network shrinkage is adaptive (quality > quantity). Cognitive habit automaticity frees resources for other tasks. But learned helplessness is clearly maladaptive. Where is the line?

3. **Biological vs. social atrophy**: Which is more determinative? Biological changes (synaptic loss, PFC gray matter) set hard limits. But social atrophy (relationship decay, institutional exit) may precede and even cause biological atrophy.

4. **Deliberate renewal**: The counterpoint (S7) shows atrophy is not irreversible. Cognitive reserve builds with engagement; neuroplasticity persists into old age. Does the existence of reversibility undermine the thesis, or merely refine it?

## What Surprised Me

- The retirement-disuse finding (ScienceDirect S6): word recall declines for 17 *years* following exit from cognitively demanding work. This is far more durable than expected.
- AI-induced skill decay (S2) operates *below performer's awareness* — possibly the most novel finding in the literature.
- Synaptic pruning removes up to 50% of connections in some regions during adolescence, and continues into the late 20s. The "critical window" thesis extends much longer than commonly assumed.
- Learned helplessness has well-characterized neural substrates (DRN serotonin, vmPFC) — this is not merely behavioral metaphor.
