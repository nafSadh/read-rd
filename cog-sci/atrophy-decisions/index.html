Atrophy & Decision

How degradation shapes agency across the lifespan.

Most decisions are not freely chosen from an open field of possibilities. They are constrained — and often effectively determined — by what cognitive capacities, skills, relationships, motivations, and identity structures have not yet atrophied. We choose from what remains, not from what we want.

This project surveys 7 dimensions of atrophy and their bearing on decision-making across 52+ sources — from synaptic pruning in adolescence through habit automaticity, social network contraction, learned helplessness, identity foreclosure, and the limits of deliberate renewal. The central question: when atrophy is the governing principle, what remains of agency?

Key Tensions

  • 01 Atrophy vs. agency: Does degradation determine decisions or merely constrain them? The same data supports both readings.
  • 02 Adaptive vs. maladaptive atrophy: SST says social narrowing is adaptive. Learned helplessness is clearly not. Where is the line, and who draws it?
  • 03 Individual vs. structural: Is “atrophy” a useful individual-level lens, or does it obscure systemic causes of constrained choice?
  • 04 Biological vs. social atrophy: Which is more determinative? Social atrophy may precede and even cause biological changes.
  • 05 Deliberate renewal: Cognitive reserve and neuroplasticity show atrophy can be resisted. Does this undermine the thesis or merely bound it?
