Brain Energy & Metabolic Psychiatry

A literature survey of the metabolic theory of mental illness — Palmer's framework, mitochondrial evidence, ketogenic interventions, neuroinflammation, and the emerging field of metabolic psychiatry.

Christopher Palmer's metabolic theory proposes that psychiatric disorders emerge from cellular energy dysfunction rather than purely neurochemical imbalances. The brain consumes 20% of body energy despite being 2% of body weight. When mitochondria fail, neurotransmitter synthesis, ion channel maintenance, and synaptic plasticity all suffer. Meta-analyses confirm Complex I/IV deficits across depression, bipolar, and schizophrenia. Beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) acts as HDAC inhibitor, NLRP3 inflammasome inhibitor, and epigenetic modifier — not merely alternative fuel.

This survey maps the landscape across 6 dimensions and 50 sources — foundational texts (Palmer 2022), meta-analyses (Hjelm 2018, 125 studies), clinical trials (JAMA Psychiatry 2025 RCT, Stanford pilot), mechanistic studies (Science 2013 BHB/HDAC), and critical perspectives (Psychiatric Times, Nature Mental Health). The most interesting finding is the gap between strong mechanistic biology and still-early clinical evidence.

Key Tensions

The most valuable findings are the contradictions — where the metabolic theory's strengths and weaknesses create unresolved tensions that will determine the field's trajectory. See the synthesis paper for full analysis.
  • T1 Mechanism vs. clinical evidence — Strong mechanistic biology (Complex I/IV meta-analyses, BHB/HDAC in Science) but early-stage clinical trials (one RCT, small pilots). The biology is ahead of the clinical proof. [S6]
  • T2 Paradigm shift vs. incremental addition — Palmer frames metabolic psychiatry as revolutionary. Critics see it as an incremental addition to existing biopsychosocial models. The truth likely sits between. [S1]
  • T3 Diet as medicine vs. diet as distraction — Ketogenic diets show promise in pilots but face adherence challenges, equity concerns, and risk of distracting from proven pharmacological treatments. [S5]
  • T4 Emerging field vs. hype cycle — 75+ trials worldwide, Nature Mental Health perspective, Stanford/Harvard programs. But also supplement industry co-option and social media oversimplification. [S6]
