# S1: A Brief History of Type — Deep Dive Notes

> 10 sources. From Gutenberg's workshop to Google's server farms — 580 years of letterforms.

---

## Gutenberg's System (1440s-1450s)

Not just movable type (Bi Sheng had that in China, 1040). Gutenberg's innovation was the complete system:
- Metal alloy (lead/tin/antimony) that held precise shape
- Hand mould for consistent, repeatable casting
- Oil-based ink that adhered to metal
- Wooden press adapted from wine/olive presses

The 42-line Bible (1455): ~2,500 type pieces per page, 180 copies, each requiring individual inking and pressing. The alloy remained standard for 550 years. By 1500, 20 million volumes printed across Europe. Information distribution fundamentally changed.

## Aldus Manutius and the Portable Book (1490s-1510s)

Venetian scholar-printer who invented the pocket book. Key innovations:
- **Octavo format**: smaller, cheaper, portable. Books left the lectern.
- **Italic type** (1501): Francesco Griffo's design based on Niccoli's 1420s humanist cursive. Virgil edition first book entirely in italic. Economic: italic fit more text per page.
- **Punctuation**: standardized comma and semicolon.
- **Aldine Press**: 100+ editions 1495-1505, mostly Greek and Latin classics.

## The Enlightenment Progression

Three typefaces trace a 76-year aesthetic arc:

**William Caslon (1722)**: "Old style." Sturdy, practical, warm. Slightly irregular serifs. Based on Dutch models. Became standard English typeface. Used for the US Declaration of Independence.

**John Baskerville (1757)**: "Transitional." More vertical axis. Sharper, bracketed serifs. Higher stroke contrast. Better paper and printing innovations. Based his forms on Caslon, shifted axis toward vertical.

**Giambattista Bodoni (1798)**: "Modern." Extreme thick/thin contrast. Hairline unbracketed serifs. Geometric precision. Inspired by Baskerville, pushed contrast further. Parallel development: Firmin Didot in France.

The progression: warmth to rationality, organicism to geometry, craft to engineering.

## Industrial Revolution (19th Century)

Typography exploded in variety and scale:
- **Wood type**: Darius Wells's lateral router (1827) enabled cheap large-scale type. Half the cost of metal.
- **Display faces**: Fat faces (Thorne/Thorowgood, ~1803), slab serifs (Figgins, 1810), sans-serifs (Caslon IV, 1816), ornamental, outlined, shadowed, condensed, expanded.
- **Poster culture**: A single poster might mix 10+ typefaces. Typography became commercial, exuberant, loud.
- **Mechanization**: Linotype (1886) and Monotype (1887) machines automated typesetting. 6,000 characters/hour vs 1,500 by hand.

## Modernist Reduction (1920s-1960s)

The reaction against Victorian excess:
- **Jan Tschichold**: Die Neue Typographie (1928). "Sans serif is the type for the present day." Asymmetric layouts. White space as design element.
- **Herbert Bayer**: Universal Alphabet (1925). Lowercase only. Geometric construction. Single-case alphabet.
- **Bauhaus**: Made typography a design discipline. Form follows function.
- **Swiss International Style** (1950s-60s): Grid systems. Helvetica (1957). Objectivity as ideal. Josef Mueller-Brockmann, Armin Hofmann, Emil Ruder.

## Desktop Publishing Revolution (1984-1985)

Three technologies converge:
1. **Apple Macintosh** (Jan 1984): WYSIWYG display, bitmap fonts by Susan Kare
2. **Apple LaserWriter** (Jan 1985): PostScript-enabled, 300dpi output
3. **Aldus PageMaker** (Jul 1985): First DTP software. Paul Brainerd coined the term.

Result: professional typesetting democratized. A $7,000 setup replaced $100,000+ typesetting systems. Designers gained direct control. But the "desktop publishing revolution" also meant non-designers were now setting type — a mixed blessing.

## Web Fonts Era (2010-present)

Before 2010, web designers had ~12 "web-safe" fonts. CSS @font-face changed everything. Google Fonts (2010) offered free, hosted fonts. Now 1,500+ families, 70+ trillion serves. Variable fonts (2016) brought dynamic weight/width to the web. Typography is no longer gatekept by technology — the constraint is now taste and knowledge.
