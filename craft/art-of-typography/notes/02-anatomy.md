## Typeface Anatomy

### The Baseline System
- **Baseline**: the invisible line letters sit on
- **X-height**: distance from baseline to top of lowercase 'x' — the single most important metric for perceived size and legibility
- **Cap height**: top of uppercase letters
- **Ascender line**: top of tall lowercase letters (b, d, f, h, k, l)
- **Descender line**: bottom of letters that drop below baseline (g, j, p, q, y)

### Stroke Components
- **Stem**: main vertical stroke
- **Crossbar**: horizontal stroke (in H, A, e)
- **Bowl**: curved stroke enclosing a counter (in b, d, p, q, o)
- **Counter**: enclosed or partially enclosed space within a letter
- **Shoulder**: curved stroke (as in h, m, n)
- **Spine**: the main curved stroke of S
- **Tail**: descending stroke on Q or the lower part of y

### Decorative Elements
- **Serif**: extension at the end of a stroke
- **Finial**: tapered or rounded end of a stroke without a serif
- **Terminal**: end of a stroke that doesn't have a serif
- **Spur**: small projection off a main stroke
- **Ear**: small stroke extending from the top of lowercase g
- **Apex**: the junction of two strokes at the top (as in A)
- **Vertex**: the junction at the bottom (as in V, W)

## Spacing Systems

**Kerning**: adjustment of space between two specific character pairs. The classic example: AV, To, We — where default spacing leaves visual gaps. Good kerning is invisible; bad kerning is immediately noticeable. Modern fonts include hundreds to thousands of kerning pairs.

**Tracking** (letter-spacing): uniform spacing adjustment across a group of characters. Increase tracking for all-caps text, headlines, or small type. Decrease for tight, impactful headlines. Unlike kerning, tracking doesn't consider individual pair relationships.

**Leading** (line-spacing): vertical distance between baselines. Named for lead strips inserted between lines of metal type. Modern best practice: 1.4-1.6x the font size for body text. Tighter for headlines, looser for small sizes.

## Classification Systems

### Vox-ATypI (1954-2021)

Maximilien Vox devised the system in 1954. ATypI adopted it in 1962. Nine categories:

**Classicals:**
1. **Humanist** (Venetian): oblique axis, low contrast, bracketed serifs. Based on pen-drawn forms. (Centaur, Jenson)
2. **Garalde**: more refined than Humanist. Named for Garamond + Aldus. Medium contrast. (Garamond, Bembo)
3. **Transitional**: vertical axis, higher contrast, sharp bracketed serifs. (Baskerville, Times New Roman)

**Moderns:**
4. **Didone**: extreme contrast, hairline serifs, vertical axis. (Bodoni, Didot)
5. **Mechanistic** (Slab Serif): heavy rectangular serifs, low contrast. (Rockwell, Clarendon)
6. **Lineale** (Sans-serif): no serifs. Subdivided into Grotesque, Neo-grotesque, Geometric, Humanist. (Helvetica, Futura, Gill Sans)

**Calligraphics:**
7. **Glyphic**: incised or chiseled appearance. (Trajan, Albertus)
8. **Script**: based on handwriting. (Zapfino, Brush Script)
9. **Blackletter**: Gothic/medieval calligraphic forms. (Fraktur, Textura)

### Why ATypI De-Adopted It (2021)

The board formally de-adopted the system because:
- Cannot classify contemporary type (what category is a variable weight geometric slab?)
- Eurocentric — no provision for Arabic, Devanagari, CJK, or other non-Latin scripts
- Categories blur in practice — many modern typefaces span multiple categories
- Working group established to study contemporary alternatives; no replacement adopted yet

### Modern Practical Classification

Working designers use a simpler taxonomy:
- **Serif**: traditional, authoritative, readable in long text
- **Sans-serif**: modern, clean, digital-friendly
- **Slab serif**: bold, sturdy, display and editorial
- **Monospace**: code, data, technical content
- **Display**: headlines and large sizes only
- **Script**: formal invitations, branding, accents
- **Blackletter**: historical, cultural, craft contexts

## Font vs Typeface

The distinction matters:
- **Typeface**: the design. Helvetica is a typeface.
- **Font**: a specific instance. Helvetica Bold Italic 12pt is a font. Helvetica.otf is a font file.
- **Analogy**: a typeface is a song; a font is a specific recording.
- **Font family**: the complete set of fonts in a typeface (all weights, widths, styles).

In practice, "font" has become the colloquial term for both. Designers who care about precision maintain the distinction.
