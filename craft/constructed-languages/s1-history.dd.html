02

Philosophical Languages

The 17th century saw a wave of “philosophical languages” — attempts to create a perfect language that would reveal the true nature of reality. The most famous is John Wilkins’ An Essay towards a Real Character, and a Philosophical Language (1668).[3]Wikipedia: Wilkins’ 1668 philosophical language →

Wilkins divided all knowledge into 40 main categories, each subdivided recursively. Every concept received a unique character. The ambition: you could deduce a word’s meaning from its spelling alone.

The project failed for a fundamental reason: knowledge is not a tree. Categories overlap, contexts shift, and new discoveries require restructuring the entire system. But the impulse influenced Leibniz’s later work on formal logic.

Zamenhof made a crucial decision that Schleyer did not: he relinquished control. Esperanto was published under a pseudonym and Zamenhof explicitly stated that the language belonged to its speakers. Today it has original literature, annual world congresses, and a small number of native speakers.

05

Ido, Interlingua & Lojban

Ido (1907): A reformed Esperanto that caused a devastating schism. The Ido movement pulled speakers from Esperanto without gaining enough of its own.[7]Medium: The Secret of International Auxiliary Languages →

Interlingua (1951): Instead of designing a new grammar, extracted common elements of major European languages. Highly readable to Romance language speakers but essentially excludes the non-European world.

Lojban (1987): A logical language designed to be syntactically unambiguous. Based on predicate logic. Originally conceived to test the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis: if people are given a perfectly logical language, do they think more logically?

Why Most IALs Failed

The failure modes of international auxiliary languages are remarkably consistent:[8]Omniglot: Why is inventing global languages doomed to fail? →

  • The chicken-and-egg problem: Nobody learns a language without speakers, and nobody becomes a speaker without learners
  • Language cannot be separated from culture: Esperanto survived because it developed its own culture. Purely functional tools never acquired the emotional attachment needed for survival
  • English won: The rise of English as global lingua franca removed the practical motivation for an IAL
  • The schism problem: Every successful conlang attracts reformers. The reform process either destroys the community (Volapuk) or creates painful splits (Ido)
  • Political neutrality is impossible: Esperanto claimed neutrality but is overwhelmingly European in vocabulary and grammar
07

What Sources Agree On

Across all sources, consistent agreement on these points:

  • Lingua Ignota is the first documented conlang (~1150s)
  • Volapuk’s failure was caused primarily by founder control, not linguistic deficiencies[9]Public Domain Review: Truth, Beauty, and Volapuk →
  • Esperanto’s survival is exceptional among IALs
  • The practical need for an IAL diminished as English became dominant
  • Community matters more than grammatical elegance
08

Where Sources Disagree

Points of genuine disagreement in the literature:

  • Whether Esperanto “failed” — it didn’t achieve world peace, but 100K+ speakers and a living culture is not nothing
  • Whether the IAL concept is inherently doomed or simply hasn’t been tried with the right language at the right time

Tension T2: Creator vs. Community

The history of IALs demonstrates that a language’s survival depends more on its community dynamics than on the elegance of its grammar. Schleyer destroyed Volapuk by clinging to control; Zamenhof saved Esperanto by letting go. This tension recurs across every tradition of language invention.
