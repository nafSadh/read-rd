LANGUAGE SURVEY · SYNTHESIS PAPER · 55 SOURCES

Constructed Languages: Six Tensions

A comparative survey of invented languages from Hildegard von Bingen to Brainfuck — spanning history, art, science, and code — organized around the six tensions that recur across every tradition of language invention.

March 20266 Dimensions55 Sources6 Tensions
ABSTRACT

Abstract

The impulse to build a language from scratch is at least 870 years old. It has been directed at world peace (Esperanto[1]Wikipedia: Esperanto →), worldbuilding (Tolkien[2]Wikipedia: Tolkien’s constructed languages →), entertainment (Klingon[3]Wikipedia: Klingon language →), philosophy of mind (Heptapod B[4]Wikipedia: Story of Your Life →), scientific discovery (Nicaraguan Sign Language[5]Wikipedia: Nicaraguan Sign Language →), and computational art (Brainfuck[6]Wikipedia: Brainfuck →). This paper synthesizes findings from a 55-source survey across six dimensions. The most valuable findings are not within any single tradition but in the tensions between them: art vs. utility, creator vs. community, strong vs. weak Whorf, natural vs. designed, formal vs. natural notation, and minimalism vs. richness.

SECTION 01

The Control Paradox

Every conlang creator must decide who controls the language. Johann Martin Schleyer[7]Wikipedia: Schleyer → destroyed Volapuk[8]Beinecke Library: Rise and Fall of Volapuk → by refusing to cede control. By 1889, Volapuk had 283 clubs, 25 periodicals, and 316 textbooks — a larger infrastructure than many natural minority languages. Within five years it was effectively dead, killed by a founder who insisted that his creation was perfect.

Zamenhof succeeded with Esperanto partly by releasing it. He published under a pseudonym and explicitly stated that the language belonged to its speakers. Tolkien kept his languages private and evolving for decades — they outlived him. Okrand[9]Wikipedia: Marc Okrand → handed Klingon to its community through the Klingon Language Institute[10]Klingon Language Institute →. Peterson[11]Wikipedia: David J. Peterson → builds languages that belong to studios. The tension is not resolvable — different purposes demand different ownership models.

T2: Creator vs. Community

Schleyer destroyed Volapuk by clinging to control. Zamenhof saved Esperanto by letting go. NSL was created entirely by its community. The pattern is clear: the more tightly a language is controlled, the more fragile it becomes.

SECTION 02

Languages Need Speakers

The most elaborate grammar means nothing without speakers. Volapuk had superior infrastructure and died. Esperanto had simpler grammar and lived. The deepest evidence comes from Nicaraguan Sign Language[12]Senghas & Coppola: Children Creating Language →: fifty deaf children created a full language because they needed to communicate. No individual designed it, no committee approved it, no authority controlled it.

Media conlangs confirm this from the opposite direction. Klingon thrives because of the KLI. Na’vi has fan communities[13]Salon: How to speak Na’vi → that expand the language beyond what Frommer[14]Campfire: Frommer on Na’vi → originally built. The Language Creation Society[15]Language Creation Society → professionalized the field by building institutional infrastructure. The lesson recurs: communities beat grammars, every time.

The failure modes of international auxiliary languages are consistent: the chicken-and-egg problem (no speakers because no utility, no utility because no speakers), the schism problem (reformers destroy community cohesion), and the English problem (a natural language won the race).[16]Omniglot: Why inventing global languages is doomed to fail →

SECTION 03

The Sapir-Whorf Spectrum

Linguistic relativity connects multiple dimensions of this survey. Tolkien intuited that language shapes thought — his languages generated his stories. Ted Chiang made it the central premise of Heptapod B[17]Emory: Story of Your Life linguistic analysis →. Boroditsky’s research[18]Winawer et al.: Russian blues (PNAS, 2007) → provides empirical grounding: Russian speakers discriminate blues faster because their language distinguishes goluboy and siniy as separate basic categories.

The evidence supports weak Whorf: language influences perception and reasoning without imprisoning it. The effects are real but modest, strongest when language is actively engaged, and well-modeled by a Bayesian framework[19]Berkeley: Sapir-Whorf and inference under uncertainty →. Strong Whorf remains unsubstantiated as science but powerful as fiction.

The computational parallel is striking: APL’s[20]Esolang Wiki: APL as radical notation → Kenneth Iverson received the Turing Award for demonstrating that notation shapes thought about computation. APL programmers think about arrays differently than C programmers. The Whorfian effect is real, even in formal languages.

T3: Strong vs. Weak Whorf

Strong Whorf is wrong as science but right as art. Weak Whorf is right as science but unsatisfying as fiction. The tension cannot be resolved — it drives both research and storytelling.

SECTION 04

Natural vs. Constructed

NSL and ABSL[21]Wikipedia: Al-Sayyid Bedouin Sign Language → challenge the entire framing. They are natural languages that emerged from scratch, in observable time, without prior input. They are “constructed” by their communities in real time, but no individual designed them. Meanwhile, Modern Hebrew was deliberately revived and standardized.

Sandler, Meir, Padden, and Aronoff[22]Language Emergence: Clues from a New Bedouin Sign Language (2005) → documented ABSL’s emergence in near-real time. Each generation’s signing is more complex than the previous. The boundary between constructed and natural is not a line but a gradient — and emergent sign languages sit at the most fascinating point on that gradient.

T4: Natural vs. Designed

NSL emerged naturally in observable time. Na’vi was designed by one linguist. Both are valid languages. The distinction between constructed and natural is a spectrum, not a binary.

SECTION 05

Programming Languages as the Most Successful Conlangs

By any measure — number of speakers, practical utility, economic impact — programming languages are the most successful constructed languages ever created. Yet the conlang and programming language communities rarely talk to each other. Both are engaged in the same fundamental activity: designing formal systems for expressing meaning.[23]Wikipedia: Esoteric programming language →

In 1978, Dijkstra[24]Dijkstra: EWD 667 (1978) → argued that formal symbolism is essential to precise thought and that natural language programming is foolish. In 2025, Marc Brooker[25]Brooker: On the success of natural language programming (2025) → responded: LLMs have made natural language programming partially successful. Both are right. Formal languages remain necessary for precision; natural language now works for specification.

The esolang tradition[26]Esolang Wiki: Timeline of esolangs → — INTERCAL, Brainfuck, Piet, Shakespeare, Whitespace — parallels the conlang community in structure and motivation. Over 1,000 esolangs are documented. They are the artistic conlangs of computing.[27]The Outline: Brainfuck — code designed to hurt →

T5: Formal vs. Natural Notation

Dijkstra argued formalism is essential. LLMs made natural language programming partly successful. The tension has new dimensions in the AI era, but formal and natural languages remain complementary, not interchangeable.

SECTION 06

The Six Tensions

The most valuable findings in any comparative survey are the contradictions. Each of the six tensions identified in this survey recurs across multiple sections and cannot be resolved by choosing one side. They are productive tensions — they drive innovation in every tradition of language invention.

T1: Art vs. Utility

Tolkien’s languages exist for their beauty. Esperanto exists to prevent war. Media conlangs occupy the middle. The same activity produces wildly different results depending on what you optimize for.

T6: Minimalism vs. Richness

Brainfuck has 8 commands and is Turing-complete. Quenya has thousands of words and full etymology spanning 50 years. Both are valid expressions of language as art, at opposite extremes of the same creative impulse.

The meta-finding: language invention is a single impulse expressed through wildly different traditions. What they share is the conviction that language is not merely found but can be made, and that making it reveals something essential about how communication works.

SECTION 07

Methods & Limitations

This survey draws on 55 sources across six dimensions: historical IALs (11 sources), Tolkien (8), media conlangs (10), linguistic relativity (10), sign language emergence (8), and programming languages (8). Source types include Wikipedia for factual grounding, academic papers from Tolkien Estate scholars[28]Tolkien Estate: Hostetter & Smith →, interviews with professional conlangers[29]TED Ideas: Peterson on Dothraki →, cognitive science research, and primary documents.

Limitations

Source bias toward English-language accounts. Non-English conlang traditions are underrepresented. Wikipedia reliance for factual grounding — mitigated by cross-referencing with academic sources. Recency bias — media conlangs receive more detailed coverage because more sources are available. The programming language parallel is novel — few sources directly compare conlangs and programming languages; the connections drawn are original analysis rather than established scholarship.
