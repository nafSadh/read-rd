LANGUAGE SURVEY · SYNTHESIS PAPER · 55 SOURCES

Constructed Languages: Six Tensions

A comparative survey of invented languages from Hildegard von Bingen to Brainfuck — spanning history, art, science, and code — organized around the six tensions that recur across every tradition of language invention.

March 20266 Dimensions55 Sources6 Tensions
ABSTRACT

Abstract

The impulse to build a language from scratch is at least 870 years old. It has been directed at objectives such as world peace (Esperanto[1]Wikipedia: Esperanto →), worldbuilding (Tolkien[2]Wikipedia: Tolkien’s constructed languages →), entertainment (Klingon[3]Wikipedia: Klingon language →), philosophy of mind (Heptapod B[4]Wikipedia: Story of Your Life →), and computational art (Brainfuck[5]Wikipedia: Brainfuck →). This paper synthesizes findings from a 55-source survey across six dimensions. Significant insights are revealed by examining the tensions between these traditions: art vs. utility, creator vs. community, strong vs. weak Whorf, natural vs. designed, formal vs. natural notation, and minimalism vs. richness.

SECTION 01

The Control Paradox

The question of linguistic control is a fundamental consideration for conlang creators. Johann Martin Schleyer[7]Wikipedia: Schleyer →'s refusal to cede control is often cited as a significant factor in the decline of Volapuk[8]Beinecke Library: Rise and Fall of Volapuk →. By 1889, Volapuk had 283 clubs, 25 periodicals, and 316 textbooks — a larger infrastructure than many natural minority languages. Within five years, its active use had significantly diminished. This decline has been attributed, in part, to the founder's insistence on maintaining sole authority over its development.

L. L. Zamenhof's approach to Esperanto, which involved publishing under a pseudonym and explicitly stating that the language belonged to its speakers, is often considered a contributing factor to its sustained development. Tolkien kept his languages private and evolving for decades — they outlived him. Marc Okrand[9]Wikipedia: Marc Okrand → facilitated community engagement with Klingon through the Klingon Language Institute[10]Klingon Language Institute →. David J. Peterson[11]Wikipedia: David J. Peterson → develops languages under the ownership of studios. The tension is not resolvable — different purposes demand different ownership models.

T2: Creator vs. Community

Schleyer's centralized control over Volapuk is frequently cited as a factor in its decline. Zamenhof's relinquishment of control is often associated with Esperanto's enduring success. NSL was created entirely by its community. A recurring observation suggests that highly centralized control can contribute to the fragility of a constructed language, while community involvement may foster resilience. However, the optimal model of control can vary significantly depending on the language's purpose and context.

SECTION 02

The Essential Role of Speaker Communities

Grammatical complexity alone does not ensure language vitality; a community of speakers is fundamental. The historical trajectories of constructed languages such as Volapük and Esperanto highlight this principle. While Volapük, despite its initial organizational efforts, saw its speaker base diminish, Esperanto developed a sustained community, contributing to its continued use. A compelling illustration of this principle is found in the emergence of Nicaraguan Sign Language[12]Senghas & Coppola: Children Creating Language →: fifty deaf children collectively created a full language to meet their communication needs. This language developed organically, without individual design, committee approval, or external authority.

The development of constructed languages within media franchises further supports this perspective. Klingon maintains a robust speaker community largely due to the efforts of the Klingon Language Institute (KLI). Na’vi has fan communities[13]Salon: How to speak Na’vi → that expand the language beyond what Frommer[14]Campfire: Frommer on Na’vi → originally built. The Language Creation Society[15]Language Creation Society → has contributed to the formalization of the field by establishing institutional infrastructure. These examples consistently demonstrate the primacy of community engagement over purely linguistic design in ensuring language vitality.

The consistent failure modes observed in international auxiliary languages include the chicken-and-egg problem (lack of speakers due to perceived lack of utility, and vice versa), the schism problem (internal reforms leading to community fragmentation), and the English problem (the dominance of a natural language).[16]Omniglot: Why inventing global languages is doomed to fail →

SECTION 03

The Sapir-Whorf Spectrum

Linguistic relativity connects multiple dimensions of this survey. Tolkien explored the concept that language shapes thought — his languages were integral to the development of his narratives. Ted Chiang made it the central premise of Heptapod B[17]Emory: Story of Your Life linguistic analysis →. Boroditsky’s research[18]Winawer et al.: Russian blues (PNAS, 2007) → provides empirical grounding: Russian speakers discriminate blues faster because their language distinguishes goluboy and siniy as separate basic categories.

The evidence supports weak Whorf: language influences perception and reasoning without rigidly constraining cognitive processes. The effects are real but modest, strongest when language is actively engaged, and well-modeled by a Bayesian framework[19]Berkeley: Sapir-Whorf and inference under uncertainty →. Strong Whorf remains unsubstantiated as science but serves as a compelling narrative device in fictional works.

A computational parallel can be observed: APL’s[20]Esolang Wiki: APL as radical notation → Kenneth Iverson received the Turing Award for demonstrating that notation shapes thought about computation. APL programmers think about arrays differently than C programmers. The Whorfian effect is real, even in formal languages.

T3: Strong vs. Weak Whorf

Strong Whorf lacks empirical support as a scientific hypothesis but serves as a compelling artistic theme. Weak Whorf is supported by scientific evidence but may offer less dramatic potential for fictional narratives. This tension persists — it informs both scientific inquiry and narrative development.

SECTION 04

Natural vs. Constructed

NSL and ABSL[21]Wikipedia: Al-Sayyid Bedouin Sign Language → challenge the entire framing. They are natural languages that emerged from scratch, in observable time, without prior input. They are “constructed” by their communities in real time, but no individual designed them. Meanwhile, Modern Hebrew was deliberately revived and standardized.

Sandler, Meir, Padden, and Aronoff[22]Language Emergence: Clues from a New Bedouin Sign Language (2005) → documented ABSL’s emergence in near-real time. Each generation’s signing is more complex than the previous. The boundary between constructed and natural is not a line but a gradient — and emergent sign languages represent a distinct point on that gradient.

T4: Natural vs. Designed

NSL emerged naturally in observable time. Na’vi was designed by one linguist. Both are languages. The distinction between constructed and natural is a spectrum, not a binary.

SECTION 05

Programming Languages as the Most Successful Conlangs

By any measure — number of speakers, practical utility, economic impact — programming languages are the most successful constructed languages ever created. Yet the conlang and programming language communities rarely talk to each other. Both are engaged in the same fundamental activity: designing formal systems for expressing meaning.[23]Wikipedia: Esoteric programming language →

In 1978, Dijkstra[24]Dijkstra: EWD 667 (1978) → argued that formal symbolism is essential to precise thought and that natural language programming is foolish. In 2025, Marc Brooker[25]Brooker: On the success of natural language programming (2025) → responded by noting that LLMs have enabled partial success in natural language programming. These contrasting perspectives highlight the evolving roles of formal and natural languages. Formal languages continue to be recognized for their necessity in achieving precision, while natural language has demonstrated utility for specification, particularly with the advent of LLMs.

The esolang tradition[26]Esolang Wiki: Timeline of esolangs → — INTERCAL, Brainfuck, Piet, Shakespeare, Whitespace — parallels the conlang community in structure and motivation. Over 1,000 esolangs are documented. These languages often share characteristics with artistic conlangs, prioritizing conceptual exploration or aesthetic principles.[27]The Outline: Brainfuck — code designed to hurt →

T5: Formal vs. Natural Notation

Dijkstra argued formalism is essential. LLMs have enabled partial success in natural language programming. While the AI era introduces new dimensions to this dynamic, formal and natural languages are often viewed as complementary rather than interchangeable.

SECTION 06

The Six Tensions

In comparative surveys, points of tension or contradiction frequently yield significant insights. Each of the six tensions identified in this survey recurs across multiple sections and resists simple resolution by favoring one extreme. Their presence is associated with diverse approaches and developments within language invention traditions.

T1: Art vs. Utility

Tolkien’s languages exist for their aesthetic qualities. Esperanto was designed with the aim of fostering international understanding. Media conlangs often occupy an intermediate position. The outcomes of language design vary significantly depending on the designer's primary optimization.

T6: Minimalism vs. Richness

Brainfuck, a programming language, features 8 commands and is Turing-complete. Quenya, a constructed natural language, possesses thousands of words and a comprehensive etymology developed over 50 years. These examples illustrate the spectrum of design choices regarding complexity and expressiveness in constructed systems.

A recurring observation is that language invention, despite its widely divergent traditions, appears to stem from a common underlying premise. A shared characteristic among practitioners is the conviction that language is not merely discovered but can be constructed, and that this construction process can illuminate fundamental aspects of communication.

SECTION 07

Methods & Limitations

This survey draws on 55 sources across six dimensions: historical IALs (11 sources), Tolkien (8), media conlangs (10), linguistic relativity (10), sign language emergence (8), and programming languages (8). Source types include Wikipedia for initial contextualization, academic papers from Tolkien Estate scholars[28]Tolkien Estate: Hostetter & Smith →, interviews with professional conlangers[29]TED Ideas: Peterson on Dothraki →, cognitive science research, and primary documents.

Limitations

Source bias toward English-language accounts. Non-English conlang traditions are underrepresented. Reliance on Wikipedia for initial contextualization: While efforts were made to cross-reference information with academic sources, this approach may introduce a potential for superficiality or lack of detailed scholarly depth in foundational information. Recency bias — media conlangs receive more detailed coverage because more sources are available. The programming language parallel is novel — few sources directly compare conlangs and programming languages; the connections drawn are original analysis rather than established scholarship.
