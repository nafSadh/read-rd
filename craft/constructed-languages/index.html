Constructed Languages

A survey of invented languages from Hildegard von Bingen to Arrival — spanning history, art, science, and code.

The impulse to build a language from scratch is at least 870 years old. It has been directed at world peace (Esperanto), worldbuilding (Tolkien), entertainment (Klingon), philosophy of mind (Heptapod B), scientific discovery (Nicaraguan Sign Language), and computational art (Brainfuck). What unites these traditions is the belief that language is not merely found but can be made — and that making it reveals something about how it works.

This survey maps the landscape across 6 dimensions and 55 sources. The most interesting findings are not within any single tradition but in the tensions between them: art vs. utility, creator vs. community, and the question of whether the language one speaks shapes how one thinks.

Six Tensions

The most valuable findings in any survey are the contradictions — places where different traditions of language invention reveal opposing truths. These six tensions recur across multiple sections and cannot be resolved by choosing one side.
  • T1 Art vs. utility — Tolkien’s languages (pure art) vs. Esperanto (pure utility). Media conlangs occupy the middle. [S2] [S1]
  • T2 Creator vs. community — Schleyer destroyed Volapuk by refusing to let go. Zamenhof saved Esperanto by releasing it. NSL was created entirely by its community. [S1] [S5]
  • T3 Strong vs. weak Whorf — Heptapod B (language determines thought) vs. Boroditsky (language influences thought). Evidence supports weak Whorf; strong Whorf remains powerful fiction. [S4]
  • T4 Natural vs. designed — NSL emerged naturally in observable time. Na’vi was designed by one linguist. The boundary between constructed and natural is a gradient, not a line. [S5] [S3]
  • T5 Formal vs. natural notation — Dijkstra argued formalism is essential. LLMs made natural language programming partly successful. Both are right. [S6]
  • T6 Minimalism vs. richness — Brainfuck (8 commands, Turing-complete) vs. Tolkien’s Quenya (full etymology, 50 years of elaboration). Both are valid expressions of language as art. [S6] [S2]
