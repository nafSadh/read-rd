# S3: Media Conlangs

> From Klingon's warrior grunts to Dothraki's 4,000 words — how Hollywood learned to take invented languages seriously.

**Sources:** 10 (M-01 through M-10)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Covers Klingon, Dothraki/Valyrian, Na'vi, the Language Creation Society, and the professionalization of conlanging.

---

## 1. Klingon: The Accidental Community (1984)

Marc Okrand, a linguist with a PhD from UC Berkeley, was hired for Star Trek III: The Search for Spock (1984) to create authentic-sounding dialogue for the Klingon characters. His task: make the language sound as alien as the characters' ridged foreheads while remaining pronounceable by human actors.

Okrand's design choices were deliberately counter-intuitive:
- **OVS word order** (Object-Verb-Subject) — used by very few natural languages
- **Unusual phoneme inventory**: including sounds rare or absent in European languages
- **No copula** ("to be" expressed differently than in English)
- **Vocabulary limited to warrior culture** initially, then expanded

What nobody expected: fans learned it. By 1992, Dr. Lawrence Schoen founded the Klingon Language Institute (KLI), a nonprofit that now certifies proficiency, publishes a journal (HolQeD), and hosts annual conferences. Speakers use Klingon for original fiction, songs, games, and everyday conversation. Shakespeare's Hamlet and much of the Bible have been translated.

Estimated fluent speakers: 20-30 worldwide, with several thousand having conversational ability. Small by natural language standards, enormous for a conlang designed for a science fiction film.

## 2. The Universal Translator Problem

Star Trek introduced a recurring narrative problem: if a universal translator exists, why bother creating alien languages? The franchise handled this inconsistently — sometimes characters speak English because of the translator, sometimes Klingon is spoken directly. This created a tension between narrative convenience and linguistic worldbuilding that persists in science fiction.

The solution in later productions (and in games, books, and fan communities) was to treat the alien language as a cultural marker rather than a communication barrier. You speak Klingon not because you have to, but because it connects you to Klingon identity.

## 3. David Peterson: The First Full-Time Conlanger

David Peterson's career represents a phase transition in the field. When HBO needed languages for Game of Thrones (2011), the Language Creation Society organized a competition. Peterson won with his Dothraki proposal and went on to create High Valyrian and its descendant languages.

Key facts:
- **40+ conlangs created** for film and television (including Dune's Chakobsa)
- **Only person known to earn a living solely from conlanging**
- **Dothraki**: 4,000+ words, full grammar, modeled on natural language evolution
- **Valyrian family**: designed as a proto-language with multiple daughter languages (High Valyrian, Astapori Valyrian, etc.) — directly inspired by Tolkien's approach
- Published *The Art of Language Invention* (2015), the definitive popular guide to conlanging

Peterson's approach differs from Okrand's in a crucial way: Peterson builds languages that mirror how real human languages work. Full morphological paradigms, naturalistic sound changes, systematic irregularity. Okrand built Klingon to sound alien; Peterson builds languages to sound *real*.

## 4. Na'vi: The Linguist's Showcase (2009)

Paul Frommer, a linguist at USC, was hired by James Cameron to create Na'vi for Avatar. Cameron had already developed 30 Polynesian-inspired words and wanted the full language to feel unfamiliar but beautiful.

Na'vi features:
- **Ejective consonants**: sounds made by compressing air in the throat, common in many non-European languages but startling to English speakers
- **Tripartite case alignment**: a rare system where agents, patients, and subjects of intransitive verbs are all marked differently
- **Infix system**: grammatical modifications inserted *inside* words, not just at the beginning or end
- **Polynesian-influenced phonology**: open syllables, vowel clusters

Cameron continues to expand Na'vi for the Avatar sequels. Some fans have achieved conversational ability. Frommer maintains the language's canonical grammar and vocabulary.

## 5. The Language Creation Society

Founded in 2007, the Language Creation Society (LCS) is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit that:
- Organizes biennial international conferences (LCC)
- Connects conlangers with entertainment industry
- Hosts publications, podcasts, and resources
- Has 13 international board members
- Ran the competition that selected Peterson for Game of Thrones

The LCS represents the professionalization of conlanging. Before the LCS, creating languages was a private hobby. Now it is a recognized field with institutional support, professional standards, and career paths.

## 6. Why Hollywood Hires Conlangers Now

The shift happened gradually:
1. **Star Trek** (1960s-80s): Ad hoc gibberish, then Okrand's Klingon
2. **Lord of the Rings** (2001-03): Tolkien's existing languages used directly
3. **Avatar** (2009): Cameron hired a linguist specifically for the language
4. **Game of Thrones** (2011): Peterson selected via LCS competition
5. **Post-2015**: Professional conlanging becomes standard practice

The drivers:
- **Authenticity**: Audiences can tell when "alien speech" is gibberish vs. real language
- **Merchandising**: A real language generates books, courses, apps, fan content
- **Cultural cachet**: Having a "real" language signals serious worldbuilding
- **Fan engagement**: Learning Na'vi or Dothraki extends franchise engagement

## 7. The Community Phenomenon

The most striking feature of media conlangs is how they develop communities:
- **Klingon**: KLI, annual qep'a' conferences, original literature
- **Na'vi**: LearnNa'vi.org, community-maintained dictionary and grammar
- **Dothraki/Valyrian**: Duolingo courses, fan communities, academic study
- **Elvish**: Tolkien linguistic societies, peer-reviewed journals

These communities often evolve the languages beyond their creators' original scope. Klingon speakers needed vocabulary for everyday Earth life. Na'vi speakers needed words Cameron never imagined. The languages, once released, take on lives of their own — echoing the dynamics of natural language evolution.

## 8. What Sources Agree On

- Professional conlanging is now an established career path
- David Peterson is the most prolific working conlanger
- Fan communities are essential to a media conlang's longevity
- The quality of media conlangs has increased dramatically since the 1960s
- Real linguistic knowledge produces better results than ad hoc invention

## 9. Where Sources Disagree

- Whether media conlangs are "real" languages (linguists debate the threshold)
- Whether fan-driven expansion counts as legitimate evolution
- Whether the entertainment industry values conlangs for artistic or commercial reasons
- Whether any media conlang will achieve the depth of Tolkien's (unlikely, given production timelines)
