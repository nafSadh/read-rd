# S2: Tolkien — The Philologist's Art

> The man who built languages so beautiful he had to invent a world to house them.

**Sources:** 8 (T-01 through T-08)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Covers methodology, specific languages, writing systems, sound changes, and the language-narrative relationship.

---

## 1. Languages First, Stories Second

The single most important fact about Tolkien's creative process: the languages came first. His own words are unambiguous: "The invention of languages is the foundation. The 'stories' were made rather to provide a world for the languages than the reverse. To me a name comes first and the story follows."

This inverts the usual assumption. Most people think Tolkien created languages *for* Middle-earth. The truth is that Middle-earth exists *because of* the languages. Quenya and Sindarin required speakers, history, migrations, wars, and cultural divisions to explain why they diverged from a common ancestor. The Silmarillion is, at its deepest level, a linguistic history.

Tolkien began inventing languages as a child, inspired by encountering Gothic, Finnish, and Welsh. He was a professional philologist — a specialist in Old English at Oxford — and brought his professional tools to his hobby. This is not a novelist dabbling in linguistics. It is a linguist channeling his expertise into fiction.

## 2. Quenya: The Finnish Connection

Quenya, the High Elvish language, draws its aesthetic inspiration from Finnish. Key parallels:

- **Agglutinative morphology**: words built by adding suffixes (Finnish: taloissani = "in my houses"; Quenya: Eldalieva = "of the Eldar")
- **Vowel harmony**: not systematically applied but aesthetically guided by Finnish sound patterns
- **Phonological character**: prevalence of vowels, liquid consonants (l, r), nasals. Few harsh consonant clusters.

Quenya functions as an archaic, literary language in Middle-earth — analogous to Latin in medieval Europe. It is the language of lore, ceremony, and high poetry. Few speak it as a daily tongue by the time of Lord of the Rings.

Tolkien's Quenya grammar includes: ten cases (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative, possessive, ablative, allative, locative, instrumental, respective), a full system of verbal conjugation, dual number (for pairs), and multiple declension classes.

## 3. Sindarin: The Welsh Inspiration

Sindarin, the language of the Grey Elves, takes its phonological character from Welsh. Key features:

- **Consonant mutation**: initial consonants change depending on grammatical context (soft mutation, nasal mutation, mixed mutation) — directly inspired by Welsh lenition
- **Sound aesthetic**: the preference for dh, th, ch, rh sounds that give Welsh its distinctive character
- **Fusional morphology**: unlike Quenya's agglutination, Sindarin changes word forms through internal modification

Sindarin is the "living" Elvish language of the Third Age — what elves actually speak day to day. Its relationship to Quenya mirrors the historical relationship of Welsh to Latin: one is the language of everyday life, the other of learning and ceremony.

## 4. The Family Tree: Comparative Philology in Action

Tolkien used the tools of real comparative philology to construct his language family. The tree:

- **Primitive Quendian** (proto-language of all Elves)
  - **Common Eldarin** (after the Great Journey)
    - **Quenya** (Noldorin and Vanyarin dialects)
    - **Telerin** (the Sea-elves)
    - **Sindarin** (evolved from Common Telerin after separation)
  - **Avarin** (languages of elves who refused the Journey)

For each branch, Tolkien specified:
- Sound changes (e.g., kw > p in Sindarin but preserved in Quenya)
- Morphological innovations
- Borrowings between languages after contact
- Dialectal variation within each language

This is not decoration. Tolkien applied the same methods used to reconstruct Proto-Indo-European. He started with the proto-language's phonological system and invented the mechanisms of sound change needed for each daughter language. The result has the consistency of a real language family.

## 5. Tengwar and Cirth: Writing Systems

**Tengwar** (the Fëanorian script): A featural writing system where the shape of each letter encodes phonetic information. Consonants are represented by tengwar (letters), and vowels by tehtar (diacritical marks). The system has multiple "modes" — different mappings of letters to sounds for different languages (Quenya mode, Sindarin mode, English mode).

The featural principle: the shape of a tengwa indicates its place and manner of articulation. For instance, letters for dental sounds share one shape family, velars another. Voiceless and voiced pairs are distinguished by a consistent visual difference. This is strikingly modern — featural scripts were not common in Tolkien's era, and the concept anticipates ideas that would later emerge in computational phonology.

**Cirth** (the Runic script): Designed for carving in stone or wood, as historical runes were. More angular than Tengwar, with simpler strokes. Used primarily by Dwarves. Less linguistically sophisticated than Tengwar.

## 6. Depth Without Limit

What distinguishes Tolkien's languages from other conlangs is depth. Most constructed languages create enough grammar and vocabulary for their purpose. Tolkien created:

- Full etymological histories for individual words, tracing them through multiple stages of sound change
- Dialectal variation within Quenya and Sindarin
- Register differences (formal vs. informal, archaic vs. modern)
- Poetry in multiple meters
- Internal documents — letters, inscriptions, songs — written *in* the languages
- The Etymologies (published posthumously): a massive document tracing word roots through the entire family tree

His languages were never "finished" because he kept revising them. New ideas about phonology or morphology would cascade through the entire system, requiring updates to etymology, poetry, and narrative. The languages were, like natural languages, always in flux.

## 7. The Lesson for Conlanging

Tolkien demonstrated that a constructed language could have the depth, beauty, and internal consistency of a natural language — given enough expertise, time, and obsessive dedication. His key innovations:

1. **Diachronic construction**: Building not just a language but its *history* — sound changes, borrowings, dialectal splits
2. **Narrative integration**: Language and story reinforcing each other
3. **Aesthetic primacy**: The sound and feel of a language mattering as much as its grammar
4. **Professional rigor**: Applying real linguistic methods, not amateur improvisation

No conlanger since has matched Tolkien's depth. Peterson and Frommer create excellent languages for media, but they work under deadlines and budgets. Tolkien worked for fifty years.

## 8. What Sources Agree On

- Tolkien's languages preceded and generated his fiction
- Finnish inspired Quenya; Welsh inspired Sindarin
- Professional philological training was essential to the quality
- The depth of the languages is unprecedented in conlang history
- Tengwar is a genuinely innovative writing system

## 9. Where Sources Disagree

- Whether Tolkien's languages are "complete" enough to hold real conversation (purists say no; practical users say close enough)
- Whether the constant revision was a feature (kept the languages alive) or a limitation (prevented final publication)
- How much of Tolkien's unpublished linguistic material represents his final intent vs. abandoned experiments
