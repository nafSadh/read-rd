# S1: A History of Language Invention

> From Hildegard's mystical alphabet to Zamenhof's dream of world peace — and why most invented languages failed.

**Sources:** 11 (H-01 through H-11)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Covers Lingua Ignota, Wilkins, Volapuk, Esperanto, Ido, Interlingua, Lojban, and failure analysis.

---

## 1. Lingua Ignota: The First Known Conlang (1150s)

Hildegard von Bingen, a Benedictine abbess, created the Lingua Ignota ("Unknown Language") in the 12th century — the earliest documented constructed language. It consists of approximately 1,000 words with a glossary (Ignotae Linguae) and an alphabet (Litterae Ignotae). The vocabulary draws from Latin and German but is otherwise original.

Hildegard's purpose was mystical, not communicative. She sought to transcend everyday language to reach closer to God. The Lingua Ignota appears in a few of her compositions, used as a sacred or angelic tongue. It was never intended for practical communication and was apparently used only by Hildegard herself and possibly close associates.

What makes Lingua Ignota significant: it establishes that the impulse to create language is at least 870 years old and precedes any practical motivation. The first conlang was art, not engineering.

## 2. Philosophical Languages (1600s-1700s)

The 17th century saw a wave of "philosophical languages" — attempts to create a perfect language that would reveal the true nature of reality. The most famous is John Wilkins' *An Essay towards a Real Character, and a Philosophical Language* (1668).

Wilkins divided all knowledge into 40 main categories, each subdivided recursively. Every concept received a unique character. The ambition: you could deduce a word's meaning from its spelling alone. If you saw the word, you would know what it referred to, even without prior learning.

The project failed for a fundamental reason: knowledge is not a tree. Categories overlap, contexts shift, and new discoveries require restructuring the entire system. But the impulse influenced Leibniz's later work on formal logic and, through him, the entire tradition of symbolic logic.

Other philosophical languages of this era include George Dalgarno's Ars Signorum (1661) and various attempts at a "universal character" — a written language everyone could read regardless of their spoken tongue.

## 3. Volapuk: Meteoric Rise, Spectacular Fall (1879-1890s)

Johann Martin Schleyer, a Catholic priest in Baden, Germany, created Volapuk in 1879. By his account, God instructed him during a sleepless night to create a universal language. The name means "world speech" (vol = world, puk = speech, both derived from English).

Volapuk's rise was extraordinary. Within a decade: 283 clubs worldwide, 25 periodicals, 316 textbooks in 25 languages. The third international Volapuk congress (Paris, 1889) was conducted entirely in Volapuk. Newspapers printed columns in it. Signs appeared in shop windows.

The collapse was equally dramatic. Tensions erupted between reformers (led by Auguste Kerckhoffs, president of the Volapuk Academy) and Schleyer, who insisted on retaining complete control over his creation. When the Academy proposed simplifications, Schleyer rejected them. The Academy abandoned Volapuk for a new project (Idiom Neutral). Most speakers defected. By the mid-1890s, Volapuk was effectively dead.

The lesson: a constructed language lives or dies by its community, and a community cannot survive a founder who refuses to let go. This is the Volapuk cautionary tale.

## 4. Esperanto: The Enduring Survivor (1887-present)

L.L. Zamenhof, an ophthalmologist in Bialystok (then part of the Russian Empire), published *Lingvo Internacia* in 1887. His motivation was deeply personal: growing up in a multilingual city where Russians, Poles, Germans, and Jews lived in mutual suspicion, he believed a neutral common language could foster peace.

Esperanto's design reflects this utopianism:
- 16 grammatical rules, no exceptions
- Agglutinative word formation (build complex words from simple roots)
- Phonetic spelling (one letter = one sound)
- Vocabulary drawn from Romance, Germanic, and Slavic languages
- No gendered grammar beyond optional feminine suffix

Zamenhof made a crucial decision that Schleyer did not: he relinquished control. Esperanto was published under a pseudonym ("Doktoro Esperanto" — Doctor Hopeful) and Zamenhof explicitly stated that the language belonged to its speakers.

Today Esperanto has an estimated 100,000 speakers (with 2 million claiming some knowledge), original literature, annual world congresses (since 1905), UNESCO recognition (resolutions of support in 1954 and 1985), and a small number of native speakers (children raised bilingual in Esperanto). It is by far the most successful international auxiliary language — and still a tiny fraction of any natural language's speaker base.

## 5. The Also-Rans: Ido, Interlingua, Lojban

**Ido** (1907): A reformed Esperanto created by a committee led by Louis Couturat. Claimed to fix Esperanto's irregularities. Instead, it caused a devastating schism. The Ido movement pulled speakers from Esperanto without gaining enough of its own. By the 1920s, Ido was a cautionary tale about perfectionism.

**Interlingua** (1951): Created by the International Auxiliary Language Association. Takes the opposite approach: instead of designing a new grammar, extract the common elements of major European languages. Result is highly readable to Romance language speakers but essentially excludes the non-European world.

**Lojban** (1987): A logical language designed to be syntactically unambiguous. Based on predicate logic. Originally conceived to test the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis: if you give people a perfectly logical language, do they think more logically? The community remains small (~1,000 active) but dedicated. Lojban connects the IAL tradition to the cognitive science questions explored in Section 4.

## 6. Why Most International Auxiliary Languages Failed

The failure modes are consistent:

1. **The chicken-and-egg problem**: Nobody learns a language without speakers, and nobody becomes a speaker without learners. Natural languages escape this trap through birth and immigration. Constructed languages cannot.

2. **Language cannot be separated from culture**: Zamenhof understood this — Esperanto developed its own culture (literature, music, community identity). Languages that remained purely functional tools never developed the emotional attachment needed for survival.

3. **English won**: The rise of English as global lingua franca removed the practical motivation for an IAL. Why learn Esperanto when English gives you access to far more people, content, and economic opportunity?

4. **The schism problem**: Every successful conlang attracts reformers who think they can improve it. The reform process either destroys the community (Volapuk) or creates painful splits (Ido). Natural languages evolve gradually; constructed languages face demands for revolution.

5. **Political neutrality is impossible**: Esperanto claimed neutrality but is overwhelmingly European in vocabulary and grammar. Interlingua is even more Eurocentric. No language can be truly neutral because language embeds cultural assumptions.

## 7. What Sources Agree On

- Lingua Ignota is the first documented conlang (~1150s)
- Volapuk's failure was caused primarily by founder control, not linguistic deficiencies
- Esperanto's survival is exceptional among IALs
- The practical need for an IAL diminished as English became dominant
- Community matters more than grammatical elegance

## 8. Where Sources Disagree

- Whether Esperanto "failed" (it didn't achieve world peace, but 100K+ speakers and a living culture is not nothing)
- Whether the IAL concept is inherently doomed or simply hasn't been tried with the right language at the right time
- The relative importance of linguistic design vs. social factors in a conlang's success
