## Content Quality

### Source Quality: A

All 52 sources now have specific authors, dates, and URLs. The Round 1 weakness (three vague S5 citations) has been resolved with appropriate papers: Sarkamo et al. (2014, Cortex) for Alzheimer's music therapy, Schulkind et al. (1999, Memory & Cognition) for the reminiscence bump, and Wallace (1994, J. Exp. Psychology) for musical mnemonics.

Source selection remains strong across the board. The project cites foundational primary research (Salimpoor 2011, Grahn 2017, Cheung 2019, Juslin 2008/2013, Plomp & Levelt 1965), includes contradicting evidence (Huron 2011 vs. Ladinig 2021), and represents non-Western traditions with substantive sources (MIT Computer Music Journal for gamelan, Smithsonian Folkways for West African rhythm). Some lower-tier sources remain (Vedantu, Vocal Media, Thermaxx Jackets blog) but are used for supplementary rather than core claims.

### Analytical Depth: A+

The strongest dimension of this project, now even more evident with the synthesis paper. Key achievements:

- The synthesis paper traces a single coherent argument across all 7 dimensions: universal physics produces culturally specific music through the mechanism of learned prediction and dopamine reward.
- The equal temperament / AI homogenization parallel is an original observation that connects S2 to S7 with genuine intellectual force.
- The BRECVEMA framework in S4 is handled with appropriate granularity, distinguishing all 8 mechanisms and correctly noting their different timescales, brain regions, and learning requirements.
- The consonance puzzle is framed with precision: solved at the physical level (simple ratios reduce beating) but not at the perceptual level (what counts as pleasant varies across cultures).
- The prolactin hypothesis failure is treated honestly -- the project does not offer a replacement, correctly noting the mechanism remains unknown.
- Cross-section connections are explicitly mapped with hyperlinks (S1 to S2, S2 to S6, S3 to S4, S4 to S5, S5 to S3, S6 to S7, S1 to S7).

Minor weaknesses: the overview deep-dive still compresses some arguments to the point where nuance is lost. The synthesis paper's summaries, while well-cited, occasionally read as condensed versions of the section content rather than fresh analytical perspective.

## Weaknesses

1. **No standalone paper deliverables.** The paper-drive methodology calls for survey papers and tensions papers as distinct deliverables. The synthesis deep-dive serves a similar function but is formatted as an interactive HTML page, not a standalone paper. No formal papers exist.

2. **No slide decks.** Zero presentation materials exist. For a 7-section project with a synthesis paper, at least one summary slide deck would be expected.

3. **No shared CSS/JS.** All 9 deep-dive HTML files embed their full CSS and JavaScript inline. This remains unchanged from Round 1. The CSS is approximately 150 lines duplicated across each file; the JS is approximately 50 lines duplicated. Any design change must be applied to 9 files independently.

4. **Overview deep-dive has no inline citations.** Every other deep-dive has 9-16 inline citations. The overview has zero. This is inconsistent and weakens the overview's credibility relative to the section pages.

5. **No audio demonstrations.** The project is about sound and music but contains no playable audio. The overview.mp3 file exists on disk but is not embedded in any HTML page. Interactive audio demonstrations (playable intervals, Fourier synthesis, tuning comparisons) would significantly strengthen the pedagogical value.

6. **Index page lacks deliverable cards.** The index uses the older section-list layout. The current paper-drive standard calls for deliverable cards at the top of the index page for papers and slides. Since no papers or slides exist yet, this is a secondary concern but should be addressed when deliverables are added.

## Recommendations

### Priority 1: Add inline citations to the overview deep-dive

The overview is the only deep-dive without inline citations. Adding 10-15 citations to key claims in the overview summaries would bring it into consistency with the rest of the project. This is low effort and high impact on project coherence.

### Priority 2: Write a survey paper

The synthesis notes and synthesis deep-dive provide a strong outline. A standalone survey paper (HTML format with 20+ source hyperlinks) would satisfy the paper-drive methodology requirement. The content already exists -- it needs to be reformatted as a formal paper rather than an interactive deep-dive.

### Priority 3: Embed audio content

The overview.mp3 should be embedded in the overview page. Even minimal audio examples would strengthen the project significantly: a pure sine wave vs. a complex tone (S1), Pythagorean vs. equal-tempered fifths (S2), a simple vs. syncopated rhythm (S3). Audio is the medium this project studies; its absence is conspicuous.

### Priority 4: Extract shared CSS/JS

The CSS and JS are functionally identical across all 9 deep-dive files. Extracting to dd-shared.css and dd-shared.js would reduce duplication and make future maintenance faster. This was recommended in Round 1 and remains unaddressed.

### Priority 5: Build a slide deck

A summary slide deck covering the 7 dimensions and key findings would satisfy the paper-drive requirement and provide a useful presentation artifact. The synthesis paper's structure maps naturally to a 15-20 slide deck.

### Priority 6: Add deliverable cards to index

When papers and slides are created, the index should be updated with deliverable cards at the top (before the section list) following the current paper-drive index standard.
