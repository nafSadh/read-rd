# Methodology — The Science of Music

## Research Question

Why does music work the way it does? An interdisciplinary investigation spanning acoustics, psychoacoustics, neuroscience, music theory, ethnomusicology, and technology.

## Scope

This project surveys the science of music across seven dimensions, deliberately crossing disciplinary boundaries that typically keep these fields separate:

1. **Physics** — The acoustic foundations (frequency, amplitude, timbre, resonance)
2. **Mathematics** — The number theory underlying consonance and tuning systems
3. **Neuroscience of Rhythm** — How the brain processes temporal patterns
4. **Neuroscience of Emotion** — Why music triggers emotional and physiological responses
5. **Memory** — How music interacts with memory systems (procedural, episodic, semantic)
6. **Culture** — What is universal vs. culturally specific in musical experience
7. **Technology** — How algorithmic and AI-driven tools are reshaping music creation and consumption

## Source Selection

Sources were selected to represent multiple disciplinary perspectives on each topic:

- **Acoustics & Physics**: HyperPhysics (GSU), Joseph Romano's PHYS 1406 lecture notes, Sabine's architectural acoustics legacy, concert hall design research
- **Music Theory & Mathematics**: Pythagorean tuning literature, Helmholtz's *On the Sensations of Tone*, Plomp & Levelt's critical bandwidth research, temperament history
- **Neuroscience**: Grahn (2017) on basal ganglia/cerebellum, Salimpoor et al. (2011) on dopamine and musical chills, Juslin's BRECVEMA framework, Huron's prolactin hypothesis
- **Memory**: Involuntary musical imagery (INMI) review literature, Alzheimer's music therapy research, reminiscence bump studies
- **Ethnomusicology**: Mehr et al. on music universals and infant-directed song, Patel's *Music, Language, and the Brain*, gamelan tuning studies, raga and maqam systems
- **Technology**: Suno/Udio platforms, Hiller's Illiac Suite history, Spotify recommendation research, BCI music interfaces

## Method

Each section follows the paper-drive methodology:

1. **Literature sweep** — 6-10 sources per section identified through web search
2. **Source adequacy check** — Minimum 5-6 credible sources required before building a section
3. **Notes** — Key findings, data points, and tensions captured in markdown
4. **Deep-dive** — Interactive HTML deep-dive with summary + detail panels, SVG diagrams, source citations
5. **Synthesis** — Cross-section connections identified in `00-synthesis.md`

## Key Tensions Identified

1. **Pinker vs. Patel**: Is music an evolutionary adaptation (Patel, Mehr) or an accidental byproduct of language processing (Pinker's "auditory cheesecake")?
2. **Universal vs. Cultural**: The pentatonic scale appears everywhere, but gamelan tuning rejects the pure octave entirely. How much of consonance is physics vs. learned?
3. **Prolactin debate**: Huron (2011) proposed prolactin explains why sad music feels good; Ladinig et al. (2021) found no prolactin increase. The mechanism remains unknown.
4. **Democratization paradox**: AI tools let anyone create music, but algorithmic homogenization may narrow what gets heard. More creators, less diversity?
5. **Western bias in research**: Most neuroscience of music uses Western tonal music as stimuli. Findings about "consonance" and "dissonance" may not generalize to non-Western systems.
