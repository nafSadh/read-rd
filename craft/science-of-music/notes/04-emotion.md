# S4: The Neuroscience of Musical Emotion — Notes

## Core Concepts

### Musical Chills and Dopamine
- Salimpoor et al. (2011, Nature Neuroscience): PET + fMRI study
  - Dopamine released during peak emotional responses to music ("chills")
  - Functional dissociation: caudate active during ANTICIPATION, nucleus accumbens during EXPERIENCE
  - Same reward circuitry as food, sex, drugs — music is treated as a primary reward
  - Physiological markers of chills: skin conductance changes, heart rate, temperature changes
- Ferreri et al. (2019, PNAS): pharmacological manipulation
  - Levodopa (dopamine precursor) ENHANCED musical pleasure
  - Risperidone (dopamine blocker) REDUCED musical pleasure
  - First causal evidence (not just correlational) for dopamine's role

### The BRECVEMA Framework
Juslin's model of 8 mechanisms through which music induces emotion:

1. **Brainstem Reflex**: Automatic response to acoustic features (loud, sudden, dissonant sounds). Fast, subcortical, no learning required.
2. **Rhythmic Entrainment**: Neural oscillations sync with rhythm → affects arousal and emotion. Links to motor system.
3. **Evaluative Conditioning**: Music paired with positive/negative experiences → conditioned emotional response. "Our song" effect.
4. **Emotional Contagion**: Music mimics vocal expression → listener "catches" the expressed emotion. Slow tempo + minor key → sadness.
5. **Visual Imagery**: Music evokes mental images → images carry emotional content. Individual and culturally variable.
6. **Episodic Memory**: Music triggers autobiographical memories → emotion attached to the memory. "Darling, they're playing our tune."
7. **Musical Expectancy**: Music violates, confirms, or delays expectations → surprise, satisfaction, tension. Based on learned stylistic norms.
8. **Aesthetic Judgment**: Reflective evaluation of music as art → appreciation, awe, being moved. Most cognitively complex mechanism.

Key insight: different mechanisms operate on different timescales, involve different brain regions, and require different amounts of musical knowledge.

### Musical Expectancy and the Amygdala
- Cheung et al. (2019, Current Biology): uncertainty and surprise interact to predict pleasure
  - High uncertainty + high surprise → maximum pleasure (unexpected resolution after suspense)
  - Low uncertainty + low surprise → comfortable but less pleasurable (predictable passages)
  - Amygdala and hippocampus: reflect interaction of uncertainty and surprise
  - Nucleus accumbens: reflects uncertainty alone
  - Musical pleasure is essentially a prediction game — the brain enjoys being uncertain and then surprised

### Why Sad Music Feels Good
- **Huron's prolactin hypothesis** (2011): sad music triggers prolactin release (consolation hormone); pleasant feeling of being comforted without actual loss
- **Ladinig et al. test** (2021): measured prolactin during sad music listening — NO significant increase. Theory not supported.
- **Alternative explanations**:
  - Aesthetic distance: we know the sadness isn't real → safe space to experience negative emotion
  - Mixed emotions: sad music triggers both sadness and beauty appreciation simultaneously
  - Catharsis: emotional release through vicarious experience
- The mechanism remains genuinely unknown as of 2025

### The Minor Key Question
- Western association: minor = sad, major = happy
- But this is NOT universal — depends on cultural musical context
- Amygdala shows differential activation for minor vs major in Western listeners
- Question: would Indian classical music listeners show the same amygdala response to Western minor keys?
