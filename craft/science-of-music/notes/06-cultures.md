# S6: Music Across Cultures — Notes

## Core Concepts

### Universals
- Every known culture has music — no exceptions documented in the ethnographic record
- Mehr et al. (2019, Science): Natural History of Song project analyzed recordings from 86 societies
- Universal song types: lullabies, dance songs, healing songs, love songs
- Shared acoustic features within types: lullabies worldwide share descending contour, narrow pitch range, repetitive structure, slow tempo
- Pentatonic scale appears independently across civilizations: China, Scotland, West Africa, Native American, Japanese
- Ancient bone flutes (40,000+ years) play pentatonic patterns

### Infant-Directed Song
- Mehr et al. (2020): 144 infants relaxed to foreign lullabies vs non-lullabies
  - Heart rate, pupillometry, EDA all showed relaxation response
  - Consistent across first year of life → not learned from exposure
  - Parents overwhelmingly chose foreign lullabies to calm fussy infants
- Aubinet (2024): systematic ethnographic review confirms lullaby universality
- Implication: some musical features are biologically recognized, not culturally learned

### Indian Raga System
- Raga = melodic framework with specific ascending/descending patterns, ornaments, and rules
- Each raga associated with: specific time of day, season, emotion (rasa)
- 22 shruti (microtones) per octave — far more granular than Western 12-semitone system
- Not just a scale — includes prescribed ornamentation, characteristic phrases, mood
- Emotional mapping is codified in theory (Natya Shastra, ~200 BCE)

### Arabic Maqam
- Maqam = modal system with quarter-tone intervals absent from Western music
- Each maqam has rules for melodic development, modulation, emotional character
- Structurally similar to raga: both are modal frameworks that prescribe melodic behavior, not just note sets
- Quarter-tones give maqam a more chromatic and nuanced sound than Western modes

### Indonesian Gamelan
- Slendro: 5-note scale with roughly equidistant intervals
- Pelog: 7-note scale with unequal intervals
- Key difference: the octave is NOT based on 2:1 frequency ratio
  - Western ethnomusicologists who try to analyze gamelan using Western acoustics miss the point
  - Tuning is aesthetic choice, not mathematical derivation
- Each gamelan set has unique tuning — instruments not interchangeable between sets
- Paired instruments deliberately detuned → interference beating creates characteristic shimmer
- In religious contexts, the beating is understood as conveying divine presence

### Chinese Pentatonic
- Gong scale: five notes per octave, documented for 24+ centuries
- Names: Gong, Shang, Jue, Zhi, Yu (corresponding to five elements, five planets, five colors)
- Cosmological framework: music as expression of cosmic order
- Pentatonic basis shared with much of East Asian traditional music

### Pinker vs. Patel vs. Mehr
- **Pinker (1997)**: Music is "auditory cheesecake" — a pleasant byproduct of adaptations for language processing. No survival value.
- **Patel (2008)**: Music and language share neural resources (SSIRH). Music may have been co-selected with language. Not just a byproduct — too deeply integrated with core cognitive systems.
- **Mehr & Krasnow (2021)**: Music evolved for credible signaling:
  - Coalition signaling: synchronized music/dance demonstrates group coordination to allies and enemies
  - Parental attention signaling: lullabies credibly signal that parent is attending to infant
  - Neither Pinker's byproduct nor purely sexual selection (Darwin's hypothesis)

### The Western Research Bias
- Most neuroscience of music uses Western tonal music as stimuli
- WEIRD populations (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) as subjects
- Findings about "universal" responses to consonance/dissonance may not generalize
- Example: minor key = "sad" is a Western association, not a universal one
- Need for cross-cultural neuroscience studies with non-Western music and listeners
