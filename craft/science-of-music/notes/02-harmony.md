# S2: Harmony & the Math of Consonance — Notes

## Core Concepts

### Pythagoras and the Blacksmith's Hammer
- Legend: Pythagoras heard harmonious sounds from blacksmith's hammers of different weights
- Discovery: consonant intervals correspond to simple integer ratios of string lengths
- Historical significance: first known connection between mathematics and sensory experience
- Modern note: the hammer story is mythological; the monochord experiments are real

### Simple Ratios and Consonance
- Octave: 2:1 (most consonant after unison)
- Perfect fifth: 3:2
- Perfect fourth: 4:3
- Major third: 5:4
- Minor third: 6:5
- Simpler ratio → more consonant (lower harmonics overlap more)

### Helmholtz's Beating Frequencies
- Hermann von Helmholtz, *On the Sensations of Tone* (1863)
- When two nearby frequencies sound simultaneously, they produce "beats" at the difference frequency
- Beats within ~15-40 Hz range perceived as "roughness" → the sensation of dissonance
- Critical bandwidth: ~1/3 octave (varies with frequency)
- Plomp & Levelt (1965): formalized the relationship between critical bandwidth and consonance/dissonance

### The Problem of Tuning
- **Pythagorean tuning**: stack pure 3:2 fifths → 12 fifths overshoots 7 octaves by 23.46 cents (the Pythagorean comma)
- **Just intonation**: all intervals as pure ratios → beautiful in one key, unusable in others
- **Meantone temperament**: narrow the fifths slightly → good thirds, limited keys (dominant ~1500-1700)
- **Well temperament**: unequal but all keys usable → each key has its own "color" (~1700-1850)
- **Equal temperament**: divide octave into 12 equal parts (100 cents each) → all keys identical, none pure

### Bach's Well-Tempered Clavier
- Two books (1722, 1742): preludes and fugues in all 24 major and minor keys
- NOT written for equal temperament — "well-tempered" means some form of unequal temperament where all keys work
- Political statement: demonstrating that music need not be confined to a handful of keys
- The title argues for tuning flexibility, not tuning uniformity
- Recent scholarship (Lehman, Lindley): attempts to reconstruct Bach's specific temperament

### Non-Western Tuning Systems
- **Javanese gamelan**: slendro (5 notes, roughly equidistant) and pelog (7 notes, unequal)
  - Deliberately rejects the pure octave
  - Each gamelan set has unique tuning → instruments not interchangeable
  - Paired instruments tuned slightly apart → intentional beating creates "shimmering" quality
  - Tuning is aesthetic choice, not mathematical system
- **Indian shruti system**: 22 microtones per octave (not all used in any one raga)
- **Arabic maqam**: uses quarter-tones absent from Western systems
- **Chinese pentatonic**: Gong scale, 24+ centuries old, five notes per octave

## Key Insight
Equal temperament is a specifically Western compromise that sacrifices tonal purity for modulatory freedom. It is neither "correct" nor universal — it is one solution to a mathematical problem that other cultures chose not to solve (or didn't recognize as a problem).
