# S3: Rhythm, Time & the Brain — Notes

## Core Concepts

### Entrainment
- Neural oscillations synchronize with external rhythmic stimuli
- Endogenous entrainment improves temporal prediction and processing
- Not just passive following — the brain actively generates predictions about when the next beat will occur
- Explains why we involuntarily tap our feet: motor system entrains to auditory rhythm

### Meter and Syncopation
- Meter: hierarchical organization of beats into strong/weak patterns (2/4, 3/4, 4/4, etc.)
- Syncopation: accent on normally weak beats → creates tension and "groove"
- Sweet spot hypothesis: moderate syncopation maximizes groove; too much syncopation becomes unpredictable and loses groove
- fMRI shows syncopated rhythms activate SMA more than isochronous rhythms

### Polyrhythm
- Multiple simultaneous rhythmic patterns with different periodicities
- West African drumming: 3:2 cross-rhythm is foundational
  - Divisive rhythm: time divided into different fractions simultaneously
  - Bell pattern (timeline): asymmetric repeating pattern that organizes the ensemble
  - Master drummer plays against the timeline, creating maximum rhythmic complexity
- Afro-Cuban clave: 3-2 or 2-3 son clave derived from West African bell patterns
  - Links Sub-Saharan African tradition to Caribbean and Latin American music
  - Structural principle, not just a rhythmic pattern
- Agawu's "asymmetrical topoi": term for the recurring rhythmic kernels in West African music

### Neural Basis of Beat Perception
- **Basal ganglia**: beat finding and internal rhythm generation
  - Patients with Parkinson's (basal ganglia disorder) have difficulty finding the beat in complex rhythms
  - Active especially for rhythms requiring internal beat generation (when the beat is implied, not stated)
- **Cerebellum**: temporally precise encoding of individual events
  - Patients with cerebellar damage show deficits with fast tempi
  - Important for encoding polyrhythmic stimuli
  - More "complex timing" vs. basal ganglia's "beat-based timing"
- **Supplementary Motor Area (SMA)**: active for metric processing
  - Bilateral activation for complex beat-based rhythms
  - Motor planning area — rhythm engages motor system even without movement
- **Auditory cortex**: frequency-locked neural tracking of rhythmic patterns
- **Premotor cortex**: audiomotor coupling

### Rhythm vs. Melody Processing
- Rhythm and pitch/melody processed by partially distinct neural networks
- Double dissociations: patients with amusia (pitch disorders) may retain rhythmic ability and vice versa
- Rhythm processing more strongly engages subcortical structures (basal ganglia, cerebellum)
- Melody processing more strongly engages cortical structures (auditory cortex, temporal regions)

### Groove
- The quality of music that makes you want to move
- Optimal complexity: moderate syncopation + clear metric framework
- Individual differences: musicians show stronger entrainment to complex grooves
- Cultural component: what "grooves" depends partly on familiar rhythmic vocabulary
