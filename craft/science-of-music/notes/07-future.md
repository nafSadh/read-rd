# S7: The Future of Music — Notes

## Core Concepts

### Algorithmic Composition History
- **Illiac Suite** (1957): first computer-composed score
  - Lejaren Hiller & Leonard Isaacson, University of Illinois
  - ILLIAC I computer (one of the first general-purpose electronic computers)
  - Four movements testing different procedures: cantus firmus generation, rule-based counterpoint, rhythmic dynamics, Markov chains
  - Not AI in the modern sense — rule-based systems and stochastic processes
- **Iannis Xenakis** (1960s): stochastic music using probability theory
- **David Cope's EMI** (1980s-90s): Experiments in Musical Intelligence — recombination of existing musical patterns
- **Google Magenta** (2016): deep learning applied to music generation
- **MuseNet/Jukebox** (OpenAI, 2019-20): transformer-based full music generation

### Modern AI Music Platforms
- **Suno**: ~25 million users; text-to-music generation
  - 82% of listeners couldn't distinguish AI from human-generated tracks
  - First AI-creator record deal: imoliver signed with Hallwood Media (July 2025)
  - Xania Monet: $3M deal with Hallwood (2025)
  - Founded by former Meta researchers
- **Udio**: founded by former Google DeepMind researchers
  - Backed by a16z, will.i.am, United Masters
  - Full song generation from text prompts
- **AIVA**: AI composition assistant, 250+ styles, three-tier pricing (free w/attribution, Standard, Pro w/full copyright)
- **Mubert**: algorithmic combination of human-created samples; API-first approach
- Market size: $642.8M (2024) → projected $3B by 2030 (29.5% CAGR)

### Spotify and Algorithmic Homogenization
- Recommendation engine based on collaborative filtering + deep learning (Echo Nest acquisition, 2014)
- Concerning trends (2018-2023):
  - Average acoustic diversity score dropped 17%
  - Median tempo of top-streamed songs: 112 BPM → 119 BPM
  - Dynamic range narrowed by 3.2 dB
- Artists producing "Spotify-friendly" music to match algorithm preferences
- Filter bubble effect: listeners exposed to narrower range of styles over time
- Counterpoint: Spotify also enables discovery of niche genres (long tail argument)

### Brain-Computer Music Interfaces (BCMI)
- EEG-based systems that translate brain activity into musical parameters
- BrainiBeats: dual-BCI using inter-brain synchrony between two users
- Neurophone: emotional neurofeedback for music interaction
- Current limitations: low spatial resolution, artifact-prone, limited bandwidth
- Future potential: as BCI technology improves (Neuralink, Synchron), direct brain-to-music interfaces could bypass traditional instruments entirely
- FDA breakthrough designations for BCI startups (2025) signal clinical maturity

### The Democratization Paradox
- AI tools let anyone create music → more creators than ever
- But: outputs cluster around Western pop conventions (4/4 time, verse-chorus, pentatonic/diatonic melodies)
- Training data bias: models trained on Western-dominated datasets reproduce Western-dominated styles
- Economic paradox: more music being created, but harder to make a living as a musician
  - Spotify pays ~$0.003-0.005 per stream
  - AI-generated content flooding platforms → more competition for attention
  - Labels signing AI creators at lower costs than human artists
- Cultural paradox: more access to tools, less diversity in output

### Synthwave and Nostalgia
- Synthwave/retrowave: genre explicitly recreating 1980s electronic sounds
- Broader pattern: each generation's "new" music often reconstructs sounds from ~20-30 years earlier
- Connection to reminiscence bump (S5): the sounds we love most are from our formative years
- AI music generation could accelerate this cycle by making it trivially easy to recreate past styles

### Music as the First AI-Disrupted Creative Domain
- Music was arguably the first creative field where AI outputs became indistinguishable from human work
- Preceded widespread AI disruption in visual art (2022), writing (2022-23), video (2024-25)
- Implications for other creative fields: music as leading indicator
- Unique challenges: copyright (training on copyrighted music), authenticity, economic displacement
