# S5: Music & Memory — Notes

## Core Concepts

### Earworms (Involuntary Musical Imagery / INMI)
- 90%+ of people experience earworms regularly
- Auditory cortex activates during INMI just as during real listening — brain can't tell the difference
- Phonological loop (working memory component) initiates and maintains the imagery independently
- Typical fragment: 15-30 seconds of a melody, looping
- Triggers: recent exposure, emotional association, environmental cue
- Correlation with cortical thickness in auditory and prefrontal regions
- More common in musicians and people with high musical engagement
- Tend to be simple, repetitive melodies with distinctive intervals

### Alzheimer's and Musical Memory
- Patients who cannot recognize family members can sing songs learned decades ago
- Musical memory uses different encoding pathways:
  - Procedural memory (motor sequences — "how to" sing) — preserved longer in Alzheimer's
  - Supplementary motor area (SMA) relatively spared in early/mid Alzheimer's
  - Emotional memory — amygdala connections preserved even when hippocampal (declarative) memory fails
- Therapeutic applications:
  - Music therapy reduces agitation and improves mood in dementia patients
  - Familiar music can unlock moments of lucidity
  - Singing bypasses damaged language pathways (speech production area ≠ singing production area)

### The Reminiscence Bump
- Autobiographical memories disproportionately cluster around ages 15-25
- Musical corollary: songs from this period are the most emotionally powerful throughout life
- "Soundtrack of your life" effect — music heard during identity formation becomes permanently linked to self-concept
- Explanations:
  - Neuroplasticity: adolescent brain is more plastic → stronger encoding
  - Hormonal: pubertal hormones enhance emotional memory encoding
  - Identity formation: music becomes part of "who I am" during this period
  - Novelty: first exposure to music genres → stronger encoding than later re-exposures
- Cross-cultural consistency: the bump appears across cultures, though the specific age range may vary

### Musical Mnemonics
- Alphabet song: nearly universal in English-speaking education
- Melody serves as a retrieval scaffold for verbal information
- Mechanism: melody adds rhythmic structure + pitch contour + emotional encoding → multiple retrieval cues
- Clinical evidence:
  - MS patients: sung material improved word memory vs spoken
  - Stroke patients: singing improved verbal narrative recall
  - Alzheimer's patients: enhanced recognition of sung (vs spoken) lyrics
- Educational applications: multiplication tables, grammar rules, historical dates
- Why it works: the melody provides temporal structure that constrains what comes next → reduces search space for recall

### The Proust Effect (Auditory)
- Music as involuntary memory trigger (analogous to Proust's madeleine)
- A few bars of a forgotten song can unlock vivid autobiographical memories with full emotional context
- More powerful than visual or olfactory cues for some people
- Mechanism: music activates emotional + episodic + motor memory systems simultaneously
