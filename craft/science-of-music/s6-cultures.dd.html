02

Lullabies Across Cultures

Mehr et al. (2020) tested whether infants respond to the universal features of lullabies even from unfamiliar cultures. In a study of 144 infants, babies relaxed more to foreign lullabies than to non-lullabies from the same cultures — measured by heart rate, pupillometry, and electrodermal activity.[2]Mehr et al.: Infants Relax to Unfamiliar Foreign Lullabies (Nature Human Behaviour, 2020) → The response was consistent across the first year of life, suggesting biological recognition rather than learned association.

A systematic ethnographic review by Aubinet (2024) confirmed the universality of lullaby features across cultures.[3]Aubinet: Lullabies and Universality — An Ethnographic Review (Music Perception, 2024) → The implication is profound: some musical features are biologically recognized, not culturally learned. Parents worldwide independently converge on the same acoustic strategies to calm infants.

03

Non-Western Modal Systems

Western music theory treats scales as collections of notes within an octave. But the major modal systems outside the West — Indian raga, Arabic maqam, Indonesian gamelan — are far more than note sets. They are comprehensive frameworks for melodic behavior, emotional expression, and sometimes spiritual practice.

Indian Raga

Melodic framework with ascending/descending patterns, prescribed ornaments, and time-of-day associations. 22 shruti (microtones) per octave.[4]Global Music Theory: Comparison of Musical Modes Worldwide →

Arabic Maqam

Modal system with quarter-tone intervals. Each maqam prescribes melodic development, modulation, and emotional character.

Javanese Gamelan

Slendro (5-note) and pelog (7-note) scales. No pure octave concept. Each ensemble uniquely tuned.[5]Exploring the Many Tunings of Balinese Gamelan (Computer Music Journal, MIT Press, 2024) →

Chinese Pentatonic

Five-note Gong scale, documented for 24+ centuries. Notes mapped to five elements, planets, and colors.

These systems are not “primitive” alternatives to Western tonality. They are sophisticated musical languages that map the physics of sound to human experience in ways that equal temperament does not and cannot. The diversity of modal systems reflects the diversity of human musical thought.

04

Pinker vs. Patel vs. Mehr

The evolutionary status of music is one of the great unresolved debates in cognitive science. Three major positions have shaped the field.

Music Is Adaptive

  • Patel (2008): shared neural resources with language via SSIRH
  • Mehr & Krasnow (2021): credible signaling of coalition strength
  • Universal presence in all cultures suggests deep evolutionary roots

Music Is Byproduct

  • Pinker (1997): “auditory cheesecake” — pleasant byproduct of language
  • No clear survival advantage for musical ability
  • Could be a technology, not an adaptation

Mehr and Krasnow (2021) proposed a middle path: music evolved for credible signaling.[6]Mehr & Krasnow: Origins of Music in Credible Signaling (BBS, 2021) → Synchronized music and dance credibly demonstrate group coordination to allies and enemies. Lullabies credibly signal that a parent is attending to an infant. Neither Pinker’s pure byproduct nor purely sexual selection (Darwin’s hypothesis).

05

The WEIRD Research Bias

Most neuroscience of music uses WEIRD populations (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) as subjects and Western tonal music as stimuli. Findings about “universal” brain responses to consonance, dissonance, and emotional associations may reflect cultural homogeneity rather than genuine universals.[7]BYU Humanities Center: Is Music Simply Auditory Cheesecake? →

The minor-key/sadness association, for example, is robust in Western listeners but may not generalize to Indian classical music listeners, who encode emotional associations through entirely different melodic and ornamental features. Cross-cultural neuroscience studies with non-Western music and listeners are urgently needed. The connection to S4 (Emotion) is direct: emotional associations in music are culturally encoded.

06

The Pentatonic Question

The pentatonic scale appears independently across civilizations — China, Scotland, West Africa, Native America, Japan. Ancient bone flutes over 40,000 years old play pentatonic-like patterns. Is this evidence for a biologically preferred scale, or merely a convergent solution to the constraints of instrument design and vocal production?

The overtone series (S1) provides a physical basis: the first five unique pitches in the harmonic series approximate a pentatonic scale. But gamelan and other traditions use scales that do not align with the overtone series, demonstrating that physical acoustics constrains but does not determine cultural scale choices. The pentatonic may be widespread because it is the simplest scale that provides melodic variety while avoiding the semitone tensions of diatonic scales — a practical optimum, not a universal law.

Section 01

Musical Universals

The Natural History of Song

The most ambitious attempt to identify musical universals is the Natural History of Song project led by Samuel Mehr and Manvir Singh.[1]Mehr & Singh: Universality in Song (2018); Mehr et al., Science (2019) → The project assembled a corpus of ethnographic recordings from 86 societies spanning every inhabited continent and analyzed them for shared acoustic features. The central finding: while the specific details of musical systems vary enormously, certain song types — lullabies, dance songs, healing songs, love songs — have recognizable acoustic signatures that transcend cultural boundaries.

What Is Universal

Lullabies worldwide share slow tempo, descending melodic contour, narrow pitch range, and repetitive structure. Dance songs share faster tempi, stronger rhythmic regularity, and wider dynamic range. These acoustic regularities suggest that some aspects of music are shaped by universal human biology — the physiology of infant soothing, the biomechanics of movement, the neuroscience of arousal regulation.

What Is Not Universal

Scales, harmonic systems, instrument types, performance practices, and aesthetic values vary enormously across cultures. The tuning diversity explored in S2 is one example. The emotional associations of specific intervals (minor = sad) are another. The universals appear to be at the level of function (what music is for) rather than form (what music sounds like).

Key Insight

Music is universal in the sense that every culture has it and certain functional categories share acoustic features. But the specific musical systems cultures build on this universal foundation are enormously diverse. The universals constrain; the culture creates.

Section 02

Lullabies Across Cultures

The Infant Response

Mehr et al. (2020) presented 144 infants with recordings of lullabies and non-lullabies from unfamiliar foreign cultures.[2]Mehr et al.: Infants Relax to Unfamiliar Foreign Lullabies (Nature Human Behaviour, 2020) → Heart rate, pupil dilation, and skin conductance all showed greater relaxation in response to lullabies than to non-lullabies, even though the music came from cultures the infants had never been exposed to. This response was consistent across the first year of life, ruling out learned familiarity as an explanation.

Universal Acoustic Features

Aubinet (2024) conducted a systematic ethnographic review confirming that lullaby features are shared cross-culturally:[3]Aubinet: Lullabies and Universality (Music Perception, 2024) → descending contour, narrow pitch range, repetitive structure, slow tempo, and soft dynamic level. These features are not arbitrary: they match the acoustic properties that physiologically promote relaxation in human infants (slow rhythms entrain cardiac rhythms; repetition reduces arousal; descending pitch mimics the vocal contour of calming speech).

Implications

The lullaby finding is the strongest evidence for biologically grounded musical universals. Parents worldwide, without any contact or communication, independently converge on the same acoustic strategies to calm their infants. This suggests that at least some musical features are recognized by innate perceptual mechanisms rather than learned through cultural exposure.

Key Insight

Lullabies may be the Rosetta Stone of musical universals. Their cross-cultural consistency and the biological basis of the infant response together provide the strongest case that some musical features are hardwired rather than culturally constructed.

Section 03

Non-Western Modal Systems

Indian Raga

A raga is not a scale but a comprehensive melodic framework. Each raga prescribes specific ascending (aroha) and descending (avaroha) patterns, characteristic phrases (pakad), required ornaments (gamaka), and associations with time of day, season, and emotion (rasa). Indian classical music uses 22 shruti (microtones) per octave — far more granular than Western music’s 12 semitones.[4]Global Music Theory: Comparative Musical Modes → The emotional mapping is codified in theory dating to the Natya Shastra (~200 BCE).

Arabic Maqam

The maqam system employs quarter-tone intervals that fall between Western semitones. Each maqam is a set of rules for melodic development, modulation between related maqams, and emotional expression. The structural similarity to raga is notable: both are modal frameworks that prescribe melodic behavior, not just pitch collections.[8]Eastern Scales and Modes: A Journey into Timeless Melodies (Barbad Piano) →

Indonesian Gamelan

Gamelan tuning challenges Western assumptions at the most fundamental level. The octave is not based on the 2:1 frequency ratio. Each gamelan set has unique tuning — instruments are not interchangeable between ensembles. Paired instruments are deliberately detuned to produce interference beats that create the characteristic shimmering quality. In religious contexts, these beats are understood as conveying divine presence.[5]Exploring the Many Tunings of Balinese Gamelan (Computer Music Journal, 2024) →

Key Insight

These systems demonstrate that the overtone series provides a universal acoustic basis, but what a culture does with that basis varies enormously. Gamelan’s deliberate beating, Indian music’s microtonal inflections, and Arabic quarter-tones are not impure versions of Western tonality — they are different systems built on the same physical foundations.

Section 04

Pinker vs. Patel vs. Mehr

Pinker: Auditory Cheesecake (1997)

Steven Pinker famously argued that music is a pleasant byproduct of adaptations evolved for language processing. Just as cheesecake exploits taste receptors evolved to detect nutritious foods, music exploits auditory processing mechanisms evolved for speech. On this view, music has no survival value and is not an adaptation.[7]BYU Humanities Center: Is Music Simply Auditory Cheesecake? →

Patel: Shared Neural Resources (2008)

Aniruddh Patel proposed the Shared Syntactic Integration Resource Hypothesis (SSIRH): music and language share neural resources for processing hierarchical sequences.[9]Patel: Music, Language, and the Brain (Oxford University Press, 2008) → This deep neural integration suggests music is not merely a byproduct but may have been co-selected with language. The overlap is too extensive and too fundamental to be merely parasitic.

Mehr & Krasnow: Credible Signaling (2021)

Mehr and Krasnow argued that music evolved specifically for credible signaling.[6]Mehr & Krasnow: Origins of Music in Credible Signaling (BBS, 2021) → Synchronized group music credibly signals coalition strength because coordination is genuinely difficult to fake. Lullabies credibly signal parental attention because singing to an infant requires sustained, focused engagement. These signals are “honest” in the evolutionary sense: they cannot be easily counterfeited.

Key Insight

The evolutionary status of music remains genuinely unresolved. What has changed is the quality of the evidence: the Natural History of Song data, the infant lullaby experiments, and the neural overlap with language have shifted the field away from Pinker’s dismissive view without settling on a definitive alternative.

Section 05

The WEIRD Research Bias

The Problem

The vast majority of neuroscience studies on music use Western tonal music as stimuli and Western participants as subjects. This creates a fundamental problem: when a study finds that “the brain” responds in a certain way to a musical feature, it has actually found that Western brains trained on Western music respond that way. The generalizability to non-Western populations is unknown.

Specific Examples

The minor-key/sadness association is robust in Western listeners but may not extend to Indian classical music listeners, who encode emotions through raga-specific melodic and ornamental features. Consonance preferences measured in Western subjects may not generalize to Javanese gamelan listeners who value deliberate dissonance. The BRECVEMA framework (S4) acknowledges that mechanisms like evaluative conditioning and musical expectancy are culturally shaped, but most empirical tests use Western stimuli.

What Needs to Change

Cross-cultural neuroscience studies using non-Western music with non-Western listeners are urgently needed. Some promising work exists: studies with Amazonian groups who have minimal Western music exposure suggest that octave equivalence may be truly universal but consonance preferences may be culturally learned. This distinction between genuinely universal and merely Western-dominant is the central challenge for the field.

Key Insight

The WEIRD bias is not merely a methodological footnote. It potentially invalidates many claims about “universal” musical cognition. Until the same experiments are run with gamelan listeners hearing gamelan, or raga listeners hearing ragas, we cannot distinguish universal neuroscience from Western neuroscience.

Section 06

The Pentatonic Question

Independent Emergence

The pentatonic scale (five notes per octave) has emerged independently in China, Scotland, West Africa, Japan, Native America, and numerous other traditions. Ancient bone flutes dating to over 40,000 years ago appear to play pentatonic-like patterns. This independent convergence across time and geography has been cited as evidence that the pentatonic represents a biologically preferred scale.

Physical Basis

The overtone series provides a possible physical explanation. The first five unique pitch classes in the harmonic series (fundamental, octave, fifth, fourth, major third) approximate the pentatonic scale. If the auditory system is tuned to the overtone series (as the consonance research in S2 suggests), then pentatonic scales would be inherently easy to process and remember.

But Not Universal

The pentatonic hypothesis faces a challenge from the existence of non-pentatonic traditions. Gamelan uses scales that do not align with the overtone series. Arabic maqam uses quarter-tones. Indian ragas use microtonal inflections between pentatonic scale degrees. The pentatonic may be the most common scale, but it is not the only scale, and its prevalence may reflect practical advantages (simplicity, vocal accessibility, instrument design constraints) rather than biological necessity.

Key Insight

The pentatonic scale may be widespread because it is the simplest scale that provides melodic variety while avoiding the small-interval tensions of diatonic and chromatic scales. It is a practical optimum for many musical contexts — but not a universal law, as gamelan and maqam demonstrate.
