Soda Fountains as Civic Space

Drugstore culture, women's public space, the temperance movement connection, and the civil rights sit-ins at segregated lunch counters — the soda fountain as contested American space.

March 20268 Sources1870s–1960s
SECTION 01

The Emergence of the Soda Fountain

The soda fountain emerged as a distinct American institution in the second half of the 19th century. Pharmacies dispensed carbonated mineral waters as health preparations, and the addition of flavored syrups made these waters palatable and popular. Robert Green is credited with adding ice cream to soda water in 1874, creating the ice cream soda. By the 1880s and 1890s, soda fountains had become standard fixtures in American drugstores, combining the dispensing function of the pharmacy with a social space for refreshment.

The soda fountain's hybrid character — part pharmacy, part refreshment counter, part social gathering place — gave it a distinctive civic role. It was neither a saloon (associated with alcohol, working-class male culture, and moral censure) nor a restaurant (associated with formal dining). It occupied a new middle ground: informal, accessible, respectable. This positioning made it available to social groups excluded from other public refreshment spaces.

SECTION 02

The Temperance Connection

The soda fountain's rise was structurally linked to the temperance movement. Temperance advocates sought to replace the saloon as a social institution, and the soda fountain offered a morally acceptable alternative: a place to socialize without alcohol. Charles Sumner Eaton's 1882 "Temperance Spa" in Boston is considered one of the first dedicated soda shops in the United States, explicitly positioned as an alternative to alcohol-serving establishments.

The Women's Christian Temperance Union actively promoted soda fountains, ice cream parlors, and non-alcoholic refreshment establishments as spaces that served the movement's goals. Drinks at soda fountains were sometimes called "White Ribbon Drinks" after the WCTU's symbol. The association between ice cream and temperance gave the product a moral valence — ice cream as virtuous refreshment, opposed to alcohol's corruption — that would persist in American cultural coding long after Prohibition ended.

Prohibition's Effect

The passage of Prohibition in 1920 dramatically increased soda fountain patronage. With saloons closed, the soda fountain became the primary American commercial social space for non-alcoholic socialization.

The Sunday Problem

The entanglement of ice cream with religious observance produced one of the product's stranger origin myths. In Evanston, Illinois — a stronghold of Methodist temperance sentiment — blue laws prohibited the sale of ice cream sodas on Sundays. Confectioners responded by serving ice cream with syrup but without the soda water, technically complying with the law. The "sodaless soda" became known as a "Sunday soda," later respelled as "sundae" to avoid the religious association. Whether or not this specific etymology is accurate, it illustrates how religious and temperance politics directly shaped ice cream product forms.

SECTION 03

Women and Public Space

The soda fountain created a new form of public space accessible to women in an era when respectable women had limited options for public socialization. The saloon was male territory. Hotels and restaurants required male escorts or formal occasions. The soda fountain, operating within the drugstore and associated with health and temperance, was gender-neutral and morally acceptable.

The social significance was noted by contemporaries. A New York Times column in March 1902 described a drugstore fountain as "the Woman's free lunch counter" — a recognition that women were primary patrons of the new space, using it for informal meals and social meetings in a way previously unavailable to them in American public life. Ice cream "saloons" had been noted as popular destinations for women, especially at the beginning of the temperance movement in the 1840s.

The soda fountain represented a genuine, if partial, expansion of women's public sphere in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. It was socially produced rather than legislated — a consequence of the fountain's positioning between the respectable and the informal, rather than an intentional design for gender equity. But its effect on female public life was real and documented.

SECTION 04

Race and Exclusion

The soda fountain's relative openness to women did not extend to Black Americans in the Jim Crow South. Ice cream counters and lunch counters at drugstores were racially segregated. Black customers could often make purchases at a standing counter or takeaway window but were excluded from seated service at the lunch counter itself. The spatial arrangements of segregation were encoded in the physical design of the soda fountain: the same counter that welcomed women as a new public category excluded Black Americans as a matter of law and custom.

This contradiction — the soda fountain as progressive space for some, exclusionary space for others — is the central tension of the section. The ice cream counter was simultaneously a site of social progress (women's public space, temperance alternative) and a material expression of structural racism. These two dimensions of the same physical space coexisted without canceling each other.

Structural Contradiction

The same institution that expanded women's access to public space operated racially segregated seating throughout the Jim Crow era. Social progress and structural exclusion occupied the same counter.

SECTION 05

Greensboro, February 1, 1960

On February 1, 1960, four Black students at North Carolina Agricultural and Technical State University — Ezell Blair Jr., Franklin McCain, Joseph McNeil, and David Richmond — sat down at the whites-only lunch counter at a Woolworth's store in Greensboro, North Carolina. They requested service, were refused, and remained seated. They returned the following day with more students. By February 5, approximately 300 students had joined the protest.

The Greensboro sit-in was not the first civil rights protest at a lunch counter, but it became the most influential. Within weeks, the movement had spread to 55 cities in 13 states. The tactic of nonviolent occupation of a segregated commercial space — demanding the right to be served ice cream and sandwiches alongside white customers — exposed the moral bankruptcy of segregation through the ordinariness of the demand. By July 1960, the Woolworth's lunch counter in Greensboro was serving Black patrons.

The Woolworth's lunch counter section is now preserved at the Smithsonian National Museum of American History. The object — a piece of commercial furniture designed for serving ice cream and sandwiches — became one of the central material artifacts of the American civil rights movement. The soda fountain's civic significance reached its fullest expression as a site of political contestation rather than comfortable socialization.

SECTION 06

Decline of the Soda Fountain

The soda fountain's decline followed the pattern of American urban life after World War II. Suburbanization dispersed the urban population that had sustained downtown drugstore culture. The automobile created drive-in alternatives. Television changed home entertainment patterns. Chain pharmacies replaced independent drugstores and eliminated the lunch counter as economically marginal to the pharmaceutical mission. By the 1970s and 1980s, the soda fountain as a going concern was largely a nostalgic artifact outside of specific regional pockets.

Its disappearance created the conditions for its nostalgic valorization. The soda fountain became one of the primary objects of mid-century American nostalgia — a space remembered as innocent, communal, and uncomplicated. This memory is selective: it elides the racial segregation, the temperance politics, and the social anxieties that the fountain both reflected and mediated. The nostalgia industry's version of the soda fountain is a curated simplification.
