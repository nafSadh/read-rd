Waffle Evolution

An anthropological survey of the waffle as technology, food, cultural artifact, and social institution — from medieval communion irons to the FEMA disaster index, across 7 dimensions and 72 sources.

March 2026 7 Dimensions 72 Sources 4 Tensions
ABSTRACT

Abstract

The waffle is older, stranger, and more culturally loaded than popular accounts suggest. Its history begins not with food but with an instrument: the hinged iron press, heated over an open fire, used since the 9th century to produce the thin wafers of the Christian Eucharist. From this sacred origin, the waffle traverses eleven centuries — through guild formation, aristocratic patronage, colonial migration, American social ritual, soul food culture, industrial food production, and global recombination — to arrive as an Eggo in a freezer aisle and as a FEMA disaster severity metric in the American South.

This survey maps the waffle's evolution across 7 dimensions drawing on 72 sources including Smithsonian Magazine, PBS Food, Wikipedia's food history articles, Garden & Gun, the FEMA/Snopes documentation of the Waffle House Index, CNN Travel, and culinary history journalism. The analysis identifies 4 tensions that run through the waffle's history: authenticity vs. industrialization; local identity vs. globalization; origin myth vs. documented history; and the sacred-to-profane transition. These tensions are not resolvable — they are the substance of the waffle's cultural meaning.

SECTION 01

Medieval Origins

The waffle's history begins with an instrument, not a food. In the 9th and 10th centuries, two closely related iron implements emerged simultaneously across northwestern Europe: the fer à hosties (communion wafer iron) and the moule à oublies (secular wafer iron).[1]Aleteia, "How waffles evolved from communion wafers," 2021 The communion wafer iron produced the Eucharistic host; the oublie iron produced a secular street food. Both instruments were nearly identical in construction — two hinged iron plates on long wooden handles, heated over an open fire — but their social contexts were radically different: one was the technology of the sacred, the other of the market.

The word family traces to Frankish wafla ("honeycomb" or "cake"), giving rise to Old French walfre (1185) and Middle Dutch wafele (late 13th century).[2]Wikipedia, "Waffle," etymology The etymology preserves the grid pattern as the defining visual feature — the technology named the food. The oublieurs guild was constituted in Paris in 1270, formalizing waffle-adjacent food production as organized labor with churchyard vending rights. The earliest known waffle recipe appears in Le Ménagier de Paris (c. 1390), marking the point at which the gaufre became a distinct, codified food with deeper grid pockets, leavened batter, butter, and eggs.[3]Gofrex, "History of waffles from the Middle Ages to the present day"

By the 15th and 16th centuries, waffle irons had become prestige objects — engraved with aristocratic coats of arms, city seals, and heraldic imagery. The iron was a medium for the display of identity and authority, not merely a cooking tool. The secularization of waffle culture occurred gradually across the 14th through 17th centuries as oublie vendors expanded from churchyards into city streets, domestic cookbooks incorporated waffle recipes, and the food's religious associations became incidental rather than definitional.

The structural persistence of the grid

From the 9th-century communion iron to the 21st-century Eggo, the defining structural feature of all waffle variants is the grid pattern pressed by the iron. Eleven centuries of culinary evolution have not changed this fundamental form.

SECTION 02

Belgian Identity

The phrase "Belgian waffle" conceals a foundational ambiguity: Belgium has two distinct waffle traditions that are genuinely different foods. The Brussels waffle — rectangular, light, crisp, yeast-leavened, topped with cream and fruit — and the Liège waffle — oval, dense, brioche-dough, pearl sugar caramelized in cooking, traditionally eaten plain — differ in dough composition, texture, shape, eating context, and historical origin. Neither tradition, in Belgium, is called a "Belgian waffle."[4]Wikipedia, "Belgian waffle"

The Liège waffle carries an origin story associating it with the 18th-century cook of the Prince-Bishop of Liège, who was asked to create a new sweet delicacy. The story is likely apocryphal. The Brussels waffle's export trajectory is precisely documented: Maurice and Rose Vermersch first introduced their Brussels waffles at the 1962 Seattle World's Fair, then at the 1964 New York World's Fair at Flushing Meadows. Observing that many Americans could not locate Brussels on a map, Maurice renamed the product "Belgian waffle." Rose added whipped cream and strawberries — a topping combination not traditional in Belgium — that proved decisive in American reception. The stand sold approximately 2,500 waffles per day.[5]Tasting Table, "How The 1964 World's Fair Helped Popularize The Belgian Waffle"

In 2011, Brussels MP Joël Riguelle raised parliamentary concern that traditional Brussels waffles were being displaced by Liège-style waffles with toppings — a hybrid satisfying tourist expectations but belonging to neither tradition. The intervention reveals the degree to which the waffle had become a protected cultural artifact in Belgian political discourse. Meanwhile, the "Belgian waffle" recognized internationally is a 1964 American construction bearing little resemblance to what Belgians actually eat.

SECTION 03

American Adoption

Waffles arrived in North America through Dutch colonial settlers in the 17th century, establishing a household and community food tradition in the mid-Atlantic colonies before any founding-era figure was involved. The popular narrative centering Thomas Jefferson — who brought four waffle irons from Amsterdam on his 1789 return from France — is documented in part but substantially mythologized. Garden & Gun's investigation of the Monticello primary record found limited evidence for Jefferson's role as a waffle innovator; the food was already known in Dutch-descended American households.[6]Garden & Gun, "A Pressing Mystery: Thomas Jefferson and the Waffle"

What is well documented is the waffle frolic — an 18th and 19th-century American social institution in which a host made waffles continuously for guests over an extended evening. The waffle iron's demands (one waffle at a time, continuous attention, ten-minute cooking over open fire) created natural conditions for sustained social gathering. At plantation households including Monticello, the labor was performed by enslaved cooks who managed the 10–12-pound iron while the food was consumed as a leisure item by the household's white members — a dimension systematically absent from popular origin narratives.

SECTION 04

Waffle House as Institution

Waffle House was founded on Labor Day weekend 1955 in Avondale Estates, Georgia, by Joe Rogers Sr. and Tom Forkner. Rogers brought restaurant experience from the Toddle House chain; Forkner proposed the name; Rogers insisted on 24-hour operations — the decision that defines the chain's cultural significance. Today Waffle House operates over 1,900 locations across 25 states, every one open 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. The chain has no locks on its doors.[7]Wikipedia, "Joe Rogers (businessman)"; Georgia Historical Society, "The Waffle House"

The Waffle House Index was coined by Craig Fugate in August 2004 following Hurricane Charley, when Fugate was directing Florida's emergency management division. The Index uses the operational status of the nearest Waffle House as a proxy for local disaster severity: green (full menu — infrastructure intact), yellow (limited menu — partial disruption), red (closed — severe disaster, maximum response required). FEMA unofficially uses the Index in disaster response assessment, confirmed by Snopes fact-checking. The Index's validity rests on Waffle House's combination of 24-hour operations, disaster preparedness investment, and rapid-response "jump teams" that reopen closed locations as soon as safety permits.[8]Wikipedia, "Waffle House Index"; Snopes fact-check confirmed

SECTION 05

Chicken & Waffles

Chicken and waffles has two documented and distinct lineages with no direct connection between them. The Pennsylvania Dutch tradition (17th–18th century) featured shredded or roasted chicken atop a waffle with white cream gravy — a German-immigrant food culture serving the dish at church dinners and community events. The Harlem soul food tradition (1930s–1940s) featured fried chicken alongside a plain waffle — a late-night meal for jazz musicians navigating the gap between dinner and breakfast at venues including the Wells Supper Club (opened 1938).[9]PBS Food, "Discover the History of Chicken and Waffles"; Wikipedia, "Chicken and waffles"

The dish's national dissemination is directly traceable to Roscoe's House of Chicken 'N Waffles, founded in Long Beach in 1975 by Harlem native Herb Hudson. Celebrity patronage (Stevie Wonder, Natalie Cole, Redd Foxx) drove rapid cultural visibility; the Los Angeles Times later called Roscoe's "such an L.A. institution that people don't even question the strange combo anymore." The mainstreaming of chicken and waffles in the 2000s–2010s involved significant cultural translation — versions appearing on suburban brunch menus often bear little resemblance to either original lineage.[10]Roscoe's House of Chicken 'N Waffles, "Our Story"; Resy retrospective, 2020

SECTION 06

Industrialization & Eggo

The electrification of the waffle iron began with the Simplex Electric Heating Company in 1906 and General Electric's prototype in 1911 (production beginning c. 1918). By the 1930s, electric waffle irons were standard middle-class kitchen appliances — the first significant democratization of waffle-making since the medieval guild era. The open-hearth iron era, spanning a millennium, ended within a generation of electrification.[11]Smithsonian Magazine, "A Brief History of the Waffle Iron"

The leap to industrial food production was accomplished by Frank Dorsa, a San Jose machinist, in 1953. Dorsa applied industrial engineering to waffle production, inventing a large carousel mechanism equipped with multiple waffle irons, rotating continuously, producing thousands of waffles per hour which were immediately flash-frozen. His frozen waffles reached shelves as "Froffles" in 1953, were renamed Eggo in 1955, and acquired by the Kellogg Company in 1968. Kellogg's advertising agency Leo Burnett developed the "L'eggo my Eggo" campaign in 1972. By 2009, Eggo held a 73% share of the U.S. frozen waffle market.[12]Wikipedia, "Eggo"; Wikipedia, "Frank Dorsa"; Mashed, "The Untold Truth Of Eggo Waffles"

SECTION 07

Global Variations

The global distribution of waffle forms raises a question about diffusion versus independent invention. The Dutch stroopwafel and Norwegian hjertevafler derive clearly from the European gaufre tradition. The stroopwafel — originating in Gouda around 1810–1840, credited to baker Gerard Kamphuisen, originally called armenkoeken ("poor people's cookies") because of its origin in bakery scraps — traveled from regional poverty food to Dutch national export commodity and global airport staple across two centuries.[13]Wikipedia, "Stroopwafel"; Dutch Waffle Company history

The Norwegian heart-shaped waffle (hjertevafler) is tied to both the concept of kos (cozy social warmth) and, through linguistic accident, to a religious feast day: March 25 is Waffle Day in Norway, Sweden, and Denmark because the Swedish Feast of Annunciation (Vårfrudagen) was phonetically contracted to Våffeldagen ("Waffle Day"). A religious occasion became a secular food celebration through the drift of spoken language — a micro-version of the waffle's entire sacred-to-profane history.[14]Visit Norway, "Waffle is the king of kos"; Bowl of Delicious, Norwegian Waffles

The Hong Kong egg waffle (雞蛋仔, gai daan jai) presents the strongest case for independent invention: arising in the 1950s from a grocery shop owner's use of excess eggs, evaporated milk, and a honeycomb mold, it reached the same basic form — egg batter pressed in a patterned iron — through a completely different cultural path. Its global spread via food tourism and social media in the 2010s represents reverse diffusion: a non-European variant traveling back to the continent of the waffle's European origins.[15]Wikipedia, "Egg waffle"; CNN Travel, "Bubble waffles"

SECTION 08

Synthesis

The waffle's eleven-century evolution traces a recurring pattern: sacred/institutional origin → secularization → national/regional identity formation → colonial and commercial migration → industrialization → global recombination. The pattern is not unique to the waffle — it describes the lifecycle of many foods that begin as ritual objects and end as commodity products — but the waffle traces it with unusual clarity because the iron technology creates a visible formal constant across all iterations. The grid is always there.

The survey's most significant finding is the pervasiveness of the origin myth problem. Nearly every major waffle origin story is contested, apocryphal, or unverifiable: the Liège waffle's Prince-Bishop, Thomas Jefferson's Amsterdam irons, Gerard Kamphuisen's Gouda scraps, the Wells Supper Club's jazz-musician clientele. This pattern is not accidental — origin myths function to anchor foods to places, people, and founding moments that confer legitimacy. The myth is often more culturally durable than the accurate history because the myth does something the history cannot: it provides an identity.

The four tensions identified in this survey — authenticity vs. industrialization, local identity vs. globalization, origin myth vs. history, sacred vs. profane — are not problems to be solved. They are the substance of the waffle's cultural meaning. The Eggo is not a debased form of the gaufre; it is the gaufre as transformed by industrial capitalism. The "Belgian waffle" is not a fraud; it is Belgian waffle culture as transformed by export economics and an American fair. The tensions hold the history together.

Core finding

The waffle is best understood not as a food with a history but as an iron technology that has generated different foods in different social contexts across eleven centuries — each claiming to be the real waffle, each being correct in its own terms.
