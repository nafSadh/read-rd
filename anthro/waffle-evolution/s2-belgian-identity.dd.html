Belgian Identity

Belgium has two distinct waffles with contested histories, genuine culinary differences, and a fractious relationship to the "Belgian waffle" the world thinks it knows — a product largely constructed in America in 1964.

SECTION 01

Two Waffles, Not One

The phrase "Belgian waffle" conceals a foundational ambiguity: Belgium has two distinct waffle traditions that are genuinely different foods. The Brussels waffle (gaufre de Bruxelles) and the Liège waffle (gaufre de Liège) differ in dough composition, texture, shape, eating context, and historical origin. Neither tradition calls itself a "Belgian waffle" — that term was coined for an American audience.[1]Wikipedia, "Belgian waffle"

Brussels Waffle

  • Rectangular shape
  • Light, crisp batter (beaten egg whites)
  • Deep, rectangular grid pockets
  • Served with toppings: cream, fruit, chocolate
  • Eaten as a dessert or street snack
  • Yeast-leavened batter
  • Origin: 18th-century Brussels

Liège Waffle

  • Oval/rounded shape, irregular edges
  • Dense, chewy brioche dough
  • Pearl sugar caramelizes in cooking
  • Traditionally eaten plain, no toppings
  • Eaten as a snack at any time of day
  • Pearl sugar dough (not batter)
  • Origin: 18th-century Liège (Wallonia)

The structural difference is fundamental: the Brussels waffle is a batter product (pourable, airy), while the Liège waffle is a dough product (dense, brioche-like). The pearl sugar in the Liège version caramelizes during cooking, creating an exterior crunch and interior chewiness that has no equivalent in the Brussels style. These are not regional variations on the same food — they are different foods that share a grid-iron technology.

SECTION 02

The Liège Waffle

The Liège waffle is associated with the Wallonia region of eastern Belgium. Its canonical origin story holds that it was invented in the 18th century by the cook of the Prince-Bishop of Liège, who was asked to create a new sweet delicacy for his patron. The cook incorporated pearl sugar — lumps of compressed sugar that do not fully dissolve during cooking — into a brioche-style dough, producing the caramelized, chewy result that defines the Liège style to this day.[2]Wikipedia, "Liège waffle"

The Prince-Bishop origin story is likely apocryphal or at minimum unverifiable from primary sources. What is documented is the waffle's deep roots in Walloon culinary culture and its long association with street food vending in Liège and surrounding cities. The use of pearl sugar (sucre perlé or sucre grains) is the waffle's defining technical feature: the sugar is added to the dough unground, survives the heat as distinct pockets, and caramelizes on the iron's surface to create the characteristic sticky-crisp exterior.[3]Taste of Belgium, "The History of Belgian Waffles: From Liège to the World"

On the Prince-Bishop story

The origin myth of the Liège waffle follows a common pattern in food history: a humble food is retrospectively attached to an aristocratic patron to confer legitimacy and antiquity. The story's plausibility depends entirely on the prestige of the supposed patron, not on documentary evidence.

SECTION 03

The Brussels Waffle

The Brussels waffle is lighter and crisper than the Liège version, achieved by using a yeast-leavened batter with beaten egg whites folded in for additional airiness. Its rectangular shape, deep grid pockets, and white interior are designed to hold toppings — traditionally whipped cream, fresh strawberries, or powdered sugar. In Belgium, Brussels waffles are a street food associated with tourism and special occasions, not a daily domestic food.[4]Ampi.be, "Differences between Brussels waffle and Liege waffle"

The Brussels waffle's export trajectory has produced an ironic reversal: the waffle that Belgians associate with tourism and foreign visitors has become, through the 1964 World's Fair mechanism, the defining image of "Belgian" food abroad. Meanwhile, the Liège waffle — the waffle Belgians actually eat on a daily basis — remains largely unknown outside Belgium and specialist food culture.

SECTION 04

The 1964 World's Fair Moment

The transformation of the Brussels waffle into the "Belgian waffle" is directly traceable to a single family and a specific set of decisions made at two consecutive American World's Fairs. Maurice and Rose Vermersch of Brussels first introduced their waffles at the 1962 Century 21 Exposition in Seattle. Two years later, they set up a stand at the 1964 New York World's Fair at Flushing Meadows Corona Park, selling waffles for 99 cents each.[5]Tasting Table, "How The 1964 World's Fair Helped Popularize The Belgian Waffle"

Two decisions made the product uniquely American. First, Maurice Vermersch renamed the food: noting that many Americans could not identify Brussels as the capital of Belgium, he changed the name from "Brussels waffle" to "Belgian waffle" — a broader, more legible national identifier. Second, Rose Vermersch added whipped cream and fresh strawberries as standard toppings, an innovation that was not traditional in Belgium but proved decisive in American market reception.[6]The World from PRX, "It was the 1964 World's Fair when Americans fell in love with Belgian waffles"

The Vermersch stand sold approximately 2,500 waffles per day, according to their daughter MariePaule, who was also present at the fair. The commercial success was such that stands representing other countries' pavilions began selling Belgian waffles as well. Within a decade, "Belgian waffle" had entered the American lexicon as a restaurant menu item and consumer product category.[7]Good Food Stories, "World's Fair Belgian Waffles"

A renaming that stuck

The decision to call a Brussels waffle a "Belgian waffle" was pragmatic and commercial, not cultural. It produced an export identity that bore little relationship to Belgian waffle culture — and that identity has since been more durable than the original.

SECTION 05

Waffle and Belgian National Identity

In Belgium, the waffle carries genuine national significance, but the internal politics of that significance are contested. The Brussels–Liège division tracks, imperfectly, onto Belgium's broader linguistic and regional fractures: Brussels is predominantly French-speaking and Flemish-adjacent, while Liège is the heart of Wallonia. The question of which waffle is "more Belgian" has never been fully resolved and occasionally surfaces in political discourse.[8]Brussels Times, "Belgium's dividing line? Brussels versus Liège waffles"

In 2011, Brussels Member of Parliament Joël Riguelle raised parliamentary concern that the traditional Brussels waffle was in danger of being displaced: waffle shops were increasingly offering Liège-style waffles with toppings traditionally associated with the Brussels style — a hybrid form that satisfied tourist expectations but belonged to neither tradition. Riguelle's intervention highlighted the degree to which the waffle had become a protected cultural artifact, not merely a food.[9]Brussels Times, 2011 parliamentary report

The deeper irony is geographic: the waffle that Belgians consider quintessentially national is largely invisible in the international market, while the "Belgian waffle" that dominates American menus, frozen food aisles, and global tourist stands is a 1964 American construction that most Belgians would not recognize as representative of their food culture. The export product has colonized the global identity of the original.[10]Swanton Street Diner, "Are Belgian Waffles Really Belgian?"
