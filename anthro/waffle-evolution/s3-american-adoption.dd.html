WAFFLE EVOLUTION · SECTION 3 · 8 SOURCES

American Adoption

How waffles entered American culture — through Dutch colonial settlers, Thomas Jefferson's possibly apocryphal Amsterdam irons, the waffle frolic as social institution, and the labor of enslaved cooks.

SECTION 01

Dutch Colonial Foundations

Waffles arrived in North America not through any single celebrated figure but through the mass migration of Dutch settlers in the 17th century. The Dutch were among the earliest European colonists in what is now New York and New Jersey, and they brought with them the waffle iron and the wafele tradition from the Low Countries. Dutch colonial foodways established waffles as a household and community food in early American settlements well before the founding period.[1]Time Magazine, "A Brief History of Waffles"

The Dutch word wafel entered colonial American vocabulary alongside the iron implement required to make it. By the early 18th century, waffle-making was established enough in the mid-Atlantic colonies that it appeared in domestic records, cookbooks, and social documentation. The Dutch colonial origin is largely absent from popular American waffle mythology, which tends to center Jefferson — but the food was already established in American households before Jefferson was born.[2]Chicago Waffles, "The History of the American Waffle"

SECTION 02

Jefferson and the Amsterdam Irons

Thomas Jefferson is the most frequently cited figure in the popularization narrative of American waffles. The standard account holds that Jefferson, while serving as American minister to France, acquired waffle irons in Amsterdam during his 1789 return journey and introduced the waffle to American social life at Monticello. The story has become the dominant origin myth of American waffle culture — appearing in museums, food histories, and popular media.[3]Garden & Gun, "A Pressing Mystery: Thomas Jefferson and the Waffle"

The reality is more complicated. Garden & Gun's investigation of the primary documentary record at Monticello found that while Jefferson did bring waffle irons from Europe, the evidence for his role in "introducing" waffles to America is thin. Waffles were already known and made in the mid-Atlantic states by Dutch-descended households. The only direct Monticello references to waffles come from two recipes in a collection attributed to Jefferson's granddaughter Septimia — one credited to a "Mrs. De Peyster" and one simply to "Monticello." Neither establishes Jefferson as a waffle innovator.[4]Garden & Gun, "A Pressing Mystery: Thomas Jefferson and the Waffle"

The myth's persistence

The Jefferson waffle story persists not because the evidence supports it but because it satisfies a cultural need: attaching a food to a founding figure confers American legitimacy and a precise founding moment. Origin myths are more useful than accurate histories.

Physical Scale of the Iron

Jefferson's Amsterdam waffle irons were substantial implements: each weighed 10 to 12 pounds and had three-foot-long handles, designed to hold the iron over an open hearth fire. Cooking a waffle required filling the iron, holding it over flame, flipping it midway through cooking — a process taking close to ten minutes and requiring considerable physical strength. At Monticello, this labor was performed by enslaved cooks, not by Jefferson himself.[5]Garden & Gun, "A Pressing Mystery"

SECTION 03

The Waffle Frolic

Whatever Jefferson's personal contribution, there is strong documentation of waffles as a social institution in 18th and 19th-century America: the waffle frolic (also called the waffle party). These were gatherings — often winter evening events — at which a host would make waffles for guests, who consumed them with butter, molasses, honey, or maple syrup while socializing. The waffle frolic was documented across the mid-Atlantic and New England states as a recognized social form.[6]Snack Stack, "The snack that's ironed out"

The waffle frolic's logic was both culinary and practical: the waffle iron required continuous attention and produced food only one waffle at a time, making waffle-making inherently a communal, time-extended activity rather than a quick preparation. The iron's demands created natural conditions for extended social gathering. In this sense, the waffle's technology shaped the social form of its consumption: the food was slow enough to require company.

By the early 19th century, waffle frolics appear in American newspapers, diaries, and social correspondence as a recognized leisure institution comparable to quilting bees or husking parties — work-adjacent social events organized around a focal task.[7]Grub Americana, "The Wondrous World of Waffles"

SECTION 04

Enslaved Labor and the Waffle Record

The American waffle's early history is inseparable from the labor of enslaved people. At plantation households including Monticello, waffles were produced by enslaved cooks who managed the physical demands of the iron — the weight, the heat, the continuous attention — while the food was consumed as a leisure item by the household's white members. The Monticello documentation makes clear that the waffle irons Jefferson brought from Amsterdam were used by enslaved cooks, not by Jefferson or his family.[8]Garden & Gun, "A Pressing Mystery: Thomas Jefferson and the Waffle"

This dimension of the American waffle's history is systematically absent from popular origin narratives, which center Jefferson's role as collector and connoisseur while rendering invisible the labor that actually produced the food. The pattern is common to American culinary history more broadly: the named figure acquires the tool or ingredient; the unnamed enslaved worker performs the skilled labor of transformation.

SECTION 05

19th-Century American Waffle Culture

Through the 19th century, American waffle culture diversified through regional cookbooks, immigrant contributions, and the expansion of the waffle into hotel and restaurant dining. The Pennsylvania Dutch tradition — German-speaking settlers who had themselves brought waffle culture from continental Europe — established a distinct regional variant featuring waffles served with stewed chicken and gravy, a tradition that would later intersect with soul food culture to produce chicken and waffles (see Section 5).[5]Grub Americana, "The Wondrous World of Waffles"

17th century Dutch settlers establish waffle tradition in mid-Atlantic colonies (New York, New Jersey). Dutch wafel culture predates any founding-era adoption.
1789 Thomas Jefferson acquires waffle irons in Amsterdam on his return from France. Four irons documented; used at Monticello by enslaved cooks.
Late 18th – early 19th c. Waffle frolics documented across mid-Atlantic and New England states. The waffle as social institution — requiring sustained attention and producing food communally.
19th century Pennsylvania Dutch tradition establishes chicken-and-gravy waffle variant. American regional waffle cookbooks proliferate. Hotel and restaurant menus add waffles as a breakfast staple.
1906 / 1911 First electric waffle irons: Simplex Electric Heating Company (1906), General Electric prototype (1911). Domestic waffle-making begins its transition from hearth technology to countertop appliance.
