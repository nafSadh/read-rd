WAFFLE EVOLUTION · SECTION 6 · 9 SOURCES

Industrialization & Eggo

From the first electric waffle iron in 1906 to Frank Dorsa's carousel machine in 1953 to Kellogg's 73% frozen-waffle market share — how industrial production transformed a homemade, labor-intensive food into a freezer-aisle commodity.

SECTION 01

The Electric Waffle Iron

The medieval waffle iron — two hinged plates on three-foot handles, held over an open fire — remained the dominant technology for waffle-making from the 9th century through the early 20th century. The first electric waffle iron was produced by the Simplex Electric Heating Company in 1906, replacing open-flame heat with electric resistance elements embedded in the iron's plates. The Smithsonian Institution's collections include an early Edison Electric / GE Hotpoint electric waffle iron as a primary artifact of this transition.[1]Smithsonian Magazine, "A Brief History of the Waffle Iron"

In 1911, Thomas J. Stackbeck developed a prototype electric waffle iron for General Electric, though commercial production did not begin until approximately 1918. By the 1930s, electric waffle irons had become standard middle-class kitchen appliances across the United States — the first significant democratization of waffle-making since the medieval guild era. The technology eliminated the need for an open hearth, reduced cooking time, and standardized temperature in ways that made consistent results accessible to ordinary domestic cooks.[2]Wikipedia, "Waffle iron"

SECTION 02

Frank Dorsa and the Carousel Machine

The leap from domestic electric appliance to industrial food production was accomplished by Frank Dorsa of San Jose, California. Dorsa was a trained machinist — not a baker — who had previously run a mayonnaise business with his brothers Anthony and Sam. In 1953, he applied his machinist's instincts to the problem of waffle production at scale, inventing a large rotating contraption equipped with multiple waffle irons arranged on a carousel mechanism.[3]Wikipedia, "Frank Dorsa"

The carousel system worked as follows: the machine rotated continuously; batter was poured onto irons at one position; strategically positioned employees flipped irons as they rotated; cooked waffles were removed at another position; and the iron continued rotating to begin again. The system could produce thousands of waffles per hour. Once cooked, waffles were immediately flash-frozen and packaged — applying the frozen food technology that Clarence Birdseye had developed for vegetables and fish in the 1920s to a fresh-baked product.[4]Mashed, "The Untold Truth Of Eggo Waffles"

The machinist's insight

Dorsa's background in mechanical engineering, not baking, was precisely the qualification that made his innovation possible. The carousel waffle iron was not a culinary invention but an industrial engineering solution applied to a food production problem.

SECTION 03

Froffles to Eggo

Dorsa's frozen waffles reached grocery store shelves in 1953 under the name Froffles — a portmanteau of "frozen" and "waffles." The name was functional but did not catch on commercially. In 1955, the product was renamed Eggo — a name derived from the egg-rich batter recipe that Dorsa used, which consumers had reportedly commented on after tasting samples.[5]Wikipedia, "Eggo"

The renaming coincided with a marketing pivot: rather than emphasizing the frozen-food technology (Froffles), the new name emphasized the product's taste and ingredient quality. The name "Eggo" carried sensory associations — freshness, richness, the egg — rather than the processing method. This was a significant marketing decision: it positioned the product as food rather than as convenience technology, a framing that has remained central to frozen waffle marketing ever since.

SECTION 04

The Kellogg Acquisition

In 1968, the Kellogg Company purchased Eggo as a diversification move — the cereal giant's entry into the frozen breakfast food category. Kellogg brought national distribution infrastructure and advertising scale that the Dorsa brothers' regional operation could not match. The acquisition transformed Eggo from a successful regional product into a nationally distributed brand.[6]Wikipedia, "Eggo"

In 1972, the Leo Burnett advertising agency developed the slogan "L'eggo my Eggo" — a campaign depicting one person attempting to take another's Eggo waffle, with the response "L'eggo my Eggo" (a pun on "let go" and the brand name). The campaign ran in television commercials for decades and became one of the most recognized advertising slogans in American food marketing. The slogan did more than sell waffles: it defined the product category as a morning indulgence associated with mild domestic conflict and childhood appetite.[7]Mental Floss, "11 Golden Facts About Eggo Waffles"

SECTION 05

Market Dominance

By mid-2009, Eggo held a 73% share of the frozen waffle market in the United States — a near-monopoly in a product category it had essentially created. The figure makes visible the scale of Eggo's transformation of the waffle: from a homemade food requiring skilled labor and specialized equipment, to a market category dominated by a single industrial brand.[8]Mashed, "The Untold Truth Of Eggo Waffles" (citing industry data)

The Eggo dominance creates the central tension of Section 6 and of the broader waffle-evolution project: what does "waffle" mean when the product most Americans associate with the word is a flash-frozen industrial disc, reheated in a toaster, produced on a rotating carousel machine by a cereal conglomerate? The communion wafer and the Eggo share the same basic grid structure and the same basic ingredient list (flour, water, eggs, fat). The 1,100-year distance between them is entirely a product of industrial technology and commercial distribution.[9]The Waffle Affair, "Evolution of Waffle Irons"
