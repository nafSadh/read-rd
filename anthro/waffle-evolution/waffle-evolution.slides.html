S1 · The Iron 3

The Iron Came First

In the 9th century, two nearly identical hinged iron implements emerged in northwestern Europe: the fer à hosties (communion wafer iron) and the moule à oublies (secular wafer iron).

Both were two hinged iron plates on long wooden handles, heated over open fire. One produced the Eucharistic host; the other produced street food. The technology named the food: Frankish wafla means “honeycomb.”

The oublieurs guild was constituted in Paris in 1270, formalizing waffle production as organized labor with churchyard vending rights.

Core insight

The waffle is best understood not as a food with a history but as an iron technology that has generated different foods across eleven centuries.

II
S2 · Two Traditions 5

Brussels vs. Liège

Belgium has two distinct waffle traditions that are genuinely different foods. Neither, in Belgium, is called a “Belgian waffle.”

Brussels waffle: rectangular, light, crisp, yeast-leavened, served warm with cream and fruit. Requires a plate.

Liège waffle: oval, dense, brioche dough with pearl sugar caramelized during cooking. Eaten plain, portable. The one Belgians actually eat daily.

In 1964, Maurice Vermersch renamed his Brussels waffles “Bel-Gem” at the New York World’s Fair because Americans couldn’t locate Brussels on a map. His wife Rose added whipped cream and strawberries — not a Belgian tradition. They sold 2,500 waffles per day.

In 2011, Brussels MP Joël Riguelle raised parliamentary concern about tourist-driven hybrids displacing both authentic traditions.

III
S3 · Colonial Waffles 7

The Waffle Frolic

Waffles arrived through Dutch colonial settlers in the 17th century — well before Thomas Jefferson’s mythologized 1789 Amsterdam irons.

The waffle frolic was an 18th–19th century social institution: a host made waffles continuously for guests over an extended evening. The iron’s demands (one waffle at a time, 10 minutes over open fire) created natural conditions for sustained social gathering.

At plantation households including Monticello, the labor was performed by enslaved cooks managing a 10–12 pound iron while the food was consumed as leisure. This dimension is systematically absent from popular narratives.

Hidden labor

The waffle frolic’s social warmth depended on invisible labor — a pattern that recurs throughout food history.

S4 · The Index 9

The Waffle House Index

Founded Labor Day 1955 in Avondale Estates, Georgia by Joe Rogers Sr. and Tom Forkner. Rogers insisted on 24-hour operations — the decision that defines everything after.

The Waffle House Index was coined by Craig Fugate in August 2004 after Hurricane Charley. It uses operational status as a proxy for local disaster severity:

Three levels GREEN = full menu (infrastructure intact)
YELLOW = limited menu (partial disruption)
RED = closed (severe disaster)

FEMA has never formally adopted the Index. Its informal status means the brand gets civic-resilience coverage at zero cost — and zero accountability.

T4: The Index is both genuinely useful and the most effective low-cost branding operation in American corporate history.

S5 · Lineages 11

Two Unrelated Origins

Pennsylvania Dutch (17th c.): Shredded or roasted chicken atop a waffle with white cream gravy. German-immigrant church dinners. A savory tradition with no cultural connection to what came later.

Harlem soul food (1930s–40s): Fried chicken alongside a sweet waffle. Born from the late-night jazz economy — musicians at Wells Supper Club (1938) needed a meal between dinner and breakfast.

Roscoe’s (1975) in Long Beach, founded by Harlem native Herb Hudson, nationalized the dish. Celebrity patronage (Stevie Wonder, Natalie Cole) drove cultural visibility. The LA Times later called it “such an L.A. institution.”

T3: Appropriation

When a culturally specific food becomes a mass-market category, who captures value and who loses history?

S6 · INDUSTRIALIZATION 12

From Iron to Eggo

Frank Dorsa, a San Jose machinist, industrialized a thousand-year-old food in 1953
VI
S6 · Dorsa & Eggo 13

The Carousel Revolution

1906: Simplex Electric Heating Co. builds the first electric waffle iron. 1911: GE prototype. By the 1930s, electric irons are standard middle-class appliances — ending a millennium of open-hearth cooking in one generation.

1953: Frank Dorsa invents a large carousel mechanism with multiple irons rotating continuously, producing thousands of waffles per hour, immediately flash-frozen.

Froffles → Eggo → Kellogg: renamed in 1955, acquired in 1968. Leo Burnett’s “L’eggo my Eggo” campaign (1972) completed the transformation. By 2009, Eggo held 73% of the U.S. frozen waffle market.

T2: The Eggo Problem

Eggo made the waffle available to everyone. It also made the artisan waffle irrelevant to most Americans. Both claims are true.

VII
S7 · World Tour 15

Global Waffle Forms

Stroopwafel (Netherlands, c. 1810–1840): Originated in Gouda as armenkoeken (“poor people’s cookies”) from bakery scraps. Now a global airport staple without losing its Gouda identity.

Hjertevafler (Norway): Heart-shaped, cardamom-spiced. March 25 is Waffle Day because the Swedish Vårfrudagen (Annunciation) contracted to Våffeldagen. A religious feast became a secular food holiday through phonetic drift.

Egg waffle (Hong Kong, 1950s): A grocer used excess eggs, evaporated milk, and a honeycomb mold. Reached the same basic form — batter in a patterned iron — through a completely independent path. Its 2010s global spread is reverse diffusion: a non-European variant traveling back to Europe.

T6: Globalization homogenizes at scale and stimulates differentiation at the artisan level — simultaneously.

SYNTHESIS 18

The Origin Myth Problem

Nearly every major waffle origin story is contested, apocryphal, or unverifiable:

The Liège waffle’s Prince-Bishop. Jefferson’s Amsterdam irons. Kamphuisen’s Gouda scraps. Wells Supper Club’s jazz clientele.

This pattern is not accidental. Origin myths function to anchor foods to places and people that confer legitimacy. The myth is often more culturally durable than the history because the myth does something history cannot: it provides an identity.

Final finding

The Eggo is not a debased gaufre; it is the gaufre as transformed by industrial capitalism. The “Belgian waffle” is not a fraud; it is Belgian waffle culture as transformed by export economics. The tensions hold the history together.
