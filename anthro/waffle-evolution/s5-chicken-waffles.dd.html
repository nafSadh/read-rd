WAFFLE EVOLUTION · SECTION 5 · 9 SOURCES

Chicken & Waffles

A dish with two distinct and contested lineages — Pennsylvania Dutch shredded chicken and gravy, and Harlem soul food fried chicken at jazz clubs — and a national dissemination path running through Los Angeles in 1975.

SECTION 01

Two Lineages, One Dish

Chicken and waffles is not a single dish with a single history but a combination that arose, in genuinely different forms, from two distinct food cultures with no direct documented connection between them. The Pennsylvania Dutch tradition produced a version featuring shredded or roasted chicken served atop a waffle with white gravy. The Harlem soul food tradition produced a version featuring fried chicken served alongside a plain waffle — a late-night meal for jazz musicians navigating the gap between dinner and breakfast.[1]Wikipedia, "Chicken and waffles"

Pennsylvania Dutch Version

  • Shredded or roasted chicken
  • White cream gravy poured over
  • Waffle as a starchy base
  • Served at church dinners, social events
  • 17th–18th century origin
  • German immigrant food culture

Harlem Soul Food Version

  • Fried chicken (crispy, seasoned)
  • Waffle served alongside, not under
  • Sweet-savory combination without gravy
  • Late-night jazz club context
  • 1930s–1940s origin (documented)
  • African-American food culture

The two versions are so different in preparation, texture, and cultural context that they might reasonably be treated as different dishes that share a name. Their convergence into a single "chicken and waffles" category in the national food consciousness is itself a historical event, driven primarily by Roscoe's House of Chicken 'N Waffles in Los Angeles after 1975.

SECTION 02

The Pennsylvania Dutch Tradition

German-speaking settlers in Pennsylvania — the "Pennsylvania Dutch" (from Deutsch) — brought both waffle culture and chicken-preparation traditions from continental Europe in the 17th and 18th centuries. The combination of shredded chicken and waffle with white gravy was established in Pennsylvania by the end of the 19th century and was documented at church dinners, hotel restaurants, and community social events across the region.[2]SAPJACK, "The World of Chicken & Waffles: History, Recipes & More!"

This version traveled southward as the dish evolved, but it underwent a significant transformation in the process: the bland, creamy Pennsylvania Dutch chicken was replaced in Southern preparations by crisp, seasoned fried chicken. The substitution changed the dish's fundamental character — from a gravy-smothered comfort food to a sweet-savory contrast — and created the conditions for the Harlem version's logic to apply to a Southern preparation.

SECTION 03

Harlem, Jazz, and the Late-Night Meal

The Harlem soul food version of chicken and waffles is associated with the African-American jazz club culture of the 1930s and 1940s. The most frequently cited locus is the Wells Supper Club on 7th Avenue in Harlem, which opened in 1938 and became a destination for jazz musicians after their late-night performances. The dish's logic was circumstantial: musicians finishing a gig at 2 or 3 a.m. were too late for dinner and too early for breakfast, and fried chicken alongside a waffle served both purposes simultaneously.[3]PBS Food, "Discover the History of Chicken and Waffles"

Other Harlem venues associated with the dish include Tillie's Chicken Shack and Richard Wells' jazz nightclub. The combination's appeal in this context was practical and structural: fried chicken was a staple of African-American home cooking and commercial food culture, waffles were a recognizable breakfast item, and the combination was filling, fast, and available at hours when few other meals could be obtained.[4]NJ Digest, "How Jazz and Harlem Created Chicken and Waffles"

On the origin myth problem

Both the Pennsylvania Dutch and Harlem lineages are documented but neither is primary: they arose independently, in different forms, from different food cultures. The question "where did chicken and waffles come from?" has no single correct answer, which itself reveals something about how origin stories function in food culture.

SECTION 04

Roscoe's: National Dissemination Vector

The national spread of chicken and waffles as a recognized dish category is directly traceable to a single institution: Roscoe's House of Chicken 'N Waffles, founded in Long Beach, California, in 1975 by Herb Hudson, a Harlem native. Hudson brought the Harlem soul food version — fried chicken, plain waffle, no gravy — to Los Angeles and found an immediate audience in the city's African-American community and entertainment industry.[5]Roscoe's House of Chicken 'N Waffles, "Our Story"

Roscoe's success was accelerated by its celebrity customer base. Among Hudson's early friends in the Los Angeles entertainment community were Stevie Wonder and Natalie Cole, who introduced the restaurant to Redd Foxx and other performers with large public followings. Word spread through the entertainment industry in a way that translated directly into cultural visibility. The Los Angeles Times later described Roscoe's as "such an L.A. institution that people don't even question the strange combo anymore."[6]Wikipedia, "Roscoe's House of Chicken 'N Waffles"

By the 1990s, Roscoe's had seven locations across greater Los Angeles and had become a pilgrimage destination for food journalists and tourists. The restaurant's cultural prominence made chicken and waffles legible as a category nationally — not merely as a regional dish but as an emblem of soul food culture more broadly.[7]Resy, "The Layered Legacy of Roscoe's House of Chicken & Waffles"

SECTION 05

From Regional to National

The dish's transition from regional soul food to mainstream restaurant menu item occurred primarily in the 2000s and 2010s, driven by the intersection of food media attention, the rise of food-focused television, and a broader cultural reconsideration of soul food as a serious culinary tradition. Chicken and waffles appeared on upscale menus, in food journalism, and in competitive cooking contexts — each iteration moving it further from its origins in late-night necessity.[8]Food Service Director, "The Secret History of Chicken and Waffles"

17th–18th century Pennsylvania Dutch settlers establish chicken-and-waffle tradition: shredded chicken, white gravy, waffle as base. Church dinners and community meals.
1930s–1940s Harlem soul food version emerges at Wells Supper Club and related venues: fried chicken alongside waffle, late-night jazz musician context.
1975 Herb Hudson opens Roscoe's House of Chicken 'N Waffles in Long Beach. Celebrity patronage drives rapid cultural visibility in Los Angeles.
1980s–1990s Roscoe's expands to seven Los Angeles locations. The dish becomes a recognized soul food category beyond its regional origins.
2000s–2010s Food media, upscale restaurant adoption, and cooking competition television accelerate national spread. Chicken and waffles enters mainstream menus across the country.

The mainstreaming process involved significant cultural translation: versions appearing on suburban chain menus or brunch menus often bear little resemblance to either the Pennsylvania Dutch or Harlem originals. The dish's widespread adoption is simultaneously evidence of soul food culture's influence on American cuisine and a site where that culture's specificity is routinely flattened.[9]Tasting Table, "The Mysterious Origins Of Chicken And Waffles"
