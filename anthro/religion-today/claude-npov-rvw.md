## Issues Found

### POV (Point of View)

1. **S3, Hindu Nationalism (line ~69, summary):** "BJP governance since 2014 has systematically eroded secular foundations." The word "systematically" is an editorial characterization. The cited source (East Asia Forum) uses this framing, but the project presents it as its own analytical voice without attribution. The detail text (line ~124) repeats it. A neutral framing would attribute: "Critics argue..." or "According to East Asia Forum..." *Moderate.*

2. **S3, Hindu Nationalism detail (line ~126):** "This isn't fringe activity -- it's mainstreamed by the ruling party of the world's largest democracy." This is editorial voice, not attributed scholarly analysis. While defensible analytically, presenting it as self-evident fact rather than an interpretation violates NPOV. *Moderate.*

3. **S3, Christian Nationalism (line ~121):** "This is not fringe ideology; it is a coordinated institutional capture strategy with mainstream Republican support." The phrase "institutional capture strategy" is a specific analytical framing from critics. The cited source (Kettering Foundation) uses similar language, but the text presents it as established fact. *Moderate.*

4. **S3, Identity Politics (line ~151):** "'Christian' in 'Christian nationalism' is less about the Sermon on the Mount and more about white cultural identity." This is a specific scholarly interpretation (associated with sociologists like Samuel Perry and Andrew Whitehead). It is presented as fact rather than attributed analysis. Defenders of Christian nationalism would dispute this characterization. *Moderate.*

5. **S3, The Irony callout (line ~154):** "The irony of religious nationalism is that it often violates the core ethical teachings of the traditions it claims to represent." This is a normative judgment. While widely held in interfaith and liberal theological circles, it is contested by adherents who would argue their nationalism IS consistent with their faith. *Moderate.*

6. **S6, India coexistence model (line ~73):** "Once a model; now a cautionary tale." This is editorial shorthand that takes a position on whether India's trajectory is negative. Defenders of the current Indian government would contest this framing. *Minor.*

7. **S5, Wellness as religion (line ~105):** "Wellness is religion with the metaphysics removed and a price tag attached." Witty but editorial. This is an analytical position, not a neutral description. *Minor.*

### RIGOR (Factual/Analytical)

1. **S1, Islam growth detail (line ~141):** "Unlike Christianity's historical spread through colonialism and missionary activity, Islam's current growth is overwhelmingly demographic." The contrast is oversimplified. Christianity also spread through demographic growth, and Islam historically spread through conquest and trade networks as well as demographics. The sentence reduces both traditions' complex histories to single mechanisms. *Moderate.*

2. **S1, MENA diversity-conflict correlation (line ~176):** "The Middle East-North Africa region is the least religiously diverse, and also the region with the highest levels of religious tension and conflict. This correlation... may reflect the absence of social mechanisms that diverse societies develop." The causal suggestion (low diversity causes conflict through absence of coexistence mechanisms) is speculative and ignores major confounding factors: geopolitics, oil, colonial borders, authoritarian governance. Correlation is noted but the causal mechanism offered is insufficiently hedged. *Moderate.*

3. **S4, New Atheism (line ~70):** "By 2025, it has largely exhausted itself." This assessment is editorial and debatable. While the four named figures are less prominent, organized atheism continues through organizations and online communities. The "exhaustion" narrative is one interpretation. *Minor.*

4. **S2, Plateau claim (line ~56):** "The 'easy switchers' already left" is presented as analysis but reads as editorializing about the nature of remaining religious adherents. *Minor.*

5. **S3, Pew vs PRRI measurement gap:** The text correctly identifies the 6% vs 32% gap between Pew and PRRI measures of US Christian nationalism. However, it does not adequately discuss why the gap exists beyond "different measurement frameworks." The Pew measure asks about theology; PRRI asks about cultural attitudes. This methodological distinction matters for interpretation. *Minor.*

### BIAS (Systemic perspective gaps)

1. **Western/US-centric framing:** Despite claiming global scope, the project is heavily US-centric. S2 (Rise of the Nones) is almost entirely about the US. S3 gives the US and India detailed treatment but compresses "Political Islam" into a single subsection covering four countries in a few paragraphs. The diversity of Muslim-majority governance models deserves more space. *Moderate.*

2. **Liberal-secular analytical lens:** The project consistently frames religious nationalism as problematic and secularization/pluralism as normatively preferable. S3's consensus section and verdict grids implicitly favor the "Democracy Under Threat" framing. The project does not seriously engage with the perspective that religious values in public life may be democratically legitimate. Scholars like Patrick Deneen or Sohrab Ahmari who argue for a positive role of religion in politics are absent. *Moderate.*

3. **Christianity and Islam receive disproportionate attention.** Hinduism appears only in the nationalism section. Judaism is barely mentioned. Sikhism, Jainism, and indigenous religions are absent. The methodology acknowledges geographic scope limits but not tradition-level gaps. *Moderate.*

4. **Absence of religious insider perspectives:** The project draws primarily on sociological, polling, and journalistic sources. Theological perspectives from within traditions (how believers understand their own experience) are mostly absent. The Dalai Lama appears in S6, but no Christian theologians, Muslim scholars beyond Azra, or Hindu thinkers are cited engaging with these trends from within their traditions. *Moderate.*

5. **S1, "Two Christianities" framing (line ~163):** The characterization of Western Christianity as "intellectual, tradition-bound" and Global South Christianity as "experiential, emotional, and community-oriented" risks reproducing a colonial-era binary that positions Western religion as rational and non-Western religion as emotional. Global South theologians would push back on this characterization. *Minor.*

### TONE

1. **S3 overall tone:** The fundamentalism section reads as more polemical than the other sections. Phrases like "measurable consequences for minority safety," "institutional capture strategy," "dehumanization of out-groups," and "coordinated institutional capture" use charged language that, while analytically defensible, creates a tone of alarm rather than scholarly description. The section is well-sourced but reads as advocacy-adjacent. *Moderate.*

2. **Callout blocks as editorials:** Similar to the flag-history project, callout blocks tend toward declarative, emphatic statements that exceed the evidence in the main text. Examples: "The religion being 'defended' is not the religion being practiced" (S3), "You can leave religion without religion leaving you" (S1). *Minor, pervasive.*

3. **S5, New Movements framing:** The wellness and digital religion sections carry a detectable undertone of skepticism toward non-institutional spirituality. "Minus the theology, plus a price tag" and "religion hollow" framing position wellness-as-spirituality negatively compared to traditional religion. The text does present both sides in the verdict grids but the prose leans toward the "unbundling as loss" interpretation. *Minor.*

## Fixes Applied

No changes were made to the content files. The issues are primarily analytical framing choices rather than factual errors. The project's verdict grids and "Consensus & Fractures" sections in each deep-dive provide structural balance, even where the prose leans toward particular interpretations.

## Remaining Concerns

1. **S3 (Fundamentalism) is the highest-priority section for NPOV remediation.** Multiple instances of editorial voice presenting contested interpretations as established fact. The section should more consistently attribute critical claims to their sources rather than adopting them as its own analytical voice.

2. **The "systematically eroded" characterization of BJP governance** should be attributed to its source rather than presented in the project's own voice.

3. **The "institutional capture strategy" characterization of Project 2025** should be attributed to the Kettering Foundation source.

4. **The liberal-secular analytical lens** is the deepest structural issue. Adding one or two sources that argue for the democratic legitimacy of religious values in public life (even while maintaining the project's analytical framework) would strengthen the claim to neutrality.

5. **The US-centrism of S2** could be partially addressed by expanding the global section (currently one subsection) to match the detail given to US data.

6. **The Islam growth/Christianity spread contrast** should be revised to avoid reducing complex histories to single mechanisms.

7. **The MENA diversity-conflict causal suggestion** should be more heavily hedged and should acknowledge confounding factors.
