# NPOV & Rigor Review — Religion Today

**Date:** 2026-04-20
**Reviewer:** Claude (npov-anthro-r2)
**Files reviewed:** survey-paper.html, tensions-paper.html

## Summary
A two-paper suite (survey + tensions) mapping contemporary religion across six dimensions (global landscape, the "nones," politicization, religion-science, new movements, interfaith/pluralism) with a synthesis proposing an "unbundling" framework. The prose is generally hedged and NPOV-disciplined — this is the most scholarly-registered of the four anthro suites reviewed. The authors use attributive language ("scholars observe", "critics contend", "some analyses suggest"), and the unbundling framework is explicitly labeled as "a hypothesis" and "conceptual model." The main concerns are: (a) occasional Western-centric framing that describes the Global South's non-secularization as something "not yet started" (implicitly framing secularization as developmental destination); (b) a few unhedged superlatives and uncited statistics; (c) asymmetric sourcing in §3 politicization, where Christian nationalism is reported through PRRI's higher-reading methodology while Hindu nationalism is reported through observers' characterizations — the treatment isn't parallel.

## POV Issues
- survey-paper.html#methods — "This paper was compiled in March 2026. Literature collection and deep-dive generation were assisted by Claude (Anthropic). All source assessments, editorial judgments, and synthesis decisions were made with human oversight." — First-person implicit ("this paper"), but conventional academic-methods register. Not a violation.
- No instances of second-person "you/your" found in exposition.
- No first-person "I/we/our" as editorial voice found in either paper.

## Logical Consistency Issues
- tensions-paper.html#t5 (paradox title/paragraph) — "The $1.8 trillion market" appears in tensions-paper.html at T5 paradox title while survey-paper.html#movements reports "The global wellness economy, valued at $6.8 trillion." The tensions paper stat row also shows "$1.8T Wellness economy." The two papers therefore give different figures for the same variable without a bridging note:
  - survey §5: "global wellness economy, valued at $6.8 trillion (growing 7.9% annually and projected to approach $10 trillion by 2029)"
  - tensions §T5: "$1.8T Wellness economy" stat card and "$1.8 trillion market" in paradox title
  — This is a genuine numerical inconsistency. The $1.8T figure is the McKinsey scope (subset), while the $6.8T is the Global Wellness Institute scope (broader). The papers do not note that they are measuring different things. Suggested fix: add a note distinguishing the two figures.

- tensions-paper.html#t5 vs. stat-row "$1.8T Wellness economy" and "$2.16B Spiritual apps" — consistent with the apps-market number in the survey.

- survey-paper.html#movements — "91% of church leaders anticipate welcoming AI into ministry by 2025. Currently, 45% actively utilize it, representing an 80% increase year over year." — Internally: 91% openness vs. 45% active use is coherent; 80% YoY increase is unverifiable from linked source but the figure is not internally contradictory.

## Scholastic Rigor Issues
- survey-paper.html#landscape — "Christians remain the world's largest group at 2.3 billion (28.8%, a decrease of 1.8 percentage points from 2010)." — Cited to Pew; good. No issue.
- survey-paper.html#landscape — "Buddhism contracted, losing 19 million adherents." — Uncited inline; presumably Pew. Add cite.
- survey-paper.html#landscape — "1.4 billion Christians now live in the Global South (constituting a majority for the first time)" — "For the first time" needs temporal attribution (first time since when — 2020? Modern era?). Suggested hedge: attribute to Pew 2025 "for the first time in Pew's series".
- survey-paper.html#landscape — "If current trajectories persist, Islam and Christianity are projected to reach population parity around 2070, each comprising approximately 32% of humanity." — Attributed to Pew's implicit projection but date/source not linked. Suggested hedge: add cite to Pew's 2070 projection paper.
- survey-paper.html#landscape — "the trends observed in wealthy nations have not yet manifested in most of the world" — "Not yet" presupposes the trends will manifest, which is the thesis under dispute. This is the Western-centric framing issue. Suggested hedge: "have not manifested in most of the world" or "have not been observed in most of the world" (drop "yet"). The SVG diagram label "Global South: not started" has the same issue — it codes the Global South as pre-secularization.
- survey-paper.html#nones — "Two-thirds left because they stopped believing. One-fifth cite dislike of organized religion — corruption, abuse scandals, anti-LGBTQ positions." — "Two-thirds" and "one-fifth" uncited inline. Suggested hedge: attribute to Pew.
- survey-paper.html#nones — "Gen Z exhibits an 18% atheist/agnostic rate, which is 4.5 times higher than the Silent Generation's 4%." — Stats uncited inline. Suggested hedge: attribute to Pew / PRRI.
- survey-paper.html#politics — "Pew's survey of 55,000 individuals across 36 countries on religious nationalism indicates Indonesia at 46% and Bangladesh at 45%." — Specific figures without direct Pew link; Pew is the cited institution but the specific study isn't linked. Add direct URL.
- survey-paper.html#politics — "Among White evangelical Protestants, 67% hold Christian Nationalist beliefs." — PRRI figure; attribution chain is present but would benefit from a direct inline cite.
- survey-paper.html#politics — "Critics contend that the governance of the BJP since 2014 has contributed to the erosion of India's secular constitutional framework. The Waqf Amendment Act and Citizenship Amendment Act have been characterized by observers as discriminatory against Muslims. Modi's 2024 campaign rhetoric was described by critics as Islamophobic." — Use of "critics contend" / "observers have characterized" is NPOV-appropriate attributive framing, but the critics are unnamed (no inline cite). Compare with the PRRI-cited Christian nationalism numbers — the sourcing for Hindu nationalism is less formal. Suggested hedge: cite specific analysts (e.g., Christophe Jaffrelot, Kapur, ACT Alliance).
- survey-paper.html#politics — "In Saudi Arabia, Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman (MBS) has actively pursued policies to reduce the political influence of the religious establishment." — Declarative without cite. Suggested hedge: attribute to a specific analysis (Vision 2030, Brookings).
- survey-paper.html#science — "The 'conflict thesis' — originating with Draper (1874) and White (1896) — decades ago. The current scholarly consensus is the 'complexity thesis'..." — Good attribution of historiography.
- survey-paper.html#science — "More than 50% of scientists in India, Italy, Taiwan, and Turkey self-identify as religious." — Ecklund RASIC is cited. Good.
- survey-paper.html#science — "Even in the UK, only 32% of scientists perceive a conflict between science and religion." — Source chain present.
- survey-paper.html#science — "Some analyses suggest a shift towards a 'post-secular' curiosity, characterized by public intellectuals exploring religion's civilizational role without necessarily advocating personal belief." — "Some analyses" vague. Suggested hedge: cite specific analyst (Taylor, Smith, Habermas).
- survey-paper.html#movements — "The global wellness economy, valued at $6.8 trillion (growing 7.9% annually and projected to approach $10 trillion by 2029)" — Global Wellness Institute cited in ref list; link in paragraph would help.
- survey-paper.html#movements — "An estimated 10 million additional Americans now engage with Scripture outside traditional church settings." — Source for "10 million" unclear (Barna? American Bible Society?). Add cite.
- survey-paper.html#movements — "Currently, 45% actively utilize it, representing an 80% increase year over year." — "80% increase year over year" unlikely and needs source. Suggested hedge: cite the Barna Group / Grey Matter Research study the figures come from.
- survey-paper.html#pluralism — "The formula 'one religion per person, many religions for humanity' has been adopted broadly and cited in interfaith contexts. However, it is particularly compatible with Buddhism's non-exclusive theology." — Interpretive claim about Buddhism's theology; uncited. Suggested hedge: attribute to a specific comparative-religion scholar or note that Buddhism is generally characterized as non-exclusive by Western scholars of religion.
- survey-paper.html#synthesis — "religion in the contemporary world is characterized not by a single trajectory but by the simultaneous occurrence of secularization, revival, and transformation" — This is the paper's thesis. Labeled hypothetically later in same section. OK.
- survey-paper.html#synthesis — "a Hindu nationalist who rarely visits a temple but strongly identifies with Hindu civilizational identity may not fit conventional definitions of religiosity" — The example is neutral but frames Hindu nationalism as the canonical religious-identity-without-practice case; this could be generalized to Christian cultural identity in the US for parity. Suggested: give parallel examples across traditions to avoid unintentional framing.

- tensions-paper.html#intro — "such contradictions are not merely anomalies but constitute fundamental aspects of the observed phenomena" — Strong claim about status of contradictions. Suggested hedge: "are often analytically significant features of the observed phenomena".
- tensions-paper.html#t1 — "more than all other religions combined" (of Islam's 347M growth) — Factual claim about net growth comparison; needs a cite (Pew analysis does support this but the attribution should be explicit).
- tensions-paper.html#t2 — "The category 'spiritual but not religious,' previously considered imprecise, may now represent a more accurate description for the experiences of tens of millions of individuals." — "Previously considered imprecise" is uncited; "may now represent a more accurate description" is evaluative. Suggested hedge: "The 'spiritual but not religious' category, once treated as imprecise by sociologists of religion, has been increasingly adopted to describe tens of millions of Americans" (with cite).
- tensions-paper.html#t4 — "The Draper-White conflict thesis (1874/1896)" — Good attribution.
- tensions-paper.html#t4 — "The persistence of the warfare narrative, despite its abandonment by historians, can be attributed to its utility for various groups." — "Can be attributed to" is hedged; good.
- tensions-paper.html#t5 — "some scholars to characterize secular wellness as a form of religion divested of its theological foundations" — "Some scholars" vague. Suggested hedge: cite a specific scholar (e.g., Jeremy Carrette & Richard King, *Selling Spirituality*).
- tensions-paper.html#t5 — "while some reports indicate a decline of approximately 19 million adherents in Buddhism, the 'mindfulness' industry has grown to an estimated $168 billion" — "$168 billion" mindfulness-industry figure needs a source (this is not the $1.8T or $6.8T figure). Suggested hedge: cite the specific McKinsey or IBISWorld source.
- tensions-paper.html#t5 — "A key question in this context is whether sacred elements can retain their original significance when integrated into a commodified framework." — This is a rhetorical/editorial framing. Acceptable if followed by attribution. Suggested hedge: "Scholars of religion and commodification (Carrette & King; Jain) raise the question whether sacred elements..."
- tensions-paper.html#t6 — "a causal relationship that the available evidence does not yet resolve" — Good hedge.

## Tone Issues
- survey-paper.html#abstract — "the analysis suggests not the triumph of any single narrative" — "Triumph" is mildly rhetorical. Neutral replacement: "the analysis suggests not the dominance of any single narrative".
- survey-paper.html#landscape — Western-centric framing via "not yet started" (SVG diagram) and "have not yet manifested" — see rigor issue above. This is a tone/framing issue as well as a rigor one. Neutral replacement: drop "yet" and "not started"; use "where these trends have not been observed".
- survey-paper.html#politics — Asymmetric treatment: the US section leads with PRRI's highest-reading methodology (32%) while the Indian section leans on unnamed "critics" and "observers". Suggested balancing: either add direct inline citation for the India analysts (Jaffrelot, etc.) or acknowledge that the Hindu-nationalism measurement base is less quantitatively developed in the sourcing.
- survey-paper.html#movements — "A key distinction from traditional religious frameworks lies in the absence of explicit theology and a transcendent referent, coupled with a consumer-product delivery model." — Acceptable framing but "the absence of explicit theology" is a claim requiring care — wellness culture does have implicit quasi-theologies. Suggested hedge: "A commonly noted distinction lies in the absence of explicit theology..."
- survey-paper.html#pluralism — "French laïcité is the archetype" — "Archetype" overstated; laïcité is one of several distinct secularist traditions. Neutral replacement: "French laïcité is often cited as an archetype".
- tensions-paper.html#intro — "Contradictory findings often represent significant insights in empirical research" — "Significant insights" is a thematic assertion; acceptable but editorial. Neutral replacement: "Contradictory findings in empirical research are often analytically productive".
- tensions-paper.html#meta — "these separated elements retain, lose, or transform their original social and meaning-making functions is an open empirical question" — Good hedge.
- tensions-paper.html#meta — "This paper argues that analyzing these tensions provides a more comprehensive framework for understanding contemporary religion than attempting to resolve them into a single narrative, though that argument itself remains open to scholarly contestation." — Well-hedged meta-argument.

## Priority Fixes
1. Resolve the $6.8T vs. $1.8T wellness-economy numerical inconsistency between survey §5 and tensions §T5 — the two figures measure different scopes (Global Wellness Institute vs. McKinsey) but are presented without this context.
2. Drop "yet" from "trends... have not yet manifested" and the SVG label "Global South: not started" — both code the Global South as pre-secularization, which is a Western-centric framing at odds with the Berger/Nature-Communications nuance the paper otherwise endorses.
3. Rebalance sourcing in §3 politicization: cite specific Hindu-nationalism scholars (Jaffrelot, Kapur, Bhatt) inline to match the PRRI-cited US section's rigor.
4. Add inline citations for the load-bearing stats: Buddhism -19M (Pew), Gen Z 18% atheist/agnostic (Pew/PRRI), Pew's 55,000/36-country Indonesia-46% study, $168B mindfulness-industry figure (McKinsey/IBISWorld).
5. Hedge the "spiritual but not religious" and "mindfulness industry" claims in tensions-paper §T5 with specific scholarly attribution (Carrette & King, Jain).

## Overall Assessment
**B+/A–.** This is the most scholarly-registered of the four anthro suites reviewed. No second-person or first-person authorial voice violations, explicit hedge language around the unbundling framework, and consistent attribution for most load-bearing claims. The chief issues are (a) the genuine numerical inconsistency between survey and tensions on the wellness economy, (b) subtle Western-centric developmental framing in the secularization-thesis section, and (c) asymmetric sourcing depth between the US-politicization and India-politicization sub-sections. Addressing priority fixes 1–3 would push this suite to clean A–.
