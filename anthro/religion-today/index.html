Religion Today

An anthropological survey of religion in the contemporary world — demographics, disaffiliation, politicization, science, new spiritualities, and pluralism.

Religion in the contemporary world is defined not by a single trajectory but by three simultaneous movements: secularization in wealthy nations, revival in the Global South, and transformation everywhere. Islam grew by 347 million in a decade while 40 million Americans left the pews. The $6.8 trillion wellness economy absorbs meditation and mindfulness from Buddhism while Buddhism itself loses members. 91% of church leaders welcome AI while 73% of Americans say AI has no place in faith.

This survey maps the landscape across 6 dimensions and 52 sources — Pew Research Center, Gallup International, PRRI, academic sociology (Inglehart, Ecklund, Molteni), market research (Global Wellness Institute, McKinsey), and journalism. Each section is built only after assessing whether sources are sufficient. The most interesting findings are the tensions — places where credible sources reach opposite conclusions about the same reality.

Six Tensions

The most valuable findings in any survey are the contradictions — places where credible sources, looking at the same evidence, reach opposite conclusions. These six tensions define religion in the contemporary world.
  • T1 Growth vs. decline — Islam grew by 347 million in a decade. Pentecostalism claims 600M+ adherents. Yet wealthy nations secularize consistently: religious self-identification fell from 68% to 56% globally. Religion is simultaneously booming and collapsing depending where you look. [S1]
  • T2 Belief vs. belonging — 29% of Americans are unaffiliated, but most still believe in God. 78% say they can be moral without religion. Leaving the institution does not mean leaving the sacred. The nones are not atheists. [S2]
  • T3 Nationalism vs. pluralism — 32% of Americans hold Christian nationalist beliefs. Hindu nationalism reshapes Indian democracy. Yet parliamentary interfaith conferences multiply and pluralistic theology advances. The same era produces both religious nationalism and interfaith cooperation. [S3] [S6]
  • T4 Science vs. faith framing — The public assumes warfare between science and religion. Historians rejected the conflict thesis decades ago. Over 50% of scientists in four countries are religious. Even atheists show intuitive preference for belief. The gap between popular narrative and scholarly consensus is enormous. [S4]
  • T5 Secular wellness vs. religious practice — The $6.8 trillion wellness economy has absorbed meditation, mindfulness, and community from religious traditions while institutional religion declines. Wellness is religion with the theology removed and a price tag attached. Is this creative transformation or slow death? [S5]
  • T6 Digital expansion vs. institutional decline — 91% of churches welcome AI. TikTok is a spiritual education platform. 10 million more Americans read Scripture digitally. Yet physical attendance collapses and 40 million have left. Digital expansion may accelerate rather than reverse institutional decline. [S5]
