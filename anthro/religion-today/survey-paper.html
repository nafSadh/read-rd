RELIGION TODAY · SURVEY PAPER · 52 SOURCES

Religion Today

An anthropological survey of religion in the contemporary world — demographics, disaffiliation, politicization, science, new spiritualities, and pluralism across 52 sources.

March 2026 6 Dimensions 52 Sources 6 Tensions
ABSTRACT

Abstract

Religion in the contemporary world is characterized not by a single trajectory but by three simultaneous movements: secularization in wealthy nations, revival in the Global South, and transformation globally. This survey maps the landscape across six dimensions — global demographics, disaffiliation, politicization, science, new spiritualities, and pluralism — drawing on 52 sources including Pew Research Center[1]Pew Research Center →, Gallup International[2]Gallup International →, PRRI[3]PRRI →, academic sociology, market research, and journalism. Key findings include the identification of *tensions*: instances where credible sources, examining the same evidence, reach opposing conclusions about the same reality.

Islam grew by 347 million in a decade while 40 million Americans disaffiliated from religious institutions. The $6.8 trillion wellness economy incorporates meditation and mindfulness practices derived from Buddhism while Buddhism itself experiences membership declines. 91% of church leaders express openness to AI while 73% of Americans state that AI has no place in faith. The secularization thesis is supported by data from Stockholm and contested by data from Lagos. The analysis suggests not the triumph of any single narrative — secular, revivalist, or transformative — but points toward a hypothesis of religious *unbundling*: that its components (belief, ritual, community, identity, morality) may be recombining in configurations that existing theoretical frameworks may not yet fully capture.

SECTION 01

The Global Religious Landscape

Pew Research Center's 2025 study, drawing on more than 2,700 data sources, provides a comprehensive snapshot of global religious demographics. Christians remain the world's largest group at 2.3 billion (28.8%, a decrease of 1.8 percentage points from 2010). Muslims grew by 347 million, reaching 2.0 billion (25.6%). The unaffiliated expanded from 1.6 billion to 1.9 billion. Buddhism contracted, losing 19 million adherents.

Geographic Shifts in Adherence

Observed shifts in 21st-century religious demographics are predominantly geographic. Sub-Saharan Africa now hosts 30.7% of the world's Christians, surpassing Europe (22%) for the first time. Christianity decreased as a share of the population in 41 countries — nearly all in the Americas and Europe. The US saw a 14-point drop; Australia lost 20 points. Meanwhile, 1.4 billion Christians now live in the Global South (constituting a majority for the first time), and Pentecostalism claims over 600 million adherents worldwide.

Islam's growth is primarily demographic, driven by higher fertility rates and younger age structures in Sub-Saharan Africa and South/Southeast Asia. Religious switching plays a minor role. If current trajectories persist, Islam and Christianity are projected to reach population parity around 2070, each comprising approximately 32% of humanity.

The Secularization Paradox

Ronald Inglehart[4]Inglehart →'s existential security thesis is supported by observations in wealthy nations: as societies achieve economic stability, individual-choice norms emerge, pro-fertility norms weaken, and religious adherence declines. Gallup International confirms this quantitatively across 42 countries (43,250 respondents): 14% atheist in high-income countries versus 3% in low-income. Globally, religious self-identification fell from 68% (2005) to 56% (2024).

However, the thesis exhibits a geographic limitation. Peter Berger[5]Peter Berger →, who previously championed secularization theory, revised his position in 1999, stating that the world is "massively religious," with only two exceptions — Western/Central Europe and an international intellectual class. The Nature Communications study[6]Nature Communications study → (2025) offers a possible resolution: religion may decline in a pattern tied to existential security, but the trends observed in wealthy nations have not yet manifested in most of the world. Whether this reflects a universal process or context-specific dynamics remains an open empirical question.

SECTION 02

Rise of the Nones

After significant growth from 16% (2007) to 29% (2024) of the US adult population, the religiously unaffiliated — the "nones" — recent data suggest a plateau. Pew's 2023[7]Pew's 2023 →–24 Religious Landscape Study found that Christian decline may have "leveled off." Religion Unplugged[8]Religion Unplugged → argues this reflects a structural ceiling: the "easy switchers" have already departed, and the remaining religious population carries stronger institutional commitment.

Belief Without Belonging

Analysis of the religiously unaffiliated indicates that while some identify as atheist (5%) or agnostic (6%), a substantial portion (19% identifying as "nothing in particular") do not. Pew's global study confirms that many unaffiliated individuals retain belief in God, the afterlife, and spiritual forces, suggesting that the majority of nones are not atheists. This trend is often characterized as dis-institutionalization rather than secularization in the Enlightenment sense, indicating a departure from religious institutions without a complete abandonment of spiritual or sacred beliefs. 78% of nones say one can be moral without religion; 54% say one doesn't need religion to be spiritual.

Why They Leave

Two-thirds left because they stopped believing. One-fifth cite dislike of organized religion — corruption, abuse scandals, anti-LGBTQ positions. A contributing structural pattern identified is a progressive cultural shift that may be outpacing religious institutional adaptation. Generational replacement is frequently cited as a significant factor in this trend; for instance, Gen Z exhibits an 18% atheist/agnostic rate, which is 4.5 times higher than the Silent Generation's 4%. Over a 25-year period, approximately 40 million Americans ceased attending religious services.

The Plateau Question

The question of whether the growth of the religiously unaffiliated has genuinely plateaued or is experiencing a temporary pause remains a significant area of inquiry. If the 29% figure represents a ceiling, approximately two-thirds of the American population would remain religiously identified in the foreseeable future, a scenario that diverges from projections based on linear extrapolation. However, Gen Z's significantly higher rate of nonreligion suggests that generational replacement could contribute to continued shifts in religious affiliation, even without further individual deconversion.

SECTION 03

Fundamentalism & Politicization

A historical prediction posited that religion would retreat into private life with the advancement of modernity. However, it has instead emerged as a significant force in global politics. Pew's survey of 55,000 individuals across 36 countries on religious nationalism indicates Indonesia at 46% and Bangladesh at 45%. The United States registers 6% on Pew's explicit nationalism measure, while PRRI's cultural-alignment methodology identifies 32% of Americans as Christian nationalism adherents or sympathizers. This disparity highlights the complexity of measurement, where the definition of religious nationalism influences reported prevalence.

Three Theaters

Christian Nationalism in the United States. PRRI data indicates that 11% of Americans identify as Christian Nationalist Adherents and 21% as Sympathizers. Among White evangelical Protestants, 67% hold Christian Nationalist beliefs. States with high concentrations include Mississippi and Oklahoma (51%), Arkansas (49%), and West Virginia (48%). In 2024, 81% of White evangelicals voted for Trump, and 85% lean or identify as Republican. Furthermore, 22% express a desire for Christianity to be declared the official US religion. The Heritage Foundation's Project 2025 serves as an institutional manifestation, outlining a policy blueprint designed to integrate evangelical priorities into governance.

Hindu Nationalism in India. Critics contend that the governance of the BJP since 2014 has contributed to the erosion of India's secular constitutional framework. The Waqf Amendment Act and Citizenship Amendment Act have been characterized by observers as discriminatory against Muslims. Modi's 2024 campaign rhetoric was described by critics as Islamophobic. The Nagpur clashes (March 2024), which were triggered by Hindu nationalist demands to demolish a Mughal-era Muslim tomb, exemplify how religious nationalism has integrated into mainstream political discourse, according to analysts.

Political Islam: The Spectrum. Islamic fundamentalism exhibits diverse manifestations rather than a monolithic structure. Indonesia upholds a wasathiyah (moderation) tradition. Turkey integrates Islamist politics with democratic structures, though under increasing authoritarian pressure. Iran's theocratic model encounters internal opposition. In Saudi Arabia, Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman (MBS) has actively pursued policies to reduce the political influence of the religious establishment. This range of expressions indicates that "political Islam" encompasses a spectrum of approaches, not a singular position.

Religion as Identity Politics

A recurring pattern suggests that religion increasingly functions as an ethnic-cultural marker rather than primarily as a belief system. Sociologists such as Perry and Whitehead contend that the term "Christian" within "Christian nationalism" primarily signifies white cultural identity rather than theological adherence. Similarly, analysts observe that Hindu nationalism utilizes Hinduism as a civilizational identity rather than a devotional one. From a Durkheimian perspective, the demarcation of group boundaries appears to hold greater significance than the specific content of belief. The ACT Alliance[9]ACT Alliance → distinguishes fundamentalism as seeking political power and rejecting pluralism. Saiya[10]Saiya →'s 2024 analysis indicates a correlation between the political empowerment of Christian nationalism and physical attacks on religious minorities. These findings suggest measurable consequences for the safety of minority groups.

SECTION 04

Religion & Science

The idea that science and religion are locked in eternal warfare has been characterized by historians of science as a persistent popular myth. Historians of science rejected the "conflict thesis" — originating with Draper (1874) and White (1896) — decades ago. The current scholarly consensus is the "complexity thesis": science and religion have had cooperative, conflictual, and independent relationships depending on era, institution, and specific question. Popular culture, however, often perpetuates the warfare narrative.

Scientists Are More Religious Than Commonly Assumed

Ecklund[11]Ecklund →'s RASIC study (9,422 scientists across 8 regions) found that more than 50% of scientists in India, Italy, Taiwan, and Turkey self-identify as religious. Even in the UK, only 32% of scientists perceive a conflict between science and religion. Scientists in Taiwan, Hong Kong, and India are often more religious than the general public. Younger scientists (under 35) are more likely to attend religious services than the older baby boomer cohort.

Even Atheists Prefer Belief

The PNAS study[12]PNAS study → (2025) indicates that even in highly secular countries, atheists intuitively associate religious belief with positive outcomes. Researchers describe this as "belief in belief" — a deep cognitive default shaped by millennia of religious cultural influence. This observation has led some scholars to propose that complete secularization may encounter inherent cognitive or biological limits.

The New Atheism's Decline

The New Atheism movement (associated with figures such as Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris, and Dennett) experienced its peak cultural prominence in the 2000s–2010s, with its influence reportedly diminishing by 2025, though organized atheist communities persist. Critics argue its aggressive tone alienated potential allies and that it failed to provide what religion offers: community, ritual, meaning, consolation. Some analyses suggest a shift towards a "post-secular" curiosity, characterized by public intellectuals exploring religion's civilizational role without necessarily advocating personal belief. Cabello[13]Cabello →'s pre-print adds nuance, suggesting that scientific literacy may reduce dogmatism without necessarily reducing belief, thereby suggesting, as an interpretation of that data, that education may transform rather than destroy religion — though this remains an open empirical question.

SECTION 05

New Movements & Spirituality

Religious practice is undergoing a process of disaggregation. The global wellness economy, valued at $6.8 trillion (growing 7.9% annually and projected to approach $10 trillion by 2029), now offers services that parallel functions historically associated with religious institutions. These include community formation through yoga studios, ritualistic practices through meditation, meaning-making through self-help methodologies, moral frameworks through mindful living, and identity construction through wellness culture. A key distinction from traditional religious frameworks lies in the absence of explicit theology and a transcendent referent, coupled with a consumer-product delivery model.

Digital Religion

91% of church leaders anticipate welcoming AI into ministry by 2025. Currently, 45% actively utilize it, representing an 80% increase year over year. Digital scripture engagement shows significant growth, with Bible apps (59%), podcasts (33%), and TikTok (25% of young people's spiritual content) being prominent platforms. An estimated 10 million additional Americans now engage with Scripture outside traditional church settings. This presents a paradox: institutional attendance declines while digital religious engagement expands. The available evidence does not establish a causal link between these trends, but the pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that digital engagement may be associated with continued disaggregation rather than institutional recovery.

Psychedelic Spirituality

The Johns Hopkins[14]Johns Hopkins → clergy study (published 2025) administered psilocybin to clergy from multiple traditions. Participants reported profound spiritual experiences, yet their metaphysical beliefs remained "almost or entirely unchanged," with no reported conversions. Concurrently, novel psychedelic spiritual communities (NPSCs) are emerging, characterized by distinct theologies and rituals. Oregon and Colorado have taken legislative steps toward decriminalization. The tension between a therapeutic framework and a sacred framework for psychedelic use remains an unresolved aspect of this development.

The Unbundling Hypothesis

The observed changes in religious practice may exhibit parallels with processes of disaggregation seen in media and education sectors. One interpretive framework proposes that various components—such as belief, ritual, community, identity, and morality—are separating from the institutional structures that historically integrated them and are being redistributed across new forms. These new forms include commercial entities (e.g., wellness apps), digital platforms (e.g., TikTok spirituality), pharmacological contexts (e.g., psychedelic churches), and hybrid models (e.g., AI pastoral care). The determination of whether this represents a creative transformation or institutional decline hinges on the persistence of the sacred within these reconfigured expressions.

SECTION 06

Interfaith & Pluralism

Interfaith dialogue has transitioned from a less institutionalized status to a more mainstream presence. Parliamentary engagement is noteworthy, with events such as Rome 2025, Sarajevo 2024, and Baku 2024. The Council of Europe[15]Council of Europe → maintains a dedicated Committee for Interreligious Dialogue. In the US, Eboo Patel's Interfaith America[16]Interfaith America → frames religious literacy as civic competence.

Two Models

Secularism protects politics from religious domination through institutional neutrality — the state is neutral, religion is private, French laïcité is the archetype. Pluralism emphasizes coexistence through mutual recognition and dialogue — religion can be public, the state facilitates engagement, Charles Taylor's influence is evident. Most democracies blend both, but the emphasis matters for how they handle diversity.

The Dalai Lama[17]Dalai Lama →'s Model

The formula "one religion per person, many religions for humanity" has been adopted broadly and cited in interfaith contexts. However, it is particularly compatible with Buddhism's non-exclusive theology. Traditions with stronger exclusive truth claims face a distinct structural tension: affirming other paths while simultaneously asserting the unique truth of one's own is a question that scholars and practitioners have not resolved uniformly.

Models in Practice

Singapore manages religious harmony through legal frameworks and cultural norms. Bosnia pursues post-conflict reconstruction through parliamentary engagement. Northern Ireland advances through community-level dialogue. India's constitutional secularism, once widely regarded as a model, faces significant challenges under Hindu nationalist governance. No single model transfers universally; scholars observe that active institutional engagement has been a common feature of the cases typically cited as successful.

SECTION 07

Synthesis: Toward an Unbundling Framework

A central observation from this survey is that religion in the contemporary world is characterized not by a single trajectory but by the simultaneous occurrence of secularization, revival, and transformation. These three dynamics interact in complex ways that existing theoretical frameworks may not fully encompass, suggesting a need for new interpretive models.

Evidence from this survey challenges the premise of the secularization thesis regarding the disappearance of religion. Similarly, the desecularization thesis, which posits a simple resurgence, appears incomplete. These tensions support a hypothesis of religious unbundling, wherein traditional components—such as belief, ritual, community, identity, and morality—may be separating from their historical institutional containers and recombining into novel configurations. This framework is proposed as a conceptual model for the complex interaction of the three observed dynamics; it requires further empirical testing rather than representing a settled conclusion.

For instance, a wellness practitioner who meditates daily, attends sound-bath retreats, and identifies as "spiritual but not religious" does not align with a purely secular categorization. A Hindu nationalist who rarely visits a temple but strongly identifies with Hindu civilizational identity may not fit conventional definitions of religiosity. Similarly, a TikTok user who engages with Buddhist philosophy through short-form videos represents a mode of engagement not historically envisioned by traditional frameworks. These examples illustrate diverse configurations that the categories "religious," "secular," and "spiritual" can only approximately capture, highlighting the utility of the unbundling framework.

Unresolved Questions

Several significant questions remain beyond the scope of current empirical evidence. These include whether Islam's growth will persist as Muslim-majority countries develop, whether the proportion of religiously unaffiliated individuals will stabilize, how institutional religion might adapt to an unbundled landscape, and whether the post-secular turn will manifest in re-affiliation or primarily as an intellectual posture. Acknowledging these open questions defines the boundary between the findings of this survey and areas requiring further investigation.

Source Types

Large-scale surveys (Pew Research Center, Gallup International, PRRI), academic sociology (Inglehart, Ecklund, Molteni), institutional reports (Barna Group[18]Barna Group →, Lifeway Research[19]Lifeway Research →, Stanford AI Index), market research (Global Wellness Institute[20]Global Wellness Institute →, Grand View Research[21]Grand View Research →, McKinsey[22]McKinsey →), and journalism (NPR, Religion News Service, Deseret News, Religion Unplugged).

Limitations

This is a discourse survey, not primary research — it maps what others have found, and those findings carry their own biases. English-language bias means non-Anglophone scholarship is underrepresented. Survey data on religion is sensitive to question framing (Pew's 6% vs. PRRI's 32% on US religious nationalism). Wellness economy data relies on market research firms with less transparent methodologies. The rapidly evolving nature of digital religion means some data points may already be outdated.
