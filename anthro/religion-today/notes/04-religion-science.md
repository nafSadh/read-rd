# S4: Religion & Science

> The idea that science and religion are locked in eternal warfare is one of the most persistent myths of modernity. Historians of science rejected it decades ago. The majority of scientists in most countries don't see a conflict. And the New Atheism movement that championed the warfare narrative has largely exhausted itself. What's replacing it is more interesting: a nuanced "complexity thesis" where the relationship depends on context, and new research suggesting that even atheists carry deep cognitive preferences for belief.

**Sources:** 8 (Ecklund RASIC survey, History for Atheists, Sociology of Religion 2025, PNAS 2025, Gallup International, phys.org, synthesized New Atheism analysis, Cabello pre-print)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Academic consensus on warfare thesis, global scientist data, cognitive science of religion.

---

## 1. The Warfare Thesis: Origin and Death

John William Draper (1874) and Andrew Dickson White (1896) created the "conflict thesis" — the idea that religion is the perpetual enemy of science. This narrative was adopted by popular culture, reinforced by selective cases (Galileo, Scopes trial), and became the default assumption.

Actual historians of science have "long since rejected" it as simplistic, misleading, and ill-founded. The current consensus is the "complexity thesis": science and religion have had cooperative, conflictual, and independent relationships depending on era, institution, and specific question. The relationship is plural, not singular.

## 2. Scientists Are More Religious Than You Think

Ecklund's RASIC study (9,422 scientists, 8 regions): more than 50% of scientists in India, Italy, Taiwan, and Turkey self-identify as religious. Even in the UK — one of the most secular countries studied — only 32% of scientists see a conflict between science and religion. The US skews more secular among scientists, but the global picture demolishes the "scientists are all atheists" assumption.

Key finding: scientists in Taiwan, Hong Kong, and India are often MORE religious than the general public in those countries. Younger scientists (<35) are more likely to attend religious services than older baby boomer cohort.

## 3. Evolution Acceptance: The Map

Global variation is enormous. Western Europe: >70% accept evolution. US: 40% accept unguided evolution (up from 33% in 2010 — quiet but steady growth). Turkey, Indonesia, Malaysia: majority reject evolution. Education is the strongest predictor across all countries.

Crucially, many religious scientists accept evolution without seeing it as contradicting their faith. The "God vs. Darwin" framing is a false binary that both New Atheists and creationists promote.

## 4. The New Atheism and Its Decline

The New Atheism movement (Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris, Dennett) peaked in the 2000s-2010s. By 2025, it has largely exhausted itself. Reasons: Hitchens died (2011), Harris pivoted to meditation and consciousness, Dawkins became increasingly controversial on cultural issues. The movement's aggressive tone alienated potential allies and failed to provide what religion offers: community, ritual, meaning, consolation.

What's replacing it: a "post-secular" curiosity. Public intellectuals defending religion's civilizational role (Jordan Peterson, Jonathan Pageau, Iain McGilchrist). Not necessarily personal belief, but recognition that religious traditions encode deep wisdom about human nature and social organization.

## 5. Even Atheists Prefer Belief

The PNAS study (2025) is remarkable: even in highly secular countries, atheists intuitively associate religious belief with positive outcomes. Researchers describe this as "belief in belief" — a deep cognitive default shaped by millennia of religious influence. The implication: complete secularization may hit a biological or cognitive floor. Humans may be wired to prefer believing communities, even if they can't believe themselves.

Related: phys.org (2025) reports research suggesting humans may have evolved cognitive biases favoring religious belief. This is evolutionary psychology of religion, and it challenges the Enlightenment assumption that rationality naturally replaces superstition.

## 6. Science-Religion Dialogue: The Productive Turn

The more interesting frontier isn't conflict or compatibility — it's active dialogue. Templeton Foundation funding, Vatican Observatory collaborations, Islamic science-faith initiatives. The conversation has moved from "are they compatible?" to "how do different ways of knowing interact?"

Cabello's pre-print (2024): scientific literacy reduces dogmatism but not necessarily belief. Educated believers hold beliefs more flexibly. This suggests education doesn't destroy religion — it transforms it.

## 7. What Everyone Agrees On

1. The warfare thesis is historically wrong — complexity thesis is the consensus
2. Most scientists globally don't see science-religion conflict
3. Evolution acceptance varies enormously by country; education is key predictor
4. New Atheism has declined as a movement
5. The relationship is context-dependent, not universal

## 8. Where the Discourse Fractures

1. Is the "conflict" purely a cultural construction, or does religion genuinely impede specific sciences (stem cell research, climate action)?
2. Are scientists who are religious doing good science despite their beliefs, or does faith complement scientific work?
3. Will the post-secular turn be lasting, or is it a temporary cultural reaction?
4. Does the PNAS "belief in belief" finding support religion or undermine it (we prefer belief, but preferences aren't evidence)?
