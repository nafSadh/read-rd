# S3: Fundamentalism & Politicization

> Religion was supposed to retreat into private life as modernity advanced. Instead, it has become one of the most potent forces in global politics. Hindu nationalism reshapes Indian democracy. Christian nationalism correlates with Trump's vote share at the state level. Political Islam takes radically different forms from Indonesia to Iran. The question is no longer whether religion is political — it always was — but whether religious politics can coexist with democracy.

**Sources:** 9 (Pew Religious Nationalism, PRRI CN 2024, PRRI CN 2025, Pew 2024 Election, East Asia Forum, ACT Alliance, Kettering Foundation, Saiya 2024, Azra 2025)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Christian nationalism well-covered via PRRI/Pew. Hindu and Islamic nationalism from academic and policy sources.

---

## 1. Measuring Religious Nationalism

Pew surveyed 55,000 people in 36 countries. Their framework: how important is the historically dominant religion to national identity, and should sacred texts guide laws? Indonesia leads (46%), Bangladesh (45%). US is relatively low (6%). But Pew measures explicit religious nationalism — PRRI's cultural-alignment approach yields higher US numbers (32%).

Israel stands out: the only high-income country where >33% emphasize religion as central to national identity.

## 2. Christian Nationalism in the United States

PRRI: 32% of Americans qualify as CN Adherents (11%) or Sympathizers (21%). White evangelical Protestants: 67% hold CN beliefs. State pattern: Mississippi/Oklahoma (51%), Arkansas (49%), West Virginia (48%). Strong correlation with Trump vote at state level.

Key metrics: 81% of White evangelicals voted Trump (2024). 85% lean/identify GOP. 61% of Trump's evangelical backers want government to support religious values. 22% want Christianity declared official religion.

The Heritage Foundation's Project 2025 represents the institutional expression: policy blueprint embedding evangelical priorities (traditional gender roles, LGBTQ opposition, church-state boundary erosion).

## 3. Hindu Nationalism in India

BJP governance since 2014 has systematically eroded secular foundations. Waqf Amendment Act, Citizenship Amendment Act discriminate against Muslims. Violence against Muslims and Christians increasing. Modi's 2024 campaign rhetoric included Islamophobic language and "love jihad" messaging.

Nagpur clashes (March 2025): Hindu nationalist groups demanded demolition of Mughal-era Muslim tomb, resulting in communal violence. This isn't fringe activity — it's mainstreamed by the ruling party in the world's largest democracy.

## 4. Political Islam: The Spectrum

Islamic fundamentalism is not monolithic:

- **Indonesia** (largest Muslim-majority democracy): the wasathiyah (moderation) tradition, championed by scholars like Azyumardi Azra, pushes back against fundamentalism. But religious nationalism still scores highest globally.
- **Turkey**: AKP blending Islamist politics with democratic structures, though increasingly authoritarian.
- **Iran**: theocratic model under increasing internal pressure from young, secular-leaning population.
- **Saudi Arabia**: MBS's modernization program intentionally reducing religious establishment's political power.

## 5. Fundamentalism vs. Orthodoxy

ACT Alliance's distinction is crucial: fundamentalism seeks political power, rejects pluralism, and claims exclusive truth. Orthodoxy maintains traditional beliefs without necessarily demanding political dominance. A devout Muslim who prays five times daily is orthodox; one who demands Sharia law for all citizens is fundamentalist. The difference is whether faith demands political imposition.

## 6. The Violence Connection

Saiya (2024, Journal for the Scientific Study of Religion): political empowerment of Christian nationalism correlates with physical attacks on religious minorities. Mechanism: dehumanization of out-groups through exclusive truth claims. This isn't abstract — it has measurable consequences for minority safety.

## 7. Religion as Identity Politics

The deeper pattern: religion functions less as belief system and more as ethnic/cultural marker. "Christian" in "Christian nationalism" is less about the Sermon on the Mount and more about white cultural identity. Similarly, Hindu nationalism uses Hinduism as civilizational rather than devotional identity.

This is religion as Durkheimian social glue, not Weberian spiritual quest. The content of belief matters less than group boundary-marking.

## 8. What Everyone Agrees On

1. Religious nationalism is a global phenomenon, not limited to one tradition
2. It correlates with democratic erosion where it gains political power
3. Fundamentalism is distinct from traditionalism/orthodoxy
4. The US, India, and several Muslim-majority states are key theaters
5. Religion as identity politics is different from religion as spiritual practice

## 9. Where the Discourse Fractures

1. Is Christian nationalism genuinely Christian, or is it cultural identity using Christian symbols?
2. Can Hindu nationalism be distinguished from Hindu cultural pride?
3. Is secularism the answer to religious nationalism, or does it create the backlash that feeds it?
4. Does political Islam inevitably lead to theocracy, or can it coexist with democracy (Turkish model)?
