# S6: Interfaith & Pluralism

> In a world simultaneously experiencing religious nationalism and rapid secularization, the interfaith movement offers a third path: learning to live together across difference. From parliamentary conferences in Rome and Sarajevo to the Dalai Lama's model of "one religion per person, many religions for humanity," interfaith engagement has moved from fringe idealism to institutional mainstream. But it faces a fundamental philosophical challenge: can traditions with incompatible truth claims genuinely embrace pluralism?

**Sources:** 8 (Inter-Parliamentary Union, Interfaith America, Dalai Lama writings, Ideals & Identities, Pew Religious Nationalism, Council of Europe, MDPI Ikeda study, Moore Liberation Theology)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Institutional, philosophical, and practical dimensions covered.

---

## 1. Institutional Growth

Interfaith dialogue is being institutionalized at unprecedented levels:

- **Parliamentary engagement:** 2nd Parliamentary Conference on Interfaith Dialogue (Rome 2025). Bosnia parliament conference with religious leaders (Feb 2024). 6th Intercultural Dialogue Forum (Baku, May 2024). This represents a shift from NGO-level to state-level engagement.
- **European institutions:** Council of Europe's Committee for Interreligious Dialogue. Human rights framework for religious coexistence. Focus on preventing radicalization through dialogue.
- **American movement:** Eboo Patel's Interfaith America. 2025 summit theme: "Chords of Democracy." Interfaith literacy as civic competence. Focus on shared values: compassion, mercy, hospitality, service.
- **Asian initiatives:** Daisaku Ikeda's philosophy of dialogue as transformative encounter (12,000+ dialogues). Buddhist-humanist approach. Dialogue as method, not just outcome.

## 2. Two Philosophical Models

The key theoretical distinction (from Ideals & Identities, 2025):

**Secularism:** Protect politics from religious domination through institutional neutrality and separation. Religion is private. The state is neutral. French laicite is the archetype.

**Pluralism:** Emphasize coexistence of multiple worldviews in the public sphere through mutual recognition and dialogue. Religion can be public. The state facilitates engagement. Charles Taylor's influence is evident.

Most democracies blend both approaches, but the emphasis matters enormously for how they handle religious diversity, education, and public symbols. Secularism can marginalize religious voices; pluralism can privilege majority religions.

## 3. The Dalai Lama Effect

The Dalai Lama's model has been enormously influential: Buddhism as "pluralistic religion par excellence." He harmonizes Buddhist-Christian doctrines, emphasizes universal soteriology over dogma, and advocates friendship across traditions rather than theological agreement.

"One religion per person, many religions for humanity" — this formula captures the pluralist ideal. But it works best with Buddhism's non-exclusive theology. Traditions with stronger truth claims (Christianity: "I am the way," Islam: "There is no god but God") face harder pluralism challenges. Can you genuinely affirm other paths while believing yours is uniquely true?

## 4. Religious Literacy as Civic Necessity

Religious literacy — appreciative knowledge of multiple traditions — is increasingly seen as essential civic competence, not optional academic interest:

- Educational programs combat ignorance, stereotypes, prejudice
- Understanding religious motivations is essential for diplomacy, conflict resolution, public health
- Interfaith America's model: first learn what you admire in other traditions
- Challenge: religious literacy requires knowledge that schools are often reluctant or unable to provide

## 5. The Nationalism-Pluralism Inverse

Pew's religious nationalism data (cross-referenced) reveals an inverse relationship: countries with high religious nationalism have lower interfaith cooperation. Indonesia leads in both religious nationalism (46%) AND in interfaith institutional engagement — the tension is real and lived, not academic.

The US presents a miniature of the global pattern: 32% hold CN beliefs while interfaith organizations grow. The question is whether these represent different populations or genuine internal tension within communities.

## 6. Coexistence Models in Practice

Several working models:

- **Singapore:** Most religiously diverse country. Interreligious harmony managed through law (Maintenance of Religious Harmony Act), education, and cultural norms. Pragmatic, state-managed pluralism.
- **Bosnia:** Post-conflict interfaith reconstruction. Parliamentary engagement. Fragile but functional.
- **India:** Constitutional secularism under stress from Hindu nationalism. Once a model; now a cautionary tale.
- **Northern Ireland:** Post-conflict interfaith bridge-building. Community-level dialogue. Generational transformation.

## 7. What Everyone Agrees On

1. Interfaith engagement has moved from fringe to institutional mainstream
2. Religious literacy is increasingly seen as civic necessity
3. Both secularism and pluralism have strengths and weaknesses as frameworks
4. The Dalai Lama's model is influential but faces limits with exclusivist traditions
5. Coexistence requires active work — it doesn't happen by default

## 8. Where the Discourse Fractures

1. Can traditions with incompatible truth claims genuinely embrace pluralism, or does pluralism require diluting core beliefs?
2. Is secularism the answer to religious conflict, or does forced secularization create the backlash that feeds religious nationalism?
3. Does interfaith dialogue change institutions, or does it remain an elite activity disconnected from grassroots?
4. Should the state promote religious literacy or maintain strict neutrality?
