# S1: The Global Religious Landscape

> The biggest religious story of the 21st century is geographic, not theological. Christianity's center of gravity has shifted from Europe to Sub-Saharan Africa. Islam grew by 347 million in a single decade. Buddhism lost members. And the secularization thesis — the idea that modernity kills religion — has been both confirmed and contradicted, depending entirely on where you look.

**Sources:** 10 (Pew 2025 Global Religious Change, Pew Religious Diversity, Pew Religion Decline, Gallup International 2024, NPR, Nature Communications, Inglehart, Molteni, Berger, Lifeway)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Covers demographics, distribution, growth, decline, and theory.

---

## 1. The Demographic Snapshot (2020)

Christians: 2.3 billion (28.8% of world population, down 1.8 percentage points from 2010). Muslims: 2.0 billion (25.6%, up 1.8pp). Hindus: 1.2 billion (15.2%). Unaffiliated: 1.9 billion (up from 1.6B). Buddhists: 324 million (down 19M — only major religion to lose members). Jews: 15.7 million. Folk religions: 429 million. Other: 61 million.

The numbers that matter most: Islam grew by 347 million people in one decade — more than all other religions combined. Christianity added 122 million but fell as a share. The unaffiliated grew by 300 million globally.

## 2. The Great Geographic Shift

Sub-Saharan Africa is now home to 30.7% of the world's Christians — surpassing Europe (22%) for the first time in history. Latin America holds 24%. The Americas and Europe together dropped from majority to minority of global Christianity.

Christianity shrank as a share in 41 countries — nearly all in the Americas and Europe. The US saw a 14-point drop. Australia: 20 points. Benin: 5 points.

Meanwhile, 99% of Hindus live in Asia-Pacific. 99% of Buddhists. 90% of folk religion adherents. Religion remains heavily concentrated geographically despite globalization.

## 3. Islam's Growth Engine

Muslim growth is driven primarily by fertility rates and young population age structures in Sub-Saharan Africa and South/Southeast Asia. Switching (conversion) is a small factor. If current trends continue, Islam and Christianity reach population parity around 2070, each at roughly 32% of humanity.

## 4. The Secularization Thesis: Confirmed and Contradicted

Inglehart's existential security thesis: as societies become wealthier and more stable, religion declines. Mechanism: security → individual-choice norms → fertility decline → religious decline. Gallup confirms quantitatively: high-income countries 14% atheist vs 3% low-income. Japan, Germany leading secularization.

But Peter Berger (the theory's former champion) reversed himself: "The world is not heavily secularized, with two exceptions — Western/Central Europe and an international intellectual class." Pentecostalism claims 600M+ adherents. Church planting thrives in Iran and China. The Global South is "more religious than ever."

The Nature Communications study (2025) offers a synthesis: religion declines in a predictable sequence, but the sequence hasn't started in most of the world. The decline pattern holds across traditions (Christian, Muslim, Hindu, Buddhist) once existential security thresholds are reached.

## 5. Christianity's Internal Rebalancing

The story isn't just decline. Within Christianity, the center of gravity is shifting dramatically:

- 1.4B Christians in the Global South (majority for first time)
- 600M+ Pentecostals/charismatics worldwide
- Bible translation in 3,400+ languages
- Church planting movements in unexpected places: Iran, China, Nepal

The Christianity that's growing (charismatic, experiential, Southern) looks very different from the Christianity that's declining (mainline Protestant, Catholic in Europe, traditional in Americas).

## 6. Buddhism's Unique Decline

Buddhism is the only major world religion that literally lost members between 2010 and 2020 (-19M). Driven primarily by demographic contraction in East Asian societies with very low fertility: Japan, South Korea, China. Paradoxically, Buddhist practices (mindfulness, meditation) have never been more popular globally — they've just been decoupled from Buddhist identity and absorbed into the wellness economy.

## 7. The Religious Diversity Puzzle

Pew's Religious Diversity Index reveals that the most religiously diverse countries (Singapore, Taiwan, Vietnam) tend to have lower religious conflict than moderately diverse ones. The least diverse region — MENA — has the highest religious tension. This may be a selection effect (diverse societies develop tolerance mechanisms) or a causation story (tolerance enables diversity).

## 8. What Everyone Agrees On

1. Islam is the fastest-growing major religion — demographically driven
2. Christianity is rebalancing from North to South
3. Wealthy nations secularize; the mechanism involves existential security
4. The "nones" are growing but aren't mostly atheist
5. Global religion is NOT disappearing — it's redistributing

## 9. Where the Discourse Fractures

1. Is secularization universal (just hasn't started in Global South yet) or culturally specific?
2. Will Islam's growth continue at current rates as Muslim-majority countries develop?
3. Does Pentecostal growth in the Global South represent genuine revival or cultural adaptation?
4. Are the "nones" a stable category or a transitional generation between Christianity and new spiritual forms?
