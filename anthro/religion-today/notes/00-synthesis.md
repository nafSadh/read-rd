# Religion in the Contemporary World — Synthesis Notes

> Synthesized from 52 sources across 6 dimensions. What the data says, where sources agree, and where they contradict each other.

---

## 1. The Global Religious Landscape

### 1.1 The Demographic Earthquake

The most important religious story of the 21st century is geographic, not theological. Christianity's center of gravity has shifted from Europe to Sub-Saharan Africa, which now hosts 30.7% of the world's Christians — surpassing Europe for the first time. Meanwhile, Islam grew by 347 million people in a single decade (2010-2020), more than all other religions combined. These are not subtle shifts; they represent the largest redistribution of religious populations in modern history.

Pew's 2025 study (2,700+ data sources) shows Christianity shrank as a share in 41 countries — nearly all in the Americas and Europe. The US alone saw a 14-point drop. Islam is projected to reach parity with Christianity around 2070. Buddhism is the only major religion that literally lost members (-19M).

### 1.2 The Secularization Paradox

Inglehart's existential security thesis holds up well: wealthier, more stable societies become more secular. Gallup International confirms it quantitatively — 14% atheist in high-income countries vs 3% in low-income. But the thesis has a major hole: the Global South. Pentecostalism alone claims 600M+ adherents. Church planting movements thrive in Iran and China. Peter Berger, who once championed secularization, reversed himself entirely: "The world is massively religious."

The resolution may lie in the Nature Communications study (2025): religion declines in a predictable sequence tied to existential security → individual-choice norms → fertility decline. But the sequence hasn't started in most of the world.

## 2. The Rise of the Nones

### 2.1 The Plateau

After explosive growth from 16% (2007) to 29% (2024) of the US population, the "nones" appear to have plateaued. Pew's 2023-24 Religious Landscape Study found Christian decline may have "leveled off." Religion Unplugged argues this is a structural ceiling: the "easy switchers" already left, and the remaining religious population has stronger commitment.

### 2.2 Belief Without Belonging

The most important finding about the nones is what they're NOT: they're not atheists. Only 5% of American nones are atheist; 19% are "nothing in particular." Pew's global study confirms that many nones retain belief in God, afterlife, and spiritual forces. The phenomenon is not secularization in the Enlightenment sense — it's dis-institutionalization. People are leaving the building, not leaving the sacred.

### 2.3 Why They Leave

67% say they stopped believing. 20% dislike organized religion (corruption, abuse, anti-LGBTQ). The deeper pattern: progressive cultural shift outpacing religious institutional adaptation. Gen Z is 18% atheist/agnostic vs 4% of the Silent Generation — a 4.5x gap that will continue reshaping demographics through generational replacement.

## 3. Fundamentalism & Politicization

### 3.1 Religious Nationalism Is Not Marginal

Pew surveyed 55,000 people in 36 countries. Indonesia (46%) and Bangladesh (45%) lead in religious nationalism. The US is relatively low (6%) — but PRRI finds 32% of Americans are Christian nationalism adherents or sympathizers. The gap between these numbers reveals measurement challenges: Pew measures explicit religious nationalism, PRRI measures cultural alignment with Christian nationalist ideology.

### 3.2 Three Flavors of Religious Nationalism

Christian nationalism (US): 81% of White evangelicals voted Trump. 22% want Christianity declared official religion. Correlation with Trump vote strong at state level.

Hindu nationalism (India): BJP since 2014 eroding secular foundations. Legislative targeting of Muslims (Waqf Amendment, CAA). Modi's 2024 campaign rhetoric included Islamophobic language.

Islamic fundamentalism: Not monolithic. Indonesia's wasathiyah (moderation) tradition pushes back against fundamentalism. Political Islam takes different forms in Turkey, Iran, Saudi Arabia, Indonesia.

### 3.3 Fundamentalism vs. Orthodoxy

ACT Alliance's distinction matters: fundamentalism is not simply conservative belief. It seeks political power, rejects pluralism, and claims exclusive truth. Traditional orthodoxy can coexist with democracy; fundamentalism cannot, by definition.

## 4. Religion & Science

### 4.1 The Warfare Thesis Is Dead (But Won't Stay Down)

Historians of science have "long since rejected" the Draper-White conflict thesis. The actual relationship is captured by the "complexity thesis" — science and religion have had cooperative, conflictual, and independent relationships depending on era, institution, and question. But popular culture keeps the warfare narrative alive.

### 4.2 Scientists Are More Religious Than You Think

Ecklund's 9,422-scientist survey across 8 regions: >50% of scientists in India, Italy, Taiwan, Turkey self-identify as religious. Only 32% of UK scientists see conflict. The US skews more secular among scientists, but the global picture is not what the New Atheists assumed.

### 4.3 Even Atheists Prefer Belief

The PNAS study (2025) is remarkable: even in highly secular countries, atheists intuitively associate religious belief with positive outcomes. This suggests deep cognitive/cultural defaults that millennia of religious influence have embedded. The implication: complete secularization may hit a biological or cognitive floor.

## 5. New Religious Movements & Spirituality

### 5.1 Wellness As Religion

The $6.8T wellness economy (growing to $9.8T by 2029) has absorbed many functions of religion: community (yoga studios), ritual (meditation practice), meaning-making (self-help), moral framework (mindful living), and identity (wellness culture). The distinction from religion is the absence of theology — and the presence of a price tag.

### 5.2 Digital Religion

91% of church leaders welcome AI in ministry (up 80% YoY). TikTok is now a spiritual education platform for 25% of young people. AI chatbots offer spiritual guidance (Text With Jesus). But 73% of Americans say AI should play no role in faith, and Catholic/Orthodox leaders issued joint warnings. The digital space is simultaneously expanding access and threatening institutional authority.

### 5.3 Psychedelic Spirituality

The Johns Hopkins clergy study (published 2025 after a decade of controversy) found psilocybin produced profound spiritual experiences but didn't change core theological beliefs. Novel psychedelic spiritual communities (NPSCs) are emerging with their own theologies and rituals. Oregon and Colorado leading decriminalization. The therapeutic frame competes with the sacred frame.

## 6. Interfaith & Pluralism

### 6.1 Institutional Growth

Interfaith dialogue is being institutionalized at unprecedented levels: parliamentary conferences (Rome 2025, Sarajevo 2024, Baku 2024), the Council of Europe's Committee for Interreligious Dialogue, Interfaith America's leadership summits. This is moving from NGO activity to state-level engagement.

### 6.2 Two Models: Secularism vs. Pluralism

The key theoretical distinction: secularism seeks to protect politics from religion through neutrality; pluralism emphasizes coexistence of worldviews through dialogue. Charles Taylor's influence is evident. In practice, most democracies blend both approaches, but the emphasis matters for how they handle religious diversity.

### 6.3 The Dalai Lama Effect

The Dalai Lama's model — "one religion per person, many religions for humanity" — has been enormously influential. But it works best with Buddhism's non-exclusive theology. Traditions with stronger truth claims (Christianity, Islam) face harder pluralism challenges.

## Cross-Cutting Tensions

The most valuable insight from this survey is that religion in the contemporary world is defined not by a single trajectory (secularization, revival, or transformation) but by the simultaneous occurrence of all three:

1. **Secularization** in wealthy nations (Inglehart's thesis)
2. **Revival** in the Global South (Berger's desecularization)
3. **Transformation** everywhere (wellness economy, digital religion, psychedelic spirituality, religious nationalism)

These three dynamics interact in ways no single theoretical framework captures. The secularization thesis was wrong about religion disappearing. The desecularization thesis was wrong about religion simply resurgming. What's actually happening is more interesting: religion is being unbundled, just as media and education have been unbundled. Different components (belief, ritual, community, identity, morality) are being recombined in new configurations.
