# S2: Rise of the Nones

> The fastest-growing "religious" group in the West isn't a religion at all. The religiously unaffiliated — atheists, agnostics, and the vast category of "nothing in particular" — now comprise 29% of Americans. But the headline obscures a more interesting story: most nones aren't atheists, the growth may have plateaued, and leaving an institution doesn't mean leaving the sacred.

**Sources:** 9 (Pew Nones 2024, Pew RLS 2023-24, Pew Global Nones, PRRI, NPR/Pew deconversion, Pew leave/stay, Barna, American Survey Center, Religion Unplugged)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Covers demographics, reasons, beliefs, generational patterns, plateau question.

---

## 1. The Numbers

US adults religiously unaffiliated: 29% (2024). Breakdown: atheist 5%, agnostic 6%, "nothing in particular" 19%. Growth trajectory: 2% (1948) → 16% (2007) → 29% (2024). The nones are the largest single "religious" category in America, surpassing any single denomination.

Globally, the unaffiliated grew from 1.6 billion to 1.9 billion between 2010 and 2020.

## 2. The Plateau Question

The growth appears to have stalled. Pew's 2023-24 Religious Landscape Study found Christian decline has "slowed, may have leveled off." Religion Unplugged argues a structural ceiling has been reached: the "easy switchers" already left. The remaining religious population has stronger commitment, social ties, and theological conviction.

Key question: is this a pause (like religion's brief stabilization in the 1990s before the next wave of decline) or a genuine equilibrium? The answer depends on whether Gen Z's disaffiliation rate holds as they age, or whether life events (marriage, children, mortality awareness) pull some back.

## 3. Belief Without Belonging

The most important finding about the nones: they're not secular in the Enlightenment sense. Pew's global study confirms that many nones retain belief in God, afterlife, and spiritual forces. 78% say they can be moral without religion. 54% say they don't need religion to be spiritual.

"Spiritual but not religious" is a global phenomenon, not just American. It represents dis-institutionalization, not desacralization. People are leaving the building, not the sacred.

## 4. Why People Leave

67% left because they stopped believing in the teachings. 20% dislike organized religion, citing corruption, clergy abuse, anti-LGBTQ stances. "Pull" factors include autonomy, modern worldview, meaningful alternatives.

The deeper pattern: progressive cultural shift outpacing institutional adaptation. People getting more ideologically progressive while their religious institutions remain static or regress on social issues.

40 million Americans have stopped going to worship in the past 25 years.

## 5. The Generational Gap

Gen Z: 18% atheist/agnostic (9% each) vs 4% Silent Generation — a 4.5x gap. 35% of adults 18-29 have no religious identity. Only 33% of young adults say religion is very important (vs 14% unaffiliated among 65+).

Barna attributes the rise to Gen Z entering adulthood, cultural normalization of non-belief, and internet access to secular arguments and alternative communities.

But one countertrend: some studies find Gen Z men and highly educated individuals showing unexpected signs of religious interest — not traditional churchgoing, but intellectual engagement with religion, philosophy, and meaning-making.

## 6. Atheism vs. Agnosticism vs. Apatheism

The nones are not a homogeneous group:

- **Atheists** (5%): active disbelief, often philosophically committed
- **Agnostics** (6%): uncertainty about God's existence, often intellectually engaged
- **"Nothing in particular"** (19%): the largest group, characterized by religious indifference. This is closer to "apatheism" — not caring about the question. They don't reject God; they just don't think about it.

The category that drove most of the growth is "nothing in particular" — not committed atheism. This matters because indifference is harder to organize politically or culturally than active disbelief.

## 7. What Everyone Agrees On

1. The nones are large and growing (or at least stable at a high level)
2. Most nones are NOT atheists — they retain spiritual beliefs
3. Generational replacement is the primary growth mechanism
4. Anti-LGBTQ stances and abuse scandals accelerate departure
5. Organized religion's institutional credibility is the biggest casualty

## 8. Where the Discourse Fractures

1. Has the nones plateau genuinely arrived, or is this a temporary pause?
2. Will "nothing in particular" remain stable, or will it slowly drift toward atheism?
3. Does the "spiritual but not religious" category represent genuine spirituality or cultural inertia?
4. Can institutional religion adapt fast enough to stem the bleeding — or is institutional form itself the problem?
