# S5: New Religious Movements & Spirituality

> Religion isn't dying — it's being unbundled. The $6.8 trillion wellness economy has absorbed meditation from Buddhism, mindfulness from contemplative Christianity, community from congregations, and meaning-making from theology — repackaging them as consumer products. Meanwhile, TikTok is a spiritual education platform, AI chatbots offer pastoral counsel, psychedelic churches hold legal services, and 91% of church leaders welcome artificial intelligence. The sacred hasn't disappeared; it's been distributed across new containers.

**Sources:** 8 (Global Wellness Institute, Grand View Research, McKinsey, Subsplash church tech, Johns Hopkins clergy study, Harvard Divinity School, aicompetence.org, LSE psychedelics analysis)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Market data, institutional surveys, academic research, emerging trends.

---

## 1. The Wellness-Religion Convergence

The global wellness economy reached $6.8 trillion in 2024, growing 7.9% annually, projected to approach $10 trillion by 2029. Within it, meditation and mindfulness: 18.9% CAGR. Mental wellness: $168B → $298B by 2032. Spiritual wellness apps: $2.16B → $4.84B by 2030.

What does this mean? Wellness has absorbed many functions historically provided by religion: community (yoga studios, retreat centers), ritual (morning meditation, breathwork), meaning-making (self-help, coaching), moral framework (mindful living, intentional consumption), identity (wellness culture). The key difference: no theology, no transcendent referent, and a consumer-product delivery model.

McKinsey notes millennials and Gen Z are driving the convergence, blurring the line between wellness and spirituality. Academic attention to "wellness as religion" thesis is growing.

## 2. Digital Religion: AI in the Pulpit

91% of church leaders say they welcome AI in ministry (2025 survey). 45% actively use AI — an 80% increase year over year. But less than 25% use it for theological content (sermons, devotionals). Most use: administrative, communication, discipleship support.

Digital scripture engagement: Bible apps (59%), podcasts (33%), TikTok (25% of young people). A remarkable finding: 10 million more Americans are reading Scripture outside church, driven by digital access. Millennials grew 9% in Bible engagement from 2024 to 2025.

The paradox: institutional attendance declines while digital religious engagement grows. This may accelerate unbundling — people consume religious content without institutional belonging.

## 3. AI and Faith

AI chatbots offering spiritual guidance: Text With Jesus, Bible.ai, EpiscoBot, Hallow, Abide. These offer 24/7 spiritual support through guided prayer, Scripture readings, and AI-personalized devotionals. Way of the Future: first AI-worshipping church (revived 2023).

But: 73% of Americans say AI should play no role in advising people about faith. Catholic Church and Eastern Orthodox leaders issued joint warnings (2024) that AI should never replace divine authority. The gap between institutional resistance and consumer adoption mirrors broader AI dynamics.

## 4. Psychedelic Spirituality

The Johns Hopkins clergy study (published 2025 after a decade of controversy) gave psilocybin to clergy from multiple traditions (rabbis, priests, ministers, Muslim leader). Key findings: profound spiritual experiences, but metaphysical beliefs "almost or entirely unchanged." No "conversions" to belief in God. Spiritual depth deepened within existing frameworks rather than creating new ones.

However, the study faced "serious non-compliance" findings from the IRB — two funders participated in the trial itself. This ethical cloud hangs over psychedelic-spiritual research.

Novel psychedelic spiritual communities (NPSCs) are emerging in North America with distinct theologies, ritual practices, and community structures. Oregon and Colorado leading decriminalization. Harvard Divinity School hosts its 4th annual Psychedelic Intersections conference in April 2026.

LSE analysis: psychedelic experiences share characteristics with religious mystical states. But Christian, Islamic, Buddhist leaders remain divided. Tension between therapeutic frame (FDA approval, clinical protocols) and sacred frame (religious context, communal ritual).

## 5. TikTok Spirituality

Platforms like TikTok have created spaces for spiritual exploration that previous generations couldn't access. Ancient wisdom meets modern storytelling. Creative content creators turn complex religious concepts into bite-sized lessons. Online ministries welcome those who'd hesitate to enter traditional worship spaces.

This democratizes access but also fragments authority. Anyone can be a spiritual teacher on TikTok. Quality control mechanisms that institutions provided (training, ordination, peer review) are absent.

## 6. New Age Persistence

Despite predictions of its demise, New Age spirituality persists and evolves: crystal healing, astrology apps, tarot reading, sound healing. The astrology app Co-Star has 25M+ downloads. What was fringe in the 1990s is mainstream in the 2020s, powered by social media distribution and a generation hungry for meaning but suspicious of institutions.

## 7. What Everyone Agrees On

1. The wellness economy is absorbing religious functions at scale ($6.8T and growing)
2. Digital religion is expanding even as institutional attendance declines
3. Psychedelic spirituality is moving from fringe to mainstream research
4. Young people are spiritually curious but institutionally allergic
5. AI in ministry is growing fast but concentrated in administration, not theology

## 8. Where the Discourse Fractures

1. Is wellness "religion lite" (beneficial) or "religion hollow" (missing what matters)?
2. Does digital religion strengthen or weaken faith?
3. Are psychedelics a legitimate spiritual technology or a pharmacological shortcut?
4. Can AI provide genuine pastoral care, or is it category error?
5. Is the unbundling of religion creative transformation or slow death?
