## Summary
A survey of Bananas Foster treated as a seven-dimension "primary document" spanning invention, banana trade, flambé theater, NOLA food culture, spread, botany, and colonialism. The paper is generally disciplined in tone and largely avoids first/second-person voice. The biggest concerns are a handful of uncited statistics (the "35,000 pounds of bananas annually" figure, the "80–90% of the US banana trade by the 1930s" claim) and an editorial framing in the synthesis that mildly tips from description into evaluative judgment (e.g., "one of the dish's defining tensions"). Overall the paper is solid but has some uncited superlatives and mild interpretive flourishes that could be hedged.

## POV Issues
None found. No instances of second-person "you/your" or first-person "I/we/our" as authorial voice appear in the paper's exposition.

## Logical Consistency Issues
None found.

## Scholastic Rigor Issues
- survey-paper.html#invention — "Brennan's processes approximately 35,000 pounds of bananas annually in Bananas Foster preparation. It remains the restaurant's most requested item." — Specific quantitative figure without inline citation to source (the "Selected Sources" list includes Brennan's own page but does not link the figure to it directly). — Suggested hedge: add a direct cite to the Brennan's Restaurant source or hedge with "according to the restaurant".
- survey-paper.html#trade — "The United Fruit Company &mdash; nicknamed 'el pulpo,' the octopus, in Central America &mdash; controlled 80&ndash;90% of the US banana trade by the 1930s." — Specific range of market share given without a cite next to the claim. — Suggested hedge: attribute the range ("historians estimate..." or link to the Ethical Consumer / JSTOR source).
- survey-paper.html#trade — "It owned 1.4 million hectares of land in Central America and the Caribbean, including more land in Guatemala than any other single owner." — Specific acreage figure uncited inline. — Suggested hedge: attribute to a specific source.
- survey-paper.html#trade — "an estimated 200,000 killed between 1960 and 1996" — Statistic given without source attribution inline. — Suggested hedge: attribute to CEH / UN reports.
- survey-paper.html#theater — "Chicago's Pump Room, opened in 1938, was among the early American venues associated with the tradition at its peak." — "At its peak" is unsourced evaluative claim without temporal anchor. — Suggested hedge: drop "at its peak" or specify when the peak occurred.
- survey-paper.html#theater — "Industry observation confirms that tableside fire creates a contagion effect in restaurant dining rooms &mdash; one flaming dessert reliably generates others." — "Reliably generates" is stated as fact without citation; "industry observation" is vague. — Suggested hedge: attribute to specific industry source or weaken ("is reported to generate", "has been described as producing").
- survey-paper.html#theater — "Tableside theatrical dining was named a defining culinary trend of 2025 by multiple industry observers" — "Multiple industry observers" is vague aggregation without names. — Suggested hedge: cite one or two specific industry outlets.
- survey-paper.html#nola — "Spanish governance added rice dishes, peppers, and seasoning intensity. Caribbean immigrants added heat and tropical ingredients. Native American knowledge contributed local herbs and filé. German and Italian immigrants added sausage and charcuterie." — Multiple contributions claims stated without source for the specific attribution chain. — Suggested hedge: attribute to the Smithsonian NMAH source.
- survey-paper.html#botany — "the artificial banana flavor in American candy is modeled on the Gros Michel, which is why it tastes different from a fresh banana" — Causal claim ("which is why") presented as settled fact when the attribution of isoamyl acetate flavor to Gros Michel specifically is contested in food-science literature. — Suggested hedge: "is commonly said to be modeled on" or "has been widely attributed to".
- survey-paper.html#colonialism — "The banana industry invested substantially in constructing a wholesome public image." — "Invested substantially" is uncited and evaluative. — Suggested hedge: attribute to a specific PR-history source or hedge quantitatively.
- survey-paper.html#synthesis — "what political scientists describe as one of the most interventionist corporations in American history" — Superlative "one of the most interventionist" without citation to which political scientists. — Suggested hedge: cite a specific scholar (e.g., Schlesinger & Kinzer's *Bitter Fruit*, or Colby & Dennett).

## Tone Issues
- survey-paper.html#invention — "The irony that a dessert named for a local crime reformer would become inseparable from the history of corporate crime at international scale is one of the dish's defining tensions." — Editorial framing ("the irony that...") with authorial voice-of-judgment. — Neutral replacement: "The dessert named for a local crime reformer became associated in global context with the corporate crimes of the banana trade — a juxtaposition the later sections examine."
- survey-paper.html#theater — "The primary effect is spectacle." — Declarative opinion stated as conclusion. — Neutral replacement: "Culinary studies of flambé generally describe its primary effect as spectacle" (with cite) or "The available evidence suggests the primary effect is spectacle."
- survey-paper.html#spread — "The most significant mutation is the commodification of the flavor concept." — "Most significant" is an editorial superlative. — Neutral replacement: "A notable mutation is..." or "Among the most commercially consequential mutations is..."
- survey-paper.html#synthesis — "What makes Bananas Foster useful as an analytical object is its specificity and its density." — Authorial evaluation ("what makes X useful..."). — Neutral replacement: "Bananas Foster's specificity and density make it amenable as an analytical object" (more neutral form).
- survey-paper.html#synthesis — "This is what food can do as a primary source..." — First-person plural implicit ("what food can do") functioning as authorial sweeping claim. — Neutral replacement: "Food, in this mode, can anchor diffuse historical forces in a specific, tangible, reproducible object."

## Priority Fixes
1. Add inline citations (or hedge) for the quantitative claims in §2 trade (market share 80–90%, 1.4M hectares, 200,000 killed) — these are the most empirically load-bearing statements in the paper.
2. Hedge the "which is why" causal claim about Gros Michel and artificial banana flavor (§6 botany) — this is widely repeated but contested.
3. Soften the synthesis framing of "one of the dish's defining tensions" and "What makes Bananas Foster useful…" — shift from authorial evaluation to descriptive register.
4. Attribute "political scientists describe as one of the most interventionist corporations…" to specific scholar(s).
5. Hedge or cite the "industry observation confirms" and "multiple industry observers" phrases in §3.

## Overall Assessment
**B+.** The paper is rigorous in structure, generally restrained in tone, and avoids the first/second-person pitfalls that plague AI-drafted survey prose. Its weaknesses are concentrated in uncited specific statistics (market shares, acreage, death tolls) and a few editorial flourishes in the synthesis. No second-person address or enthusiast framing, and no logical contradictions. Addressing the priority fixes would move it cleanly to A–.
