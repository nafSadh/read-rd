S2.01

New Orleans as the Banana Capital

In the early 20th century and through the 1950s, New Orleans processed roughly half of all banana imports entering the United States. Bananas arrived at the Thalia Street and Desire Street wharves from Honduras, Guatemala, Costa Rica, Colombia, and Panama. From those wharves, fruit was loaded onto Illinois Central Railroad cars for distribution across the country.

This was not casual commerce. The banana trade was among the most consequential import businesses in American history — shaping a city's physical infrastructure, labor market, and cultural geography. The surplus of bananas that prompted Owen Brennan's 1951 culinary challenge to his chef was a direct product of this infrastructure.

The Port's Transformation

Between 1903 and 1940, eighteen new wharves were built at the Port of New Orleans. The Inner Harbor Navigation Canal was completed, linking the Mississippi River with Lake Pontchartrain. This infrastructure expansion was driven in part by the demands of the fruit trade. The banana, more than any other single commodity, shaped the physical development of the city's waterfront during this period.

S2.02

The United Fruit Company: El Pulpo

The United Fruit Company was founded in 1899 through the merger of the Boston Fruit Company and Minor Keith's railroad and banana operations in Central America. By the 1920s it had become what historians describe as the first true modern multinational corporation, and its nickname in Central America was "el pulpo" — the octopus — for the reach of its tentacles across governments, railroads, port facilities, and land.

By the 1930s, UFC controlled 80–90% of the US banana trade. It owned 1.4 million hectares (3.5 million acres) of land in Central America and the Caribbean, making it the single largest landowner in Guatemala. It controlled road, rail, and port infrastructure in Honduras. It operated a telegraph network, a steamship line, and a private police force.

The Scope of Control

UFC did not merely buy and sell bananas. It built and owned the physical infrastructure of the nations it operated in — roads, railroads, ports, telegraphs, housing. This meant that host governments depended on UFC infrastructure for basic state functions, creating a structural dependency that made political intervention both possible and, from UFC's perspective, necessary.

S2.03

Samuel Zemurray and New Orleans

Samuel Zemurray, known as "Sam the Banana Man," built the Cuyamel Fruit Company in Honduras before selling it to UFC in 1929 for $31.5 million in UFC stock. When UFC's stock collapsed during the Depression and he found himself the company's largest shareholder, Zemurray staged a boardroom coup and took operational control. He then moved UFC's headquarters to New Orleans, Louisiana, where he was based.

By 1920, UFC had bought up several buildings in New Orleans and constructed a new 10-story, 50,000-square-foot Spanish Renaissance property that stands today as the United Fruit Company Building on St. Charles Avenue. The company's New Orleans headquarters made the city not only the physical entry point for bananas into the US but the corporate nerve center of the banana empire.

Zemurray's Legacy

Zemurray was simultaneously a New Orleans civic figure and a practitioner of extreme corporate political intervention. He funded and organized the 1911 overthrow of the Honduran government when it moved to block Cuyamel's operations. Later, under his leadership, UFC pressured the Eisenhower administration into supporting the 1954 Guatemala coup. His building still stands on St. Charles Avenue as an Atlas Obscura landmark, a short distance from the French Quarter where Bananas Foster was created.

S2.04

"Banana Republic": O. Henry's Coinage

The term "banana republic" was coined by American writer O. Henry (William Sydney Porter) in his 1904 short story collection Cabbages and Kings, based on his six-month residence in Honduras. The term described a small, poor country economically dependent on a single export crop and politically dominated by foreign corporations. Honduras's economy was at the time heavily controlled by American fruit companies.

The countries that would become banana republics — Honduras, Guatemala, Costa Rica, Colombia, Panama — were not underdeveloped by accident. UFC and its competitors (Standard Fruit, Cuyamel) actively lobbied for low taxes, controlled labor laws, and used US diplomatic and military support to maintain favorable conditions. In Honduras, the fruit corporations controlled road, rail, and port infrastructure, and Honduras's government depended on the revenue their export taxes generated.

The Political Economy

Banana republics were not failed states. They were states whose political economy had been deliberately structured by foreign corporations with US government support. The structural conditions — monoculture dependence, foreign ownership of infrastructure, suppressed labor rights — were engineered outcomes, not natural ones.

S2.05

Guatemala 1954: Operation PBSUCCESS

The most consequential single political intervention tied to the banana trade was the 1954 CIA-organized coup that overthrew Guatemalan President Jacobo Árbenz. Árbenz had nationalized 400,000 acres of UFC's uncultivated land — compensating UFC at the company's own declared tax value — as part of a land reform program. UFC, working through Zemurray, pressured the Eisenhower administration by framing the nationalization as communist expropriation during the height of Cold War anxiety.

Operation PBSUCCESS replaced Árbenz with a military junta. The coup inaugurated decades of political instability and civil war in Guatemala, during which an estimated 200,000 people were killed between 1960 and 1996. The declassified CIA history of the operation acknowledges that UFC's lobbying was central to the decision to proceed.

The Banana Massacre (1928)

The 1928 Banana Massacre in Ciénaga, Colombia predated PBSUCCESS but illustrates the systemic pattern. UFC workers on strike for better pay and conditions consistent with Colombian labor law were met by the Colombian army. The army opened fire on unarmed protesters; casualty estimates range from dozens to hundreds killed. The event is fictionalized in Gabriel García Márquez's One Hundred Years of Solitude.

S2.06

The Infrastructure Behind One Ingredient

By 1951, when Bananas Foster was created, the banana's journey from Central American plantation to a tableside flambé at Brennan's involved: UFC-owned plantations staffed by racially segmented workforces paid partly in company-store vouchers; UFC-owned railroads carrying the fruit to UFC-controlled ports; UFC-operated steamships to New Orleans; wharf labor at the Port of New Orleans; and Illinois Central Railroad cars north. Every link in this chain was shaped by decades of corporate political intervention, labor suppression, and US government support.

New Orleans's banana surplus — the practical fact that prompted the dish's creation — was the terminus of this supply chain. The dish that emerged from that surplus became an international icon of New Orleans hospitality and culinary creativity, its supply-chain origins largely invisible in the story told about it.

After the Banana Trade Left

In the 1970s, amid labor unrest on New Orleans docks, banana shippers moved their operations to Gulfport, Mississippi. The banana trade that had built the city's port infrastructure and prompted the creation of Bananas Foster had largely departed. Brennan's continued to prepare 35,000 pounds per year of the dish, now supplied through different channels.
