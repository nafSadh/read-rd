S3.01

The History of Flambéing

Flambé — from the French flamber, to flame — is a cooking technique in which alcohol is added to a hot pan and ignited. The resulting flame burns off much of the alcohol while producing a brief visual spectacle. The technique's culinary justification is modest: flame-roasting creates brief caramelization and concentrates flavors. The visual justification is overwhelming: fire at the table transforms dinner service into performance.

The technique became associated with luxury dining in the 19th century and reached its apex in the mid-20th century, when tableside service was considered the definitive mark of a fine restaurant. Setting food on fire in front of guests was considered the height of luxury for a substantial portion of the 20th century, deployed at high-end restaurants and on cruise ships for dishes including roast duckling, Steak Diane, baked Alaska, Cherries Jubilee, and Crêpes Suzette.

S3.02

Victorian Fire: Royal Origins

The two canonical origin stories of flambé both involve royalty and spectacle. Auguste Escoffier, the French chef widely regarded as the architect of modern French cuisine, is credited with creating Cherries Jubilee for Queen Victoria's Diamond Jubilee in 1897. The dish — cherries in a brandy sauce, flambéed at the table — was prepared for one of the defining public celebrations of the Victorian era.

Crêpes Suzette's most widely accepted origin story places it at the Café de Paris in Monte Carlo in 1895, where a young waiter named Henri Charpentier was preparing crêpes for the Prince of Wales (later Edward VII) when a liqueur accidentally caught fire. Charpentier found the flavor improved and served the dish to the Prince, who was reportedly delighted. An alternative account attributes the creation to a 1897 performance at the Comédie-Française in Paris, where the restaurant owner flambéed crêpes to keep them warm for actors and attract the audience's attention.

Fire and Social Signal

Both origin stories connect fire to aristocratic or celebrity attention. The flambé was not merely a technique — it was a social gesture, a way of marking a guest as worthy of spectacle. The fire at the table said: this occasion matters.

S3.03

Guéridon Service: From Russian to French

Guéridon service — the preparation, cooking, or finishing of dishes tableside on a small rolling cart (the guéridon) — traces its origins to service à la russe, introduced to Paris around 1810 by Russian ambassador Prince Alexander Kurakin. Russian service replaced service à la française (all dishes on the table simultaneously) with sequential courses, requiring waitstaff to serve guests individually from a sideboard.

After World War I, the practice evolved in luxury French restaurants. The sideboard became a cooking cart; simple carving became elaborate tableside preparation, saucing, and flambéing. Auguste Escoffier codified these practices and is credited with popularizing the guéridon as a vehicle for theatrical fine dining. The service spread through luxury hotels and casinos across Europe and then to the United States, where it became the hallmark of mid-century American fine dining.

The American Adoption

By the 1930s and 1940s, guéridon service with tableside flambé was standard at the most prestigious American restaurants. Chicago's Pump Room, which opened in 1938, became particularly associated with the tradition. Dishes like the tableside Caesar salad (attributed to Caesar Cardini in Tijuana, 1924), Steak Diane, and Bananas Foster were all products of this mid-century culture of visible, theatrical restaurant preparation.

S3.04

The Mid-Century American Apex

Tableside flambé reached its American peak in the period from roughly 1930 to 1960 — the precise era in which Bananas Foster was created. This was the golden age of American fine dining: the expense-account dinner, the hotel dining room, the French-inspired white-tablecloth restaurant. In this context, tableside service was not merely a culinary option but a social institution, signaling that a restaurant was serious and that a guest was important.

Bananas Foster entered this tradition in 1951 and became its most recognizable American expression. Where Cherries Jubilee and Crêpes Suzette were European in origin, Bananas Foster was distinctly American — built from the commodity infrastructure of the Americas, performed in a New Orleans restaurant, named for a local civic figure.

The Social Mechanics of Fire

Research and industry observation suggest that tableside service creates a contagion effect: once one table orders a flambé, neighboring tables follow. The visible flame and aroma are a form of ambient advertising. Tableside service also makes customers feel important and willing to pay more for what is often food of lesser quantity than a conventionally plated dish.

S3.05

The Technique: What Fire Actually Does

The culinary question beneath the theater: what does igniting the alcohol actually accomplish? The answer is more modest than the spectacle suggests. Flambéing does burn off some alcohol, producing a brief caramelization and concentrating the remaining sugars and flavors. The flame also briefly heats the sauce to temperatures that create some Maillard reaction products not achievable at lower temperatures.

However, the alcohol does not burn off completely — a significant proportion remains in the sauce even after ignition. The flavor change from flambéing is real but subtle; tasters in controlled conditions have difficulty reliably distinguishing flambéed from non-flambéed preparations of the same recipe. The primary effect of the flambé, from a culinary standpoint, is theatrical rather than strictly gustatory.

The Theater Defense

Defenders of tableside flambé argue that the theater is itself legitimate culinary value: the aroma released during ignition, the visual drama, the ritual of watching the fire, and the sensory priming of the diner all contribute to the experience of the meal. Gastronomy has never been purely about nutrition or even flavor; it has always been partly about occasion, memory, and social performance. The flambé, on this account, is honest theater rather than deceptive spectacle.

S3.06

The 2025 Revival

After decades of decline driven by the rise of casual dining, nouvelle cuisine's rejection of tableside excess, and the operational costs of maintaining trained guéridon staff, tableside theatrical dining has reemerged as a notable culinary trend. Industry observers named it one of the defining trends of 2025, noting that post-pandemic diners are seeking experience and occasion rather than merely food.

The revival of tableside theater operates in a different context than the original: it is now often framed as nostalgic, retro, or experiential rather than as straightforwardly luxurious. Bananas Foster benefits from this framing — it is one of the few American dishes from the mid-century tableside tradition that never fully disappeared, maintaining continuous service at Brennan's and many New Orleans restaurants throughout the decades of the tradition's decline.

Bananas Foster's Continuity

Most mid-century tableside dishes faded significantly during the 1970s and 1980s. Bananas Foster maintained continuous prominence in part because of its association with New Orleans, a city whose food culture functions as a form of civic identity. The dish survived not as a retro revival but as an unbroken tradition.
