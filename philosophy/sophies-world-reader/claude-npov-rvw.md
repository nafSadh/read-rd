## Issues Found

### POV (Point of View)

- **P-01 (strength):** The reader closely tracks the novel's narrative, presenting Sophie's philosophical journey through her own experience. This is not a neutral encyclopedia entry -- it is a guided reading companion. The genre inherently involves interpretive framing, and this is handled well.
- **P-02 (minor):** The text presents Gaarder's metaphor of the white rabbit/magician's hat without critical distance. This is appropriate for a reader companion (the goal is to explicate the novel's ideas) but could briefly note that the metaphor itself embeds assumptions (e.g., that there is a "Magician" at all).
- **P-03 (negligible):** Sophie's existential reasoning ("You cannot experience being alive without realizing that you have to die") is presented as insight rather than one philosophical position among several. In the context of explicating a novel's philosophical content, this is acceptable.

### RIGOR

- **R-01 (minor):** The term "thrownness" is introduced parenthetically ("Philosophers call this 'thrownness'") without attributing it to Heidegger specifically. Since Geworfenheit is a Heideggerian technical term, attribution would improve precision.
- **R-02 (negligible):** The Socrates callout ("declared the wisest man in Athens") accurately references the Delphic oracle story from Plato's Apology. Standard.
- **R-03 (negligible):** The "coin of existence" metaphor (life and death as two sides) is attributed to Sophie's reasoning in the novel. Accurate to the source.

### BIAS

- **B-01 (minor):** The reader presents philosophy as inherently valuable and wonder as a faculty to be cultivated. This is Gaarder's own thesis, and the reader companion format legitimately adopts this framing. A reader companion is not required to be neutral about its source text's central argument -- it is required to present it accurately, which it does.
- **B-02 (negligible):** The myth-to-reason transition is presented as progressive (from mythological to natural explanation). This reflects standard historiography of early Greek philosophy. Some scholars (e.g., Vernant, Detienne) have complicated this narrative, but the reader tracks Gaarder's framing.

### TONE

- **T-01 (observation):** The reader has the most literary tone of all five projects, with close attention to Sophie's emotional experience and Gaarder's narrative craft. Phrases like "The question lodges in her mind like a seed" and "Her ordinary afternoon shatters" are novelistic rather than encyclopedic. This is entirely appropriate for the genre.
- **T-02 (negligible):** The diagrams (coin of existence, magician's rabbit) serve pedagogical purposes and do not introduce bias.
