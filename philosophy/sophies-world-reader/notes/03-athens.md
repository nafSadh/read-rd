## Chapter: Fate (pp 47–55)

### Fatalism and Determinism
Alberto introduces the Greek concept of **fate** (*moira*) — the belief that everything is predetermined. The Oracle at Delphi (priestess Pythia) gave ambiguous prophecies. The Greeks believed trying to escape your fate was futile (Oedipus as the classic example).

### Key Concepts

**Greek fatalism:** Every person has their allotted portion. The Fates (Moirai) spin, measure, and cut the thread of life. This worldview meant illness was divine punishment, and healing required divine intervention.

**Hippocrates (c. 460–377 BC):** Revolutionized medicine by arguing illness has natural causes, not divine ones. Founded medical ethics ("First, do no harm"). Best medicine = moderation and healthy living. The "four humors" (blood, phlegm, yellow bile, black bile) as medical parallel to Empedocles' four elements.

**The philosophical shift:** From asking "What is nature made of?" (natural philosophers) to asking "What is our place in it?" and "How should we live?" This pivot from cosmology to ethics sets up Socrates.

## Chapter: Socrates (pp 56–70)

### The Sophists — Philosophy for Hire
Around 450 BC, Athens became the cultural center of Greece. Traveling teachers called **Sophists** taught rhetoric, argument, and the art of persuasion — for a fee. Key Sophist insight: we can't know absolute truth about nature, so we should focus on our place in society.

**Protagoras:** "Man is the measure of all things." No objective right or wrong — only what seems right to each person/society. This is **relativism**.

### Socrates (470–399 BC)

**Biography:** Stonemason's son, never left Athens, never wrote anything down. Everything we know comes from his student **Plato**. Had a famously ugly appearance (snub nose, bulging eyes, potbelly). His wife Xanthippe was reputedly difficult.

**The Socratic Method:**
1. **Feigned ignorance:** Socrates pretended to know nothing, asking "innocent" questions
2. **Dialogue as tool:** Through conversation, he exposed contradictions in people's thinking
3. **The midwife analogy:** His mother was a midwife; Socrates saw himself as a "midwife of ideas" — he didn't deliver knowledge, but helped others give birth to their own understanding
4. **Socratic irony:** Pretending ignorance to draw out the other person's assumptions

**Key philosophical positions:**
- **"I know that I know nothing"** — the wisest person is the one who recognizes the limits of their knowledge (confirmed by the Oracle at Delphi, which declared Socrates the wisest man)
- **The inner voice (*daimonion*):** Socrates claimed a divine inner voice that warned him when he was about to do wrong
- **Knowledge = virtue:** Anyone who truly knows what is right will do right. Evil comes from ignorance, not malice. (This is why education matters so much)
- **Universal truths exist:** Against the Sophists' relativism, Socrates believed reason can discover absolute standards of right and wrong

**The trial and death (399 BC):**
- Charged with "corrupting the youth" and "introducing new gods"
- Could have escaped but chose to accept the sentence (hemlock)
- His conviction: it's better to suffer injustice than to commit it
- Death as the ultimate demonstration that principles matter more than survival
