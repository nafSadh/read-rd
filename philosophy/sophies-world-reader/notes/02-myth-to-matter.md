## Chapter: The Myths (pp 22–28)

### The Mythological Worldview
Before philosophy, humans explained the world through myths. Alberto presents Norse mythology as the example closest to Sophie's Norwegian culture: Thor's hammer explains thunder, Ragnarok explains winter/spring cycles.

### Key Concepts

**Myths as proto-explanations:** Myths aren't random stories — they're attempts to explain natural phenomena (why it rains, why crops grow, why winter comes). The key insight: humans have always needed explanations. The question is whether you explain via stories about gods or via rational investigation.

**The myth-to-philosophy transition (~600 BC):** The first philosophers didn't just offer new answers — they asked a new kind of question. Instead of "which god causes thunder?" they asked "what is the underlying natural process?" This is the birth of rational inquiry.

**Mythological thinking persists:** Alberto notes that even as philosophy emerged, mythological thinking didn't disappear overnight. Sophie realizes she still half-believes in Santa Claus-like explanations for things she doesn't understand.

## Chapter: The Natural Philosophers (pp 29–41)

### The Philosopher's Project
Alberto introduces a crucial method: understand each philosopher by identifying their **project** — the specific question they're trying to answer. The Natural Philosophers' project: **What is the basic substance underlying all change?**

### The Milesian School

**Thales (c. 624–546 BC):** The source of all things is **water**. Observed: Nile floods bring fertility, rain brings life, water ↔ ice ↔ vapor. First person we know of to seek a natural (not supernatural) explanation.

**Anaximander (c. 610–546 BC):** The source is **the Boundless** (*apeiron*) — something indefinite and unlimited. Reasoning: if the source creates all things, it can't be any particular thing (like water). Our world is one of many that evolve and dissolve.

**Anaximenes (c. 585–528 BC):** The source is **air/vapor**. Mechanism: air condenses → water → earth. Air rarefies → fire. Elegant because it explains transformations as degrees of a single substance.

### The Problem of Change

**Parmenides (c. 515–450 BC) — The Rationalist:**
- Nothing can come from nothing
- Nothing that exists can become nothing
- Therefore: **no real change is possible**
- Our senses deceive us — reason must override perception
- This is **rationalism**: reason as primary source of knowledge

**Heraclitus (c. 535–475 BC) — The Empiricist:**
- "Everything flows" (*panta rhei*)
- "You cannot step twice into the same river"
- The world is characterized by **opposites** (sick/well, war/peace, winter/spring)
- Behind the flux: **Logos** — universal reason guiding nature
- Trust your senses — change is the fundamental reality

### The Resolution

**Empedocles (c. 490–430 BC):**
- Both Parmenides and Heraclitus are half-right
- The error: assuming ONE basic substance
- Solution: **four elements** (earth, air, fire, water) — each eternal and unchanging
- Change = elements combining and separating (like mixing paint colors)
- Two forces: **Love** (binds) and **Strife** (separates)
- Perception works because the elements in our eyes match elements in objects

**Anaxagoras (c. 500–428 BC):**
- Not four elements but infinitely many **seeds** (*spermata*)
- "There is something of everything in everything" — each part contains fragments of all others
- Modern analogy: DNA in every cell contains blueprint for entire body, holograms contain whole image in each fragment
- Ordering force: **Mind/Intelligence** (*Nous*)
- First philosopher in Athens; exiled for saying the sun is a hot stone, not a god

## Chapter: Democritus (pp 42–46)

### The Lego Question
Sophie receives: "Why is Lego the most ingenious toy in the world?" She rebuilds with Lego and discovers: blocks are (1) indivisible, (2) varied in shape, (3) solid, (4) connectable via hooks, (5) reusable infinitely.

### The Atom Theory

**Democritus (c. 460–370 BC):**
- Everything is built from tiny invisible **atoms** (*a-tomos* = "un-cuttable")
- Atoms are eternal, immutable, indivisible — like Lego blocks
- Atoms differ in shape: round, smooth, jagged, hooked
- They combine → things form. Things die → atoms separate → recombine into new things
- **No "soul" or spiritual force** — everything is mechanical, governed by necessity
- Even the soul = special round, smooth "soul atoms" that disperse at death
- **Materialism**: only atoms and void exist
- Sense perception = atoms from objects entering our sense organs ("moon atoms" entering the eye)

### Why This Matters
Democritus represents the culmination of pre-Socratic natural philosophy. He resolves the tension between Parmenides (nothing changes) and Heraclitus (everything changes): the atoms don't change, but their arrangements do. Sophie recognizes this as "so simple and yet so ingenious."
