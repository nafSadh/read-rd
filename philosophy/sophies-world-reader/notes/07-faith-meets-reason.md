## Setting the Scene

Sophie meets Alberto Knox in person for the first time — at St. Mary's Church, where he's dressed as a medieval monk. He introduces the Middle Ages through a brilliant **24-hour clock metaphor**:

| Clock Time | Historical Period | Key Event |
|-----------|------------------|-----------|
| Midnight (0) | Year 0 | Birth of Jesus |
| 1:30 AM | ~50 AD | Paul's missionary journeys |
| 3:00 AM | ~300 AD | Christianity tolerated |
| 4:00 AM | ~400 AD | **Middle Ages begin** |
| 5:30 AM | 529 AD | Plato's Academy closed; Benedictine order founded |
| 8:00 AM | ~800 AD | Charlemagne; convent schools |
| 10:00–11:00 | 1000–1100 AD | Cathedral schools |
| 12:00 noon | 1200 AD | First universities; Gothic cathedrals |
| 2:00 PM | ~1400 AD | **Middle Ages end; Renaissance begins** |

"So the Middle Ages lasted for ten hours" — and each hour was a hundred years.

## The Three Cultures

After Rome fell (476 AD), the old Greco-Roman world split into three:

1. **Roman Catholic West** (Rome) — inherited Neoplatonism
2. **Byzantine East** (Constantinople) — inherited Plato
3. **Arabic South** (Mecca, Baghdad, Spain) — inherited Aristotle

At the end of the Middle Ages, these three streams reconverged in Northern Italy → the Renaissance.

**Key cultural note:** The Arabs preserved and advanced Greek science — mathematics, chemistry, astronomy, medicine. Arabic numerals, algebra, algorithms all come from this tradition. In many fields, Arabic culture was superior to Christian Europe during this period.

## St. Augustine (354–430 AD)

### Background
Born in Tagaste (North Africa). Searched through Manichaeism, Stoicism, and Neoplatonism before converting to Christianity. Bridges late antiquity and early Middle Ages.

### Philosophy

**"Christianizing Plato":**
- Plato's Ideas → relocated into the **mind of God**
  - Before creating the world, God held the eternal Ideas/Forms in his Divine mind
  - This preserves Platonic eternal forms within a Christian creation framework
- Neoplatonist emanation → becomes Christian creation *ex nihilo* (from nothing)
  - Greeks: the world always existed
  - Augustine: God created the world from nothing (Biblical doctrine)

**The Problem of Evil:**
- Manichaeans: evil is a force equal to good (dualism)
- Augustine (following Plotinus): evil is the **absence of God**, not a separate force
  - Evil has no independent existence — it's a privation, like darkness is absence of light
  - Evil comes from **human disobedience**, not from a malevolent cosmic force

**The Immortal Soul (with caveats):**
- Man is a spiritual being with a material body
- The body decays ("moth and rust doth corrupt"), but the soul can know God
- However: there is an **insurmountable barrier** between God and world (contra Plotinus, who said all is one)

**City of God vs City of the World:**
- All history is a struggle between the Kingdom of God and the Kingdom of the World
- These aren't separate political entities — they struggle **inside every person**
- The Church became identified with the City of God; the State with the City of the World
- "No salvation outside the Church" — a doctrine that persisted until the Reformation

**History as progress:**
- Augustine is the first philosopher to draw **history** into philosophy
- Linear view (from the Old Testament): creation → fall → redemption → end times
- Divine foresight directs human history like the life of one person from childhood to old age

**Predestination:**
- God has chosen who will be saved; it is preordained
- We have free will, but God has "foreseen" how we will use it
- Sophie objects: "Isn't that rather unfair? Socrates said we all had the same common sense"
- This tension — between Athenian humanism and Augustinian predestination — is a thread that runs through the rest of the book

## St. Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)

### Background
From Aquino (between Rome and Naples), taught at the University of Paris. Worked during the High Gothic period when Arab scholars were bringing Aristotle's writings back into European consciousness.

### Philosophy

**"Christianizing Aristotle":**
(Just as Augustine Christianized Plato 800 years earlier)

**The great synthesis of faith and knowledge:**
- There need be **no conflict** between philosophy/reason and Christian revelation/faith
- Both paths lead to truth — they often arrive at the same conclusions from different directions

**Two paths to God:**
1. **Faith and revelation** — through the Bible (the surest path)
2. **Reason and the senses** — through observing nature (Aristotle's path)

**The Athens analogy:** Saying "Athens is in Europe" is true but incomplete. Saying "Athens is the capital of Greece in southeastern Europe" is the fuller truth. Aristotle gives you the first; the Bible gives you the second. They don't contradict — one simply goes further.

**Natural theology vs revealed theology:**
- Some truths are accessible through both faith AND reason (e.g., God exists) — these are "natural theological truths"
- Some truths are accessible ONLY through faith (e.g., the Trinity, Incarnation)
- Aristotle's philosophy covers the natural truths; revelation covers the rest
- The thunderstorm analogy: reason = seeing the lightning; faith = hearing the thunder. Best to have both.

**God as "formal cause":**
- Aristotle's philosophy presumes a God who sets all natural processes going
- Aquinas: we can recognize God's existence through reason, by observing that everything requires a formal/final cause
- God's nature (love, forgiveness, Trinity) — only accessible through scripture

**Nature's Scale extended:**
- Aristotle: rocks → plants → animals → humans
- Aquinas adds: → angels → God
- Angels have immediate knowledge (no need to reason step by step); they have no bodies, so they cannot die
- God sees and knows everything in "one single coherent vision"
- Time doesn't exist for God the way it exists for us

**The Hilde connection:** Sophie notices that Hilde's father wrote something chillingly similar — "even if it takes a week for Sophie, that doesn't have to mean it will be that long for us." Alberto frowns: "He ought to be ashamed of himself!" This equates Hilde's father with a God-like figure who exists outside the characters' time.

### Views on Women
- Aquinas adopted Aristotle's view: women are "incomplete men," passive in reproduction
- BUT: women's souls are equal to men's souls; in Heaven, gender differences cease
- Hildegard of Bingen (1098–1179): nun, preacher, physician, botanist, naturalist — proof that women thinkers thrived even in the male-dominated medieval church

### Sophia = Wisdom
- In Greek, *Sophia* means wisdom
- God has a female side: Sophia, "God's mother nature"
- Sophia appeared to Hildegard in a vision
- Sophie's own name carries this philosophical weight
