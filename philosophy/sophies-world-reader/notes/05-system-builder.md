## Aristotle (384–322 BC)

Student of Plato for 20 years at the Academy, then tutor to young Alexander the Great, then founder of his own school (the **Lyceum**) in Athens. Where Plato was a poet-philosopher (writing dialogues, using myths and analogies), Aristotle was the great **systematizer** — organizing knowledge into categories that still structure academia today.

### No Innate Ideas — Forms Are in Things

**The fundamental break with Plato:**
- Plato: Forms exist in a separate world; physical things are imperfect copies
- Aristotle: Forms exist **within** the things themselves; the "form" of a horse IS the characteristics that make all horses horses
- There is no "Idea of Horse" floating in a separate realm — just real horses, each embodying the horse-form

**The chicken form:** Gaarder's example — the "form" of a chicken is what it does: clucking, laying eggs, flapping wings. If a chicken dies, its form ceases. If you see a chicken-shaped cookie, you know it's a cookie, not a chicken, because it lacks the chicken form.

**Consequence:** Knowledge comes from **experience** (not recollection of a previous life in the world of Ideas). We perceive individual things first, then abstract their form through reason. This makes Aristotle the father of **empiricism**.

### Form and Substance (Matter)

Every thing is a combination of:
- **Form** = what makes it what it is (its characteristics, capabilities, purpose)
- **Matter** = the raw material it's made of

Example: A marble statue
- Matter = marble
- Form = the shape of the statue

Neither exists independently — there's no "pure form" without matter (against Plato), and no "formless matter" in nature.

### The Four Causes

To fully explain anything, Aristotle says you need four causes:

1. **Material cause** — what is it made of? (marble)
2. **Efficient cause** — what brought it into being? (the sculptor)
3. **Formal cause** — what is its form/design? (the shape)
4. **Final cause** — what is its purpose? (to beautify the temple)

Applied to rain:
1. Material: water vapor
2. Efficient: cooling of vapor
3. Formal: the nature of water to fall downward
4. Final: plants and animals need water to live

The **final cause** is distinctively Aristotelian — nature has inherent purpose (*telos*). This is **teleology**.

### Logic — The Science of Reasoning

Aristotle invented formal logic. His key tool: the **syllogism**.

Example:
- All living creatures are mortal (major premise)
- Hermes is a living creature (minor premise)
- Therefore Hermes is mortal (conclusion)

He also pioneered **classification**: dividing the natural world into categories and subcategories. Living things → Plants / Animals. Animals → those with blood / those without. This taxonomic approach persists in biology.

### Nature's Scale (*Scala Naturae*)

A hierarchy of existence:
1. **Nonliving things** (rocks, water) — no potential for change
2. **Plants** — can nourish and reproduce, but can't move or feel
3. **Animals** — can sense and move, but can't reason
4. **Humans** — can reason, making them the highest earthly beings

Each level includes all the capabilities of levels below it plus something new. This "ladder of nature" was enormously influential.

### Ethics — The Golden Mean

**Three forms of happiness:**
1. A life of **pleasure and enjoyment**
2. A life as a **free and responsible citizen**
3. A life as a **thinker and philosopher**

All three are needed; none alone suffices. This is about **balance**.

**The Golden Mean:** Virtue lies between two extremes.
- Courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness
- Generosity is the mean between miserliness and extravagance
- Modesty is the mean between self-deprecation and vanity

This echoes Hippocrates' medical advice: health = balance, disease = excess or deficiency.

### Politics
- Humans are "political animals" — we flourish only in society
- Three good forms of government: **Monarchy** (one rules well), **Aristocracy** (group of best), **Polity** (many rule well)
- Three corrupted forms: **Tyranny**, **Oligarchy**, **Democracy** (mob rule)

### Views on Women
Aristotle held that women were "incomplete men" — passive in reproduction, inferior in reason. Sophie/Alberto acknowledge this as regrettable. Important context: biology hadn't yet discovered the female egg (not until 1827).
