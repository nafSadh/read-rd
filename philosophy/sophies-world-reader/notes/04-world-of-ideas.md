## Chapter: Plato (pp 77–91)

### Plato (428–348 BC)
Student of Socrates, founder of the Academy in Athens (operating for 900 years until closed by the Church in 529 AD). Unlike Socrates, Plato wrote extensively — 35 philosophical dialogues survive.

### The World of Ideas (Theory of Forms)

**The core insight:** Behind the changing physical world lies an eternal, unchanging realm of perfect **Ideas** (or **Forms**). Everything we see is an imperfect copy of its perfect Idea.

**The cookie mold analogy:** Gaarder's signature teaching image for Plato:
- The **mold** = the Idea/Form (eternal, perfect, one)
- The **gingerbread cookies** = physical things (imperfect copies, many)
- You can make hundreds of gingerbread men from one mold; they may be slightly different, but they all derive from the same form
- Even if every cookie crumbles, the mold remains

**Why this solves a problem:** Plato reconciles Parmenides and Heraclitus at a deeper level than Empedocles:
- The physical world **changes** (Heraclitus is right about the material world)
- But the Ideas are **eternal and unchanging** (Parmenides is right about ultimate reality)
- Physical things come and go; their Forms persist forever

### True Knowledge vs. Opinion

**Two realms, two types of knowing:**
- **The senses** give us only **opinion** (*doxa*) about the changing physical world
- **Reason** gives us **true knowledge** (*episteme*) about the eternal world of Ideas
- Example: We can measure physical triangles (always imperfect), but we can know the mathematical idea of a triangle (perfect, eternal)

### The Immortal Soul

**The soul as a dual creature:**
- **Body** = belongs to the physical world, subject to change, mortality
- **Soul** = belongs to the world of Ideas, existed there before birth, returns there after death
- When the soul inhabits a body, it **forgets** the Ideas it once knew
- Learning = **recollection** (*anamnesis*) — the soul gradually remembers the perfect Forms

**The chariot analogy (from Phaedrus):** The soul is a charioteer driving two horses:
- One horse pulls **upward** (toward the world of Ideas — reason, spirit)
- One horse pulls **downward** (toward bodily pleasures — appetite)
- The charioteer (reason) must master both horses

### The Allegory of the Cave

**The scenario:** Prisoners chained in a cave since birth, facing a wall. Behind them, a fire casts shadows of objects carried along a walkway. The prisoners take the shadows for reality.

**The escape:** One prisoner breaks free, turns around, sees the fire and objects. Climbs out of the cave into sunlight — blinded at first, then gradually sees the real world. Realizes the shadows were mere reflections.

**The return:** The freed prisoner returns to the cave to tell the others. They don't believe him — the shadows are all they've ever known. They might even kill him (as Athens killed Socrates).

**The levels of reality:**
- Shadows on the wall = our sensory impressions of physical things
- Objects by the fire = physical things themselves
- The world outside = the world of Ideas
- The sun = the highest Idea — the Idea of the Good

### The Philosopher King

**Plato's political theory (The Republic):**
- The soul has three parts: **Reason** (head), **Will/Spirit** (chest), **Appetite** (abdomen)
- Each part has a virtue: **Wisdom**, **Courage**, **Temperance**
- The ideal state mirrors the ideal soul:
  - **Rulers** (philosopher-kings) = reason → wisdom
  - **Auxiliaries** (soldiers) = will → courage
  - **Laborers** (workers) = appetite → temperance
- Justice = each part doing its proper function, no part overstepping

**Plato on women:** Surprisingly progressive for his era — argued women have the same capacity for reason as men, and should receive the same education and opportunities to govern. (Alberto notes this is exceptional for ancient Greece.)

## Chapter: The Major's Cabin (pp 92–100)

### The Mystery Deepens
Sophie discovers Alberto's cabin by a lake. Inside she finds:
- A mirror that seems to **wink** at her (she sees a girl who winks but Sophie doesn't)
- **Hilde Møller Knag's wallet** with ID, school card, money
- An envelope marked "SOPHIE" containing questions about Aristotle

### Significance
This chapter is a narrative hinge. The mirror that winks suggests Sophie's world may not be what it seems — foreshadowing the meta-fictional revelation. The Hilde clues accumulate: someone is manipulating both Sophie's reality and Hilde's birthday present simultaneously.
