After Aristotle's death (322 BC), philosophy shifted. The grand cosmological questions seemed less urgent. Instead: How should I live in an uncertain world? Four schools emerged, each offering a path to tranquility.

The Cynics rejected external things entirely. The Stoics accepted fate rationally. The Epicureans pursued wise pleasure. The Neoplatonists sought mystical union with the divine. Each was a response to the anxiety of a chaotic age.

Stoicism

  • Rational acceptance of fate
  • Virtue is within your control
  • Natural law guides all people

Limitations

  • Requires impossible detachment
  • Leaves suffering unaddressed
  • Denies free will theoretically

Two great streams flowed through the ancient world. Indo-European culture: Greek, Roman, Persian—polytheistic, cyclical history, the sense of sight dominant. Semitic culture: Hebrew, Arabic—monotheistic, linear history, the sense of hearing dominant.

They never met until Christianity. Jesus was born into the Semitic tradition. Paul carried Christianity into the Greek world. The rest of Western thought is the dialogue between these two ways of seeing.

01 — Hellenistic Schools

Four Ways to Live

After Aristotle (d. 322 BC), Alexander the Great's conquests spread Greek culture across the Mediterranean. Small city-states gave way to vast empires. Philosophy's center of gravity shifted from cosmology ("What is reality made of?") to ethics ("How should I live?").

The Cynics

Diogenes (c. 412–323 BC) is the founder. His method: strip away every unnecessary desire. He lived in a barrel, owned almost nothing. When Alexander the Great asked what he wanted, Diogenes said: "Move—you're blocking my sunlight." The Cynics believed true happiness doesn't depend on external things (wealth, beauty, power, health). It depends only on virtue—the strength to live simply and without shame. The word "cynic" comes from the Greek for "dog" because Cynics lived like dogs, unburdened by civilization's pretenses.

The Stoics

Zeno (c. 334–262 BC) founded Stoicism in the Stoa (painted porch) in Athens. Core doctrine: everything happens by natural necessity. The universe is governed by Logos—divine reason, divine intelligence. There are no accidents, no random events. Everything that happens was bound to happen.

The Stoic response: don't fight fate. Accept what happens with equanimity. Virtue lies not in external success or pleasure, but in assenting rationally to the inevitable. The Stoics coined the term natural law—universal moral principles that apply to all humans regardless of nationality. Every person is a citizen of the world (cosmopolitan).

The Epicureans

Epicurus (341–270 BC) is often misunderstood. Modern "epicurean" means hedonistic, but Epicurus himself lived modestly. His goal: pleasure, yes, but defined as absence of pain (aponia) and mental disturbance (ataraxia). The highest pleasures are simple: friendship, philosophical discussion, plain food eaten with friends. He advocated living quietly, avoiding politics and trouble.

Neoplatonism

Plotinus (c. 205–270 AD) synthesized Plato with mysticism. Reality emanates from the One—ultimate reality, pure goodness, indescribable. From the One flows Mind (nous), from which flows Soul, from which flows Matter. Each level is like concentric circles of light radiating from a source. The closest to the One is pure spirit; the farthest is dark matter.

The Neoplatonic goal: mystical union with the One, where the boundaries between self and divine dissolve. Plotinus reportedly achieved this state of ecstatic union four times during his life. This philosophy bridged ancient polytheistic religion and Christianity by providing a mystical framework that resonated with Christian spirituality.

02 — Two Cultures

Greece Meets Judea

Alberto introduces a sweeping cultural framework. The Indo-European and Semitic traditions shaped Western civilization differently.

Indo-European Culture

Spans from India to Scandinavia via Iran, Greece, and Rome. Polytheistic (many gods). Cyclical view of history—eternal return, the wheel of time, seasons repeating. The sense of sight is primary ("I see, I understand"). Key words share roots across languages: Sanskrit vidya → Greek idea → Latin video (all meaning "see" or "know"). This culture produced philosophy, science, and democracy.

Semitic Culture

Hebrew, Arabic, Aramaic peoples. Monotheistic (one God). Linear view of history—creation, fall, redemption, end times. The sense of hearing is primary ("Hear, O Israel"). God speaks; humans listen and obey. This culture gave the world Judaism, Christianity, Islam.

The Collision

When Christianity emerged from Judaism, it carried Semitic assumptions into the Greek world. Paul's speech at the Areopagus in Athens was the first great collision: a monotheistic, revelation-based faith meeting a rationalistic, sight-based culture. The result was synthesis—centuries of theological and philosophical struggle to reconcile reason and revelation, Athens and Jerusalem.
