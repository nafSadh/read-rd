The Butlin–Long Framework
Consciousness in AI (2023) · indicator properties · Chalmers, Bengio, Schwitzgebel, Simon
01

Historical Roots

Three classical theories do the load-bearing work: Global Workspace Theory (Baars 1988, Dehaene) — consciousness as a low-dimensional attentional bottleneck; Higher-Order Thought (Rosenthal 1986) — a mental state is conscious when accompanied by a higher-order representation of itself; Recurrent Processing Theory (Lamme 2006) — consciousness correlates with feedback (not feedforward) processing.

The framework also draws on predictive processing, attention schema theory, and agency/embodiment. Each supplies distinct indicator properties.

02

The 2023 Report

Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness (Butlin, Long, Elmoznino, Bengio, Birch, plus ~15 co-authors; Chalmers as advisor) is the field’s foundational document. Authorship spans academic philosophy, cognitive science, biology, and AI.

Three principal claims: (1) contemporary theories are developed enough to ground a tractable research programme; (2) indicator properties can be specified with enough precision to be checked against AI architectures; (3) current AI systems largely do not satisfy the indicators.

03

Indicator Properties

The methodological pivot. Indicators are framed at a computational level of description — they specify the kind of information-processing a system needs, not the physical substrate. “A limited-capacity workspace” is satisfied by any system with the right bottleneck, whatever it is made of.

This is the functionalist commitment the framework makes by design. Defenders argue the computational framing is conditional (if functionalism is right, then indicators identify candidates). Lerchner argues this reading is too weak to be defensible: computational framing is itself a substantive commitment.

04

The Theories Drawn From

Six frameworks. Recurrent Processing yields indicators around architectural feedback. Global Workspace yields broadcast and selection. Higher-Order theories yield metarepresentation. Predictive Processing yields generative modelling. Attention Schema yields self-modelling. Agency/Embodiment yields world-engagement properties.

A system satisfying indicators from more frameworks is treated as a stronger candidate. The list is open-ended — as the science matures, indicators update.

05

Chalmers on LLMs

David Chalmers’ Could a Large Language Model Be Conscious? (Boston Review, August 2023) is the most widely cited defence of cautious functionalism. Six candidate requirements LLMs lack: unified agency, genuine embodiment, recurrent processing, global workspace, right self-model, right integration. Aggregate credence that current LLMs are conscious: <10%.

On Lerchner’s account, Chalmers’ requirements are vulnerable to the same mapmaker objection as the Butlin–Long indicators: “recurrent processing” is an architectural description that requires an observer to pick out which circuits count.

06

The ACT Test

Susan Schneider’s Artificial Consciousness Test is a behavioural probe: open-ended questions designed to reveal whether a system has a “felt quality” to its mental life. A zombie could not answer consistently; a conscious system could.

Schneider’s 2023 update treats LLM responses as a “weak marker” and supplements with the Chip Test — substrate-sensitive, looking at whether internal parts support human-like consciousness. This distinguishes Schneider from Butlin–Long: her Chip Test is substrate-sensitive; their indicators are substrate-neutral.

07

Objections

Udell and Schwitzgebel argue any output-dependent probe can be gamed or failed for reasons unrelated to consciousness. A system might output consciousness-appropriate responses without being conscious (the Shanahan role-play case); a system might be conscious but fail to report it.

The argument extends to computational indicators: a system might instantiate the computational structure without being conscious (Lerchner), or be conscious without instantiating it (biological naturalism, S5).

08

Lerchner’s Target

The framework’s methodological commitment — consciousness specified at a computational level, theories generating indicators at that level, more indicators → stronger candidate — is precisely what Lerchner calls incoherent.

Three replies in the literature: methodological neutrality (indicators are conditional); restricted implementation (non-trivial implementation theories block triviality, see S7); pragmatic (best available programme, alternative leaves nothing tractable). None of the three is decisively settled.

01

Historical Roots

Three classical theories do most of the load-bearing work in the indicator-properties framework. Each supplies a distinct family of indicator properties, and the Butlin–Long methodology is to draw on all three (and others) without committing to which is correct.

Global Workspace Theory (Baars 1988)

Bernard Baars’s A Cognitive Theory of Consciousness (CUP, 1988) proposed that consciousness is a low-dimensional state produced by a selective attentional bottleneck that broadcasts to multiple specialist processors. The theatre metaphor: a spotlight of attention illuminates selected content for the audience of cognitive consumers. GWT gives a clean functional signature — selective attention, broadcasting, reportability — and the theory has been developed into a neural-architectural form by Stanislas Dehaene and collaborators. GWT is the ancestor of Bengio’s Consciousness Prior (S2).

Higher-Order Thought Theory (Rosenthal 1986)

David Rosenthal’s Two Concepts of Consciousness (Philosophical Studies, 1986) argued a mental state is conscious only if accompanied by a higher-order representation of itself. Conscious states are states one is in some sense aware of being in. HOT supplies candidate indicator properties around self-monitoring, metarepresentation, and introspective access.

Recurrent Processing Theory (Lamme 2006)

Victor Lamme’s Towards a true neural stance on consciousness (Trends in Cog Sci, 2006) proposed that consciousness correlates with recurrent (feedback) processing in sensory cortex, distinct from feedforward processing which handles unconscious perception. RPT supplies indicator properties around architectural recurrence rather than specific functional roles.

The Methodology

The Butlin–Long report does not select among these theories. Each is treated as potentially correct; each supplies its own indicators. The methodology is meant to be robust to theoretical uncertainty: a system satisfying indicators drawn from multiple theories is stronger evidence for consciousness than satisfying indicators from any one.

02

The 2023 Report

The August 2023 report Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness is the foundational document of the contemporary AI-consciousness assessment programme. Its credentials are unusual in a field that has historically been divided between speculative philosophy and empirical neuroscience.

The Authorship

Nineteen primary authors (Butlin, Long, Elmoznino, Bengio, Birch, Constant, Deane, Fleming, Frith, Ji, Kanai, Klein, Lindsay, Michel, Mudrik, Peters, Schwitzgebel, Simon, VanRullen), with Chalmers as advisor. The list spans academic philosophers, cognitive scientists, biologists (Birch’s work on invertebrate consciousness is notable), and AI researchers. Affiliations include Eleos AI Research, NYU, Cambridge, Toronto, and corporate research labs.

The Three Principal Claims

First, that contemporary neuroscientific theories of consciousness are sufficiently developed to ground a tractable research programme on AI consciousness. This is a metasubstantive claim: it asserts not that any particular theory is correct, but that the best-supported theories collectively generate enough constraints to make the question empirically adjudicable.

Second, that indicator properties — computational characteristics entailed by these theories — can be specified with enough precision to be checked against existing and future AI architectures. The report catalogues fourteen indicator properties drawn from six theories.

Third, and more cautiously, that current AI systems largely do not satisfy the indicator properties. Transformer-based language models satisfy few of them. The report treats this as a provisional finding subject to revision as architectures evolve.

The 2025 Follow-up

Butlin and Long’s 2025 article in Trends in Cognitive Sciences refines the indicator framework and responds to two years of commentary. The response does not directly address Lerchner’s mapmaker argument, which post-dates the follow-up’s conception.

03

Indicator Properties

The methodology of the framework is worth examining closely because it is the specific move Lerchner attacks.

The Computational Framing

Indicators are framed at a computational level of description. They do not specify physical substrate, neural architecture, or biological instantiation. They specify the kind of information-processing a system would need to do to satisfy the relevant theory. For example, “a limited-capacity workspace” is satisfied by any system that has a bottleneck of the relevant functional type, whether implemented in neurons, silicon transistors, or any other substrate.

This is the functionalist commitment the framework makes by design. The report is explicit: the indicators are computational because the theories they draw from are taken to apply at a computational level of abstraction. If Global Workspace Theory is true, what matters is that a system has the right bottleneck, not what the bottleneck is made of.

The Conditional Reading

Defenders argue the computational framing is conditional: if computational functionalism is right and the theory in question is right, then a system satisfying the indicators is a candidate for consciousness. The framework is methodologically neutral on whether functionalism is right; it only says what follows if it were.

The Lerchner Objection

Critics argue the conditional reading is too weak to defend. Specifying indicators computationally is substantively committing to the view that computational specification is the right level at which to identify consciousness. If the mapmaker argument is correct, the indicators are not picking out consciousness-candidates; they are picking out descriptions an observer imposes. The computational framing is not neutral.

Why This Is the Collision

The framework’s foundational move — specifying indicators at a computational level — is exactly the move Lerchner’s argument targets. This is not a coincidence. The report is the largest and most credentialed articulation of the programme; the Abstraction Fallacy is the most focused contemporary critique of that programme. The two documents are in direct collision by construction.

04

The Theories Drawn From

The framework draws indicators from six theoretical frameworks. Each generates a distinct set of properties, and a system satisfying indicators from more frameworks is treated as a stronger candidate.

Recurrent Processing

Architectural recurrence — input modules generating organized perceptual representations, feedback connections producing representations accessible to higher cognition.

Global Workspace

Broadcast and selection — a limited-capacity workspace, selective attention choosing workspace contents, multiple modules receiving the contents.

Higher-Order Thought

Metarepresentation — generative higher-order models of lower-order states, perception of one’s own perception, introspective capacity.

Predictive Processing

Generative modelling — top-down generative perception, hierarchical predictive processing, active inference.

Attention Schema

Self-modelling — an explicit model of one’s own attention, capacity to represent one’s own representational states.

Agency & Embodiment

World-engagement — agency under the guidance of conflicting values, embodiment in the sense of modelling a system-environment interaction.

Open-Ended by Design

The framework treats the list as non-exhaustive. Theories not yet formalized may yield additional indicators. Theories later shown to be false would subtract from the list. The methodology is meant to be open-ended in a specific way: as the science of consciousness matures, the indicator list updates. This is either the framework’s strength (robustness to theoretical uncertainty) or its weakness (no principled way to close the list), depending on how one reads it.

05

Chalmers on LLMs

David Chalmers’ Could a Large Language Model Be Conscious? (Boston Review, August 2023; edited from a NeurIPS 2022 talk) is the most widely cited defense of cautious functionalism in the contemporary literature. Chalmers advises on the Butlin–Long report but the essay is his own position.

The Argument

Current LLMs are unlikely to be conscious, but successors may be, and we should take the possibility seriously. Chalmers catalogues six candidate requirements that current LLMs lack — unified agency, genuine embodiment, recurrent processing, global workspace architecture, the right kind of self-model, and the right kind of integration. He estimates each as a probabilistic constraint and reaches an aggregate credence of less than 10% that current LLMs are conscious, with successors potentially at higher credences if architectural gaps are filled.

Two Things Worth Noting

First, the requirements are drawn from the same theoretical sources as the Butlin–Long indicators, though organized at the requirement level rather than the property level. Chalmers and the Butlin–Long authors are intellectually close; the papers should be read together.

Second, Chalmers’ conclusion is cautious on both sides. He does not argue current LLMs are conscious, but he also does not endorse strong skepticism. The middle position is that the question is live.

Vulnerable to the Same Objection

On Lerchner’s account, Chalmers’ requirements are vulnerable to the same mapmaker objection as the Butlin–Long indicators. The “recurrent processing” requirement, for instance, is an architectural description that requires an observer to pick out which circuits count as recurrent and which do not. If the mapmaker move is right, the requirement does not identify a feature of the system independent of the observer’s description of it.

Chalmers’ Position in 1996 vs 2023

Chalmers’ own 1996 paper “Does a Rock Implement Every Finite-State Automaton?” developed a non-trivial theory of implementation meant to block the Putnam triviality result. The 2023 LLM essay does not revisit that argument. Whether the 1996 account is sufficient to restrict the mapmaker’s freedom enough to defend the 2023 position is the subject of S7.

06

The ACT Test

Susan Schneider’s Artificial Consciousness Test (ACT, developed with Edwin Turner; updated 2023 for LLMs) is a behavioural probe distinct from the Butlin–Long indicators.

The Original Test

The ACT is analogous to the Turing test but targets phenomenal experience rather than behavioural equivalence. The probe consists of open-ended questions designed to reveal whether a system has a “felt quality” to its mental life — questions a philosophical zombie could not answer consistently and a conscious system could. The questions are deliberately underdetermined; genuine phenomenal experience is supposed to supply the ground for consistent responses across novel reformulations.

The 2023 Update

Schneider’s 2023 update acknowledges a problem: LLMs can produce fluent, philosophically-sophisticated responses to ACT-style questions without (plausibly) having phenomenal experience. The update proposes treating LLM responses as a “weak marker” rather than a strong one, and supplements the ACT with what Schneider calls the Chip Test — a probe that looks at whether the system’s internal substrate has the kind of parts that support human consciousness.

The Chip Test’s Significance

The Chip Test is explicitly substrate-sensitive. If a machine has the same kinds of parts that could support human consciousness, we should consider that it might also be conscious. This distinguishes Schneider’s position from the Butlin–Long framework: the Butlin–Long indicators are substrate-neutral by design; Schneider’s Chip Test is not.

On biological-naturalism grounds (S5), Schneider’s move is in the right direction but unprincipled: it is not clear why "same kinds of parts" is the relevant criterion rather than "same kinds of biological dynamics." On Lerchner’s grounds, the Chip Test inherits the mapmaker problem via a different route: specifying what counts as “the same kind of part” requires a choice of abstraction level.

07

Objections

David Udell and Eric Schwitzgebel’s Susan Schneider’s Proposed Tests for AI Consciousness: Promising but Flawed (2021) develops a general critique that extends beyond Schneider’s specific tests.

The Core Argument

Any probe that relies on the system’s outputs — linguistic, behavioural, or otherwise — can be gamed or failed for reasons unrelated to consciousness. A system might output consciousness-appropriate responses without being conscious (the role-play case Shanahan develops in S2). A system might be conscious but fail to output consciousness-appropriate responses due to architectural constraints on self-reporting.

Extension to the Butlin–Long Framework

The Udell–Schwitzgebel argument has structural bearing on the Butlin–Long framework as well. The indicators are framed computationally rather than behaviourally, but the same class of worry applies: a system might instantiate the computational structure an indicator picks out without being conscious (the Lerchner worry), or might be conscious without instantiating it (the substrate-dependent reply, S5).

What the Argument Supports

Udell and Schwitzgebel do not argue that empirical assessment of AI consciousness is impossible. They argue that the existing probes — behavioural or computational — cannot deliver determinate verdicts under the uncertainty that actually obtains. The policy implication is closer to Askell’s constitutional hedge (S3) than to the indicator framework’s optimism: the question cannot be resolved by available techniques; what remains is how to act under irreducible uncertainty.

08

Lerchner’s Target

Lerchner’s Abstraction Fallacy names the Butlin–Long framework as the target of its critique. This section is worth reading carefully because it is the direct collision.

What Lerchner Targets

The framework’s methodological commitment — consciousness is specifiable at a computational level, theories can generate indicators at that level, satisfying more indicators makes a system a stronger candidate — is precisely the view Lerchner calls incoherent. On his argument: indicators are observer-dependent descriptions; specifying indicators computationally presupposes the level at which causal and phenomenal facts can be identified; the aggregative structure inherits the problem in proportion to its weight.

Three Defender Replies

Methodological neutrality

The indicators are conditional on functionalism, not an endorsement of it. Depends on the plausibility of treating outputs as genuinely conditional.

Restricted implementation

Non-trivial implementation theories (Piccinini, Miłkowski) can restrict which mappings count, blocking triviality. Moves discussion to S7.

Pragmatic

The framework is the best available programme; the alternative (Lerchner’s denial) leaves nothing tractable. Not an answer to the objection but a claim about what to do under uncertainty.

The fourth reading

Hold both as genuine uncertainty about what empirical consciousness science is; let welfare response adjust. Arguably the implicit position of Taking AI Welfare Seriously.

What This Section Establishes

The Butlin–Long framework is the clearest functionalist programme for empirical AI consciousness assessment. It is also the framework Lerchner targets most directly. The collision is not rhetorical; it is structural. How one reads it is itself a substantive philosophical commitment that the survey’s remaining sections (S5, S6, S7, S8) articulate in turn.
