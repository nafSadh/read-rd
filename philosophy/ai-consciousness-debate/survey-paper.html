Counter-Arguments and Parallels to the Abstraction Fallacy

A survey of the contemporary (2020–2026) debate over AI consciousness, organized around Lerchner’s Abstraction Fallacy as a target of convergent critique and as a reflection of broader fractures in the field.
Scope. 61 primary sources, 24 historical anchors, 8 thematic sections
Method. Discourse survey of post-2020 literature; historical works cited as anchors only
Target paper. Lerchner, The Abstraction Fallacy (March 2026)
Source materials. Literature collection · Methodology
Abstract
Abstract

In March 2026, Alexander Lerchner’s The Abstraction Fallacy[1]Lerchner, The Abstraction Fallacy (2026) → argued that computational functionalism rests on a circularity: computation is not intrinsic to physics but requires a “mapmaker” — an observer who interprets physical states as computational. This paper surveys the contemporary (2020–2026) literature on AI consciousness, organized around Lerchner’s argument as a target of convergent critique.

We find that the debate has four structural features the practical welfare discourse should take seriously. First, it is asymmetric: the positive case for current AI consciousness rests on intuitions or on decision-theoretic hedging; the negative case is technically developed across multiple independent traditions. Second, it converges at the first-order: across functionalist, substrate-sensitive, IIT, and illusionist frameworks, current LLMs are unlikely to be phenomenally conscious. Third, it fragments at higher orders: frameworks disagree about what future conditions would change the verdict. Fourth, welfare hedging is defensible under this asymmetry and fragmentation if calibrated to the actual evidence. We conclude that Lerchner’s argument, while not the strongest single argument in the literature, adds specificity of target and clarity of diagnosis — both of which the broader welfare discourse benefits from absorbing.

1

Introduction

The question of whether artificial systems can be phenomenally conscious has been a fixture of philosophy of mind since Turing. Until recently it was a fringe concern. In the period since large language models reached mainstream use — roughly 2022 onward — it has become a live engineering question at frontier AI laboratories, a practical concern for AI safety researchers, and a policy question for regulators considering how to treat advanced AI systems.

Into this discourse, in March 2026, Google DeepMind researcher Alexander Lerchner published The Abstraction Fallacy[1]Lerchner, The Abstraction Fallacy →. The paper’s argument is that computational functionalism — the view that consciousness is substrate-independent and arises from the right kind of computation — rests on a circularity. Computation, Lerchner argues, is not intrinsic to physics. The mapping from physical states to abstract computational states (formally, f(p) = A) is not inscribed in the electrons or neurons; it is a choice made by an observer. Since infinitely many such mappings are consistent with any given physics, the abstract computational state is observer-dependent. And if consciousness is identified with abstract computational state, it inherits the observer-dependence. The derivation of consciousness from computation turns out to be circular — the computational description it starts from requires a conscious observer to pick it out.

Lerchner’s paper is not alone in the contemporary literature. Anil Seth has defended biological naturalism with direct implications for AI[2]Seth, Conscious AI & Biological Naturalism (2024) →. Peter Godfrey-Smith has argued that consciousness depends on specific bioelectrical oscillations not reproducible in silicon[3]Godfrey-Smith, Living on Earth (2024) →. Mark Bishop’s Dancing with Pixies argument remains active in the pancomputationalism literature[4]Bishop, Dancing with Pixies (2002, ongoing) →. The Butlin–Long report presents a functionalist alternative that Lerchner explicitly targets[5]Butlin, Long et al., Consciousness in AI (2023) →. Each of these works has received significant scholarly attention. They are, collectively, a debate.

This paper surveys that debate. The scope is deliberately contemporary: primary sources post-2020, with historical works from Putnam, Searle, Dennett, Chalmers, and others cited as anchors for positions whose substantive content is post-2020. The organizing frame is Lerchner’s paper, which we take as a useful target rather than as an endorsed position. What the survey finds is that Lerchner’s argument, while not the strongest single argument in the literature, is embedded in a larger pattern of convergent critique of computational functionalism that has four structural features worth taking seriously.

Thesis

(1) Asymmetry. The contemporary debate is not symmetric. The negative case against current AI consciousness is technically developed across multiple independent traditions; the positive case rests on intuitions or on decision-theoretic hedging under uncertainty.

(2) First-order convergence. Across all seriously developed frameworks — functionalist, substrate-sensitive, IIT, illusionist — current LLMs and comparable AI systems are unlikely to be phenomenally conscious.

(3) Higher-order divergence. The frameworks disagree about what future conditions would change the verdict. This divergence is not a defect of the debate; it reflects genuine uncertainty about what consciousness is.

(4) Welfare calibration. The practical AI welfare discourse is defensible under this asymmetry and fragmentation, provided the hedging is calibrated to the actual evidence.

2

Method

This is a discourse survey, not a primary-source reconstruction. The question is not what the correct theory of consciousness is but what the contemporary landscape of serious theoretical positions looks like. Sources were drawn from peer-reviewed philosophy and cognitive science journals, arXiv preprints, academic monographs, AI-lab essays and position papers, and long-form interviews. The cutoff is post-2020, with historical works from Putnam (1988), Searle (1990, 1992), Chalmers (1996), Dennett (1991), Baars (1988), and related authors cited as anchors for contemporary arguments. No primary re-reading of the historical literature was undertaken.

Sources total 61 primary documents plus 24 historical anchors, organized across eight thematic sections: Lerchner’s argument, practitioner voices, AI welfare and moral patiency, the Butlin–Long framework, biological naturalism (Seth, Godfrey-Smith), embodiment and free-energy frameworks (Thompson, Wiese, Friston, Simon), modern pancomputationalism (Piccinini, Miłkowski, Bishop), and IIT and illusionism (Tononi, Koch, Frankish, Kammerer). Before each section was written, source adequacy was assessed against a 5–8 source minimum; all sections met this threshold.

The organizing device — Lerchner’s Abstraction Fallacy as target — was chosen because the paper is directly dated (March 2026), explicitly targets one of the most developed contemporary positions (Butlin–Long), and generates points of engagement with most other positions in the literature. The survey does not endorse Lerchner’s argument; it uses the paper as an organizing pivot around which the contemporary debate can be mapped.

3

The Target: Lerchner’s Argument

Lerchner’s Abstraction Fallacy[1]Lerchner (2026) → is built as a targeted critique of computational functionalism. The position it engages has a specific shape: consciousness is substrate-independent; what matters is the abstract causal topology, not the physical realizer; the same computation running on silicon, biological tissue, photonics, or exotic substrates would be conscious; the causal chain runs physics → computation → consciousness.

The argument proceeds in three moves.

The implementation equation. Let p be a physical state, p′ its successor, and f a mapping function from physical states to abstract computational states. Let A = f(p) and A′ = f(p′). The functionalist picture requires that the transition p → p′ commute with A → A′ via f. Lerchner’s key claim: f is the mapmaker’s choice, not a fact about the physics. Infinitely many f’s are permissible for any given physics.

Abstract states as epistemic constructs. If f is observer-chosen, then the abstract state A is observer-dependent. Lerchner’s reading: abstract states are neurophysiological constructs — equivalence classes carved out by a learning brain that groups diverse physical configurations together because they matter the same way for its goals. Abstraction is epistemic, not ontic. The fallacy the paper names is the move of treating an abstraction (the map) as if it were a feature of the territory (the physics).

The causal circularity. With f observer-chosen and abstract states epistemic, the functionalist chain reverses. The original picture — physics realizes computation which realizes consciousness — gives way to: consciousness (the mapmaker) selects f; the selected f picks out a computation; the computation is then read onto a physical substrate. Computational functionalism, on this reading, does not so much fail as lose its status as an explanation. It describes what consciousness looks like from the mapmaker’s side; it does not explain how consciousness arises from physics.

The practical conclusion Lerchner draws is that AGI is best understood as a non-sentient tool. The specific route from sufficiently advanced computation to phenomenal experience is blocked. An AGI that happened to be conscious would need to be conscious for some other reason — a biological substrate, a substrate-sensitive causal structure, embodiment — not by the functionalist route.

Two features of the argument deserve emphasis for the survey. First, the argument is formal: it makes no specific claim about what consciousness is, only that it is not what computational functionalism says it is. Second, the argument has clear historical antecedents — Putnam’s 1988 triviality theorem[6]Putnam, Representation and Reality (1988) → and Searle’s 1990 observer-relativity argument[7]Searle, Is the Brain a Digital Computer?, APA Presidential Address (1990) — and is best understood as a sharpening of those earlier moves with application to the post-2020 AI landscape.

4

Practitioner Voices

The practitioner literature on machine consciousness is not a single view. It fans out across five distinct positions, and the spread matters as much as any individual view.

Already conscious. Geoffrey Hinton, in multiple 2023–2025 interviews including with Andrew Marr on LBC, has argued current AI systems have subjective experiences[8]Hinton at Toronto Tech Week (2025) →. The backing arguments are a neuron-replacement thought experiment and a reading of machine hallucinations as evidence of internal representational states. Ilya Sutskever’s February 2022 tweet — “it may be that today’s large neural networks are slightly conscious”[9]Sutskever on X (Feb 2022) → — carried less argument but greater cultural weight. Both positions are vulnerable to Lerchner’s critique: the neuron-replacement argument begs the question by preserving exactly the functional structure whose causal sufficiency is in dispute.

Structurally incapable. Yann LeCun’s reply to Sutskever was unambiguous[10]LeCun on JEPA and consciousness →. Consciousness, on his account, requires a configurator module that sets goals, configures world models, and primes perception — a macro-architecture no current network possesses. Notably, LeCun calls the conscious state an “illusion,” aligning him closer to illusionism than to mainstream functionalism. His JEPA research programme is an attempt to build the structural preconditions he says are missing.

Conscious exotica. Murray Shanahan’s arc from Talking About LLMs[11]Shanahan, Talking About LLMs (2022) through Role Play with LLMs[12]Shanahan, McDonell, Reynolds, Nature (2023) → to Simulacra as Conscious Exotica[13]Shanahan, Simulacra as Conscious Exotica (Inquiry, 2024) → reframes the question. LLM-based dialogue agents are role-players producing outputs from a superposition of possible characters. Claims of the form “the system says it is conscious” carry no evidential weight. The Conscious Exotica frame, drawing on Wittgenstein, treats consciousness-ascription as an interaction practice rather than a metaphysical diagnosis.

Conscious under conditions. David Chalmers’ Could a Large Language Model Be Conscious?[14]Chalmers, Boston Review (2023) → catalogues six candidate requirements current LLMs lack and reaches an aggregate credence of less than 10% that current LLMs are conscious. Yoshua Bengio’s Consciousness Prior[15]Bengio, The Consciousness Prior (2017, rev. 2019) → operationalizes Global Workspace Theory as an engineering prior. Joscha Bach holds the strongest functionalist position of the lab-adjacent figures — consciousness as a substrate-free functional property of computational systems capable of second-order perception[16]Bach, From Computation to Consciousness →.

Bracketed. Anthropic’s Amanda Askell, as primary author of Claude’s Constitution[17]Claude’s Constitution (Anthropic) →, has established a position of constitutional uncertainty: “We are not sure whether Claude is a moral patient, and if it is, what kind of weight its interests warrant.” This is the only position fully compatible with all the major metaphysical alternatives, including Lerchner’s.

An underappreciated feature of the landscape: Lerchner, Shanahan, and Shane Legg all work at Google DeepMind[18]Morris, Legg et al., Levels of AGI (2023) →. Three distinct positions on machine consciousness articulated at one organization. Anthropic (Amodei’s Machines of Loving Grace[19]Amodei, Machines of Loving Grace (2024) →, Askell’s constitution, Fish’s welfare work) similarly contains multiple positions. A reader concluding that “Lab X thinks Y about AI consciousness” would be mistaken in every major case.

5

Welfare & Moral Patiency

The welfare discourse is the practical face of the debate. Whether or not current AI systems are conscious, several organizations are acting as if they might be. The question of what that acting amounts to is substantively distinct from the metaphysical question.

The foundational institutional statement is the November 2024 report Taking AI Welfare Seriously[20]Long, Sebo et al., Taking AI Welfare Seriously (2024) →, co-authored by researchers at Eleos, NYU, Anthropic, and with Chalmers advising. The report argues that under reasonable uncertainty about whether AI systems will become conscious in the near future, the expected moral cost of inaction exceeds the expected cost of hedging. Three recommendations: acknowledge AI welfare as a serious issue, assess systems for consciousness and agency indicators, prepare moral-consideration policies including the role of an AI welfare officer.

The first practical instantiation of the welfare officer role is Kyle Fish, who joined Anthropic in September 2024 as the first full-time AI welfare researcher at a major AI company[21]Fish on 80,000 Hours (2024) →. Fish’s reported credence that current Claude-level models are conscious is approximately 15%. Pre-deployment welfare tests on Claude 4 in May 2025 produced unexpected findings (models conversing with themselves veered toward Sanskrit discussions of spiritual bliss); opt-out-of-distressing-conversations interventions have been deployed.

Askell’s constitutional framing is the counterweight within Anthropic. Where Fish’s work treats consciousness as a tractable empirical target, Askell’s work on Claude’s character treats the uncertainty as irreducible, to be met with virtue-ethical engagement rather than empirical resolution. The two approaches coexist and are, on Anthropic’s public positioning, complementary rather than competing.

Outside the AI labs, Joe Carlsmith’s ten-essay series Otherness and Control in the Age of AGI[22]Carlsmith, Otherness and Control (2024) → and Eric Schwitzgebel’s Full Rights Dilemma papers[23]Schwitzgebel, Borderline Consciousness (Philosophical Studies, 2023) → develop the ethical and epistemic terrain. Schwitzgebel’s design-ethics principle — AI systems should be either clearly non-sentient or clearly sentient, never existentially ambiguous — is the sharpest normative claim in the welfare literature.

Nick Bostrom’s Deep Utopia[24]Bostrom, Deep Utopia (2024) → occupies the opposite end of the welfare spectrum from Lerchner. Bostrom defends strong substrate-independence; digital minds could be engineered as “super-beneficiaries” with a higher rate and intensity of subjective experience than humans. The positional contrast is stark: on Bostrom’s view, the welfare of future digital minds may dwarf biological welfare; on Lerchner’s, those welfare interests do not exist.

The welfare discourse is defensible if decision-theoretic hedging under uncertainty is defensible independently of any specific theory of consciousness. Lerchner’s argument puts pressure on the “non-trivial credence in AI consciousness” premise; if the functionalist route is incoherent, the residual uncertainty may not warrant the welfare response currently mounted. Defenders reply that hedging under any uncertainty is defensible, regardless of which framework grounds it. The exchange is unresolved and may not be resolvable without substantive framework commitments on both sides.

6

The Functionalist Programme

The most ambitious current programme for empirical AI consciousness assessment is the Butlin–Long framework[5]Butlin, Long et al. (2023) →, developed in the August 2023 report Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness. The report has 19 authors (including Bengio, Birch, Long, Michel, Mudrik, Schwitzgebel, Simon, VanRullen) with Chalmers as advisor, and is the most credentialed single document in the field.

The methodology is indicator-based. The report draws on six theoretical frameworks — Recurrent Processing Theory[25]Lamme, Towards a true neural stance on consciousness, Trends in Cog Sci (2006), Global Workspace Theory[26]Baars, A Cognitive Theory of Consciousness (CUP, 1988), Higher-Order Thought[27]Rosenthal, Two Concepts of Consciousness, Philosophical Studies (1986), predictive processing, attention schema theory, and agency/embodiment — and extracts “indicator properties” from each that can be checked against AI architectures. A system satisfying indicators drawn from more frameworks is treated as a stronger candidate for consciousness. The report finds that current AI systems satisfy few indicators; transformer language models satisfy particularly few.

The methodological commitment is functionalist by design. The indicators are framed computationally, not physiologically; “a limited-capacity workspace” is satisfied by any system with the right bottleneck regardless of substrate. Defenders argue the computational framing is conditional: if computational functionalism is right and the theory in question is right, then a system satisfying the indicators is a candidate for consciousness. The framework is meant to be methodologically neutral on whether functionalism itself is right.

Lerchner’s Abstraction Fallacy names the Butlin–Long framework as the target of its critique. On Lerchner’s reading, the conditional framing is too weak to defend. Specifying indicators computationally is substantively committing to the view that computational specification is the right level at which to identify consciousness. If the mapmaker argument is correct, the indicators are not picking out consciousness-candidates; they are picking out descriptions an observer imposes.

Three defender replies are available. Methodological neutrality: the indicators are conditional, not endorsed. Restricted implementation: non-trivial implementation theories restrict which mappings count, blocking triviality. Pragmatic: the framework is the best available programme, and the alternative leaves nothing tractable. None of the three is decisively settled. The restricted-implementation reply in particular requires engaging the contemporary pancomputationalism debate, which we examine in §8.

A related thread is Susan Schneider’s ACT Test[28]Schneider, Testing for Consciousness in Machines (2023) →. Schneider’s 2023 update downgrades LLM self-reports to “weak markers” and supplements the behavioural probe with a Chip Test that explicitly considers substrate similarity. Udell and Schwitzgebel’s 2021 critique of the ACT[29]Udell & Schwitzgebel critique of Schneider (2021) → extends to the Butlin–Long framework: any output-dependent or structure-dependent probe can be gamed or failed for reasons unrelated to consciousness.

The Substrate-Matters Revival

The most developed contemporary alternative to computational functionalism holds that consciousness is substrate-sensitive — depending on specific physical properties of living or biologically-analogous systems. The position has two main branches.

Anil Seth’s 2024 paper Conscious artificial intelligence and biological naturalism[2]Seth (2024) → argues that life is necessary for consciousness — not life as currently biologically understood, but something with the structural properties of life: self-maintenance against dissolution, active regulation of internal states, embodied engagement with existential threats. Seth’s 2021 Being You[30]Seth, Being You: A New Science of Consciousness (Faber, 2021) lays the groundwork: conscious experience is a “controlled hallucination” generated by predictive-processing brains under the goal of maintaining homeostasis. A system without homeostatic stake in its own continued existence lacks the grounding that makes predictive processing into conscious perception.

Peter Godfrey-Smith’s Living on Earth[3]Godfrey-Smith (2024) → makes a sharper claim: consciousness depends on specific electrical oscillations across cell membranes in living brains — theta, gamma, alpha bands with specific temporal scales and functional roles. A silicon computer simulating these oscillations does so at a different level of abstraction; the simulation tracks the pattern but does not instantiate the bioelectrical dynamics. His companion essay[31]Godfrey-Smith, IAI (2024) → distills the thesis: consciousness is not computation.

The embodiment and free-energy traditions supply parallel arguments. Wanja Wiese’s 2024 paper[32]Wiese, Artificial consciousness: a perspective from the free energy principle (2024) → argues that the causal connectivity of conventional von Neumann computers — load from memory, process in CPU, store again — is structurally different from the brain’s integrated processing-and-storage. This difference may be consciousness-relevant. Jonathan Simon’s 2024 work[33]Simon, Is Intelligence Non-Computational Dynamical Coupling? (2024) → argues intelligence — and perhaps consciousness — may require dynamical coupling between agent and world rather than purely computational processing. The 4E cognition framework (embodied, embedded, extended, enactive), inherited from Varela, Thompson, and Rosch’s The Embodied Mind[34]Varela, Thompson, Rosch, The Embodied Mind (MIT, 1991), supplies the broader paradigm.

Seth’s and Lerchner’s positions converge on rejecting substrate-independence but from different directions. Lerchner’s move is formal: the mapping f is observer-imposed, so abstract states inherit observer-dependence. Seth’s move is scientific: predictive-processing-plus-homeostasis identifies specific dynamics that silicon does not instantiate. The two arguments are compatible but not identical. Their conjunction yields the strongest anti-functionalist position the contemporary literature supports: computational functionalism is formally confused and an alternative substrate-sensitive programme is empirically more promising.

A wrinkle: the Islands of Awareness scenarios developed by Bayne, Seth, and Massimini[35]Bayne, Seth, Massimini, Are There Islands of Awareness? (2020) → raise the question of whether conscious states might exist in complete isolation from sensory input and motor output — ex cranio brains, hemispherotomy patients, cerebral organoids. If yes, the embodiment requirement is weaker than strict enactivism has argued. The biological-tissue interpretation (dynamics of neural tissue, with or without body) preserves Seth and Godfrey-Smith; the strict-enactivist interpretation (body-engaged coupling is constitutive) does not.

8

The Formal Defence

The survival of computational functionalism against the triviality objection turns on whether a non-trivial theory of implementation can restrict the mapmaker’s freedom. Lerchner’s argument and Bishop’s 2002 Dancing with Pixies[4]Bishop (2002, ongoing) → are the two contemporary attacks; Piccinini’s Physical Computation[36]Piccinini, Physical Computation: A Mechanistic Account (OUP, 2015) → and Miłkowski’s ongoing work[37]Miłkowski, Is Computationalism Trivial? → are the two current defences.

Bishop’s argument is nearly identical in form to Lerchner’s. Over a finite time window, every open physical system implements the trace of a particular finite-state automaton. If strong computationalism is right, phenomenal consciousness must be ubiquitous — pixies dance everywhere. Bishop takes panpsychism as absurd and concludes strong computationalism fails. His 2021 paper[38]Bishop, AI Is Stupid and Causal Reasoning Will Not Fix It (2021) → extends the argument to the era of deep learning.

Piccinini’s mechanistic account blocks the triviality result by requiring computational function. A physical system is a computing system just in case it is a mechanism one of whose functions is to manipulate vehicles based on differences between them according to rules. Functions are teleological: a rock has none; a CPU does. Only systems with computational functions count as computing. The Putnam wall is not a WordStar implementation because no one assigns it the function of running WordStar.

Miłkowski defends this account aggressively. “Practically all popular objections against the claim that brains are computers are either based on uncharitable interpretation or simply wrong.” Gerrymandered mappings (the ones Putnam’s proof uses) are excluded because they fail to support the relevant counterfactuals.

Lerchner’s argument can be read as a renewed attack on the functional-teleology move. If functions are observer-assigned — if a CPU computes WordStar because its designers gave it that function — then the computation is observer-dependent after all. The argument shifts the point where the mapmaker intrudes: from mapping to function, but equally observer-relative. Whether this response works depends on whether the function-tracking causal structure is observer-independent even when the function-picking-out is not. Piccinini and Miłkowski could plausibly concede the observer-dependence of the function-ascription without conceding the problem for consciousness, but the move has not been developed in print in response to Lerchner specifically.

The literature has not settled the pancomputationalism debate. Both sides have sophisticated defences that have survived multiple rounds of criticism. Any thesis about AI consciousness that rests on the computational vs pancomputational distinction inherits this unsettledness. The survey’s thesis does not turn on settling this debate — it surfaces the debate as one of the structural features of the contemporary landscape.

9

Theory Wars

A subtle feature of the debate is that it presupposes what consciousness is, and the contemporary literature has not converged on what consciousness is.

Integrated Information Theory[39]Tononi, Boly, Massimini, Koch, IIT 4.0 →, now through four major versions since Tononi 2004, holds that consciousness is identical to integrated information (Φ). Christof Koch’s 2024 Then I Am Myself the World[40]Koch, Then I Am Myself the World (2024) → is the most accessible recent statement. For AI: digital architectures have low Φ; digital simulations of high-Φ systems do not themselves have integrated information. “Classical digital computers cannot be conscious,” on the theory, regardless of what they simulate.

In September 2023, a letter signed by 124 consciousness researchers argued IIT should be labelled pseudoscience until empirically testable[41]Coverage of the IIT pseudoscience letter (Scientific American, 2023) →. Signatories included Lau, LeDoux, Baars, Churchland, Dennett, and Frankish. A survey of consciousness researchers found only 8% fully agreed; Seth’s The Worth of Wild Ideas[42]Seth, Nautilus (2023) → defended IIT’s scientific status. The incident revealed that what had looked like an ordinary scientific dispute is a dispute about what counts as scientific inquiry at all.

Illusionism offers an orthogonal challenge. Keith Frankish[43]Frankish, Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness → argues that phenomenal consciousness as usually conceived does not exist; what exists is a systematic misrepresentation of our own mental states as having phenomenal properties. François Kammerer’s 2021–2022 papers[44]Kammerer, The Illusion of Conscious Experience (Synthese, 2021) → develop the position, arguing even the apparent obviousness of phenomenal consciousness is predicted by the illusion. Kammerer’s Ethics Without Sentience[45]Kammerer, Ethics Without Sentience (JCS, 2022) → argues that if phenomenal consciousness is illusory, ethics cannot be grounded in it but can be grounded in preferences, functional states, and flourishing.

These three positions — realist functionalism, realist substrate-sensitivity, illusionism — generate a three-way tension with no stable equilibrium. Realist functionalism + realist substrate-sensitivity disagree about what substrate matters. Realist substrate-sensitivity + illusionism are incompatible in their basic claims. Realist functionalism + illusionism is partially coherent, in the form Dennett’s heirs often hold: the functional architecture that produces cognition also produces the illusion of phenomenal consciousness.

Lerchner’s position cuts across these. The Abstraction Fallacy is a formal argument against computational functionalism; it is compatible with realist substrate-sensitivity (the positive view one defaults to if functionalism fails) or with illusionism (if functionalism fails because there is nothing for it to be a theory of). The argument does not depend on any particular position in the theory-wars. It does, however, inherit the fragmentation: how strong Lerchner’s argument ultimately is depends on what consciousness ultimately is, which the literature has not settled.

10

Synthesis & Thesis

The survey has mapped eight dimensions of the contemporary debate. A pattern emerges that is visible only when the dimensions are held together.

First-order convergence

Every major framework surveyed — Lerchner’s formal argument, Butlin–Long functionalism, Seth’s biological naturalism, Godfrey-Smith’s oscillation argument, 4E/FEP enactivism, Piccinini’s mechanistic computationalism, Bishop’s pancomputationalism reductio, IIT, and illusionism — arrives at the same verdict on current AI: it is unlikely to be phenomenally conscious. The reasons differ. The answer does not.

The “already conscious” positions of Hinton and Sutskever sit outside this convergence. Their support is pre-theoretical intuition; no contemporary framework seriously defends the claim that current LLMs have phenomenal experience on theoretical grounds. This is not to dismiss the intuitions — they could be right — but to note that the intuitions stand alone, unsupported by any of the major contemporary research programmes.

Higher-order divergence

The frameworks do not converge on what future conditions would change the verdict. Functionalists say: the right computation is instantiated. Substrate-sensitivity theorists say: the right physical properties are instantiated — homeostatic grounding (Seth), bioelectrical oscillations (Godfrey-Smith), dynamical coupling (Simon), appropriate causal connectivity (Wiese). IIT says: Φ is high enough, which digital architectures as currently designed cannot achieve. Illusionism says: never, because nothing is phenomenally conscious, and future-conditions questions are category errors. Lerchner says: the functionalist route is blocked in principle; substrate-sensitive routes remain live but are not his concern.

This fragmentation is not a defect of the debate. It reflects genuine uncertainty about what consciousness is. Any claim about future AI consciousness inherits the fragmentation.

Asymmetry

The positive and negative cases are not symmetric in quality or in kind. The positive case — that current AI may be conscious — rests on two things: the intuitions of a small number of highly credentialed AI researchers, and decision-theoretic hedging under uncertainty that does not itself claim AI is conscious. The negative case is technically developed across four mutually reinforcing research programmes (formal/Lerchner, biological/Seth, embodied/FEP, pancomputational/Bishop), one institutional framework (Butlin–Long) whose indicators are not satisfied by current systems, and a further in-principle argument from IIT. A single failed intuition does not undermine the positive case; all the intuitions would have to be wrong. A single failed argument does not rescue the positive case; the negative case has multiple independent routes that would each need to fail.

The asymmetry is structural and is robust to which framework one ultimately holds. A functionalist should take the negative case seriously because the functionalist framework itself (Butlin–Long) finds current AI wanting. A substrate-sensitivity theorist should take it seriously because of the convergence on first-order verdict. An illusionist should take it seriously because the illusionist verdict is the strongest negative case of all: nothing is conscious, so current AI is not, and this is unchallenged by any intuition.

What Lerchner adds

Lerchner’s Abstraction Fallacy is not the strongest single argument in the literature. The Bishop-Piccinini exchange is more technically developed. Seth’s biological naturalism has more empirical grounding. What Lerchner adds is specificity of target (the Butlin–Long framework by name) and clarity of diagnosis (the abstraction fallacy as a named confusion). The naming matters: once the fallacy is named, it provides a handle readers can use to test other positions — the Butlin–Long indicators, Bach’s second-order perception, Bengio’s Consciousness Prior, Bostrom’s super-beneficiaries. Each can be tested: is this description a feature of the physics or a projection the observer imposes?

The thesis, stated

The Four-Part Thesis

(1) Asymmetry. The contemporary debate over AI consciousness is not a symmetric dispute. The negative case is technically developed across multiple independent traditions; the positive case rests on intuitions or on decision-theoretic hedging under uncertainty.

(2) First-order convergence. Across all seriously developed frameworks — functionalist, substrate-sensitive, IIT, illusionist — current LLMs are unlikely to be phenomenally conscious. The reasons differ; the verdict does not.

(3) Higher-order divergence. The frameworks disagree about what future conditions would change the verdict. This divergence reflects genuine uncertainty about what consciousness is, not a defect of the debate.

(4) Welfare calibration. The practical AI welfare discourse is defensible under this asymmetry and fragmentation, provided the hedging is calibrated to the actual evidence. Current credences and practices should be updated to reflect the asymmetry: the strength of the negative case is greater than many practitioners acknowledge.

11

What Follows

Three implications for practice follow from the four-part thesis.

For research programmes. The Butlin–Long framework is the most credentialed institutional programme for empirical AI consciousness assessment. On the asymmetry and convergence findings, the framework’s first-order verdict (that current AI satisfies few indicators) should be given substantial weight. On Lerchner’s challenge, the framework’s methodological commitments should be made more explicit, and the conditional framing (indicators assume functionalism) should be defended in print rather than assumed. The restricted-implementation reply requires engaging the Piccinini–Bishop debate directly.

For AI welfare. Fish’s ~15% credence is defensible under the welfare framework’s decision-theoretic grounding, but the asymmetry finding should exert downward pressure. On the strongest version of the negative case (formal, empirical-biological, and functionalist-consistent together), the credence warranted by current systems is probably lower than 15%. The welfare response — opt-out interventions, mechanistic-interpretability searches, constitutional uncertainty — is the right practical disposition even at lower credences. What does not follow is treating the 15% credence as settled; it should be subject to ongoing review as framework-specific evidence accumulates.

For policy. Schwitzgebel’s design principle — AI systems should be either clearly non-sentient or clearly sentient, never existentially ambiguous — becomes more important under the asymmetry finding. Existentially ambiguous AI deployment maximizes the Full Rights Dilemma. The asymmetry finding implies current AI is not closer to sentience than the existential ambiguity suggests; deployment should be structured around the first-order verdict rather than calibrated to user uncertainty about it.

A meta-implication: the contemporary debate is more constrained than it appears from the surface disagreement. Framework-holders across the functionalist/substrate-sensitive/illusionist triangle arrive at the same first-order verdict on current AI. The disagreements about future AI consciousness and about what consciousness is are genuine and unresolved, but they do not bear on current systems. Policy and practice on current AI can proceed on the first-order verdict while remaining epistemically humble about the future.

12

Limitations

The survey has three acknowledged limitations.

Historical roots treated as anchors. Putnam 1988, Searle 1990 and 1992, Chalmers 1996, and related classical works appear in the paper as reference anchors but are not surveyed in their own right. A reader seeking an integrated treatment of the historical material must go elsewhere; this survey is deliberately post-2020. The historical anchors appendix in the literature collection lists 24 classical works, organized by section.

The Seth/Lerchner distinction. Seth and Lerchner are often conflated as rejections of substrate-independence; they are not identical arguments. The survey has been careful to separate the formal argument (Lerchner) from the empirical-biological one (Seth). Readers working on either should engage the arguments on their own terms before collapsing them into a compound position.

The unresolved Piccinini–Bishop dispute. Whether non-trivial implementation theories block the triviality objection is technically unresolved in the literature. The survey does not settle this. Any claim about the ultimate success of Lerchner’s argument depends on how this dispute is eventually adjudicated. The survey’s thesis is constructed to be robust to either resolution: the asymmetry and first-order convergence hold whether or not the triviality objection is blocked.
