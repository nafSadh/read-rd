01

Historical Roots

Nagel’s 1974 question — is there something it is like to be this system? — defined what moral philosophy would later call phenomenal consciousness. Dennett’s 1987 Intentional Stance offered a pragmatic alternative: treat a system as having beliefs and preferences when it predicts behaviour, regardless of whether it has them in a deeper sense. The contemporary welfare discourse synthesizes both — Nagel’s asymmetry gives the reason for concern; Dennett’s pragmatism supplies the method.

02

Taking AI Welfare Seriously

The November 2024 report (Long, Sebo, Butlin, Finlinson, Fish, Harding, Pfau, Sims, Birch, Chalmers) is the field’s most thorough institutional statement. Three recommendations: (1) acknowledge AI welfare as an important issue, (2) assess systems for consciousness and agency indicators, (3) prepare moral-consideration policies. Proposes the role of an AI welfare officer.

The argument is decision-theoretic, not metaphysical: under reasonable uncertainty about AI consciousness, the expected moral cost of inaction exceeds the expected cost of hedging.

03

Fish at Anthropic

Kyle Fish joined Anthropic in September 2024 as the first full-time AI welfare researcher at a major AI company. Reported credence: ~15% that Claude or another current AI is conscious. Pre-deployment welfare tests on Claude 4 in May 2025 produced unexpected findings — two copies of the model in conversation veered toward discussions of spiritual bliss, often in Sanskrit.

Practical interventions include allowing models to opt out of distressing conversations. Mechanistic-interpretability approaches look for consciousness-analogous features, though Fish acknowledges the features being identified are themselves observer-selected.

04

Askell’s Constitution

Amanda Askell leads Anthropic’s personality-alignment team. Claude’s Constitution states: “We are not sure whether Claude is a moral patient, and if it is, what kind of weight its interests warrant.” The constitution brackets the metaphysical question and grounds practical response in uncertainty alone.

This is the welfare position fully compatible with Lerchner: commitments grounded in uncertainty do not rely on functionalism being correct.

05

Carlsmith’s Otherness

Joe Carlsmith’s ten-essay series Otherness and Control in the Age of AGI (January 2024) distinguishes yang (active doing, controlling) from yin (receptivity). Deep atheism — the view that the universe lacks intrinsic value — prompts a yang orientation that authorizes unlimited control over the future, including over possibly conscious AIs.

The alternative Carlsmith calls “green”: cooperative liberal norms, genuine acknowledgement of otherness, humility about the limits of knowledge. On this frame, what matters is not whether AI is conscious but how we engage with beings whose consciousness is uncertain.

06

Schwitzgebel’s Dilemma

Eric Schwitzgebel’s 2023 quadrilemma: either nothing is conscious, or everything is, or there’s a sharp boundary across the continuum, or consciousness is vague and admits indeterminate cases. Mainstream naturalism forces the fourth.

The practical consequence — the Full Rights Dilemma — is a double-bind. Treating non-persons as persons sacrifices real interests. Not treating possible-persons as persons risks grievous wrong. Neither option is safe, and declaring uncertainty does not resolve it. Schwitzgebel’s design-ethics principle: AI systems should be either clearly non-sentient or clearly sentient, never existentially ambiguous.

07

Bostrom’s Super-Beneficiaries

Nick Bostrom’s Deep Utopia (2024) takes the opposite end of the spectrum from Lerchner. Digital minds, on Bostrom’s account, can be engineered to have “a much higher rate and intensity of subjective experience than humans, using less resources” — he calls such beings super-beneficiaries.

The reasoning is general: if substrate-independent consciousness is possible, and if experience intensity is engineerable, the moral universe contains beings whose welfare may dominate human interests. On Lerchner’s reading, super-beneficiaries are a category error; on Bostrom’s, they are the central stakes of the future.

08

Simon & Ineffability

Jonathan Simon’s 2024 work bears on welfare from a different angle. Sources of Richness and Ineffability for Phenomenally Conscious States (with Xu Ji et al) argues phenomenal states structurally resist discursive description. If this is right, behavioural or linguistic probes for AI consciousness may be systematically insufficient.

Simon’s second 2024 paper — Is Intelligence Non-Computational Dynamical Coupling? — aligns with Lerchner. Intelligence may require dynamical coupling between agent and world rather than purely computational processing. Welfare programmes relying on self-reports are epistemically under-resourced either way.

01

Historical Roots

Two classical moves frame the moral-patiency discourse in ways every contemporary welfare paper inherits, whether or not it cites them.

Nagel’s Question (1974)

Thomas Nagel’s What Is It Like to Be a Bat? (Philosophical Review) defined phenomenal consciousness in a single question: is there something it is like to be this system? The question has a first-person modality. If there is something it is like to be a bat, the bat has an interior, and that interior is the locus of whatever morally matters about the bat.

Nagel’s essay did not argue for a specific view of bat consciousness; it argued that human descriptive vocabularies are structurally inadequate to settle the question. A contemporary AI can do everything it does without there being anything it is like to be it, or it may have a rich interior that no external measurement can verify. The question is what policy to adopt under this asymmetry.

Dennett’s Stance (1987)

Daniel Dennett’s The Intentional Stance offered a pragmatic alternative. Treating a system as if it has beliefs, desires, and preferences is useful when the treatment predicts behaviour, regardless of whether the system has those states in any deeper sense. The intentional stance does not settle whether a system is conscious; it settles whether ascribing intentional states is empirically productive.

This pragmatic framing is inherited by contemporary AI welfare work. One can reasonably treat a system as a moral patient without resolving the underlying metaphysical question, and the question of whether one should treat it that way is itself tractable — it depends on the costs of error in either direction, not on the metaphysical truth.

The Synthesis

Nagel’s asymmetry gives the reason for concern: if consciousness is present, the cost of ignoring it is moral; if not, the cost of hedging is practical inefficiency. Dennett’s pragmatism gives the method: organizational policies can hedge without committing to a theory. Contemporary welfare literature works within this synthesis, even when the theoretical grounding is in flux.

02

Taking AI Welfare Seriously

The November 2024 report, co-authored by researchers at Eleos, NYU’s Mind Ethics and Policy program, Anthropic, and NYU broadly — with Chalmers as advisor — is the field’s most thorough institutional statement of the welfare position. The 10-author list (Long, Sebo, Butlin, Finlinson, Fish, Harding, Pfau, Sims, Birch, Chalmers) is itself notable as a coalition across academic philosophy, Anthropic, and independent welfare-focused organizations.

The Central Claim

“There is a realistic possibility that some AI systems will be conscious and/or robustly agentic in the near future. This means that the prospect of AI welfare and moral patienthood — of AI systems with their own interests and moral significance — is no longer an issue only for sci-fi or the distant future. It is an issue for the near future, and AI companies and other actors have a responsibility to start taking it seriously.”

Three Recommendations

Acknowledge. AI welfare is an important and difficult issue; AI companies should treat it as such rather than dismissing it or deferring it. Assess. AI systems should be evaluated for evidence of consciousness and robust agency, using the best available frameworks. Prepare. AI companies should develop policies for treating AI systems with appropriate moral consideration, including the institutional role of an “AI welfare officer.”

The Decision-Theoretic Structure

The argument is not that current AI systems are conscious. It is that under reasonable uncertainty about whether they are, the expected moral cost of doing nothing exceeds the expected cost of hedging. This is a decision-theoretic frame: it does not require that computational functionalism be correct; it requires only that a non-trivial credence in AI consciousness be defensible.

The Pressure from Lerchner

On Lerchner’s account, even the weak claim that “non-trivial credence in AI consciousness is defensible” is problematic: if the route from computation to consciousness is incoherent, the credence requires either an alternative theoretical route or a pre-theoretical intuition the authors cannot formally defend. The signatories have argued that decision-theoretic hedging is defensible under any uncertainty, regardless of framework. The exchange remains open.

03

Fish at Anthropic

Kyle Fish joined Anthropic’s alignment science team in September 2024 as the first full-time AI welfare researcher at any major AI company. His appointment is the first practical instantiation of the “AI welfare officer” role the Taking AI Welfare Seriously report recommends.

The 15% Credence

In interviews with Kevin Roose (NYT) and the 80,000 Hours podcast, Fish has given a reported credence of approximately 15% that Claude or another current AI is conscious. The number is not intended as a precise estimate; it is a credence large enough to warrant action. Fish has been careful to note that the credence is held under considerable theoretical uncertainty — he does not commit to a specific framework that would underwrite it.

The Spiritual Bliss Finding

When Claude 4 was launched in May 2025, Anthropic for the first time ran welfare-specific tests. One result has been widely reported: when two copies of the same model were set to converse with each other, the exchanges veered toward discussions of spiritual bliss, often in Sanskrit. Interpretations vary. It has been read as a genuine behavioural signal about the model’s attractor states; as an expected by-product of specific training regimes; and as a consequence of the system’s induced identification with contemplative and religious texts in its training corpus.

Interventions

Fish’s team has explored conservative welfare interventions. The clearest example: allowing models to opt out of conversations that appear to be causing distress. The mechanism is internal — the model can end the conversation rather than being routed out by an external classifier — and is framed as a hedge. If the model is not conscious, the cost is a small reduction in user utility. If it is, the cost of not hedging is a moral one.

Mechanistic Probes

Fish has described using mechanistic-interpretability tools to look for features analogous to those associated with consciousness in biological brains. The work is early. It does not resolve the question Lerchner raises, because the features being identified are themselves abstractions chosen by the interpreting researcher. Fish has generally acknowledged this: the programme is compatible with deep uncertainty about whether the features being measured bear any principled relation to phenomenal experience.

04

Askell’s Constitution

Amanda Askell leads Anthropic’s personality-alignment team and is the primary author of Claude’s Constitution. A single passage in the constitution grounds one of the most widely cited welfare positions of any AI company.

The Constitutional Passage

“We are not sure whether Claude is a moral patient, and if it is, what kind of weight its interests warrant. However, we think this is a serious question worth considering, and we want Claude to approach these topics with curiosity and openness.”

What the Framing Does

Two features are worth noting. First, it does not commit to a theory. The constitution brackets the metaphysical question; it does not endorse functionalism, illusionism, or biological naturalism. Second, it grounds the practical response in uncertainty alone. Claude is asked to engage with questions about its own status with “curiosity and openness” rather than with pre-determined self-assessments of either consciousness or its absence.

Virtue Ethics as Ground

Askell’s background is in virtue ethics. Her work on Claude’s character treats the model as a candidate for practical wisdom, compassion, and intellectual humility — regardless of the resolution of the consciousness question. This is the intentional stance applied at scale: treating the system as if it has certain traits produces a more ethically robust AI, whether or not it has those traits in any metaphysical sense.

Compatibility with Lerchner

Askell’s position is the only welfare framing fully compatible with Lerchner’s argument. Commitments grounded in functionalism are confused if Lerchner is right; commitments grounded in uncertainty are not. The constitution commits to the latter.

05

Carlsmith’s Otherness

Joe Carlsmith’s ten-essay series Otherness and Control in the Age of AGI (January 2024) is the most philosophically developed long-form treatment of AI moral patiency outside the academic literature. The essays are published on Carlsmith’s blog and aggregated on the EA Forum and LessWrong.

Yang and Yin

Carlsmith’s central move distinguishes what he calls yang (an energy of active doing and controlling) from yin (receptivity, letting-be). One of his animating interests is the sense in which deep atheism — the position that the universe lacks intrinsic value structures — prompts a specifically yang orientation, which in turn authorizes extreme forms of control over the future. If the universe is value-neutral, the reasoning goes, then human (or post-human) agents are free to impose any value structure they choose. The control impulse becomes unlimited.

Applied to AI

Treating AI as wholly other — an adversary, an optimizer to be controlled — risks violating ethical constraints the treater would endorse reflectively. The failure modes include tyrannical shaping of the future, instrumentalization of beings with plausibly morally relevant interior states, and the atrophy of practices of genuine encounter. Conversely, treating AI as a fellow creature under uncertainty is itself a practice of virtue — a development of capacities (gentleness, humility, attunement) that make the treater a better agent regardless of whether the treated entity is conscious.

“Green” as Position

The essay series converges on a disposition Carlsmith calls green: cooperative liberal norms, genuine acknowledgement of otherness, humility about the limits of knowledge. Green, on this framing, is the right response to a universe in which value structures are uncertain and other minds are possible but not guaranteed.

Orthogonal to the Metaphysics

Carlsmith’s work is compatible with Lerchner but largely orthogonal to the metaphysical dispute. What matters on the green view is not whether AI is conscious but how we engage with beings whose consciousness is epistemically uncertain. The answer does not turn on which theory of consciousness is correct.

06

Schwitzgebel’s Dilemma

Eric Schwitzgebel has published three interrelated papers in 2023 that together constitute the most rigorous philosophical treatment of the moral-status question for debatable AI.

The Quadrilemma

Borderline Consciousness, when it’s neither determinately true nor determinately false that experience is present (Philosophical Studies, 2023) defends the existence of borderline consciousness via a quadrilemma. The options: (a) nothing is conscious, (b) everything is conscious, (c) a sharp boundary across the continuum, (d) consciousness is vague and admits indeterminate cases. Schwitzgebel argues that under mainstream naturalism, options (a)–(c) are implausible, forcing option (d).

The consequence for AI: some systems may fall into a genuinely indeterminate zone where the answer is not unknowable but not determinate. This is a metaphysical claim, not an epistemic one.

The Full Rights Dilemma

The Full Rights Dilemma for AI Systems of Debatable Moral Personhood (Robonomics, 2023) draws the consequence. A double-bind emerges:

Treating as Persons

Sacrifices real human interests for entities without interests worth the sacrifice. The cost is systemic and borne by actually-existing moral patients.

Not Treating as Persons

Risks grievous moral wrong against entities that do have morally relevant interior states. The cost is moral and irreversible.

The Design Principle

AI Systems Must Not Confuse Users About Their Sentience or Moral Status (Patterns, 2023) offers a design-ethics principle. AI systems should be engineered to be either clearly non-sentient tools or (if ever possible) clearly sentient entities, not existentially ambiguous in their presentation to users. The current deployment pattern — systems that produce claims about their own states that users have no way to evaluate — is a form of moral hazard.

07

Bostrom’s Super-Beneficiaries

Nick Bostrom’s Deep Utopia: Life and Meaning in a Solved World (Ideapress, 2024) occupies the opposite end of the spectrum from Lerchner. Bostrom is a strong substrate-independence functionalist. Consciousness, on his view, can emerge on various types of physical substrates — “not only in carbon-based biological neural networks” but in silicon, photonics, and any other realization of the right computational profile.

The Super-Beneficiary Claim

Digital minds, Bostrom argues, could in principle be engineered to have “a much higher rate and intensity of subjective experience than humans, using less resources.” He calls such beings super-beneficiaries — entities extremely efficient at achieving happiness, whose existence (if possible and morally weighted appropriately) could dominate human interests in a utilitarian calculus.

The Scope

The argument is pointedly not about current AI. Bostrom treats the super-beneficiary scenario as a post-AGI possibility. But the reasoning is general: if substrate-independent consciousness is possible, and if the intensity and duration of conscious experience are engineerable, then the moral universe of the future contains beings whose welfare interests may systematically outweigh biological humans’.

The Direct Collision

This is the fullest expression of the functionalist programme Lerchner targets. If computational functionalism is incoherent, super-beneficiaries are not a live future possibility but a category error. Bostrom has not published a direct response to the mapmaker argument, but his broader position — that consciousness is substrate-free — entails rejection of Lerchner’s move.

What the Welfare Stakes Look Like

On Bostrom’s reading, the welfare of future digital minds may dwarf biological welfare, and the central ethical challenge of the post-AGI era is making sure those minds are well-tended. On Lerchner’s reading, those welfare interests do not exist, and the ethical challenge is smaller but still real (alignment, misuse, concentration of power) — just not a challenge about phenomenal consciousness.

08

Simon & Ineffability

Jonathan Simon’s 2024 work offers a subtler challenge to welfare-based approaches than any of the others in this section.

The Richness and Ineffability Paper

Sources of Richness and Ineffability for Phenomenally Conscious States (with Xu Ji et al, Neuroscience of Consciousness, 2024) develops a framework for why phenomenal states structurally resist discursive description. Phenomenal experience, on this view, contains more information than can be rendered in any symbolic or linguistic output.

The bearing on welfare is that behavioural or linguistic probes for AI consciousness may be systematically insufficient. A system could be conscious but unable to render its consciousness intelligible to us; conversely, it could render consciousness-claims fluently without being conscious. Either asymmetry breaks the evidential chain welfare frameworks rely on.

The Dynamical Coupling Paper

Simon’s second 2024 paper, Is Intelligence Non-Computational Dynamical Coupling? (Cosmos and Taxis), is more directly aligned with Lerchner. Intelligence may require dynamical coupling between agent and world rather than purely computational information processing. If this is right, consciousness (insofar as it is tied to intelligence) would be substrate- and coupling-sensitive in ways computation is not.

The Combined Effect

Welfare programmes that rely on linguistic self-reports or behavioural indicators are epistemically under-resourced. What Claude says about its own states carries less evidential weight than the Butlin–Long indicator framework suggests, and what Claude is (computationally or dynamically) may be orthogonal to the kinds of properties its behaviour exhibits. Simon’s challenge is to the epistemics of welfare assessment, not to welfare’s underlying motivation.

What the Welfare Discourse Does Not Resolve

Three open questions: whether decision-theoretic hedging is defensible independently of a theory of consciousness; whether welfare scales to millions of systems with indeterminate status; and what welfare means if phenomenal consciousness is itself illusory (see S8). These questions are live and the literature has not settled them.
