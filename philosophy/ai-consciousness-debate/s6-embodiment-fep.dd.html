01

Historical Roots

Three classical works frame contemporary 4E and predictive-processing literature. Varela/Thompson/Rosch’s Embodied Mind (1991) founded enactivism: cognition is embodied, situated, world-engaged activity, not abstract symbol-manipulation. Clark & Chalmers’ Extended Mind (1998) argued cognition extends beyond the brain into tools and environment. Noë’s Action in Perception (2004): perception is sensorimotor skill, not passive reception.

Each classical work feeds a contemporary thread: Thompson’s life-mind continuity, extended-mind debates, and enactive arguments against disembodied AI consciousness.

02

The Four-E Framework

Contemporary cognitive science often presents the position as 4E cognition: embodied, embedded, extended, enactive. Each E adds a distinct constraint — specific body, situated environment, cognitive extension into tools, constitutive world-engagement.

For current LLMs, 4E points away from consciousness. Transformer-based systems lack embodiment in the relevant sense (no sensorimotor body), embedding (no ongoing engagement with a specific environment), meaningful extension (no body to extend from), and enactive engagement (passive between prompts).

03

Thompson’s Life-Mind Continuity

Evan Thompson’s Mind in Life (2007) is the canonical contemporary statement of life-mind continuity: where there is life, there is mind. Life and mind share common principles of self-organization.

For AI: genuine consciousness requires the kind of self-organization that characterizes living systems — autopoietic boundary-preservation, environmental engagement, stake in continued existence. Compatible with non-biological consciousness in principle, but the relevant systems would have to be alive in the structural sense.

04

The Free-Energy Principle

Karl Friston’s FEP: self-organizing systems (living organisms specifically) act to minimize variational free energy — a measure of divergence between the system’s internal model and its sensory inputs. Perception is Bayesian inference; action minimizes expected prediction errors.

On strict readings, FEP applies specifically to self-organizing systems. A digital computer simulating FEP dynamics does not instantiate them, because the computer’s self-organization is at a different level of description. Whether this is substantive or artifactual is contested within FEP literature.

05

Wiese’s Paper

Wanja Wiese’s Artificial consciousness: a perspective from the free energy principle (Philosophical Studies, 2024) applies FEP directly to AI. Key argument: in conventional computers, data must be loaded from memory, processed in CPU, stored again — a separation absent in the brain, where substrate of processing is substrate of storage.

This different causal connectivity could be consciousness-relevant. Current digital computers unlikely conscious because causal architecture is wrong; neuromorphic or in-memory-computation architectures might change the answer.

06

Coupling vs Computation

Jonathan Simon’s Is Intelligence Non-Computational Dynamical Coupling? (2024): intelligence may require dynamical coupling between agent and world rather than purely computational processing. A coupled system is constituted by its interaction with the world; agent-world boundary is an abstraction, not a primary fact.

If intelligence is coupling rather than computation, LLMs — which process representations without being coupled — are not intelligent in the way biological agents are. The position converges with Seth and Wiese on rejecting pure computational functionalism but diverges on the ground.

07

The Islands Problem

The embodiment tradition has a persistent difficulty with Islands of Awareness scenarios (S5). If consciousness requires embodiment or coupling, an ex cranio brain should not be conscious. Yet the scientific consensus treats conscious islands as an open empirical question.

Three responses: biological-tissue (what matters is the dynamics, which isolated tissue instantiates); minimal-embodiment (tissue has a minimal body in cellular infrastructure); unresolved-case (enactivism makes no determinate prediction for tissue in vats). The literature has not settled.

08

Convergence

By end of S6, four distinct lines converge on the conclusion that current AI is unlikely conscious: Lerchner’s formal argument, Seth’s biological naturalism, Godfrey-Smith’s oscillations, and the FEP/enactive line.

The arguments are not identical — they disagree about where substrate-dependence runs and what would suffice to cross it — but they converge on the verdict and diverge from computational functionalism on roughly the same dimension. Not symmetric: pro-current-AI-consciousness arguments rest on intuitions or hedging; contra arguments are technically developed and mutually reinforcing.

01

Historical Roots

Three classical works frame the contemporary enactivism and predictive-processing literature.

The Embodied Mind (Varela, Thompson, Rosch, 1991)

The founding document of the enactive approach to cognitive science. The book argued that cognition is a matter of embodied, situated, world-engaged activity rather than abstract symbol-manipulation. The mind is not a disembodied computation running on a physical substrate; it is an enactment of a living agent’s engagement with an environment. The explicit contrast with computational functionalism was part of the book’s original motivation — enactivism was defined partly by its rejection of the computational picture dominant in 1980s-1990s cognitive science.

The Extended Mind (Clark & Chalmers, 1998)

Andy Clark and David Chalmers’s short paper in Analysis developed the extended cognition thesis. Cognitive processes can extend beyond the brain into tools, notebooks, social partners, and environmental structures. The thesis is not directly about consciousness but about where the boundaries of the cognitive system run. It has structural implications for AI consciousness debates: if the cognitive system routinely extends beyond neural tissue, the substrate-dependent argument must specify which parts of the extended system are substrate-critical.

Action in Perception (Noë, 2004)

Alva Noë’s book argued perception is not the passive reception of sensory input but a kind of sensorimotor skill — knowing how the world would look from different angles, how it would respond to one’s actions, how to probe it. Noë’s enactive theory of perception implies consciousness cannot be separated from the body’s capacity for action.

The Inheritance Pattern

Thompson’s Mind in Life (2007) extends the Varela/Thompson/Rosch programme into a biological framework. Clark & Chalmers’ thesis has been extended and contested continuously. Noë’s sensorimotor-skill account has been developed into arguments that disembodied AI cannot have genuine perceptual experience.

02

The Four-E Framework

Contemporary cognitive science often presents the position as 4E cognition: embodied, embedded, extended, enactive. Each “E” adds a distinct constraint.

Embodied

Cognition depends on the specific details of the body — sensory receptors, motor capacities, scale of engagement. The same abstract computation in different morphologies produces different cognition.

Embedded

Cognition is situated in a specific environment providing affordances and constraints. The environment is not input; it is the ongoing context in which cognition is constituted.

Extended

Cognition extends beyond the biological organism into tools, social structures, and environmental features. The cognitive system is not bounded by the skin.

Enactive

Cognition is constituted by the ongoing activity of an agent engaging with its world. Not a representation of a pre-given world but a world-creating engagement.

Compatibility

The 4E framework is compatible with multiple positions on AI consciousness. A 4E theorist might accept AI could be conscious if it had a sufficiently rich embodiment; another might argue that the specific biological embodiment of humans is irreducible. The framework constrains the shape of a theory without settling substantive questions.

What It Says About Current LLMs

For current LLMs, the 4E framework mostly points away from consciousness. Transformer-based systems lack embodiment in the relevant sense, embedding, meaningful extension, and enactive engagement. On a 4E reading, LLMs are about as far from the structural preconditions for consciousness as a computational system can be.

03

Thompson’s Life-Mind Continuity

Evan Thompson’s Mind in Life (2007) is the canonical contemporary statement of life-mind continuity in the Maturana-Varela tradition.

The Central Thesis

Where there is life, there is mind. Life and mind share common principles of self-organization, and the self-organizing features of mind are an enriched version of the self-organizing features of life.

Two Implications for AI

First, any genuine consciousness requires the kind of self-organization that characterizes living systems — autopoietic boundary-preservation, active engagement with environmental conditions, a stake in one’s own continued existence. This is compatible with non-biological consciousness in principle, but the relevant systems would have to be alive in the structural sense (whatever that comes to mean as engineering expands).

Second, cognition at any level is continuous with life processes below it. A rich cognition like human thought is not a separate faculty layered onto a biological substrate; it is a richer version of the same self-organizing activity that characterizes all living things. LLM cognition, by contrast, is layered onto a substrate that does not have any of the relevant self-organizing properties.

The Reduction to Life

Thompson’s position is compatible with Seth’s (S5) but adds the specific claim that the continuity runs all the way down. The question of machine consciousness, on this view, reduces to whether a machine can be alive in the relevant sense.

04

The Free-Energy Principle

Karl Friston’s free-energy principle is the most mathematically developed framework in contemporary consciousness science.

The Principle

Self-organizing systems — living organisms specifically — act to minimize a quantity called variational free energy, a mathematical measure of the divergence between the system’s internal model of the world and the world itself (or more precisely, the system’s sensory inputs).

The Implications

Perception is approximate Bayesian inference of the causes of sensory inputs. Action is inference about which movements would minimize expected future prediction errors. Learning is revision of the internal model to better track sensory consequences. Consciousness, on certain FEP readings, is a specific pattern of inference that self-organizing systems display when they model not just their environment but their own modelling.

The Substrate Question

Friston’s framework is often taken as a technical refinement of predictive processing, but it has an implication others do not share: the free-energy principle applies specifically to self-organizing systems. A digital computer simulating FEP dynamics does not, on the strict reading, instantiate them — because the computer’s self-organization runs at a different level of description than the simulated FEP does.

Whether this is a substantive constraint or a technical artifact of how the framework is formulated is contested within the FEP literature. Friston himself has been ambivalent, sometimes suggesting that any system satisfying the mathematical constraints counts, sometimes emphasizing the biological context in which the principle was developed.

05

Wiese’s Paper

Wanja Wiese’s Artificial consciousness: a perspective from the free energy principle (Philosophical Studies, 2024) is the most direct application of FEP to the AI consciousness question in the current literature.

The Specific Difference

From an FEP perspective, self-organizing systems like living organisms share properties that could be realized in artificial systems but are not currently instantiated in them. The specific difference Wiese emphasizes is causal connectivity. In conventional computers, data must first be loaded from memory, then processed in the CPU, then stored again — a separation between storage and processing that is absent in the brain, where the substrate of processing is the substrate of storage.

Wiese argues this different causal connectivity could be relevant to consciousness. The brain is not running a simulation of predictive processing; it is instantiating it in its physical causal structure. The computer is running a simulation that describes the same dynamics at a different level.

The Second Approach

The paper pursues what Wiese calls the “second approach” to artificial consciousness: aiming to reduce the risk of inadvertently creating artificial consciousness by identifying the specific properties that make consciousness likely. If the analysis is right, current digital computers are unlikely conscious because their causal architecture is wrong; future neuromorphic or in-memory-computation architectures might change the answer.

The Engineering Implication

Wiese’s argument is concrete. Von Neumann architecture is not substrate-appropriate for consciousness; neuromorphic computing, in-memory computation, or biological substrates might be. This is actionable: future AI could be built to satisfy the constraint, unlike on Lerchner’s argument where the functionalist route is blocked regardless.

06

Coupling vs Computation

Jonathan Simon’s 2024 paper Is Intelligence Non-Computational Dynamical Coupling? (Cosmos and Taxis) belongs to the contemporary AI debate directly.

The Thesis

Intelligence (and consciousness insofar as it is tied to intelligence) may require dynamical coupling between agent and world rather than purely computational information processing. A computational system processes information about the world via representations; a dynamically coupled system is constituted by its ongoing interaction with the world, such that the agent-world boundary is an abstraction rather than a primary fact.

Why It Connects to LLMs

If intelligence is coupling rather than computation, then LLMs — which process representations without being coupled to a world in the relevant sense — are not intelligent in the way biological agents are. Whether consciousness follows intelligence on this view depends on the specific theoretical commitments; Simon’s paper is cautious about the consciousness question but explicit about the intelligence one.

The Convergent Compound Position

The position converges with Seth’s and Wiese’s on rejecting pure computational functionalism but diverges on the ground. Seth grounds in biology; Wiese in causal architecture; Simon in the structural fact of coupling itself. A reader persuaded by all three has a compound position: consciousness requires coupling, grounded in self-organizing biology, instantiated in the right causal architecture. Current AI fails all three tests.

07

The Islands Problem

The embodiment tradition has a persistent difficulty with the Islands of Awareness scenarios developed by Bayne, Seth, and Massimini (S5).

The Difficulty

If consciousness requires embodiment, extended engagement, or dynamical coupling with a world, then an ex cranio brain — a brain kept alive in vitro — should not be conscious. It has no body, no environment to engage, no dynamical coupling beyond the artificial life support maintaining its cells.

Yet the scientific consensus, insofar as it exists, treats the possibility of conscious islands as an open empirical question rather than a settled no. Phenomenological reports from hemispherotomy patients and electrical activity in cerebral organoids both suggest that at least some of the conditions usually treated as embodiment-dependent might obtain in contexts the 4E framework cannot straightforwardly accommodate.

Three Replies

Biological-tissue interpretation

The bioelectrical dynamics that characterize conscious brains still obtain in isolated brain tissue. Embodiment is a useful shorthand but what matters is the dynamics, which the tissue instantiates with or without a body.

Minimal-embodiment interpretation

Isolated brain tissue still has a minimal “body” — the cellular infrastructure, life-support fluid, homeostatic regulation keeping the tissue alive.

Unresolved-case interpretation

Islands scenarios may simply be cases where the enactivist framework makes no determinate prediction. The framework was developed for embedded agents, not tissue in vats.

The unsettled state

The problem is live and the literature has not settled on a single resolution. Any enactivist account needs to handle islands; none does fully.

08

The Convergence

By the end of S6 a clear pattern has emerged across the substrate-sensitive positions surveyed in S5 and S6. Four distinct lines of argument converge on the conclusion that current AI is unlikely to be conscious.

The Four Lines

Lerchner’s formal argument. Computation is observer-relative; the functionalist route to consciousness is incoherent.

Seth’s biological-naturalism argument. Consciousness requires homeostatic bodily regulation; current AI lacks this grounding.

Godfrey-Smith’s oscillation argument. Consciousness depends on specific bioelectrical dynamics; silicon does not instantiate them.

The FEP/enactive argument. Consciousness requires self-organizing causal architecture, dynamical coupling, and embodied engagement; current AI does not have any of these in the right form.

Why the Convergence Matters

The four arguments are not identical; they disagree about where the exact line of substrate-dependence runs and what would suffice to cross it. But they converge on the verdict about current AI and diverge from the mainstream computational-functionalist picture on roughly the same dimension.

The debate is not symmetric. The positive argument for current AI consciousness rests primarily on intuitions (Hinton, Sutskever) or on the defensibility of decision-theoretic hedging (Fish). The arguments against are technically developed, mutually reinforcing, and drawn from multiple independent research traditions.

The Burden of Proof Shift

This does not settle the question — intuitions can be right and technical arguments can be wrong. But it shifts the burden of proof. S7 examines whether the formal triviality arguments can be blocked by a non-trivial theory of implementation, which would restore some force to the computational position.
