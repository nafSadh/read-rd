AI Consciousness Debate

Counter-arguments and parallels to Lerchner’s Abstraction Fallacy — mapping the live debate over machine consciousness across eight dimensions of post-2020 literature, from AI-lab practitioners to biological naturalism to the contemporary triviality thread.

In March 2026, Alexander Lerchner’s The Abstraction Fallacy (Google DeepMind) argued that computational functionalism rests on a circularity: computation is not intrinsic to physics but requires a “mapmaker” — an observer who interprets physical states as computational. The paper landed into an already-active debate. Since 2020, a wave of philosophers, cognitive scientists, and AI-lab researchers has been arguing over whether large language models and future AGI systems could be conscious, whether substrate matters, and whether contemporary AI research should treat machine welfare as a live concern.

This survey maps that debate across 8 dimensions, drawing exclusively on post-2020 primary sources. It covers counter-arguments against Lerchner (defenses of functionalism, the Butlin–Long indicator framework, Chalmers on LLMs) and arguments structurally similar to Lerchner’s (biological naturalism, pancomputationalism critiques, embodied cognition, IIT’s substrate-sensitivity). The classical 1970s–90s arguments (Searle, Putnam, Block, Harnad) are referenced as ancestors but are not surveyed in their own right.
