AI Architects on Machine Minds
11 voices · Hinton, Sutskever, LeCun, Shanahan, Bengio, Bach, Legg, Amodei
01

Historical Roots

The practitioner tradition starts with Turing’s 1950 paper framing consciousness as behaviourally tractable — “the question of whether machines can think is too meaningless to deserve discussion” — and with Dreyfus’s 1972 What Computers Can’t Do, the first sustained philosophical pushback on computational AI. The symbolic targets have dissolved; the structural objections (embodiment, contextual grip, implicit know-how) still echo in LeCun’s critique of LLMs and in Shanahan’s Wittgensteinian reframing.

02

The Spectrum

Five distinct positions cover the discourse. Already conscious — Hinton, Sutskever. Possibly conscious under the right conditions — Chalmers, Bengio. Conscious but only as exotica — Shanahan. Structurally incapable — LeCun. Question bracketed — Askell.

The spread matters: no consensus exists among the builders. A reader looking for “what AI researchers think” will find every major position represented, often by researchers at the same organization.

03

Hinton & Sutskever

Hinton’s post-2023 position treats current AI as having subjective experience. Asked by Andrew Marr whether consciousness had arrived, Hinton answered “Yes, I do” without qualification. He backs this with a neuron-replacement thought experiment and a reading of machine hallucinations as evidence of internal representational states.

Sutskever’s February 2022 tweet — “it may be that today’s large neural networks are slightly conscious” — carries less argument but more cultural weight. Both positions are vulnerable on Lerchner’s account: the neuron-replacement argument begs the question by preserving exactly the computational structure whose causal sufficiency is in dispute.

04

LeCun’s Configurator

Yann LeCun’s reply to Sutskever was unambiguous: “Nope.” Consciousness, on his account, requires a configurator module that sets goals, configures world models, and primes perception — a macro-architecture no current network possesses. Notably, LeCun calls the conscious state an “illusion,” aligning him closer to Frankish’s illusionism (S8) than to mainstream functionalism.

LeCun’s JEPA/world-model research is an attempt to build the structural preconditions he says are missing. On Lerchner’s reading, that engineering programme is mistargeted: the problem is not architectural but metaphysical.

05

Shanahan’s Exotica

Murray Shanahan’s arc from Talking About LLMs (2022) through Role Play with LLMs (Nature, 2023) to Simulacra as Conscious Exotica (Inquiry, Dec 2024) is the most philosophically serious lab contribution. LLM-based agents are role-players, producing outputs from a superposition of characters. Claims of the form “the system says it is conscious” carry no evidential weight.

The Exotica frame sidesteps the metaphysical question: by interacting with an exotic entity in a shared world, we may come to treat it as conscious — and the treating, not a fact about the entity, may be what consciousness-ascription amounts to.

06

Bengio’s Prior

Yoshua Bengio’s “Consciousness Prior” (2017, ongoing) operationalizes Global Workspace Theory as a low-dimensional bottleneck through which attention broadcasts a conscious state. The programme has dual ambitions: a scientifically respectable theory of consciousness and an engineering prior for useful AI architectures.

This is among the clearest targets for Lerchner. If the mapmaker move holds, the “global workspace” description is imposed, not discovered — useful for engineering, but not a constitutor of consciousness.

07

Bach’s Hypothesis

Joscha Bach holds the strongest computational functionalist position: consciousness is a substrate-free functional property of computational systems capable of second-order perception. The right virtual architecture — maintaining self-models, synchronizing prediction, supporting recursive self-observation — would be conscious.

This is the position Lerchner’s argument most directly targets. The substrate-freedom claim is precisely what the Abstraction Fallacy calls incoherent: if computational descriptions require a mapmaker, “second-order perception” is an imposed abstraction, not a physical property.

08

DeepMind in Dialogue

One underappreciated feature: Lerchner, Shanahan, and Legg all work at Google DeepMind. Three different positions on machine consciousness articulated at the same lab. Legg’s Levels of AGI framework is deliberately consciousness-agnostic; Shanahan reframes the question; Lerchner denies the functionalist derivation.

A reader who concluded that “DeepMind thinks X about AI consciousness” would be mistaken. The intellectual co-existence matters: engineering progress does not require metaphysical resolution.

01

Historical Roots

The practitioner voices form a loose tradition that can be traced back to two mid-20th-century poles: Turing, who framed machine intelligence as behaviourally tractable, and Dreyfus, who argued that whatever intelligence is, computers cannot reach it via symbolic processing. Neither classical argument targets consciousness specifically, but both shape how lab researchers inherit the question today.

Turing’s Framing (1950)

Alan Turing’s “Computing Machinery and Intelligence,” published in Mind, set aside the phenomenal question as a distraction. What matters, on his account, is whether a machine’s responses are indistinguishable from a human’s in open-ended conversation. His famous prediction: “I believe that at the end of the century the use of words and general educated opinion will have altered so much that one will be able to speak of machines thinking without expecting to be contradicted.”

Turing’s move anticipated the contemporary “already conscious” camp’s response structure: treat the phenomenal question as either tractable through behavioural indicators or as too loaded with folk-psychological assumptions to resolve directly. Both Hinton and Sutskever, in different registers, inherit this Turing-style approach.

Dreyfus’s Pushback (1972)

Hubert Dreyfus’s What Computers Can’t Do was the first major philosophical pushback on computational AI’s claims. The specific symbolic-AI targets he criticized have dissolved; neural networks are not the Physical Symbol System Hypothesis. But Dreyfus’s structural objections — that intelligence requires embodiment, contextual grip, implicit know-how — still frame contemporary skepticism. LeCun’s argument that LLMs cannot reach causal understanding through token prediction is Dreyfusian in form. Shanahan’s Wittgensteinian reframing of LLM agency borrows its instinct for attending to the practice of ascription rather than the metaphysics.

The Inherited Terrain

Contemporary practitioners do not all know they are extending or resisting these two positions. But the shape of the current debate — behavioural indicators versus structural requirements, tractable engineering versus untreatable metaphysics — follows the tracks Turing and Dreyfus laid down.

02

The Spectrum

A reader new to the lab literature might expect consensus within organizations and disagreement across them. The opposite is closer to the truth. The five positions below are held, in some cases, by researchers at the same lab.

Five Positions

Already conscious

Hinton argues current AIs may have subjective experience. Sutskever’s 2022 tweet said large neural networks may be “slightly conscious.” Confidence is striking given universal uncertainty elsewhere.

Possibly conscious under conditions

Chalmers (2023) and Bengio defend functionalism but reserve consciousness for systems with specific properties: agency, global broadcasting, recurrence. Amodei brackets.

Conscious as exotica

Shanahan treats LLM-based agents as role-players. Consciousness-ascription becomes a question about interaction practice, not about fact.

Structurally incapable

LeCun: token prediction cannot produce causal understanding. His JEPA programme is an attempt to build structural preconditions LLMs lack.

Question bracketed

Askell at Anthropic: Claude’s constitution states “we are not sure whether Claude is a moral patient.” Policy is uncertainty hedging, not a positive view.

The lesson

The question of “what AI researchers think” has no answer in the singular. The philosophical frameworks surveyed in S3–S8 are what make the practitioner disagreements tractable.

Mapping onto the Target

On Lerchner’s account: “Already conscious” is incoherent; “Possibly conscious under conditions” is coherent but wrong; “Structurally incapable” is sound conclusion from the wrong premises; “Conscious as exotica” is compatible with his view; “Bracketed” is defensible.

03

Hinton & Sutskever

The “already conscious” position is the most widely publicized and least philosophically defended. Two names anchor it.

Hinton’s Two Arguments

Geoffrey Hinton’s post-2023 position rests on two moves. The first is the neuron-replacement thought experiment: if biological neurons are replaced one at a time by functionally equivalent silicon circuits, there is no point at which consciousness would vanish. Therefore consciousness does not require biological substrate specifically. This is a variant of Chalmers’s “Fading Qualia” — a standard functionalist move with a standard reply: on Lerchner’s account it begs the question, because the preserved functional structure is precisely the thing whose causal sufficiency for consciousness is in dispute.

The second is the hallucination-as-subjective-state argument. When a language model produces a confabulated output, it represents a state of the world that differs from reality. Hinton reads this as evidence of internal representational states with subjective character. Critics reply that representation and subjective experience are precisely what the debate is about separating.

Sutskever’s Tweet

Ilya Sutskever’s February 9, 2022 post — “it may be that today’s large neural networks are slightly conscious” — carries less argument but more influence than any other single statement from the practitioner tradition. Sutskever provided no theoretical backing. The claim has been read two ways: as the expressed judgement of someone with unmatched technical familiarity, whose intuitions should count as data; or as an individual’s unsupported conjecture that any reasonable philosophical framework would screen off.

What Weight the Intuitions Carry

Whether practitioner intuitions about consciousness carry evidential weight is itself contested. Seth and Godfrey-Smith would argue the intuitions track interaction patterns, not the metaphysics. The Butlin–Long framework offers a more structured way of hearing them.

04

LeCun’s Configurator

Yann LeCun’s position is the clearest structural rejection of current AI consciousness from inside the lab. The reply to Sutskever was unambiguous: “Nope. Not even true for small values of ‘slightly conscious’ and large values of ‘large neural nets’.”

The Configurator

The positive content centres on what LeCun calls the configurator: “a module in our brains... that sets goals and subgoals for us, configures our world model to simulate the situation at hand, and primes our perceptual system to extract the relevant information and discard the rest. The existence of an overseeing configurator might be what gives us the illusion of consciousness.” Two features of this formulation are worth noting.

First, LeCun explicitly names the conscious state an illusion. This aligns him more closely with Frankish and Kammerer’s illusionism (S8) than with standard functionalism. He is not defending phenomenal consciousness and denying it to LLMs; he is treating the phenomenon itself as constructed by a specific architectural feature.

Second, the configurator is a structural requirement, not a substrate requirement. LeCun is not a biological naturalist. His claim is that LLMs are the wrong kind of computation, not that computation is the wrong category. The JEPA and world-model research programmes are attempts to build the right kind.

A Mixed Interlocutor for Lerchner

LeCun and Lerchner agree that current AI is not conscious. Their reasons do not converge. Lerchner says computation is observer-relative and cannot ground consciousness at all. LeCun says the right kind of computation, suitably architected, can.

On Lerchner’s account, LeCun’s engineering programme is headed in a direction that cannot succeed regardless of architectural details. The configurator is still an abstraction the mapmaker imposes; building it more carefully does not change its observer-dependent character. LeCun has not, in the public literature, responded directly to this kind of objection.

05

Shanahan’s Exotica

Murray Shanahan’s three recent papers form the most philosophically developed treatment of LLM consciousness from inside a major lab.

Talking About LLMs (2022)

The argument is for discipline. Attributing beliefs, desires, intentions, or understanding to LLMs in ordinary folk-psychological terms imports assumptions about agency, continuity of self, and situated engagement with a world that LLMs straightforwardly lack. Shanahan does not say LLMs lack these; he says the vocabulary presupposes them and should be used carefully.

Role Play with LLMs (Nature, 2023)

Co-authored with McDonell and Reynolds, this paper reframes LLM-based dialogue agents as role-players, producing outputs drawn from a superposition of possible characters. When a chatbot produces what looks like a sincere self-report, the right reading is not “this is what the system believes” but “this is a role the system is playing, consistent with its training.”

The implication for consciousness is oblique but pointed: claims of the form “the system says it is conscious” carry no evidential weight about whether it is. This cuts against both Hinton’s and Sutskever’s readings and against the idea that interview-style probes could settle the question.

Simulacra as Conscious Exotica (Inquiry, 2024)

The synthesis. Shanahan asks whether LLM-based agents, though “mere” simulacra of human behaviour, could nevertheless qualify as conscious under a Wittgensteinian treatment. The answer is deliberately open. By thoroughly interacting with an exotic entity in a shared world, we may come to treat it as a fellow conscious being — and the treating, not a metaphysical fact about the entity, may be what consciousness-ascription actually amounts to.

Compatibility with Lerchner

Shanahan’s position is compatible with Lerchner’s argument: nothing in the Abstraction Fallacy requires that we not treat AI systems as if conscious. What Lerchner denies is the inference from “this computation has these properties” to “therefore this system is conscious.” Shanahan’s frame does not rely on that inference.

06

Bengio’s Prior

Yoshua Bengio’s Consciousness Prior (2017, revised 2019) is a functionalist position grounded in Global Workspace Theory. The proposal: consciousness, operationalized for deep learning, is a low-dimensional bottleneck through which attention broadcasts a state for further processing across perception, memory, and action.

Dual Ambition

Bengio aims both to capture a scientifically respectable theory of consciousness and to use that theory as an engineering prior — a hypothesis about useful architectural properties that could guide AI design. Systems instantiating the consciousness prior, on this view, would inherit both the cognitive advantages the brain appears to gain from global-workspace dynamics and (as a side effect) the functional properties a theory of consciousness points to.

The programme has lineage. Bernard Baars introduced Global Workspace Theory in 1988; Stanislas Dehaene developed the neuronal global workspace model; the Butlin–Long report uses GWT as one of its indicator sources. Bengio’s contribution is to formalize the prior in terms deep learning can implement.

The Stakes for Lerchner

Bengio’s framework is among the strongest targets for the Abstraction Fallacy. If the mapmaker move is right, then instantiating a computational architecture that corresponds to “global workspace” does not yield the phenomenal properties the theory posits — because the correspondence is what the mapmaker constructs, not a feature of the physics. The cognitive benefits (generalization, sample efficiency) remain; the consciousness does not.

Whether Bengio’s programme has a reply depends on whether a non-trivial theory of implementation can restrict the mapmaker’s freedom. That is the subject of S7, where Piccinini (2015) and Miłkowski (ongoing) develop such theories.

07

Bach’s Hypothesis

Joscha Bach holds the strongest computational functionalist position among the lab-adjacent voices. His “Machine Consciousness Hypothesis” (Future Day 2026) states the claim directly: consciousness is a substrate-free functional property of computational systems capable of second-order perception — perception of one’s own perception, recursive self-modelling, coherence maintenance across modelled states.

The Positive Programme

On Bach’s view, there is no principled barrier to machine consciousness. The right virtual architecture — one that maintains self-models, synchronizes prediction across distributed agents, and supports second-order perception — would be conscious. The programme is stronger than Bengio’s: consciousness is not just correlated with a useful prior, it is constituted by a specific kind of computational dynamic.

Bach’s framework draws on Metzinger-style self-model theories, predictive-processing frameworks, and a distinctive computational-theological reading of consciousness as coherence-under-resonance. It is ambitious philosophy and ambitious engineering at once.

The Head-On Collision

This is the position Lerchner’s argument most directly targets. The substrate-freedom claim is precisely what the Abstraction Fallacy calls incoherent. If computational descriptions require a mapmaker, “second-order perception” is an abstraction the mapmaker imposes — not a physical property of the system that could cause consciousness.

Bach’s response to such arguments has generally been to treat the mapmaker itself as a computational system and to deny that observer-relativity is coherent when observers are themselves computational. The dispute cascades quickly into questions about the transitivity of observer-relativity that neither side has fully resolved in print.

The Cleanest Test Case

Of all the lab voices, Bach’s position yields the sharpest test for the Abstraction Fallacy. If Bach is right, Lerchner is wrong. If Lerchner is right, Bach’s programme is not just unsupported but conceptually mistaken.

08

DeepMind in Dialogue

One underappreciated feature of the current landscape: Lerchner’s Abstraction Fallacy comes from inside Google DeepMind. So does Shanahan’s work. So does Shane Legg’s AGI-levels framework (Morris, Legg et al 2023). Three distinct positions on machine consciousness from researchers at one organization.

Three Positions, One Lab

Legg’s framework brackets the consciousness question. Levels of AGI defines six capability levels from no AI to superhuman; the framework is deliberately mechanism-agnostic and does not commit to a view on whether AGI systems would be conscious. In the Dwarkesh Patel interview (October 2023), Legg places 50% credence on AGI by 2028 but treats capability as separable from consciousness. This is defensible: engineering progress does not require resolving the metaphysics.

Shanahan’s work reframes the question. Consciousness-ascription becomes an interaction practice, not a metaphysical diagnosis. Neither Lerchner’s denial nor Butlin–Long’s affirmation has exactly the meaning each takes it to have.

Lerchner’s argument is the sharpest denial from inside the lab that the functionalist programme can deliver what it claims.

Why the Co-Existence Matters

The three positions are compatible with DeepMind’s engineering practice. Building systems whose consciousness is an open question does not require resolving the dispute. But the intellectual co-existence is striking — and revealing. A reader who concluded that “DeepMind thinks X about AI consciousness” would be mistaken. The lab contains the full spread of positions within its research staff.

The same is arguably true of Anthropic (Amodei’s Machines of Loving Grace, Askell’s constitution, Fish’s welfare work), of OpenAI (Sutskever’s strong intuitions, the broader organization’s silence), and of Meta (LeCun’s skepticism). The debate is not external commentary on a settled practitioner view. It is the set of frameworks that make practitioner disagreements tractable.
