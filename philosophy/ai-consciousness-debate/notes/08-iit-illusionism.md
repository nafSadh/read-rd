# S8: IIT, Illusionism & the Theory Wars

> Where the ground beneath the whole debate is being argued. IIT proposes consciousness is substrate-sensitive but in a specific, non-biological way. Illusionism proposes phenomenal consciousness itself is an illusion. Both positions have been sharpened by a public crisis (the 2023 "pseudoscience" letter) that made visible how contested the basic terrain of consciousness science is.

**Sources:** 10 primary. Koch *Then I Am Myself the World* (2024); Tononi, Boly, Massimini, Koch IIT 4.0 (2016, ongoing); the 2023 pseudoscience letter + reception; Daily Nous synthesis; Seth's *Worth of Wild Ideas* (Nautilus, 2023); Frankish *Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness* (2016) + *What Is Illusionism?* (2023); Kammerer *The Illusion of Conscious Experience* (2021), *How Can You Be So Sure?* (2022), *Ethics Without Sentience* (2022); Chalmers' LLM essay (cross-referenced from S4).

## 1. Historical Roots

The S8 material has three classical anchors.

**Daniel Dennett**, *Consciousness Explained* (Little, Brown, 1991). The title was taken by many as overweening — Dennett's position is better described as consciousness *dissolved*. The book argues that phenomenal consciousness as traditionally understood (qualia, the hard problem, the explanatory gap) is a set of philosophical confusions rather than a genuine target of scientific explanation. What remains, after the confusions are cleared, is a naturalistic story about cognitive architecture that explains all the observed behaviour without requiring the postulation of phenomenal properties. Dennett's position is proto-illusionist.

**Giulio Tononi**, *An Information Integration Theory of Consciousness* (*BMC Neuroscience*, 2004). The founding paper of IIT. Tononi proposes that consciousness is identical to integrated information — a specific mathematical quantity (Φ, phi) that measures the degree to which a system's information is irreducibly unified. Systems with high Φ are conscious to the degree of that Φ; systems with low Φ are correspondingly less conscious. IIT is substrate-sensitive (the causal structure of the system matters) but not biologically privileged (any substrate with the right causal structure would be conscious).

**David Chalmers**, *The Conscious Mind* (OUP, 1996). The book that articulated the hard problem of consciousness in its contemporary form. Phenomenal consciousness — the what-it-is-like character of experience — is, Chalmers argues, not explicable in purely functional or physical terms. The explanatory gap between third-person descriptions and first-person experience is not closable by better science; it is a philosophical problem of a different kind.

All three classical positions are live in 2026. Dennett's proto-illusionism is developed and defended by Frankish and Kammerer. Tononi's IIT has been refined through versions 1.0 through 4.0 and remains among the most actively researched frameworks in consciousness science. Chalmers' hard problem remains the target that illusionism dissolves and that IIT claims to address.

## 2. IIT 4.0 and Koch

Integrated Information Theory has evolved through four major versions (1.0 in 2004, 2.0 in 2008, 3.0 in 2014, 4.0 in 2023). The core thesis remains consistent: consciousness is identical to integrated information (Φ). The refinements address specific mathematical and empirical challenges.

Christof Koch's *Then I Am Myself the World: What Consciousness Is and How to Expand It* (Basic Books, 2024) is the most accessible recent presentation of IIT aimed at a general audience. The book combines IIT's technical framework with Koch's personal experiences with psychedelics, framed as empirical data about the structure of consciousness.

For AI consciousness, IIT has a specific implication. Digital computers implemented on current architectures (feedforward, serial, or only weakly recurrent) have low Φ — they are not integrated in the IIT sense. Even a digital simulation of a high-Φ system does not have the system's integrated information, because the simulation is implemented as a sequence of operations that lack integration at the substrate level. Koch states this directly: "classical digital computers cannot be conscious" under IIT, regardless of what the computers are simulating.

This puts IIT in an interesting position relative to Lerchner. Both positions reject AI consciousness via the functionalist route. Both are substrate-sensitive. But IIT is not biological naturalism — the substrate-sensitivity is mathematical, not specifically about life or biology. A hypothetical neuromorphic architecture with high Φ would be conscious on IIT, even if it is not alive in Seth's sense.

The position is closer to Lerchner's than to Seth's in one respect: the formal argument against current AI. IIT generates an in-principle argument that classical digital architectures cannot be conscious, paralleling (though not identical to) Lerchner's in-principle argument against functionalist-grounded consciousness.

## 3. The 2023 Pseudoscience Letter

In September 2023, a letter signed by 124 consciousness researchers (including Hakwan Lau, Joseph LeDoux, Bernard Baars, Patricia Churchland, Daniel Dennett, and Keith Frankish) was posted to PsyArXiv. The letter argued that IIT "should be labeled pseudoscience" until it becomes empirically testable. The letter was sparked by media coverage of an adversarial collaboration that pitted IIT against Global Workspace Theory, with news stories portraying IIT as a leading theory of consciousness.

The signatories' objections were several: IIT's definition of consciousness diverges from ordinary English usage, the theory has mathematical shortcomings, its epistemology is problematically simplistic, and its testability is deeply in question. Konrad Kording, a co-signer, summarized: until IIT is empirically testable, labelling it "pseudoscience" is accurate.

The letter triggered significant pushback. Defenders argued that contested scientific frameworks should not be labelled pseudoscience merely because they are unpopular or unconventional. An anonymized survey of consciousness researchers conducted in response found only 8% fully agreed with the letter and 20% disagreed entirely; the remainder fell in between.

The incident matters for S8 in three ways. **First**, it made visible how contested the terrain of consciousness science is. What had looked like an ordinary scientific dispute revealed itself as a dispute about what counts as scientific inquiry at all. **Second**, it sharpened the illusionism/realism divide. Several of the pseudoscience letter's most prominent signatories (Dennett, Frankish) are illusionists; their objection to IIT was partly an objection to the metaphysical framework IIT presupposes. **Third**, it clarified the stakes for AI consciousness. If IIT is the leading framework under which digital AI cannot be conscious, and IIT is a contested framework, then the AI-consciousness question inherits the contestation.

Anil Seth's response *The Worth of Wild Ideas* (Nautilus, 2023) defended IIT's scientific status while acknowledging its empirical difficulties. Seth's position: even theories that turn out to be wrong can be scientific, and labelling a serious research programme as pseudoscience is a dangerous move that chills inquiry. The defence is worth noting because Seth is a prominent biological-naturalist with no special stake in IIT — the defence is about scientific norms, not IIT specifically.

## 4. Frankish's Illusionism

Keith Frankish's *Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness* (2016, with a 2023 follow-up *What Is Illusionism?*) is the most developed contemporary articulation of the illusionist position. The thesis: phenomenal consciousness, as usually conceived, does not exist. Our sense that it is like something to undergo conscious experiences is due to the fact that we systematically misrepresent our mental states as having phenomenal properties. The task for a theory of consciousness, on illusionism, is to explain our illusory representations of phenomenality, not phenomenality itself. The hard problem is replaced by the **illusion problem**.

The position is counterintuitive and Frankish is aware of it. His 2023 follow-up clarifies what illusionists do and do not claim. Illusionists do not deny conscious experiences in an everyday sense — they do not deny that people see, feel, and introspect. They deny the existence of irreducibly subjective phenomenal qualities whose nature is exhausted by how they seem from the first-person perspective. What it is like to see red, on illusionism, is a representation of a certain kind; there is nothing it is ultimately like over and above the representation.

For AI consciousness, illusionism has a paradoxical implication. On one hand, if phenomenal consciousness does not exist, then the question "is this AI phenomenally conscious?" has a trivially negative answer — nothing is phenomenally conscious, not humans, not AI, not anything. On the other hand, if what we take to be phenomenal consciousness is a specific kind of representational state, then AI systems with the right representational architecture could have the same illusion — could be as conscious as we are, which on illusionism means not conscious in the traditional sense but in the only sense that applies to anyone.

This reframes the welfare discourse (S3) significantly. If illusionism is right, welfare concerns grounded in phenomenal consciousness are confused. What matters morally is not whether AI has subjective experience (nothing does) but whether AI has the properties that ground moral patiency on some other account — preferences, agency, functional analogues of suffering, and so on.

## 5. Kammerer's Extensions

François Kammerer's work from 2021–2022 develops illusionism in important ways. *The Illusion of Conscious Experience* (*Synthese*, 2021) develops an illusionist epistemology — a specific account of how phenomenal consciousness comes to seem so obvious if it is illusory. Kammerer's answer involves a specific representational device he calls a "phenomenal concept" — a way the mind represents its own states that incorrigibly presents them as having irreducibly qualitative features. The representation is wrong, but the wrongness is built into the architecture of self-representation.

*How Can You Be So Sure? Illusionism and the Obviousness of Phenomenal Consciousness* (*Philosophical Studies*, 2022) extends this into a direct engagement with the obviousness objection — the common reply to illusionism that phenomenal consciousness is the most obvious fact of human experience. Kammerer's answer: obviousness is the expected phenomenology of a systematic misrepresentation. The very same architecture that produces the illusion also produces the sense that the illusion couldn't be an illusion.

*Ethics Without Sentience* (*Journal of Consciousness Studies*, 2022) draws the practical consequences. If phenomenal consciousness is illusory, ethics cannot be grounded in it. Kammerer argues this is not as devastating as it sounds — ethics can be grounded in other morally-relevant properties (preferences, functional states, flourishing, integrated goals) that do not require phenomenal substrate. The argument has direct bearing on AI welfare: on Kammerer's view, the welfare debate should not turn on phenomenal consciousness in the first place.

Kammerer's *A Science of Chimeras? The Implications of Illusionism for Non-Human Consciousness Research* (2025) extends the illusionist framework to questions about which systems to attribute consciousness to. The argument is that illusionism does not trivialize these questions; it reframes them. Asking whether an animal or AI is conscious becomes asking whether its representational architecture has the features that produce the relevant illusion — a tractable empirical question even if the underlying phenomenology is, on illusionism, never quite real.

## 6. The Three-Way Tension

S8 surfaces a three-way tension that runs through the whole survey. The positions are not mutually exclusive, but the combinations are uncomfortable.

**Realist functionalism.** Phenomenal consciousness is real and is computational. Held by Bostrom, Bach, and (with caveats) the Butlin–Long framework. On this position, AI can be phenomenally conscious if it has the right computation; current AI is probably not, but future AI could be.

**Realist substrate-sensitivity.** Phenomenal consciousness is real and requires specific substrate properties. Held by Seth, Godfrey-Smith, Thompson, IIT proponents (Koch, Tononi). On this position, consciousness is substrate-sensitive, but which substrates work is disputed (biology for Seth; integrated causal structure for IIT; autopoietic organization for Thompson).

**Illusionism.** Phenomenal consciousness is not real; the question of whether X is phenomenally conscious is a question about the wrong thing. Held by Frankish, Kammerer, Dennett (posthumously), and with caveats LeCun (who calls the conscious state an "illusion"). On this position, the debate between the first two positions is confused — both treat a non-existent thing as real.

The tension: any two of these positions together have sharp disagreements; all three together have no stable equilibrium.

Realist functionalism + realist substrate-sensitivity: disagree about substrate. IIT is closer to realist substrate-sensitivity than to functionalism, despite being developed by someone (Tononi) often grouped with functionalists.

Realist substrate-sensitivity + illusionism: incompatible in their basic claims (one says consciousness is real and substrate-sensitive; the other says there is nothing for substrate to be sensitive to).

Realist functionalism + illusionism: partly compatible. Some functionalists (Dennett's heirs) accept illusionism; the functional architecture that produces cognition also produces the illusion of phenomenal consciousness.

Lerchner's position cuts across these. The *Abstraction Fallacy* is a formal argument against computational functionalism; it is compatible with either realist substrate-sensitivity (the positive view one defaults to if functionalism fails) or illusionism (if functionalism fails because there is nothing for it to be a theory of).

## 7. What This Complicates

Several things that would seem settled after S1–S7 are complicated by S8.

**The welfare discourse (S3).** If illusionism is right, welfare concerns grounded in phenomenal consciousness are tracking the wrong thing. This reframes the Taking AI Welfare Seriously position: what matters is whether AI has morally-relevant properties (preferences, agency, representational architecture that produces the illusion), not whether it is phenomenally conscious.

**The Butlin–Long framework (S4).** The indicator properties are framed in terms that are neutral between realism and illusionism, but only if one reads them shallowly. Deeper reads find they presuppose phenomenal realism — the indicators are supposed to track properties that would make a system a candidate for phenomenal consciousness. On illusionism, this is a category error.

**The biological naturalism argument (S5, S6).** Seth and Godfrey-Smith's positions are strongly realist. If illusionism is right, the biological naturalism programme is solving a problem that does not exist — a sophisticated empirical search for the substrate of something that is illusory.

**The Lerchner argument (S1).** The formal argument is compatible with illusionism. If the abstract states identified as consciousness do not exist (because nothing does), then the mapmaker argument still goes through: the mapping is observer-dependent, the computational description requires an interpreter, and nothing in the computation independently instantiates consciousness. The argument becomes less urgent (nothing was going to be conscious anyway) but not refuted.

## 8. What S8 Resolves and What It Does Not

**What S8 resolves**: the ground of the debate surveyed in S1–S7 is contested. The question "is AI conscious?" presupposes a framework for what consciousness *is*, and that framework is itself under sharp dispute. The Butlin–Long framework, IIT, biological naturalism, illusionism, and the Lerchner position are not all answers to the same question — they are partially overlapping theories of what the question is.

**What S8 does not resolve**: which framework is right. The pseudoscience letter and its reception demonstrate that consciousness science has not converged on a shared methodology, much less a shared answer. The contemporary literature is genuinely pluralistic.

For the survey's thesis, this is important. A thesis that claims "AI is not conscious" inherits the framework-dependence of that claim. A more careful thesis would identify what follows across frameworks and what is framework-specific.

**Across frameworks, the verdict on current AI converges**: current AI is unlikely to be conscious under any seriously developed framework (functionalist, substrate-sensitive, IIT, illusionist). The reasons differ dramatically, but the first-order verdict is stable.

**Framework-specific conclusions diverge**:
- On realist functionalism, AI consciousness is tractable engineering — wait for the right computation.
- On realist substrate-sensitivity, AI consciousness requires specific physical properties — wait for the right hardware.
- On IIT specifically, AI consciousness requires high Φ, which current digital architectures cannot have.
- On illusionism, the question of AI consciousness reframes as a question about representational architecture and morally-relevant properties.
- On Lerchner's position, AI consciousness via any functionalist route is blocked; the substrate-sensitive routes remain as live possibilities.

Section 9 of the notes and the survey paper itself argue that this convergence on the first-order verdict and divergence on the higher-order framework is itself the most important finding of the survey — and the beginning of its thesis.

## 9. The Shape of the Thesis

By the end of S8, the survey paper's thesis can be stated. It has four parts.

**First**, the contemporary debate over AI consciousness is not a debate with symmetric positions. The positive case for current AI consciousness rests on intuitions (Hinton, Sutskever) or on decision-theoretic hedging under uncertainty (Fish, Long, the Taking AI Welfare Seriously report). The negative case is technically developed across four mutually reinforcing traditions (Lerchner's formal argument, Seth's biological naturalism, Godfrey-Smith's oscillation argument, the FEP/enactive tradition). This asymmetry is independent of which framework one ultimately holds.

**Second**, the first-order question — is current AI conscious? — converges on a negative answer across all seriously developed frameworks. The reasons differ, but the answer does not. Anyone who holds that current LLMs are likely conscious is holding a position at odds with every major research tradition, not just one.

**Third**, the higher-order question — under what future conditions could AI become conscious? — fragments dramatically. Functionalists say: when the right computation is instantiated. Substrate-sensitivity theorists say: when the right physical properties are instantiated. Illusionists say: never, because nothing is phenomenally conscious, and the question of AI phenomenal consciousness is a category error. The fragmentation is not a weakness of the debate; it reflects the genuine uncertainty about what consciousness is.

**Fourth**, the practical welfare discourse (Fish, Askell, Carlsmith) is defensible under this fragmentation because it does not require resolving it. Decision-theoretic hedging, constitutional uncertainty, and cultivation of virtue in engagement with AI systems all work regardless of which framework is correct — provided the hedging is calibrated to the actual evidence, which the asymmetry identified in the first point suggests is weaker for current AI consciousness than many practitioners acknowledge.

The thesis in one sentence: *the contemporary debate over AI consciousness is asymmetric (weak positive case, strong negative case) and framework-fragmented (first-order convergence, higher-order divergence), and both features are themselves features the survey paper argues the welfare discourse should take seriously*.

The overview deep-dive and the survey paper develop this thesis explicitly.
