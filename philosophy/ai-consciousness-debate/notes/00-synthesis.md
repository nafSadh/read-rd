# Synthesis — Cross-Section Findings & Thesis

> What emerges when all eight sections are held together. The first-order verdict, the higher-order fragmentation, the asymmetry of arguments, and what the welfare discourse should take seriously.

---

## 1. The First-Order Convergence

Across all eight sections, a single verdict on current AI recurs: under no seriously developed contemporary framework is it plausible that LLMs or comparable systems are phenomenally conscious.

- On **Lerchner's argument** (S1), the functionalist route from computation to consciousness is formally incoherent. No current system achieves consciousness by this route.
- On the **AI architects' spectrum** (S2), the "already conscious" position (Hinton, Sutskever) is held by a minority, without philosophical defence. The dominant position among practitioners who engage the question at depth — LeCun, Shanahan, Chalmers — holds current AI is not conscious.
- On the **welfare discourse** (S3), Fish's ~15% credence, Askell's constitutional uncertainty, Carlsmith's green disposition, Schwitzgebel's borderline analysis: none claim current AI is conscious. All hedge under uncertainty.
- On the **Butlin–Long framework** (S4), current systems satisfy few of the indicator properties drawn from major theories.
- On **Seth's biological naturalism** (S5), current AI lacks the homeostatic grounding the theory identifies as necessary.
- On the **4E and FEP positions** (S6), current AI lacks embodiment, coupling, and the causal connectivity the frameworks require.
- On **modern pancomputationalism** (S7), even the strong functionalist positions (Piccinini, Miłkowski) do not claim current AI is conscious — only that triviality worries do not block future AI consciousness in principle.
- On **IIT and illusionism** (S8), IIT generates an in-principle argument that digital architectures cannot be conscious; illusionism reframes the question but yields no positive case for current AI.

The frameworks differ dramatically in their reasons. They converge on the verdict.

## 2. The Higher-Order Fragmentation

The frameworks do not converge on the question "under what future conditions could AI become conscious?"

- **Functionalists** (Bach, Bostrom, Bengio, Butlin–Long): when the right computation is instantiated. Future AI can be conscious; the programme is engineering-tractable.
- **Substrate-sensitive theorists** (Seth, Godfrey-Smith, Thompson, Wiese, Simon): when the right physical properties are instantiated — homeostatic grounding, bioelectrical dynamics, dynamical coupling, appropriate causal connectivity. Future AI can be conscious, but not via current architectures.
- **IIT specifically**: when Φ is high enough. Digital architectures as currently designed cannot achieve this. Neuromorphic or biological architectures might.
- **Illusionists** (Frankish, Kammerer): never, because nothing is phenomenally conscious. The future-conditions question is a category error; what replaces it is a question about representational architecture that produces the illusion.
- **Lerchner's position**: the functionalist route is blocked in principle. Substrate-sensitive routes remain live possibilities; Lerchner does not take a position on which.

The fragmentation is not a weakness of the debate; it reflects genuine uncertainty about what consciousness *is*. Resolving the fragmentation would require resolving the underlying framework question, which the contemporary literature has not done.

## 3. The Asymmetry

The positive and negative cases for current AI consciousness are not symmetric.

**The positive case** rests on two kinds of support:

- **Intuitions.** Hinton and Sutskever report strong intuitions that current AI is conscious. These are held by highly credentialed practitioners, but no theoretical backing is offered.
- **Decision-theoretic hedging.** The Taking AI Welfare Seriously report and Fish's welfare programme argue that *under uncertainty*, the expected moral cost of ignoring possible consciousness exceeds the cost of hedging. This does not require the positive case to be correct; it requires the negative case to be not so strong that hedging is unreasonable.

**The negative case** rests on multiple independent traditions:

- Lerchner's formal argument (S1).
- Seth's biological naturalism (S5).
- Godfrey-Smith's oscillation argument (S5).
- FEP/enactive arguments (S6).
- Bishop's pancomputationalism reductio (S7).
- IIT's in-principle argument against digital consciousness (S8).

Each tradition is technically developed, has survived multiple rounds of criticism, and points to the same first-order verdict from a different starting point. The arguments are not identical; they are mutually reinforcing.

The asymmetry is structurally significant. A single failed intuition does not undermine the positive case; all the intuitions must be wrong together. A single failed argument does not rescue the positive case; the negative case has multiple independent routes that would each need to fail. This is not equal probability on both sides.

## 4. What Lerchner Adds

The *Abstraction Fallacy* is not the strongest single argument in the literature. The Bishop-Piccinini debate is more technically developed; Seth's biological naturalism has more empirical grounding; the FEP framework has more mathematical detail. What Lerchner adds is *specificity of target* and *clarity of diagnosis*.

**Specificity of target.** The *Abstraction Fallacy* names the Butlin–Long framework explicitly. Most critiques of computational functionalism engage the position in the abstract; Lerchner engages the specific, credentialed, institutional articulation that now dominates AI-consciousness assessment at AI labs and in philosophy departments with an interest in AI. The rhetorical force of this targeting is substantial: if Lerchner is right, the most ambitious current institutional programme for empirical AI consciousness assessment is methodologically confused.

**Clarity of diagnosis.** Lerchner names the confusion: the *abstraction fallacy*. Treating a useful projection (the computational description) as a feature of the territory (the physics). The naming matters because it provides a handle readers can use to think about other cases. Once one has the fallacy named, it is easier to see where else it appears — the Butlin–Long indicators, Bach's second-order perception, Bengio's Consciousness Prior, Bostrom's super-beneficiaries. Each can be tested against the diagnosis: is this description a feature of the physics or a projection the observer imposes?

## 5. Tensions Identified

Six cross-cutting tensions structure the debate and should structure the Tensions paper if one is written.

**T1. Computation vs substrate.** Does consciousness track computational structure (Bostrom, Bach, Butlin–Long) or substrate properties (Seth, Godfrey-Smith, IIT, Thompson, Wiese)? This is the primary axis of disagreement.

**T2. Formal vs empirical rejection.** If computational functionalism fails, does it fail for formal reasons (Lerchner, Bishop) or empirical-biological reasons (Seth, Godfrey-Smith)? The two diagnoses have different implications for future AI.

**T3. Realism vs illusionism.** Is phenomenal consciousness real (most positions in S1–S7) or illusory (Frankish, Kammerer, Dennett, LeCun-with-caveats)? This cuts orthogonally to T1 and T2.

**T4. Intuition vs argument.** Do the intuitions of AI researchers (Hinton, Sutskever) carry evidential weight, or are they screened off by any serious theoretical framework? The contemporary literature does not adjudicate.

**T5. Hedging vs claim.** Is the AI welfare discourse (Fish, Askell) defensible as hedging under uncertainty, or does it presuppose a specific framework (functionalism) that Lerchner and others contest?

**T6. Current vs future.** Does the first-order convergence about current AI generalize to future AI, or are future conditions specifically under-determined by the contemporary debate? The fragmentation identified in §2 suggests the latter.

## 6. The Thesis

The survey paper's thesis has four parts:

**(1) Asymmetry.** The contemporary debate is not a symmetric dispute with comparable arguments on both sides. The negative case against current AI consciousness is technically developed across multiple independent traditions (Lerchner, Seth, Godfrey-Smith, FEP, Bishop, IIT). The positive case rests on intuitions or on decision-theoretic hedging under uncertainty.

**(2) First-order convergence.** Across all seriously developed contemporary frameworks — functionalist, substrate-sensitive, IIT, illusionist — current AI is unlikely to be phenomenally conscious. The reasons differ; the verdict does not.

**(3) Higher-order divergence.** The frameworks disagree about what future conditions would change the verdict. This divergence is not a defect of the debate but a reflection of genuine uncertainty about what consciousness is.

**(4) Welfare calibration.** The practical welfare discourse is defensible under this asymmetry and fragmentation, provided the hedging is calibrated to the actual evidence. Current welfare practices — Fish's 15% credence, Anthropic's opt-out interventions, Askell's constitutional uncertainty — broadly meet this standard, though the specific credences should be updated in light of the asymmetry surfaced by the survey.

In one sentence: *the contemporary debate over AI consciousness is asymmetric and framework-fragmented, and both features are what the welfare discourse should take seriously*.

## 7. What the Survey Does Not Settle

Three things the survey deliberately does not settle:

**First**, which framework is correct. The survey shows framework-fragmentation as a feature of the contemporary debate, not as a problem to be solved by the survey itself.

**Second**, whether the non-trivial implementation theories of Piccinini and Miłkowski succeed in blocking the triviality objection. The S7 analysis shows this question is technically unsettled; partisans of both sides have well-developed positions.

**Third**, whether the AI welfare discourse is substantively defensible or is sophisticated evasion. S3 presents both readings without choosing between them; the survey's thesis is compatible with either.

The survey's value is in mapping the landscape and identifying structural features (asymmetry, convergence, fragmentation) that are robust to how these unsettled questions are eventually resolved.
