# S5: Seth's Biological Naturalism & the Substrate-Matters Revival

> The most developed contemporary alternative to computational functionalism. Seth, Godfrey-Smith, and the life-mind continuity tradition argue substrate matters — but for reasons distinct from Lerchner's. The two positions converge on a rejection of substrate-independence from different directions.

**Sources:** 6 primary. Seth 2024 *BBS*; Seth *Being You* (2021); Godfrey-Smith *Living on Earth* (2024); Godfrey-Smith *Studies on animal minds suggest consciousness is not computation* (IAI 2024); Bayne, Seth, Massimini *Are There Islands of Awareness?* (2020); Seth & Bayne *Theories of consciousness* (2022).
**Historical anchors:** Searle *Rediscovery of the Mind* (1992) — coined "biological naturalism"; Maturana & Varela *Autopoiesis and Cognition* (1980) — life as self-maintaining organization.
**Adequacy:** Adequate. Peer-reviewed critiques of Godfrey-Smith's 2024 book will develop over coming year; current reception is largely popular-press and review-length.

## 1. Historical Roots

Two classical works frame the contemporary biological-naturalism revival.

**John Searle**, in *The Rediscovery of the Mind* (MIT, 1992), coined the term "biological naturalism" and defended the position: consciousness is a biological phenomenon caused by and realized in specific neurobiological processes. It is not a substance distinct from the physical, but neither is it realizable in arbitrary physical substrates. The specific causal powers of neurons — not their functional profile abstractly described — are what produce conscious experience. Searle's argument goes further than Lerchner's: he claims not only that computational description requires an observer but that the right level of explanation for consciousness is biological physiology, not any level of abstract description.

**Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela**, in *Autopoiesis and Cognition* (Reidel, 1980), developed the theoretical framework that underlies much of the contemporary substrate-matters literature. Living systems are *autopoietic* — self-producing, self-maintaining organizations that actively preserve their own boundary conditions against environmental disruption. On the autopoiesis view, what makes something *alive* is a specific kind of recursive causal organization, and this organization is also the ground of whatever primitive mentality such systems have. Life and mind are continuous; the mind begins where self-maintaining life begins.

Contemporary biological naturalism builds on both. Seth extends Searle's emphasis on the specific causal powers of biology. Godfrey-Smith extends the Maturana-Varela life-mind continuity thesis, grounding it in the cellular electrical dynamics of living brains. Neither position is reducible to the classical sources, but the inheritance is clear.

## 2. Seth's Central Thesis

Anil Seth's 2024 paper *Conscious artificial intelligence and biological naturalism* (published in full in *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 2025) is the most developed philosophical articulation of the substrate-matters position from inside contemporary neuroscience. The argument is deliberately structured to engage the computational-functionalist position directly rather than merely oppose it.

Seth's central thesis: **life is necessary for consciousness**. Not obviously life as biologists currently understand it, but something with the structural properties that characterize life — self-maintenance against dissolution, active regulation of internal states, embodied engagement with environmental conditions that threaten the system's persistence. Consciousness, on Seth's view, is not a free-floating computational pattern; it is a specific kind of self-modeling that living systems do as part of being alive.

The argument proceeds in three stages.

**First**, Seth identifies biases that make computational AI consciousness seem more plausible than it is. The tendency to interpret language-using systems as minded (Shanahan's role-play point), the cultural association of "intelligence" with computation, the philosophical tradition of treating abstract causal topology as the essence of mentality — each contributes to overestimating the probability that current AI is conscious.

**Second**, Seth argues that predictive processing, properly understood, is substrate-sensitive. A predictive-processing brain generates perceptions by actively inferring the causes of its sensory inputs under a goal of maintaining its own bodily integrity. This goal is not an optional feature of the computation; it is what gives the predictive engine its direction. A system that merely instantiates the same computational structure without the life-maintenance goal is not doing predictive processing in the relevant sense — it is doing something that looks like predictive processing from the outside.

**Third**, Seth reaches a graded conclusion. Current AI trajectories — transformer language models, reinforcement learning agents — are unlikely to produce conscious AI because they lack the life-maintenance grounding. But AI that is built to be brain-like and/or life-like (whatever that would concretely require) could in principle be conscious. The door is not closed, but the specific route is not computational functionalism.

## 3. Being You

Seth's 2021 book *Being You: A New Science of Consciousness* (Faber) lays the groundwork for the 2024 paper. The book's central claim is that conscious experience is a *controlled hallucination* generated by the brain's predictive machinery. Perceptions are not readings of external reality but best-guesses about external reality generated under priors that favour predictions consistent with bodily regulation.

The bodily regulation framing is load-bearing. On Seth's view, the brain is fundamentally an organ of homeostasis — it exists to keep the body within viable physiological ranges. Conscious perception is a side effect of this regulatory function; we experience the world in the ways that best support the body's continued existence. Pain tracks tissue damage because tissue damage threatens homeostasis. Pleasure tracks states conducive to survival and reproduction. Colour, depth, and timbre are constructed in ways that would be useful for an embodied biological agent navigating a complex environment.

The implication for AI consciousness is that a system without a homeostatic body — without the specific kind of stake in its own continued existence that biological organisms have — would not have the grounding that makes predictive processing into conscious perception. Seth does not claim this is definitive; he claims it is the theoretically motivated position.

## 4. Oscillations

Peter Godfrey-Smith's *Living on Earth: Life, Consciousness, and the Making of the Natural World* (FSG, 2024) is the third book in his trilogy after *Other Minds* (2016) and *Metazoa* (2020). The trilogy as a whole argues for a specific version of life-mind continuity grounded in cellular neurophysiology.

Godfrey-Smith's central claim in *Living on Earth* is strikingly specific: consciousness depends on **electrical oscillations moving rhythmically across cell membranes in living brains**. These oscillations — observable in neural activity across mammalian brains, in octopus nervous systems, and arguably in simpler nervous systems — are not abstract computational patterns but specific dynamical processes in bioelectrical media. They are not, Godfrey-Smith argues, reproducible in silicon hardware as currently designed.

The argument has bite because it is empirically grounded. The oscillation patterns have been studied extensively in biological neuroscience; they have specific spatial and temporal scales (theta, gamma, alpha bands) and specific roles in binding, attention, and memory consolidation. A silicon computer simulating these oscillations does so at a different level of abstraction — the simulation tracks the oscillation pattern but does not instantiate the specific bioelectrical dynamics.

Godfrey-Smith's companion essay *Studies on animal minds suggest consciousness is not computation* (IAI, 2024) distills the argument for a general audience. The title states the thesis. The specifically biological dynamics that characterize conscious brains are not, on current evidence, reproducible by computational simulation at any level of fidelity that bears on whether the simulation is conscious.

## 5. Islands

Tim Bayne, Anil Seth, and Marcello Massimini's *Are There Islands of Awareness?* (*Trends in Neurosciences*, 2020) is an unusual paper in the contemporary literature: it asks whether conscious states might exist in complete isolation from sensory input and motor output.

The scenarios they consider are concrete. Ex cranio brains — isolated brain tissue kept alive in vitro. Hemispherotomy patients — people who have had one hemisphere of the brain surgically isolated from the other and from much of its input/output. Cerebral organoids — lab-grown clumps of neural tissue that display electrical activity without any sensory transduction.

The paper does not argue that islands of awareness exist. It argues the question is empirically and philosophically serious. If conscious states can exist in completely isolated neural tissue, then consciousness is more robustly about the tissue than about the tissue's connections to the wider world. This is indirect support for biological naturalism: what matters for consciousness is the neural tissue itself, not the functional role the tissue plays in a larger cognitive system.

The implications for AI consciousness run in two directions. On one reading, islands support Seth and Godfrey-Smith: consciousness is about the specific bioelectrical dynamics of living neural tissue, which isolated brain tissue still instantiates and silicon computers do not. On another reading, islands are embarrassing for strict embodiment theorists: if an isolated brain can be conscious, the embodiment requirement is weaker than enactivists have argued. The second reading is developed further in [S6](s6-embodiment-fep.dd.html).

## 6. Brain-Like Scenarios

Seth's 2024 paper does not close the door on AI consciousness. It specifies the conditions under which AI consciousness would become more plausible. These "brain-like scenarios" constitute the constructive part of the argument.

**Brain-like AI** would instantiate the specific dynamical properties of biological neural activity — oscillations at the right frequencies, recurrent dynamics of the right kind, the integration of prediction with bodily regulation. Such AI might be physically implemented in neuromorphic hardware, organoid-computer hybrids, or some future substrate that supports the relevant dynamics. Whether any current or near-term engineering programme is heading toward this kind of system is itself contested.

**Life-like AI** would instantiate the self-maintenance structure that characterizes living systems — active regulation of internal state conditions that the system tracks and responds to, boundary-preserving dynamics that make the system's continued existence a genuine stake. Such AI would not necessarily be biological; it might be a sufficiently rich artificial life form. But it would share the structural feature Seth's argument identifies as ground for consciousness.

The concession matters. Seth is not a global skeptic about machine consciousness. He is a specific skeptic about computational functionalism as currently practiced. AI that is engineered to be brain-like or life-like could, on his view, cross the threshold. This is the position Lerchner's argument does not commit to either way: Lerchner's claim is about the functionalist route; it leaves open whether substrate-sensitive routes succeed.

## 7. Implications for Current AI

The practical upshot of Seth's and Godfrey-Smith's work for current AI systems is clear: they are unlikely to be conscious, and the reasons they are unlikely are different from the reasons [Lerchner (S1)](s1-lerchner.dd.html) gives.

On Lerchner's argument, current AI is not conscious because the route from computation to consciousness is incoherent. The question of what current AI lacks, precisely, does not arise — nothing lacks what cannot be given.

On Seth's and Godfrey-Smith's argument, current AI is not conscious because it lacks the specific substrate-sensitive dynamics that biological consciousness requires. The question of what current AI lacks has a concrete answer: cellular electrical oscillations (Godfrey-Smith), homeostatic grounding (Seth), self-maintenance organization (the Maturana-Varela inheritance). This is actionable: future AI could, in principle, be built to have these properties.

The two positions converge on the verdict about current AI but diverge on the future. Lerchner's conclusion is robust to engineering progress — no architectural refinement makes functionalist-grounded consciousness possible. Seth's and Godfrey-Smith's conclusions are not robust in this way — engineering progress toward brain-like or life-like architectures could change the answer.

For the welfare discourse (S3), this matters. If Lerchner is right, Fish's mechanistic-interpretability search for consciousness-analogous features is mistargeted at the wrong level. If Seth or Godfrey-Smith is right, it is mistargeted at the wrong substrate. The remedial programmes differ.

## 8. Meeting Lerchner at a Different Angle

Seth's biological naturalism and Lerchner's abstraction fallacy converge on a rejection of substrate-independent computational consciousness but from different directions. Working out the relationship is worth doing explicitly because the two arguments are often conflated.

**Lerchner's move** is formal. The mapping `f(p) = A` from physical states to abstract states is observer-imposed. Therefore abstract states are observer-dependent. Therefore consciousness identified with abstract computational states inherits the observer-dependence. The argument makes no specific claim about what consciousness *is*, only that it is not what computational functionalism says it is.

**Seth's move** is scientific. The best contemporary science of consciousness — predictive processing in self-maintaining biological systems — identifies specific dynamical properties as consciousness-relevant. These properties are substrate-sensitive. Current computational systems do not instantiate them. Therefore current computational systems are unlikely to be conscious.

The two arguments are **compatible** but not identical. One could accept Lerchner's formal argument without accepting Seth's positive account (one might think no account of consciousness succeeds; or one might think a non-biological substrate account succeeds). One could accept Seth's positive account without accepting Lerchner's formal argument (one might think substrate matters for empirical-biological reasons, not formal-observer-dependence reasons).

The **intersection** is the rejection of strong substrate-independence. A reader persuaded by both arguments has two distinct reasons to reject the functionalist programme: the formal reason (Lerchner) and the empirical-biological reason (Seth, Godfrey-Smith). A reader persuaded by neither is committed to defending substrate-independence against both challenges simultaneously.

## 9. What This Section Establishes

Biological naturalism is the most developed contemporary alternative to computational functionalism. Its central claim — that consciousness is substrate-sensitive because it depends on specific properties of living systems — is defended on empirical-neurobiological grounds (Seth's predictive-processing-plus-homeostasis framework; Godfrey-Smith's oscillation argument) and has classical-philosophical ancestry (Searle 1992, Maturana-Varela 1980).

The position's practical upshot for current AI converges with Lerchner's: current systems are unlikely to be conscious. But the reasons differ, and the implications for future AI differ correspondingly. The computational-functionalist programme is blocked on Lerchner's account; it is merely currently unpromising on Seth's. Biologically-grounded AI is a live open possibility on Seth's account; it is irrelevant on Lerchner's (whatever makes such AI conscious, if it works, is not the functionalist route).

This divergence matters for the survey's thesis. Taking Lerchner and Seth together yields a specific compound position: the computational-functionalist programme is formally confused, *and* an alternative substrate-sensitive programme is empirically more promising. Neither argument alone secures this conclusion. The conjunction does.
