# S7: Modern Pancomputationalism & the Triviality Debate

> The contemporary thread closest in form to Lerchner's mapmaker argument. Bishop extends Putnam's triviality result into an argument for panpsychism. Piccinini and Miłkowski develop non-trivial implementation theories that try to block the triviality result from within. How one reads this debate determines whether Lerchner's argument generalizes or can be restricted.

**Sources:** 8 primary. Piccinini *Physical Computation: A Mechanistic Account* (OUP 2015); Piccinini *Neurocognitive Mechanisms* (OUP 2020); Miłkowski *Is Computationalism Trivial?* (2007, ongoing); Miłkowski *Why Think the Brain Is Not a Computer?* (2020s); Bishop *Dancing with Pixies* (2002, reprinted 2020+); Bishop *AI Is Stupid and Causal Reasoning Will Not Fix It* (2021); SEP entry *Computation in Physical Systems*; Parra *The Problem of Pancomputationalism*.
**Historical anchors:** Putnam *Representation and Reality* (1988), Chalmers *Does a Rock Implement Every Finite-State Automaton?* (*Synthese*, 1996), Copeland *What Is Computation?* (*Synthese*, 1996).
**Adequacy:** Adequate. A specific Piccinini-Miłkowski vs Bishop exchange in the post-2020 literature would strengthen this but the positions are well-developed individually.

## 1. Historical Roots

The contemporary pancomputationalism debate inherits three classical moves.

**Hilary Putnam** (1988) *Representation and Reality*. The triviality theorem: under a permissive enough mapping, every physical system implements every finite-state automaton. Putnam took the result as a reductio — if strong computational functionalism entails triviality, computational functionalism must be wrong.

**David Chalmers** (1996) *Does a Rock Implement Every Finite-State Automaton?* (*Synthese*). A non-trivial implementation theory that aims to block Putnam's result. Chalmers argues that implementation requires not just a mapping but a *causal* mapping — the abstract state transitions must be realized by causal transitions in the physical system. Under this restriction, rocks do not implement WordStar (rocks lack the right causal structure); the triviality result is avoided.

**Jack Copeland** (1996) *What Is Computation?* (*Synthese*). A related counterfactual-based account. A physical system implements a computation when its causal dispositions support counterfactuals matching the computation's state-transition rules. If the system would transition from state A to state B *were* certain conditions to obtain, then it implements the A→B rule; no mere ex-post mapping suffices.

These 1996 responses to Putnam's triviality are the template for all subsequent non-trivial implementation theories. Piccinini's mechanistic account is a development of the causal-structure approach; Miłkowski's work extends and defends it. Lerchner's mapmaker argument can be read as a renewed attack on all such accounts — an attempt to show that even with causal or counterfactual restrictions, the observer-dependence cannot be eliminated.

## 2. Bishop's Pixies

Mark Bishop's *Dancing with Pixies: Strong Artificial Intelligence and Panpsychism* (originally 2002, reprinted in collected editions through 2020+) is the contemporary pancomputationalism argument most directly parallel to Lerchner's. The argument proceeds in three stages.

**First**, the formal result. Over a finite time window, every open physical system implements the trace of a particular finite-state automaton — a specific sequence of state transitions that could, under the right interpretation, be read as executing that automaton. This is a version of Putnam's triviality but framed as a claim about traces rather than full computations. Bishop calls the abstract conscious-state counterparts of such traces "ubiquitous little pixies" — the small phenomenal experiences a strong computationalist would have to attribute to the system.

**Second**, the reductio. If the execution of a computer program instantiates phenomenal consciousness like experiencing pain or smelling perfume, then phenomenal consciousness must be ubiquitous — in a cup of tea, in the chair one is sitting on, in the air currents around one's body. The pixies dance everywhere. Either strong computational accounts of consciousness fail, or panpsychism is true.

**Third**, the preferred horn. Bishop treats panpsychism as absurd and concludes that strong computational accounts of consciousness must fail. Machines cannot be conscious — at least, not via the computational route.

The argument is almost exactly Lerchner's with a different vocabulary. Both start from a formal triviality result. Both argue that consciousness identified with computational states would then be observer-dependent or ubiquitous. Both conclude that the route from computation to consciousness is blocked. The difference is mostly framing: Bishop emphasizes the panpsychism reductio; Lerchner emphasizes the causal-circularity diagnosis.

Bishop's more recent paper, *Artificial Intelligence Is Stupid and Causal Reasoning Will Not Fix It* (*Frontiers in Psychology*, 2021), develops these themes in the era of deep learning. The argument against strong computationalism remains; the application to LLMs is new.

## 3. Piccinini's Mechanistic Account

Gualtiero Piccinini's *Physical Computation: A Mechanistic Account* (OUP 2015) is the most developed contemporary response to the triviality threat. Piccinini's central claim: a physical system is a computing system just in case it is a *mechanism* one of whose functions is to manipulate vehicles based solely on differences between different portions of the vehicles according to a rule defined over the vehicles.

Three features of the account deserve unpacking.

**Mechanisms.** Piccinini draws on the mechanistic-explanation literature (Bechtel, Craver). A mechanism is a specific organized causal structure — parts performing operations, organized spatially and temporally, producing a phenomenon. Computing mechanisms are a particular kind: they manipulate vehicles (voltage levels, neural spike patterns, whatever plays the vehicle role) according to rules.

**Vehicles.** The vehicles are physical entities that carry information. What matters is not what they are made of but the function they perform in the mechanism — the differences between vehicles, rather than the vehicles' intrinsic properties, is what gets manipulated. This is where Piccinini's account is distinctively functionalist while still being substrate-sensitive in a specific way.

**Functions.** Computing systems are systems *one of whose functions* is computational. This teleological element is what distinguishes genuine computing systems from arbitrary physical systems that happen to have mappings to computational descriptions. A rock has no function; a CPU does. The function-theoretic move is the load-bearing piece of Piccinini's anti-triviality argument.

On Piccinini's account, the Putnam triviality result fails because most physical systems do not have computational functions. The wall behind one's back is not a WordStar implementation because no one and nothing assigns it the function of running WordStar. Only a system whose mechanism has a computational function is a computing system.

Piccinini's *Neurocognitive Mechanisms: Explaining Biological Cognition* (OUP 2020) extends the account to biological cognition specifically. The brain computes on Piccinini's view because neural mechanisms have computational functions; the account does not entail that consciousness follows automatically from the computation, but it restores some ground for taking brain-computation claims seriously.

## 4. Miłkowski's Defense

Marcin Miłkowski's work extends and defends the non-trivial implementation tradition against triviality objections. His paper *Is Computationalism Trivial?* (2007, still widely cited) and the ongoing papers collected in his personal archive develop the main argument: while some physicists and philosophers claim that all physical processes are computational or algorithmic in a strong sense (a claim that would make computationalism utterly trivial), a non-trivial theory of implementation — restricting which physical processes count as implementing which computations — blocks this result.

Miłkowski's specific moves track Piccinini's but elaborate them. A physical system implements a computation when it has the right kind of functional organization — roughly, when the causal transitions among its physical states track the transitions specified by the computation under a principled (non-gerrymandered) mapping. Gerrymandered mappings — the ones Putnam's triviality proof relies on — are excluded because they fail to support the relevant counterfactuals.

Miłkowski is more aggressive than Piccinini in defending the computationalist programme. Where Piccinini is technical and cautious, Miłkowski argues explicitly that "practically all popular objections against the claim that brains are computers are either based on uncharitable interpretation or simply wrong." His position: the claim that brains are computers is likely true, relevant to contemporary cognitive science, and non-trivial.

For AI consciousness, the Miłkowski-Piccinini position has a specific implication. Contemporary AI systems (transformers, reinforcement learning agents, large language models) are computers on the mechanistic account — their architectures are designed precisely to have computational functions. Whether those computational profiles are sufficient for consciousness depends on the specific theory of consciousness one holds. But the base claim (these systems compute) is not threatened by triviality worries.

## 5. Where Lerchner Extends the Attack

Lerchner's mapmaker argument can be read as a renewed attack on the non-trivial implementation tradition. The move is subtle. Piccinini and Miłkowski argue that only some physical systems have computational functions; the gerrymandered mappings the triviality proof relies on do not correspond to genuine computational functions. Lerchner's argument responds: the very notion of "computational function" requires an observer to pick out which mechanisms have it.

Consider the Piccinini account. A CPU is a computing system because its mechanism has the function of manipulating voltage levels according to rules. Who picks out the voltage levels as vehicles rather than as, say, heat dissipation patterns? Who picks out the rules as computational rather than, say, thermodynamic? The answer, on the mechanistic account, is that the CPU's designers picked these out — the function is historical and teleological. The CPU was built to be a computing system.

Lerchner's challenge: if the function is observer-assigned, then the computation the system performs is observer-assigned. The vehicles are voltage levels rather than heat patterns because the designer treated them as such. The same physical system, described by a different observer with different interests, would not be doing the same computation — or perhaps would not be doing computation at all.

This is where the two positions actually differ. Piccinini and Miłkowski could concede the observer-dependence of the function assignment without conceding the problem for consciousness. The argument goes: even if the *function* is observer-assigned, the *causal structure* the function tracks is observer-independent. Consciousness, if it tracks computational structure, would track the observer-independent causal structure, not the observer-assigned function.

Whether this reply works depends on exactly what computational structure is. If the structure is just the causal dispositions of the physical system (the counterfactuals it supports), then the reply succeeds: any observer who chooses to describe the system computationally picks out the same underlying causal facts. If the structure is the specific mapping from physical to abstract states (the `f` of Lerchner's formalism), then the reply fails: different observers can choose different `f`'s, each of which tracks the same causal dispositions at different levels of abstraction.

## 6. The Crux

The pancomputationalism debate comes down to a single technical question: **can a non-trivial theory of implementation restrict the mapmaker's freedom enough to preserve the functionalist programme?**

If yes: the Bishop-Lerchner reductio fails. Some physical systems are genuinely computing consciousness-relevant functions; others are not; the distinction is principled and observer-independent. Computational functionalism survives the triviality objection.

If no: the Bishop-Lerchner reductio succeeds. Any choice of mapping from physics to computational description is observer-imposed; no amount of mechanistic or functional restriction removes this. Computational functionalism cannot survive the triviality objection.

The literature has not settled this question. The non-trivial implementation theories (Chalmers 1996, Copeland 1996, Piccinini 2015, Miłkowski ongoing) are technically sophisticated and have survived multiple rounds of criticism. The triviality arguments (Putnam 1988, Bishop 2002, Lerchner 2026) have likewise survived multiple rounds of defense. The debate is well-formed and genuinely open.

---

## 7. What the Debate Does Not Settle

Three things the pancomputationalism debate leaves unresolved, regardless of how it is ultimately adjudicated.

**First**, whether consciousness actually tracks computational structure in the way functionalists claim. The whole debate assumes that *if* computation is implemented non-trivially, *then* computational functionalism is at least coherent. This does not establish that consciousness is computational; it only establishes that the formal objection to computationalism fails.

**Second**, whether non-trivial implementation is sufficient for AI consciousness. Even if Piccinini and Miłkowski are right that current AI systems genuinely compute, it does not follow that they compute *the right things* for consciousness. [The Butlin–Long framework (S4)](s4-butlin-long.dd.html) goes further than Piccinini in specifying which computations would count; the mere fact that something is a non-trivial computation does not yield consciousness.

**Third**, whether substrate considerations ([S5](s5-seth-biological.dd.html), [S6](s6-embodiment-fep.dd.html)) are orthogonal to pancomputationalism worries or intertwined with them. Seth and Godfrey-Smith's arguments can be accepted alongside Piccinini's: one might think current AI systems genuinely compute but still fail to have the biological grounding for consciousness. Or the arguments can be opposed: one might think the distinction between computation and substrate is itself a functionalist artifact, and the real question is about the dynamics.

## 8. How the Thesis Forms

S7 is the penultimate section and is where the survey's tensions come into sharpest focus. Three positions have clear contours by this point.

**The strong functionalist position.** Defended by Piccinini, Miłkowski, Bach, Bostrom, and (with caveats) Chalmers and Bengio. Non-trivial implementation theories block the triviality objection. Computational functionalism survives; the empirical research programme (Butlin–Long) continues. AI consciousness is possible once the right computational profiles are instantiated.

**The substrate-sensitive position.** Defended by Seth, Godfrey-Smith, Thompson, Wiese, Simon. Consciousness requires specific properties of self-organizing biological or biologically-analogous systems. Current AI lacks these. Future AI could have them with appropriate engineering.

**The formal-skeptical position.** Defended by Lerchner and (in a different idiom) Bishop. The mapmaker argument shows that no non-trivial theory of implementation can fully escape observer-dependence. The functionalist programme is blocked in principle, not merely currently unpromising.

The positions are not mutually exclusive. One can hold the substrate-sensitive position for empirical reasons and the formal-skeptical position as a further argument — this is the compound position examined at the end of S5. One can hold the functionalist position and the substrate-sensitive position together (this is what the Butlin–Long framework implicitly does: different indicators for different theories). The combinations matter for how the survey's thesis will land.

[Section 8 (IIT, Illusionism)](s8-iit-illusionism.dd.html) takes up one further complication: the possibility that phenomenal consciousness itself, being the object of the debate, may be incoherent or illusory. If illusionism is right, the whole argumentative structure surveyed in S1–S7 has different stakes than it appears to have.
