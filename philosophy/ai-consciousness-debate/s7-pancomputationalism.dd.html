01

Historical Roots

Three classical moves frame the debate. Putnam 1988 — the triviality theorem: under a permissive mapping, every physical system implements every finite-state automaton. Chalmers 1996 — Does a Rock Implement Every Finite-State Automaton? argued implementation requires causal mapping, not just formal mapping. Copeland 1996 — counterfactual-based account: implementation supports the right conditional dispositions.

These 1996 responses are the template for all subsequent non-trivial implementation theories.

02

Bishop’s Pixies

Mark Bishop’s Dancing with Pixies (2002, ongoing) is the contemporary pancomputationalism argument most directly parallel to Lerchner. Over a finite time window, every open physical system implements the trace of some finite-state automaton. If strong computationalism is right, phenomenal consciousness is ubiquitous — “pixies” dance everywhere. Either strong computationalism fails or panpsychism is true.

The argument is almost exactly Lerchner’s with different vocabulary. Both start from a formal triviality result; both argue consciousness identified with computational states becomes observer-dependent or ubiquitous; both conclude the functionalist route is blocked.

03

Piccinini’s Mechanistic Account

Gualtiero Piccinini’s Physical Computation: A Mechanistic Account (OUP 2015) is the most developed contemporary response. A physical system is a computing system just in case it is a mechanism one of whose functions is to manipulate vehicles based on differences between their parts according to rules over the vehicles.

The load-bearing move is teleological: computing systems are those with computational functions. A rock has no function; a CPU does. On this account, most physical systems do not compute, and the Putnam triviality result fails.

04

Miłkowski’s Defence

Marcin Miłkowski extends and defends the non-trivial implementation tradition. Gerrymandered mappings (the ones Putnam’s proof relies on) fail to support the relevant counterfactuals and are excluded on principled grounds.

Miłkowski is aggressive: “practically all popular objections against the claim that brains are computers are either based on uncharitable interpretation or simply wrong.” For AI: current systems compute on the mechanistic account. Whether that computation is sufficient for consciousness depends on the further theory one holds.

05

Lerchner’s Extension

Lerchner’s mapmaker argument is a renewed attack on the non-trivial implementation tradition. Piccinini and Miłkowski argue only some systems have computational functions; gerrymandered mappings don’t correspond to genuine functions. Lerchner responds: the notion of “computational function” itself requires an observer to pick out which mechanisms have it.

Who picks voltage levels as vehicles rather than as heat dissipation patterns? The CPU’s designers. The function is historical and teleological — and therefore observer-assigned.

06

The Crux

The debate comes down to a single technical question: can a non-trivial theory of implementation restrict the mapmaker’s freedom enough to preserve the functionalist programme?

If yes: Bishop-Lerchner reductio fails; functionalism survives. If no: reductio succeeds; functionalism cannot survive. The literature has not settled this. Both sides have sophisticated defences and survive multiple rounds of criticism. The debate is well-formed and genuinely open.

07

What the Debate Does Not Settle

Even if non-trivial implementation succeeds: it does not establish consciousness is computational; it does not establish current AI instantiates the right computations for consciousness; it does not resolve whether substrate considerations (S5, S6) are orthogonal or intertwined.

The debate is about the coherence of the functionalist programme, not about whether that programme is true.

08

Three Positions

After S7, three positions have clear contours. Strong functionalist (Piccinini, Miłkowski, Bach, Bostrom, Chalmers-with-caveats, Bengio): non-trivial implementation blocks triviality; functionalism survives. Substrate-sensitive (Seth, Godfrey-Smith, Thompson, Wiese, Simon): consciousness requires specific substrate properties. Formal-skeptical (Lerchner, Bishop): mapmaker argument defeats all implementation theories.

The positions are combinable. Substrate-sensitive + formal-skeptical is the strongest anti-functionalist compound. The combinations determine how the survey’s thesis lands.

01

Historical Roots

The contemporary pancomputationalism debate inherits three classical moves.

Putnam 1988 — The Triviality Theorem

In Representation and Reality, Hilary Putnam proved that under a permissive enough mapping, every physical system implements every finite-state automaton. The wall in a room, interpreted the right way, implements word processing software. Putnam took the result as a reductio — if strong computational functionalism entails triviality, computational functionalism must be wrong.

Chalmers 1996 — Non-Trivial Implementation

In Does a Rock Implement Every Finite-State Automaton? (Synthese), David Chalmers developed a non-trivial implementation theory to block Putnam’s result. Implementation requires not just a mapping but a causal mapping — abstract state transitions must be realized by causal transitions in the physical system. Under this restriction, rocks do not implement WordStar (rocks lack the right causal structure); the triviality result is avoided.

Copeland 1996 — Counterfactual Support

In What Is Computation? (Synthese), Jack Copeland developed a related counterfactual-based account. A physical system implements a computation when its causal dispositions support counterfactuals matching the computation’s state-transition rules. If the system would transition from state A to state B were certain conditions to obtain, then it implements the A→B rule; no mere ex-post mapping suffices.

The Template

These 1996 responses to Putnam’s triviality are the template for all subsequent non-trivial implementation theories. Piccinini’s mechanistic account is a development of the causal-structure approach; Miłkowski’s work extends and defends it. Lerchner’s mapmaker argument can be read as a renewed attack on all such accounts.

02

Bishop’s Pixies

Mark Bishop’s Dancing with Pixies: Strong Artificial Intelligence and Panpsychism (originally 2002, reprinted in collected editions through 2020+) is the contemporary pancomputationalism argument most directly parallel to Lerchner.

The Three Stages

First, the formal result. Over a finite time window, every open physical system implements the trace of a particular finite-state automaton — a specific sequence of state transitions that could, under the right interpretation, be read as executing that automaton. Bishop calls the abstract conscious-state counterparts of such traces “ubiquitous little pixies.”

Second, the reductio. If the execution of a computer program instantiates phenomenal consciousness like experiencing pain or smelling perfume, then phenomenal consciousness must be ubiquitous — in a cup of tea, in the chair one is sitting on, in the air currents around one’s body. Either strong computational accounts of consciousness fail, or panpsychism is true.

Third, the preferred horn. Bishop treats panpsychism as absurd and concludes that strong computational accounts of consciousness must fail. Machines cannot be conscious — at least, not via the computational route.

The Almost-Identity with Lerchner

The argument is almost exactly Lerchner’s with a different vocabulary. Both start from a formal triviality result. Both argue that consciousness identified with computational states would then be observer-dependent or ubiquitous. Both conclude that the route from computation to consciousness is blocked. The difference is mostly framing: Bishop emphasizes the panpsychism reductio; Lerchner emphasizes the causal-circularity diagnosis.

The 2021 Development

Bishop’s more recent paper, Artificial Intelligence Is Stupid and Causal Reasoning Will Not Fix It (Frontiers in Psychology, 2021), develops these themes in the era of deep learning. The argument against strong computationalism remains; the application to LLMs is new.

03

Piccinini’s Mechanistic Account

Gualtiero Piccinini’s Physical Computation: A Mechanistic Account (OUP 2015) is the most developed contemporary response to the triviality threat.

The Central Claim

A physical system is a computing system just in case it is a mechanism one of whose functions is to manipulate vehicles based solely on differences between different portions of the vehicles according to a rule defined over the vehicles.

Three Load-Bearing Features

Mechanisms. A mechanism is a specific organized causal structure — parts performing operations, organized spatially and temporally, producing a phenomenon. Computing mechanisms manipulate vehicles according to rules.

Vehicles. Physical entities that carry information. What matters is not what they are made of but the function they perform in the mechanism — differences between vehicles, not intrinsic properties, get manipulated.

Functions. Computing systems are systems one of whose functions is computational. This teleological element distinguishes genuine computing systems from arbitrary physical systems that happen to have mappings to computational descriptions.

Why Triviality Fails

On Piccinini’s account, the Putnam triviality result fails because most physical systems do not have computational functions. The wall behind one’s back is not a WordStar implementation because no one and nothing assigns it the function of running WordStar. Only a system whose mechanism has a computational function is a computing system.

The 2020 Extension

Piccinini’s Neurocognitive Mechanisms: Explaining Biological Cognition (OUP 2020) extends the account to biological cognition. The brain computes because neural mechanisms have computational functions; the account does not entail that consciousness follows from computation, but restores ground for taking brain-computation claims seriously.

04

Miłkowski’s Defence

Marcin Miłkowski’s work extends and defends the non-trivial implementation tradition against triviality objections.

The Main Argument

While some physicists and philosophers claim that all physical processes are computational or algorithmic in a strong sense — a claim that would make computationalism utterly trivial — a non-trivial theory of implementation blocks this result by restricting which physical processes count as implementing which computations.

A physical system implements a computation when it has the right kind of functional organization — roughly, when the causal transitions among its physical states track the transitions specified by the computation under a principled (non-gerrymandered) mapping. Gerrymandered mappings are excluded because they fail to support the relevant counterfactuals.

The Aggressive Defence

Miłkowski is more aggressive than Piccinini in defending the computationalist programme. His explicit claim: “practically all popular objections against the claim that brains are computers are either based on uncharitable interpretation or simply wrong.” The claim that brains are computers is, on his view, likely true, relevant to contemporary cognitive science, and non-trivial.

For AI

Contemporary AI systems (transformers, reinforcement learning agents, large language models) are computers on the mechanistic account — their architectures are designed precisely to have computational functions. Whether those computational profiles are sufficient for consciousness depends on the specific theory of consciousness. But the base claim that these systems compute is not threatened by triviality worries.

05

Lerchner’s Extension of the Attack

Lerchner’s mapmaker argument can be read as a renewed attack on the non-trivial implementation tradition.

The Move

Piccinini and Miłkowski argue that only some physical systems have computational functions; the gerrymandered mappings the triviality proof relies on do not correspond to genuine computational functions. Lerchner’s argument responds: the very notion of “computational function” requires an observer to pick out which mechanisms have it.

The Example

Consider the Piccinini account. A CPU is a computing system because its mechanism has the function of manipulating voltage levels according to rules. Who picks out the voltage levels as vehicles rather than as, say, heat dissipation patterns? Who picks out the rules as computational rather than, say, thermodynamic? The answer, on the mechanistic account, is that the CPU’s designers picked these out — the function is historical and teleological.

The Challenge

If the function is observer-assigned, then the computation the system performs is observer-assigned. The vehicles are voltage levels rather than heat patterns because the designer treated them as such. The same physical system, described by a different observer with different interests, would not be doing the same computation — or perhaps would not be doing computation at all.

The Possible Reply

Piccinini and Miłkowski could concede the observer-dependence of the function assignment without conceding the problem for consciousness. The argument: even if the function is observer-assigned, the causal structure the function tracks is observer-independent. Consciousness, if it tracks computational structure, would track the observer-independent causal structure, not the observer-assigned function. Whether this reply works depends on exactly what computational structure is.

06

The Crux

The pancomputationalism debate comes down to a single technical question: can a non-trivial theory of implementation restrict the mapmaker’s freedom enough to preserve the functionalist programme?

The Two Outcomes

If yes

The Bishop-Lerchner reductio fails. Some physical systems are genuinely computing consciousness-relevant functions; others are not; the distinction is principled and observer-independent. Computational functionalism survives the triviality objection.

If no

The Bishop-Lerchner reductio succeeds. Any choice of mapping from physics to computational description is observer-imposed; no amount of mechanistic or functional restriction removes this. Computational functionalism cannot survive the triviality objection.

The State of Play

The literature has not settled this question. The non-trivial implementation theories (Chalmers 1996, Copeland 1996, Piccinini 2015, Miłkowski ongoing) are technically sophisticated and have survived multiple rounds of criticism. The triviality arguments (Putnam 1988, Bishop 2002, Lerchner 2026) have likewise survived multiple rounds of defence. The debate is well-formed and genuinely open.

How one reads the debate is often correlated with background commitments about what consciousness is. Those persuaded that consciousness is essentially computational are inclined to read the non-trivial implementation theories as successful. Those persuaded that consciousness has substrate-sensitive or phenomenal-qualitative features are inclined to read the triviality arguments as conclusive.

07

What the Debate Does Not Settle

Three things the pancomputationalism debate leaves unresolved, regardless of how it is ultimately adjudicated.

Computation Is Not Sufficient for Consciousness

Whether consciousness actually tracks computational structure in the way functionalists claim. The whole debate assumes that if computation is implemented non-trivially, then computational functionalism is at least coherent. This does not establish that consciousness is computational; it only establishes that the formal objection to computationalism fails.

Non-Trivial Implementation Is Not Sufficient for AI Consciousness

Even if Piccinini and Miłkowski are right that current AI systems genuinely compute, it does not follow that they compute the right things for consciousness. The Butlin–Long framework (S4) goes further than Piccinini in specifying which computations would count; the mere fact that something is a non-trivial computation does not yield consciousness.

Substrate vs Computation Is Orthogonal

Whether substrate considerations (S5, S6) are orthogonal to pancomputationalism worries or intertwined with them. Seth and Godfrey-Smith’s arguments can be accepted alongside Piccinini’s: one might think current AI systems genuinely compute but still fail to have the biological grounding for consciousness. Or the arguments can be opposed: one might think the distinction between computation and substrate is itself a functionalist artifact.

08

Three Positions Emerge

S7 is the penultimate section and is where the survey’s tensions come into sharpest focus. Three positions have clear contours by this point.

The Strong Functionalist Position

Defended by Piccinini, Miłkowski, Bach, Bostrom, and (with caveats) Chalmers and Bengio. Non-trivial implementation theories block the triviality objection. Computational functionalism survives; the empirical research programme (Butlin–Long) continues. AI consciousness is possible once the right computational profiles are instantiated.

The Substrate-Sensitive Position

Defended by Seth, Godfrey-Smith, Thompson, Wiese, Simon. Consciousness requires specific properties of self-organizing biological or biologically-analogous systems. Current AI lacks these. Future AI could have them with appropriate engineering.

The Formal-Skeptical Position

Defended by Lerchner and (in a different idiom) Bishop. The mapmaker argument shows that no non-trivial theory of implementation can fully escape observer-dependence. The functionalist programme is blocked in principle, not merely currently unpromising.

The Combinations

The positions are not mutually exclusive. One can hold the substrate-sensitive position for empirical reasons and the formal-skeptical position as a further argument — this is the compound position examined at the end of S5. One can hold the functionalist position and the substrate-sensitive position together (this is what the Butlin–Long framework implicitly does: different indicators for different theories). The combinations determine how the survey’s thesis will land. S8 adds one further complication: that phenomenal consciousness itself may be illusory.
