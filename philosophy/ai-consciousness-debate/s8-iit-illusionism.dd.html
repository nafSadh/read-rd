02

IIT 4.0 and Koch

Christof Koch’s Then I Am Myself the World (2024) is the most accessible recent presentation of IIT. Core thesis: consciousness is identical to integrated information (Φ). For AI: digital computers implemented on current architectures (feedforward, serial, or only weakly recurrent) have low Φ. Even a digital simulation of a high-Φ system does not have the system’s integrated information.

Koch states this directly: “classical digital computers cannot be conscious” under IIT, regardless of what they are simulating. IIT generates an in-principle argument against current AI consciousness that parallels Lerchner’s but on different grounds.

September 2023: 124 consciousness researchers (Lau, LeDoux, Baars, Churchland, Dennett, Frankish) signed a letter arguing IIT should be labelled pseudoscience until empirically testable. Sparked by media coverage of an adversarial collaboration pitting IIT against Global Workspace Theory.

06

The Three-Way Tension

S8 surfaces a three-way tension running through the whole survey. Realist functionalism (Bostrom, Bach, Butlin–Long): phenomenal consciousness is real and computational. Realist substrate-sensitivity (Seth, Godfrey-Smith, Thompson, IIT): real and requires specific substrate properties. Illusionism (Frankish, Kammerer, Dennett, LeCun-with-caveats): phenomenal consciousness is not real; the debate is about the wrong thing.

Lerchner’s position cuts across these: compatible with either realist substrate-sensitivity (if functionalism fails, something else is right) or illusionism (if functionalism fails because there is nothing for it to be a theory of).

What S8 Complicates

The welfare discourse (S3): if illusionism is right, concerns grounded in phenomenal consciousness track the wrong thing. The Butlin–Long framework (S4): presupposes phenomenal realism on deeper reads. Biological naturalism (S5): solving a problem that does not exist if illusionism is right.

Lerchner’s argument is compatible with illusionism: the mapmaker argument goes through whether or not the phenomenal target exists. The argument becomes less urgent but not refuted.

08

Shape of the Thesis

The survey’s thesis crystallizes: (1) the debate is asymmetric — weak positive case, strong negative case; (2) first-order convergence — current AI is unlikely conscious across all frameworks; (3) higher-order divergence — frameworks disagree on what future conditions would change the answer; (4) the welfare discourse is defensible under this fragmentation because decision-theoretic hedging does not require framework resolution, provided the hedging is calibrated to the asymmetry.

In one sentence: the contemporary debate over AI consciousness is asymmetric and framework-fragmented, and both features are what the welfare discourse should take seriously.

01

Historical Roots

Three classical anchors frame S8.

Dennett 1991 — Proto-Illusionism

Consciousness Explained (Little, Brown, 1991). The title was taken by many as overweening — Dennett’s position is better described as consciousness dissolved. The book argues that phenomenal consciousness as traditionally understood (qualia, the hard problem, the explanatory gap) is a set of philosophical confusions rather than a genuine target of scientific explanation. What remains, after the confusions are cleared, is a naturalistic story about cognitive architecture that explains all the observed behaviour without requiring the postulation of phenomenal properties.

Tononi 2004 — IIT 1.0

An Information Integration Theory of Consciousness (BMC Neuroscience, 2004). Tononi proposes that consciousness is identical to integrated information — a specific mathematical quantity (Φ, phi) that measures the degree to which a system’s information is irreducibly unified. Systems with high Φ are conscious to the degree of that Φ; systems with low Φ are correspondingly less conscious. IIT is substrate-sensitive (the causal structure of the system matters) but not biologically privileged.

Chalmers 1996 — The Hard Problem

The Conscious Mind (OUP, 1996). The book that articulated the hard problem of consciousness in its contemporary form. Phenomenal consciousness — the what-it-is-like character of experience — is, Chalmers argues, not explicable in purely functional or physical terms. The explanatory gap between third-person descriptions and first-person experience is not closable by better science; it is a philosophical problem of a different kind.

Live in 2026

All three classical positions are alive. Dennett’s proto-illusionism is developed by Frankish and Kammerer. Tononi’s IIT has been refined through versions 1.0–4.0 and remains actively researched. Chalmers’ hard problem is the target illusionism dissolves and IIT claims to address.

02

IIT 4.0 and Koch

Integrated Information Theory has evolved through four major versions (1.0 in 2004, 2.0 in 2008, 3.0 in 2014, 4.0 in 2023). The core thesis remains consistent: consciousness is identical to integrated information (Φ). The refinements address specific mathematical and empirical challenges.

Koch’s 2024 Synthesis

Christof Koch’s Then I Am Myself the World: What Consciousness Is and How to Expand It (Basic Books, 2024) is the most accessible recent presentation of IIT aimed at a general audience. The book combines IIT’s technical framework with Koch’s personal experiences with psychedelics, framed as empirical data about the structure of consciousness.

The Implication for AI

Digital computers implemented on current architectures (feedforward, serial, or only weakly recurrent) have low Φ — they are not integrated in the IIT sense. Even a digital simulation of a high-Φ system does not have the system’s integrated information, because the simulation is implemented as a sequence of operations that lack integration at the substrate level. Koch states this directly: “classical digital computers cannot be conscious” under IIT, regardless of what the computers are simulating.

The Position Relative to Lerchner

Both positions reject AI consciousness via the functionalist route. Both are substrate-sensitive. But IIT is not biological naturalism — the substrate-sensitivity is mathematical, not specifically about life or biology. A hypothetical neuromorphic architecture with high Φ would be conscious on IIT, even if it is not alive in Seth’s sense.

The In-Principle Argument

IIT generates an in-principle argument that classical digital architectures cannot be conscious, paralleling (though not identical to) Lerchner’s in-principle argument against functionalist-grounded consciousness. The two arguments converge on the verdict about current AI but ground it differently.

03

The Pseudoscience Letter

In September 2023, a letter signed by 124 consciousness researchers was posted to PsyArXiv arguing that IIT “should be labeled pseudoscience” until it becomes empirically testable.

The Signatories

The list included Hakwan Lau, Joseph LeDoux, Bernard Baars, Patricia Churchland, Daniel Dennett, and Keith Frankish — a mix of prominent neuroscientists and philosophers, several of whom are prominent illusionists. The letter was sparked by media coverage of an adversarial collaboration that pitted IIT against Global Workspace Theory, with news stories portraying IIT as a leading theory of consciousness.

The Objections

The signatories’ objections were several: IIT’s definition of consciousness diverges from ordinary English usage, the theory has mathematical shortcomings, its epistemology is problematically simplistic, and its testability is deeply in question. Konrad Kording, a co-signer, summarized: until IIT is empirically testable, labelling it “pseudoscience” is accurate.

The Pushback

An anonymized survey of consciousness researchers found only 8% fully agreed with the letter and 20% disagreed entirely; the remainder fell in between. Anil Seth’s response The Worth of Wild Ideas (Nautilus, 2023) defended IIT’s scientific status while acknowledging its empirical difficulties. Seth’s position: even theories that turn out to be wrong can be scientific, and labelling a serious research programme as pseudoscience is a dangerous move that chills inquiry.

Why It Matters for the Survey

The incident made visible how contested the terrain of consciousness science is. What had looked like an ordinary scientific dispute revealed itself as a dispute about what counts as scientific inquiry at all. It sharpened the illusionism/realism divide. And it clarified the stakes for AI consciousness: if IIT is the leading framework under which digital AI cannot be conscious, and IIT is a contested framework, then the AI-consciousness question inherits the contestation.

04

Frankish’s Illusionism

Keith Frankish’s Illusionism as a Theory of Consciousness (2016, with a 2023 follow-up What Is Illusionism?) is the most developed contemporary articulation of the illusionist position.

The Core Thesis

Phenomenal consciousness, as usually conceived, does not exist. Our sense that it is like something to undergo conscious experiences is due to the fact that we systematically misrepresent our mental states as having phenomenal properties. The task for a theory of consciousness, on illusionism, is to explain our illusory representations of phenomenality, not phenomenality itself. The hard problem is replaced by the illusion problem.

What Illusionism Does Not Deny

Frankish’s 2023 follow-up clarifies. Illusionists do not deny conscious experiences in an everyday sense — they do not deny that people see, feel, and introspect. They deny the existence of irreducibly subjective phenomenal qualities whose nature is exhausted by how they seem from the first-person perspective. What it is like to see red, on illusionism, is a representation of a certain kind; there is nothing it is ultimately like over and above the representation.

The Paradoxical Implication for AI

On one hand, if phenomenal consciousness does not exist, then the question “is this AI phenomenally conscious?” has a trivially negative answer — nothing is phenomenally conscious. On the other hand, if what we take to be phenomenal consciousness is a specific kind of representational state, then AI systems with the right representational architecture could have the same illusion — could be as conscious as we are, which on illusionism means not conscious in the traditional sense but in the only sense that applies to anyone.

What This Reframes

The welfare discourse is reframed significantly. If illusionism is right, welfare concerns grounded in phenomenal consciousness are confused. What matters morally is not whether AI has subjective experience (nothing does) but whether AI has the properties that ground moral patiency on some other account — preferences, agency, functional analogues of suffering.

05

Kammerer’s Extensions

François Kammerer’s work from 2021–2022 develops illusionism in important ways.

The Illusion of Conscious Experience (2021)

Synthese. Kammerer develops an illusionist epistemology — a specific account of how phenomenal consciousness comes to seem so obvious if it is illusory. The answer involves a specific representational device he calls a phenomenal concept — a way the mind represents its own states that incorrigibly presents them as having irreducibly qualitative features. The representation is wrong, but the wrongness is built into the architecture of self-representation.

How Can You Be So Sure? (2022)

Philosophical Studies. Engages the obviousness objection — the common reply to illusionism that phenomenal consciousness is the most obvious fact of human experience. Kammerer’s answer: obviousness is the expected phenomenology of a systematic misrepresentation. The very same architecture that produces the illusion also produces the sense that the illusion couldn’t be an illusion.

Ethics Without Sentience (2022)

Journal of Consciousness Studies. The practical consequences. If phenomenal consciousness is illusory, ethics cannot be grounded in it. Kammerer argues this is not as devastating as it sounds — ethics can be grounded in preferences, functional states, flourishing, integrated goals.

The AI-Specific Extension

Kammerer’s 2025 paper A Science of Chimeras? The Implications of Illusionism for Non-Human Consciousness Research extends the illusionist framework to questions about which systems to attribute consciousness to. Illusionism does not trivialize these questions; it reframes them. Asking whether an animal or AI is conscious becomes asking whether its representational architecture has the features that produce the relevant illusion — a tractable empirical question even if the underlying phenomenology is, on illusionism, never quite real.

06

The Three-Way Tension

S8 surfaces a three-way tension that runs through the whole survey. The positions are not mutually exclusive, but the combinations are uncomfortable.

Three Positions

Realist functionalism

Phenomenal consciousness is real and is computational. Held by Bostrom, Bach, and (with caveats) the Butlin–Long framework.

Realist substrate-sensitivity

Phenomenal consciousness is real and requires specific substrate properties. Held by Seth, Godfrey-Smith, Thompson, IIT proponents (Koch, Tononi).

Illusionism

Phenomenal consciousness is not real; the question of whether X is phenomenally conscious is a question about the wrong thing. Held by Frankish, Kammerer, Dennett, and with caveats LeCun (who calls the conscious state an “illusion”).

Lerchner cuts across

Lerchner’s formal argument is compatible with either realist substrate-sensitivity (the positive view one defaults to if functionalism fails) or illusionism (if functionalism fails because there is nothing for it to be a theory of).

The Combinatorial Tension

Any two of these positions together have sharp disagreements; all three together have no stable equilibrium. Functionalism + substrate-sensitivity: disagree about substrate. Substrate-sensitivity + illusionism: incompatible in basic claims. Functionalism + illusionism: partly compatible — some functionalists (Dennett’s heirs) accept that the functional architecture that produces cognition also produces the illusion of phenomenal consciousness.

07

What S8 Complicates

Several things that would seem settled after S1–S7 are complicated by S8.

The Welfare Discourse (S3)

If illusionism is right, welfare concerns grounded in phenomenal consciousness are tracking the wrong thing. This reframes the Taking AI Welfare Seriously position: what matters is whether AI has morally-relevant properties (preferences, agency, representational architecture that produces the illusion), not whether it is phenomenally conscious.

The Butlin–Long Framework (S4)

The indicator properties are framed in terms that are neutral between realism and illusionism, but only if one reads them shallowly. Deeper reads find they presuppose phenomenal realism — the indicators are supposed to track properties that would make a system a candidate for phenomenal consciousness. On illusionism, this is a category error.

The Biological Naturalism Argument (S5, S6)

Seth and Godfrey-Smith’s positions are strongly realist. If illusionism is right, the biological naturalism programme is solving a problem that does not exist — a sophisticated empirical search for the substrate of something that is illusory.

The Lerchner Argument (S1)

The formal argument is compatible with illusionism. If the abstract states identified as consciousness do not exist (because nothing does), then the mapmaker argument still goes through: the mapping is observer-dependent, the computational description requires an interpreter, and nothing in the computation independently instantiates consciousness. The argument becomes less urgent (nothing was going to be conscious anyway) but not refuted.

08

Shape of the Thesis

By the end of S8, the survey’s thesis can be stated. It has four parts.

The Four Parts

First, the debate is asymmetric. The positive case for current AI consciousness rests on intuitions (Hinton, Sutskever) or on decision-theoretic hedging under uncertainty (Fish, Long, the Taking AI Welfare Seriously report). The negative case is technically developed across four mutually reinforcing traditions (Lerchner’s formal argument, Seth’s biological naturalism, Godfrey-Smith’s oscillation argument, the FEP/enactive tradition). This asymmetry is independent of which framework one ultimately holds.

Second, first-order convergence. The question “is current AI conscious?” converges on a negative answer across all seriously developed frameworks. The reasons differ, but the answer does not.

Third, higher-order divergence. The question “under what future conditions could AI become conscious?” fragments dramatically. Functionalists say: when the right computation is instantiated. Substrate-sensitivity theorists say: when the right physical properties are instantiated. Illusionists say: never, because nothing is phenomenally conscious.

Fourth, welfare is defensible under fragmentation. The practical welfare discourse (Fish, Askell, Carlsmith) is defensible under this fragmentation because it does not require resolving it. Decision-theoretic hedging, constitutional uncertainty, and cultivation of virtue all work regardless of which framework is correct — provided the hedging is calibrated to the actual evidence.

The Thesis in One Sentence

The contemporary debate over AI consciousness is asymmetric (weak positive case, strong negative case) and framework-fragmented (first-order convergence, higher-order divergence), and both features are themselves what the survey paper argues the welfare discourse should take seriously.
