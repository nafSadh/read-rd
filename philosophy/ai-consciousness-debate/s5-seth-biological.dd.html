01

Historical Roots

Searle’s Rediscovery of the Mind (1992) coined “biological naturalism” — consciousness is a biological phenomenon caused by and realized in specific neurobiological processes, not realizable in arbitrary substrates. Maturana & Varela’s Autopoiesis and Cognition (1980) developed the life-mind continuity thesis: living systems are self-producing, and what makes them alive is also the ground of whatever primitive mentality they have.

Seth extends the first thread; Godfrey-Smith extends the second. Neither reduces to the classical sources, but the inheritance is clear.

02

Seth’s Central Thesis

Conscious artificial intelligence and biological naturalism (2024 BBS) argues: life is necessary for consciousness. Not life as biologists currently understand it, but something with the structural properties of life — self-maintenance, active regulation, embodied engagement with existential threats. Consciousness is self-modelling that living systems do as part of being alive.

Three moves: (1) identify biases that make computational AI consciousness seem more plausible than it is; (2) argue predictive processing is substrate-sensitive; (3) reach a graded conclusion — current AI trajectories unlikely, brain-like/life-like AI possible.

03

Being You

Seth’s 2021 book argues conscious experience is a controlled hallucination — perceptions are best-guesses about external reality generated under priors favouring predictions consistent with bodily regulation. The brain is fundamentally an organ of homeostasis; conscious perception is a side effect of regulatory function.

The implication for AI: a system without homeostatic stake in its own continued existence lacks the grounding that makes predictive processing into conscious perception.

Peter Godfrey-Smith’s Living on Earth (2024) makes a strikingly specific claim: consciousness depends on electrical oscillations moving rhythmically across cell membranes in living brains. Theta, gamma, alpha bands. Not abstract computational patterns but specific dynamical processes in bioelectrical media — not reproducible in silicon as currently designed.

A silicon computer simulating these oscillations does so at a different level of abstraction. The simulation tracks the pattern but does not instantiate the specific bioelectrical dynamics.

06

Brain-Like Scenarios

Seth does not close the door on AI consciousness. Brain-like AI would instantiate specific dynamical properties of biological neural activity — oscillations at the right frequencies, recurrent dynamics, prediction integrated with bodily regulation. Possibly implementable in neuromorphic hardware or organoid-computer hybrids.

Life-like AI would instantiate self-maintenance structure — active regulation of internal conditions, boundary-preserving dynamics making continued existence a genuine stake. The concession matters: Seth is not a global skeptic, only a specific one about functionalism as currently practiced.

07

Implications for Current AI

Current AI is unlikely to be conscious — but the reasons diverge from Lerchner’s. On Lerchner: the functionalist route is incoherent. On Seth/Godfrey-Smith: current AI lacks specific substrate-sensitive dynamics (oscillations, homeostatic grounding, self-maintenance).

For Fish’s welfare work (S3): if Lerchner is right, mechanistic-interpretability searches are at the wrong level; if Seth is right, they are at the wrong substrate. The remedial programmes differ.

08

Meeting Lerchner at a Different Angle

The two arguments converge on rejecting substrate-independence but from different directions. Lerchner’s move is formal — f(p) = A is observer-imposed, so abstract states inherit observer-dependence. Seth’s move is scientific — predictive-processing-plus-homeostasis identifies specific dynamics that silicon doesn’t instantiate.

The arguments are compatible but not identical. Their conjunction yields a specific compound position: computational functionalism is formally confused and an alternative substrate-sensitive programme is empirically more promising. Neither argument alone secures this.

01

Historical Roots

Two classical works frame the contemporary biological-naturalism revival.

Searle’s Biological Naturalism (1992)

John Searle, in The Rediscovery of the Mind (MIT, 1992), coined the term biological naturalism and defended the position: consciousness is a biological phenomenon caused by and realized in specific neurobiological processes. It is not a substance distinct from the physical, but neither is it realizable in arbitrary physical substrates. The specific causal powers of neurons — not their functional profile abstractly described — are what produce conscious experience.

Searle’s argument goes further than Lerchner’s: he claims not only that computational description requires an observer but that the right level of explanation for consciousness is biological physiology, not any level of abstract description.

Autopoiesis (Maturana & Varela, 1980)

Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela’s Autopoiesis and Cognition (Reidel, 1980) developed the theoretical framework that underlies much of the contemporary substrate-matters literature. Living systems are autopoietic — self-producing, self-maintaining organizations that actively preserve their own boundary conditions against environmental disruption.

On the autopoiesis view, what makes something alive is a specific kind of recursive causal organization, and this organization is also the ground of whatever primitive mentality such systems have. Life and mind are continuous. The mind begins where self-maintaining life begins.

The Two Threads

Seth extends Searle’s emphasis on the specific causal powers of biology. Godfrey-Smith extends the Maturana-Varela life-mind continuity thesis, grounding it in the cellular electrical dynamics of living brains. Neither position is reducible to the classical sources, but the inheritance is clear.

02

Seth’s Central Thesis

Anil Seth’s 2024 paper Conscious artificial intelligence and biological naturalism (published in Behavioral and Brain Sciences, 2025) is the most developed philosophical articulation of the substrate-matters position from contemporary neuroscience. The argument is structured to engage computational functionalism directly rather than merely oppose it.

The Central Claim

Life is necessary for consciousness. Not obviously life as biologists currently understand it, but something with the structural properties that characterize life — self-maintenance against dissolution, active regulation of internal states, embodied engagement with environmental conditions that threaten the system’s persistence. Consciousness is not a free-floating computational pattern; it is a specific kind of self-modelling that living systems do as part of being alive.

Three Stages of Argument

First, Seth identifies biases that make computational AI consciousness seem more plausible than it is: the tendency to interpret language-using systems as minded (the Shanahan role-play point), the cultural association of “intelligence” with computation, the philosophical tradition of treating abstract causal topology as the essence of mentality.

Second, predictive processing, properly understood, is substrate-sensitive. A predictive-processing brain generates perceptions by actively inferring the causes of its sensory inputs under a goal of maintaining its own bodily integrity. A system that merely instantiates the same computational structure without the life-maintenance goal is not doing predictive processing in the relevant sense.

Third, the graded conclusion. Current AI trajectories — transformer language models, reinforcement learning agents — are unlikely to produce conscious AI because they lack the life-maintenance grounding. But AI built to be brain-like and/or life-like could in principle be conscious. The door is not closed, but the route is not computational functionalism.

Why This Matters for the Debate

Seth’s position is constructive where Lerchner’s is diagnostic. Lerchner says the functionalist route fails. Seth says an alternative substrate-sensitive route might succeed. The two positions are compatible but supply different futures for AI consciousness research.

03

Being You

Seth’s 2021 book Being You: A New Science of Consciousness (Faber) lays the groundwork for the 2024 paper. The book’s central claim: conscious experience is a controlled hallucination generated by the brain’s predictive machinery.

The Predictive-Processing Framework

Perceptions are not readings of external reality but best-guesses about external reality generated under priors that favour predictions consistent with bodily regulation. The bodily regulation framing is load-bearing. The brain is fundamentally an organ of homeostasis — it exists to keep the body within viable physiological ranges. Conscious perception is a side effect of this regulatory function.

We experience the world in ways that best support the body’s continued existence. Pain tracks tissue damage because tissue damage threatens homeostasis. Pleasure tracks states conducive to survival and reproduction. Colour, depth, and timbre are constructed in ways useful for an embodied biological agent navigating a complex environment.

Implication for AI

A system without a homeostatic body — without the specific kind of stake in its own continued existence that biological organisms have — would not have the grounding that makes predictive processing into conscious perception. Seth does not claim this is definitive; he claims it is the theoretically motivated position.

Overlap with Free Energy (S6)

Seth’s framework overlaps substantially with Karl Friston’s free-energy principle, which Wiese develops further in S6. Both treat predictive processing as grounded in self-organizing life-maintenance. The main difference is emphasis: Seth centres the biological body; Friston centres the mathematical structure.

04

Electrical Oscillations

Peter Godfrey-Smith’s Living on Earth: Life, Consciousness, and the Making of the Natural World (FSG, 2024) is the third book in his trilogy after Other Minds (2016) and Metazoa (2020).

The Specific Claim

Consciousness depends on electrical oscillations moving rhythmically across cell membranes in living brains. These oscillations — observable across mammalian brains, in octopus nervous systems, and arguably in simpler nervous systems — are not abstract computational patterns but specific dynamical processes in bioelectrical media. They are not, Godfrey-Smith argues, reproducible in silicon hardware as currently designed.

Why the Argument Has Bite

The oscillation patterns are empirically grounded. They have specific spatial and temporal scales (theta at 4–8 Hz, gamma at 30–80 Hz, alpha at 8–13 Hz) and specific roles in binding, attention, and memory consolidation. A silicon computer simulating these oscillations does so at a different level of abstraction — the simulation tracks the oscillation pattern but does not instantiate the specific bioelectrical dynamics.

The Companion Essay

Godfrey-Smith’s Studies on animal minds suggest consciousness is not computation (IAI, 2024) distills the argument for a general audience. The title states the thesis. The specifically biological dynamics that characterize conscious brains are not, on current evidence, reproducible by computational simulation at any level of fidelity that bears on whether the simulation is conscious.

05

Islands of Awareness

Tim Bayne, Anil Seth, and Marcello Massimini’s Are There Islands of Awareness? (Trends in Neurosciences, 2020) is unusual in the contemporary literature: it asks whether conscious states might exist in complete isolation from sensory input and motor output.

The Concrete Scenarios

Ex cranio brains

Isolated brain tissue kept alive in vitro. Technical barriers remain, but the scenario is increasingly concrete given advances in keeping neural tissue viable outside bodies.

Hemispherotomy

Patients who have had one hemisphere surgically isolated from the other and from much of its input/output. The isolated hemisphere continues neural activity.

Cerebral organoids

Lab-grown clumps of neural tissue that display electrical activity without any sensory transduction. Whether they reach consciousness-relevant complexity is contested.

The methodological point

If any of these cases supports conscious states, the phenomenon is more robustly about the tissue than about its connections to the wider world.

Why It Matters

Islands run in two directions. On one reading, they support Seth and Godfrey-Smith: consciousness is about the specific bioelectrical dynamics of living neural tissue, which isolated brain tissue still instantiates and silicon computers do not. On another reading, islands are embarrassing for strict embodiment theorists: if an isolated brain can be conscious, the embodiment requirement is weaker than enactivists have argued (S6).

06

Brain-Like Scenarios

Seth’s 2024 paper does not close the door on AI consciousness. It specifies the conditions under which AI consciousness would become more plausible. These “brain-like scenarios” constitute the constructive part of the argument.

Brain-Like AI

Brain-like AI would instantiate the specific dynamical properties of biological neural activity — oscillations at the right frequencies, recurrent dynamics of the right kind, the integration of prediction with bodily regulation. Such AI might be physically implemented in neuromorphic hardware, organoid-computer hybrids, or some future substrate that supports the relevant dynamics. Whether any current or near-term engineering programme is heading toward this kind of system is itself contested.

Life-Like AI

Life-like AI would instantiate the self-maintenance structure that characterizes living systems — active regulation of internal state conditions that the system tracks and responds to, boundary-preserving dynamics that make the system’s continued existence a genuine stake. Such AI would not necessarily be biological; it might be a sufficiently rich artificial life form. But it would share the structural feature Seth’s argument identifies as ground for consciousness.

The Concession

Seth is not a global skeptic about machine consciousness. He is a specific skeptic about computational functionalism as currently practiced. AI engineered to be brain-like or life-like could, on his view, cross the threshold. This is the position Lerchner’s argument does not commit to either way: Lerchner’s claim is about the functionalist route; it leaves open whether substrate-sensitive routes succeed.

07

Implications for Current AI

The practical upshot for current AI systems is clear: they are unlikely to be conscious, and the reasons they are unlikely differ from the reasons Lerchner (S1) gives.

Two Convergent but Distinct Verdicts

On Lerchner’s argument, current AI is not conscious because the route from computation to consciousness is incoherent. The question of what current AI lacks, precisely, does not arise — nothing lacks what cannot be given.

On Seth’s and Godfrey-Smith’s arguments, current AI is not conscious because it lacks the specific substrate-sensitive dynamics that biological consciousness requires. The question of what current AI lacks has a concrete answer: cellular electrical oscillations (Godfrey-Smith), homeostatic grounding (Seth), self-maintenance organization (the Maturana-Varela inheritance).

The Futures Diverge

The two positions converge on the verdict about current AI but diverge on the future. Lerchner’s conclusion is robust to engineering progress — no architectural refinement makes functionalist-grounded consciousness possible. Seth’s and Godfrey-Smith’s conclusions are not robust in this way — engineering progress toward brain-like or life-like architectures could change the answer.

What It Means for Welfare

For welfare research (S3), this divergence matters. If Lerchner is right, Fish’s mechanistic-interpretability search for consciousness-analogous features is mistargeted at the wrong level. If Seth or Godfrey-Smith is right, it is mistargeted at the wrong substrate. The remedial programmes differ.

08

Meeting Lerchner at a Different Angle

Seth’s biological naturalism and Lerchner’s abstraction fallacy converge on a rejection of substrate-independent computational consciousness but from different directions. The two arguments are often conflated; they should not be.

Lerchner’s Move (Formal)

The mapping f(p) = A from physical states to abstract states is observer-imposed. Therefore abstract states are observer-dependent. Therefore consciousness identified with abstract computational states inherits the observer-dependence. The argument makes no specific claim about what consciousness is, only that it is not what computational functionalism says it is.

Seth’s Move (Scientific)

The best contemporary science of consciousness — predictive processing in self-maintaining biological systems — identifies specific dynamical properties as consciousness-relevant. These properties are substrate-sensitive. Current computational systems do not instantiate them. Therefore current computational systems are unlikely to be conscious.

Compatible but Not Identical

One could accept Lerchner’s formal argument without accepting Seth’s positive account (one might think no account of consciousness succeeds; or one might think a non-biological substrate account succeeds). One could accept Seth’s positive account without accepting Lerchner’s formal argument (one might think substrate matters for empirical-biological reasons, not formal-observer-dependence reasons).

The Compound Position

The intersection is the rejection of strong substrate-independence. A reader persuaded by both arguments has two distinct reasons to reject the functionalist programme: the formal reason (Lerchner) and the empirical-biological reason (Seth, Godfrey-Smith). The conjunction is the strongest anti-functionalist position the contemporary literature supports.
