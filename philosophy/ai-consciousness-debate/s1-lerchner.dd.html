Section 1 of 8 · The Target
Lerchner’s Argument
The abstraction fallacy, the mapmaker, causal circularitySource: Lerchner 2026
01

Historical Roots

Lerchner’s argument descends directly from two classical works: Putnam’s triviality theorem in Representation and Reality (1988) and Searle’s observer-relativity argument in his 1990 APA Presidential Address. Both claim that computation is not intrinsic to physics — that the same physical substrate implements different computations under different interpretations.

The Abstraction Fallacy reworks these observations into a sharper claim specifically about consciousness: if computation is observer-dependent, and consciousness is identified with computation, then the observer becomes a hidden precondition for consciousness itself — a circularity the functionalist programme cannot absorb.

02

The Functionalist Target

Computational functionalism holds that what matters for consciousness is the abstract causal topology, not the physical realizer. Silicon, neurons, photonics, or exotic substrates could all realize the same conscious state. The causal chain is: physics → computation → consciousness.

This is the programme operationalized by the Butlin–Long report into “indicator properties,” and cautiously defended by Chalmers (2023) in Could a Large Language Model Be Conscious? Lerchner names it the target.

03

The Implementation Equation

The paper’s formal pivot is a simple relation. Given physical state p, its successor p', and a mapping function f from physical to abstract states, functionalism requires: f(p) = A, f(p') = A'. The physical dynamics, viewed through f, must commute with the computational dynamics.

Lerchner’s key claim: f is the mapmaker’s choice, not a fact about physics. Infinitely many f’s are permissible for any given physics. The map is imposed; it is not inscribed in the electrons or the neurons.

04

Map vs Territory

Abstract states, for Lerchner, are neither Platonic ideals nor metaphysical entities. They are neurophysiological constructs — equivalence classes carved out by a learning brain that groups diverse physical configurations together because they matter the same way for its goals. Abstraction is epistemic, not ontic.

The title earns its bite here. The fallacy is treating an abstraction (the map) as if it were a feature of the territory (the physics). Substrate-independent consciousness, on this diagnosis, mistakes a useful projection for a causal structure.

05

The Causal Circularity

With f observer-chosen and abstract states epistemic, the functionalist chain reverses. The original picture — physics → computation → consciousness — gives way to: consciousness (mapmaker) → computation (selected f) → physics (interpreted through the map).

The charge is not that consciousness cannot be computational. It is that the functionalist derivation is underdetermined, because the computational description it starts from requires consciousness to be picked out in the first place.

06

Simulation vs Instantiation

Three analogies carry the intuition into everyday reasoning. A simulation of the heart does not pump blood. A GPU simulating photosynthesis does not make sugar. A melody is an abstract pattern; the musicians, not the melody, cause the sound.

Defenders of functionalism reply that consciousness is unlike pumping, photosynthesis, and melodies in the relevant respect: it is the computational pattern, not something merely represented by one. This is where Chalmers’ dancing-qualia strategy and the Butlin–Long indicator framework do their work.

07

AGI as Non-Sentient Tool

The practical conclusion is framed as relief. If computational functionalism fails, AGI systems are not candidates for phenomenal consciousness merely by virtue of their computational profile. Moral-patiency concerns tied to substrate-independent consciousness lose their foothold.

Lerchner does not argue that AGI cannot in principle be conscious — only that the specific route from sufficiently advanced computation to phenomenal experience is blocked. A conscious AGI would need to be conscious for some other reason: biological substrate, substrate-sensitive causal structure, embodiment.

08

Where the Argument Invites Pushback

Each major move is a pivot point for a different section of this survey. The mapmaker argument is the direct parallel of contemporary pancomputationalism debates (S7). The biological alternative is developed by Seth and Godfrey-Smith (S5). The welfare implication reframes the Anthropic model-welfare programme (S3).

The practitioners disagree with each other more than Lerchner disagrees with any of them. Hinton and Sutskever think current systems may already be conscious; LeCun rejects the possibility; Shanahan argues the question is malformed.

01

Historical Roots

Two classical works frame almost every contemporary discussion of whether consciousness can be computational, and both anticipate Lerchner’s central move. Neither is treated as a target in its own right by this survey — the focus is post-2020 literature — but both are load-bearing for reading the Abstraction Fallacy correctly.

Putnam’s Triviality Theorem (1988)

Hilary Putnam’s Representation and Reality proves what became known as the triviality theorem: under a permissive enough mapping, every physical system implements every finite-state automaton. The wall in a room, interpreted the right way, implements word processing software. Putnam took this as a reductio — computational functionalism, taken literally, cannot be a theory of anything determinate, because computation is not intrinsic to physics.

Searle’s Observer-Relativity (1990)

John Searle’s APA Presidential Address, Is the Brain a Digital Computer?, pushes a kindred observation in a different key. Syntax is observer-relative. A physical process becomes a computation only under an interpretation assigned from outside. “The wall behind my back is right now implementing the WordStar program, because there is some pattern of molecule movements that is isomorphic with the formal structure of WordStar.” Searle’s argument appears again in his 1992 Rediscovery of the Mind, which coins “biological naturalism” and grounds the contemporary substrate-matters literature.

Why These Count as Roots

The Abstraction Fallacy reworks the triviality result and the observer-relativity observation into a targeted argument about consciousness. The novelty is not the formal result but the causal gloss. Putnam showed the mapping f is permissive; Searle showed syntax is observer-relative; Lerchner combines them into the claim that consciousness, if identified with computation, inherits the observer-dependence — and that this inheritance is fatal to the functionalist programme.

The Continuity

Contemporary work in the same lineage — Bishop’s Dancing with Pixies (2002, ongoing) and Piccinini’s mechanistic response (2015) — is surveyed in S7. Lerchner’s contribution is not that the result is new but that its implications for consciousness are sharper than previously drawn.

02

The Functionalist Target

The position Lerchner sets out to undermine has a precise shape. Identifying it precisely matters because the paper’s force depends on the target being an accurate description of what contemporary functionalists actually claim.

Three Commitments of Computational Functionalism

Substrate independence. What matters for consciousness is the abstract causal topology, not the physical realizer. The same computation running on silicon, biological tissue, photonics, or exotic substrates would be conscious.

The realization relation. Physics realizes computation; computation realizes (or is identical with) consciousness. The chain is: physics → computation → consciousness.

Practical implication for AGI. Artificial systems, whatever their substrate, are candidates for moral patiency once their computational profile approximates that of a conscious organism.

Who Holds This View

The Butlin–Long report (2023) operationalizes functionalism into “indicator properties” drawn from multiple neuroscientific theories. Chalmers (2023), in Could a Large Language Model Be Conscious?, cautiously defends functionalism while noting the case for current LLMs is weak. Joscha Bach holds a strong version — consciousness as substrate-free second-order perception. Nick Bostrom assumes substrate-independence in Deep Utopia (2024) when he posits digital minds as “super-beneficiaries.”

What Functionalists Would Deny

Functionalists do not typically claim any pattern of computation produces consciousness. Most require specific architectural features: recurrence, global broadcasting, higher-order representation, agential unity. The Butlin–Long framework catalogues these as indicators. Lerchner’s argument does not dispute that some computations are more plausibly consciousness-related than others — it disputes that the computational description is substrate-producing at all.

Steel-Manning the Target

The strongest version of computational functionalism Lerchner must defeat is not “any computation is conscious,” but “the right kind of computation, under the right non-trivial theory of implementation, is conscious.” This is the ground where Piccinini (2015) and Miłkowski argue the triviality result can be blocked.

03

The Implementation Equation

The formal pivot of the paper can be stated with a handful of symbols. The stakes of the argument rest on how these symbols are interpreted.

The Commutative Relation

Let p be a physical state, p' its successor under the physical dynamics. Let f be a mapping function from physical states to abstract computational states. Let A = f(p) and A' = f(p').

The functionalist picture requires the transition p → p' to commute with A → A' via f. That is: the physical dynamics, viewed through f, look like the right abstract computation. If they do, the physical system is said to implement that computation.

Where the Paper Locates the Problem

The commutativity condition is permissive. For any physical dynamics, infinitely many mapping functions f satisfy it — some yielding one computation, some another, some none that we would recognize as computation at all. Lerchner’s claim is that f is not a fact about the electrons or neurons. It is a choice made by an interpreting observer.

If f is observer-chosen, then the abstract state A is observer-dependent. And if consciousness is identified with A, consciousness inherits the observer-dependence. This is the first move in the circularity argument developed in section 05.

04

Map vs Territory

The ontology of abstract states is where the paper argues much of its case. Two natural readings are rejected before the positive account is offered.

What Abstract States Are Not

Not Platonic ideals. Nothing in the paper commits to a separate realm of forms in which A exists independently of any physical or cognitive system. Lerchner is explicit: treating abstract states as Platonic entities would solve the observer-dependence problem but at an unacceptable metaphysical cost.

Not metaphysical causal substances. Abstract states are not entities that sit alongside the physical ones, exerting their own causal powers. The paper treats this position as incoherent for the same reason: it would require abstractions to have causal structure independent of their physical realization.

What Abstract States Are

Neurophysiological constructs. Specifically, equivalence classes carved out by an experiencing brain or other learning system. The organism encounters a diversity of physical configurations, learns that certain configurations matter the same way for its goals, and groups them conceptually.

On this reading, “abstract state” names something epistemic — a tool of the mapmaker — not something ontic. Abstractions live in heads, or in equivalent representational systems. They do not live in the territory.

Why the Title Earns Its Bite

The fallacy the paper names is the move of treating an abstraction as if it were a feature of the territory. Maps are useful. They are also imposed. A map of the subway is an incredibly efficient tool for navigating stations; it is not a feature of the concrete and rails beneath. Substrate-independent consciousness, on the diagnosis of the Abstraction Fallacy, confuses the utility of the map with the causal structure of the territory.

The Point of Connection to S5

The biological naturalism defended by Seth (2024) and Godfrey-Smith (2024) agrees with Lerchner that abstractions do not have the causal structure required for consciousness, but grounds the claim differently — in the specific neurobiological or cellular dynamics of living systems. The two routes to substrate-sensitivity are compatible but non-identical.

05

The Causal Circularity

With the mapmaker established and abstract states identified as epistemic, the standard functionalist chain reverses. This is the paper’s central diagnosis.

The Standard Chain

On the functionalist picture, causation runs one way: physics realizes computation, which realizes (or constitutes) consciousness. The direction of explanation matches the direction of metaphysical dependence.

The Revised Chain

Lerchner argues the actual order is: consciousness (in the form of a mapmaker) selects f; the selected f picks out a computation; the computation is then read onto a physical substrate. The direction of selection runs opposite to the direction of realization.

The Precise Charge

The paper is careful about what the circularity claim amounts to. It is not the strong claim that consciousness cannot be computational. It is the weaker but more corrosive claim that the functionalist derivation of consciousness from computation is underdetermined: the computational description it starts from requires an already-conscious interpreter to be picked out.

If the argument goes through, computational functionalism does not so much fail as lose its status as an explanation. It describes what consciousness looks like from the mapmaker’s side; it does not explain how consciousness arises from the physics on the other side of f.

06

Simulation vs Instantiation

Three analogies carry the formal argument into more familiar terrain. Each contrasts a simulation of a process with an instantiation of it. Each is contested.

The Heart Analogy

A computer simulation of the heart represents the causal structure of pumping. It tracks pressures, valve positions, muscle contractions. It does not pump blood. Blood pumping requires muscle, vessels, and pressure differentials — specific material configurations the simulation describes but does not realize.

The intended parallel: a simulation of consciousness may represent the computational structure without instantiating the experience. The information flow is the same; the realization is different.

The GPU and Photosynthesis

A GPU simulating the quantum chemistry of photosynthesis does not make sugar. The chemistry requires specific molecular arrangements in leaves, not patterns of floating-point arithmetic that track them. The worry generalizes: pattern-tracking is not the phenomenon.

The Melody Paradox

A melody is an abstract pattern of pitches and intervals. It can be played on piano, guitar, or synthesizer and remains the same melody. But “melody” is a category of musical experience, not a force that causes vibrations. The musicians, not the melody, cause the sound. Consciousness-as-abstract-pattern, on Lerchner’s reading, conflates the pattern with the physical causes that realize it.

The Contested Premise

Defenders of functionalism reply that consciousness is unlike pumping, photosynthesis, and melodies in the relevant respect: it is the computational pattern, not merely represented by one. The functionalist slogan — “consciousness is software, not wetware” — is the denial that the heart analogy transfers. This is where Chalmers’ dancing-qualia thought experiments and the indicator-properties framework do their work: the functionalist claims certain kinds of patterns are constitutive of experience, not merely describable by it.

Why the Analogies Are Charged

If one accepts the heart analogy, Lerchner’s conclusion follows quickly. If one rejects it, the paper’s sharp end is blunted. The disagreement is about whether consciousness is more like a pattern of pumping (which needs the pumping) or more like arithmetic (which does not care what realizes it). No analogy settles this; it is the substantive question.

07

AGI as Non-Sentient Tool

The paper’s practical conclusion is framed as relief rather than loss. If computational functionalism fails, the prospect of AGI raises different worries than those currently dominating AI-safety discourse.

What the Conclusion Says

AGI systems, however capable, are not candidates for phenomenal consciousness merely by virtue of their computational profile. Moral-patiency concerns tied to substrate-independent consciousness have no foothold if substrate-independence itself is incoherent. The remaining concerns are behavioural and agential: powerful non-sentient optimizers can still cause catastrophic harm without having experiences.

What the Conclusion Does Not Say

Lerchner does not argue that AGI cannot in principle be conscious. The argument is narrower: the specific route from sufficiently advanced computation to phenomenal experience is blocked. An AGI that happened to be conscious would need to be conscious for some other reason — a biological substrate, a substrate-sensitive causal structure, embodiment, a non-functionalist property of its dynamics — not by the functionalist route.

Where This Intersects the Welfare Discourse

If Lerchner is right, the Butlin–Long recommendations to assess AI systems for consciousness indicators are tracking the wrong thing: the indicators presuppose a framework whose central claim is incoherent. If Lerchner is wrong, the welfare programme retains its full motivation. The practical stakes of the argument are substantial regardless of how it is ultimately adjudicated.

The Hedging Move

Practitioners in the welfare space (Long, Fish, Askell) have generally argued that welfare hedging is defensible under uncertainty, independent of which theory of consciousness turns out to be correct. On this view, even if Lerchner’s argument has force, the policy conclusion does not automatically follow. See S3 for how this hedging case is made.

Six Pressure Points

The mapmaker move

Does the observer-dependence of f follow from the formal triviality result, or can a non-trivial theory of implementation restrict f to a principled subset? See S7.

The biological alternative

What succeeds functionalism if it fails? Seth and Godfrey-Smith offer biological naturalism on different grounds than Lerchner. See S5.

The practitioner view

Hinton and Sutskever think current systems may be conscious; LeCun rejects the possibility; Shanahan reframes the question. See S2.

The welfare implication

Is the Anthropic model-welfare programme confused, or does it hedge responsibly under uncertainty? See S3.

The indicator framework

Are Butlin–Long indicators agnostic about the metaphysics, or do they presuppose the position Lerchner targets? See S4.

The illusionist dissolution

If phenomenal consciousness is itself illusory (Frankish, Kammerer), the question Lerchner is asking may lack a determinate answer. See S8.

A Note on What This Section Does Not Do

The summary above maps the argument and flags the moves open to dispute. It does not adjudicate. Whether the mapmaker move generalizes, whether the biological alternative is coherent, whether functionalism can be rescued by a non-trivial implementation theory — these are questions the remaining seven sections of this survey take up one by one.
