Stoicism — Ancient to Modern

A survey of Stoic philosophy from Zeno’s Painted Porch to Silicon Valley — across ancient foundations, Roman practice, core doctrines, the modern revival, cognitive behavioral therapy, and the tradition’s fiercest critics.

Stoicism is arguably more widely practiced today than at any point since the Roman Empire. Ryan Holiday’s books have sold over 10 million copies. Albert Ellis and Aaron Beck built cognitive behavioral therapy on explicitly Stoic foundations. The Modern Stoicism organization runs experiments showing measurable wellbeing gains from just one week of practice. And yet the most interesting questions remain open: Can Stoic ethics survive without Stoic cosmology? Does the dichotomy of control breed political quietism? Is the modern revival a genuine recovery of ancient wisdom or a dilution of it?

This survey maps the landscape across 6 dimensions and 58 sources — academic encyclopedias (SEP, IEP, Routledge), scholarly analyses, psychological research, popular treatments, and critical essays. Each section is built only after assessing whether sources are sufficient to support its claims. The most interesting findings are not the consensus points but the fractures — the places where serious interpreters look at the same tradition and reach opposite conclusions.

Five Tensions

The most valuable findings in any philosophical survey are the fractures — places where serious interpreters, looking at the same tradition, reach opposite conclusions. These five tensions define the Stoic discourse today.
  • T1 Metaphysics vs. ethics — Can Stoic ethics survive without Stoic physics? Pigliucci says update the cosmology; traditionalists say the system loses coherence without the cosmic logos. Most modern practitioners are implicitly Pigliuccian. [S1]
  • T2 Emotions: transformation or extirpation? — Nussbaum argues Stoic apatheia leads to dehumanization. Pigliucci says she attacks a straw-man (the Stoics recognized positive emotions). Robertson says CBT validates the Stoic approach empirically. [S6]
  • T3 Politics: engagement or quietism? — Critics charge that the dichotomy of control breeds acceptance of injustice. Defenders point to Cato, Seneca, and the Stoic Opposition. The historical record is genuinely mixed — and the slavery silence remains the sharpest version of this tension. [S6]
  • T4 Pop vs. philosophy — Ryan Holiday’s 10 million copies sold have done more for Stoic awareness than any academic publication. But scholars worry about dilution: resilience-for-profit without virtue, emotional suppression marketed as strength, the “manosphere” co-opting Stoic language. Gateway or distortion? [S4]
  • T5 CBT: borrowed or stolen? — Cognitive behavioral therapy explicitly drew on Stoic foundations. But CBT detaches the techniques from the ethical framework. Robertson argues the techniques work better within a coherent philosophical worldview. Most CBT practitioners are indifferent to the philosophical origins. [S5]
