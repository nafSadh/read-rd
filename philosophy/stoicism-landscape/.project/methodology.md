# Methodology — Stoicism Landscape Survey

## Research Question

What is the full arc of Stoic philosophy — from its founding as a complete philosophical system in ancient Athens, through its Roman transformation, to its 21st-century revival as both a self-help phenomenon and a foundation for cognitive behavioral therapy — and what are the most significant tensions within and critiques of the tradition?

## Approach

This is a **discourse survey**: mapping what scholars, practitioners, therapists, and critics say about Stoicism, rather than a primary reading of all ancient sources.

### Source Types
- **Academic encyclopedias**: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy — for authoritative summaries of ancient doctrines
- **Scholarly articles**: Peer-reviewed papers on Stoicism-CBT connection (PMC, Springer), academic analyses of Stoic doctrines
- **Popular philosophy**: Pigliucci (academic philosopher), Robertson (CBT therapist-scholar), Evans (journalist-philosopher) — for the modern revival narrative
- **Popular treatments**: Holiday, Irvine — for the self-help dimension and its reach
- **Critical essays**: Nussbaum, New Statesman, Philosophy Now, Aeon — for the critique dimension
- **Research data**: Modern Stoicism organization's Stoic Week data, psychological studies

### What This Survey Does Not Do
- Does not read the primary ancient texts (Discourses, Meditations, Letters) in full — relies on scholarly summaries
- Does not cover Middle Stoicism (Panaetius, Posidonius) in depth — they left fewer surviving texts and are less relevant to the modern revival
- Does not cover Stoic influence on every subsequent tradition — focuses on Christianity, CBT, and the modern movement
- Does not take a position on whether Stoicism is "correct" — maps the discourse

## Source Adequacy Discipline

Before building each section deep-dive, sources were assessed for:
1. **Coverage**: Do they address the key sub-topics?
2. **Balance**: Do they include multiple perspectives?
3. **Authority**: Do they draw on credible scholarship?
4. **Recency**: Are they current for topics that evolve (modern revival, CBT research)?

All 6 sections were assessed as adequate before construction. Total: 58 sources across 6 sections + 3 cross-cutting.

## Limitations

1. **Fragment problem**: Our knowledge of Early Stoicism comes almost entirely from fragments and secondary reports. The scholarly consensus we summarize is itself based on reconstruction.
2. **Popular bias**: The modern revival section necessarily covers popular authors (Holiday, Irvine) whose relationship to ancient Stoicism is contested by scholars.
3. **Western focus**: This survey does not cover parallels with Eastern philosophy (Buddhism, Taoism) except in passing, despite significant scholarly interest in these connections.
4. **Living tradition**: The modern Stoicism movement is actively evolving. Some sources may not reflect the latest developments.
