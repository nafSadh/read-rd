# S3: Core Doctrines

> The actual philosophical claims of Stoicism: what they argued, why they argued it, and how it fits together.

**Sources:** 8 (SEP, IEP, Routledge, Philosophy Break, Orion Philosophy, Daily Stoic, Figs in Winter, Stoicism University)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Covers all major doctrines with both scholarly and accessible treatments.

## 1. The Dichotomy of Control

The most famous Stoic doctrine, stated at the very opening of Epictetus's Enchiridion: "Some things are within our power, while others are not. Within our power are opinion, motivation, desire, aversion — in a word, whatever is of our own doing. Not within our power are our body, our property, reputation, office — in a word, whatever is not of our own doing."

This is not passive acceptance but a framework for directing effort. The Stoic does not stop trying to change the world — but invests emotional energy only in what is genuinely under their control (their own judgments and choices), not in outcomes.

William Irvine modified this into a "trichotomy of control" — things we fully control, things we partially control, things we don't control at all. Scholars (including Robertson) have criticized this modification as departing from the ancient sources without philosophical justification.

## 2. Virtue as the Sole Good

The most radical Stoic claim: virtue (arete) is the only thing that is genuinely good; vice is the only thing that is genuinely bad; everything else — health, wealth, reputation, even life itself — is "indifferent" (adiaphoron) with respect to happiness.

The four cardinal virtues:
- **Wisdom** (sophia/phronesis): knowledge of what is truly good, bad, and indifferent
- **Courage** (andreia): endurance in the face of difficulty
- **Justice** (dikaiosyne): treating others as they deserve; fairness
- **Temperance** (sophrosyne): self-control and moderation

The virtues are unified: you cannot have one without the others. The perfectly virtuous person (the sage, sophos) acts appropriately in all circumstances. The sage is an ideal — a normative standard, not a realistic expectation. Progress toward virtue (prokope) is the realistic goal.

## 3. Preferred and Dispreferred Indifferents

The Stoics recognized that calling health, wealth, and life "indifferent" seems counterintuitive. Their response: indifferents can be "preferred" (proegmena) or "dispreferred" (apoproegmena).

Preferred indifferents (health, wealth, education, good reputation) have positive "selective value" (axia) — in normal circumstances, we should choose them. Dispreferred indifferents (disease, poverty, bad reputation) have negative selective value. Strictly indifferent things (the number of hairs on your head) have no selective value at all.

The crucial point: preferred indifferents are worth pursuing, but they are not constitutive of happiness. A person can be fully happy (eudaimon) while sick, poor, and enslaved — if they are virtuous. This is the claim that critics find most difficult to accept.

Selection among indifferents according to their value constitutes "appropriate action" (kathekon) — doing what the situation calls for. The virtuous person selects well among indifferents while recognizing that their happiness does not depend on the outcome.

## 4. Living According to Nature

The Stoic ethical formula: the goal of life is "to live in accordance with nature" (homologoumenos te physei zen). This means:

1. **Rational nature**: Living according to our nature as rational beings — using reason to guide choices
2. **Cosmic nature**: Living in harmony with the rational order of the universe (the logos)
3. **Social nature**: Fulfilling our roles as members of the human community

"Nature" here is not the romantic notion of wilderness but the rational structure of reality. The Stoics believed the universe is governed by an immanent rational principle (logos), and the good life consists in aligning oneself with it.

## 5. Oikeiosis and the Path to Cosmopolitanism

Oikeiosis (self-appropriation or affinity) is the Stoic account of moral development. It begins with the natural impulse of every living thing to preserve itself and extends through reason to care for others:

1. Self-preservation (innate in all animals)
2. Care for offspring (natural extension)
3. Rational recognition that others share our nature
4. Extension of concern to family, friends, fellow citizens, all humanity

Hierocles' concentric circles illustrate this: the self at the center, then family, extended family, local community, fellow citizens, and finally all of humanity. The ethical task is to "draw the circles toward the center" — to treat strangers with something approaching the concern we naturally feel for ourselves.

This grounds Stoic cosmopolitanism: we are citizens of the cosmos (kosmopolitai) before any particular city. All human beings share rational nature and deserve moral concern.

## 6. The Theory of Emotions (Passions)

The Stoics had a distinctive and sophisticated theory of emotions (pathe, traditionally translated "passions"):

- Emotions are not irrational forces but false judgments — specifically, assent to false impressions about what is good or bad
- The four primary passions: appetite (desire for false good), fear (aversion to false evil), pleasure (elation at false good obtained), distress (depression at false evil occurring)
- The Stoic goal: apatheia — freedom from destructive passions, NOT emotional flatness
- Positive emotions (eupatheiai) are preserved: joy (rational elation), wish (rational desire), caution (rational aversion)

This is the doctrine most commonly misunderstood. The Stoics did not advocate emotional suppression. They advocated emotional transformation — replacing irrational emotional reactions with appropriate rational responses.

## 7. Determinism and Moral Responsibility

Stoic physics is deterministic: everything happens through antecedent causes. But the Stoics (especially Chrysippus) defended moral responsibility through compatibilism:

- External events trigger our impressions, but we choose whether to assent
- The character of the agent is itself a cause — Chrysippus's analogy of the cylinder that rolls because of its shape (internal cause) when pushed (external cause)
- "Fate" is not external coercion but the rational order of causes

This makes the Stoics the first compatibilists in Western philosophy — a position still defended today.

## 8. What Sources Agree On

1. The dichotomy of control is the most practically influential Stoic doctrine
2. "Virtue is the sole good" is the most philosophically radical claim
3. Apatheia does NOT mean emotional suppression — this is the most common misreading
4. Stoic cosmopolitanism genuinely anticipated modern universalism
5. The preferred indifferents doctrine is a sophisticated response to the obvious objection

## 9. Where Sources Disagree

1. Whether the dichotomy of control should be modified (Irvine's trichotomy vs. traditional binary)
2. Whether "virtue is sufficient for happiness" is credible (the torture objection: can a virtuous person on the rack really be happy?)
3. Whether Stoic cosmopolitanism has practical implications or remains merely theoretical
4. Whether apatheia, even properly understood, is psychologically healthy or achievable
