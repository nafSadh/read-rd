# S4: The Modern Revival

> How a 2,300-year-old philosophy became a 21st-century self-help phenomenon, with conferences, apps, and bestsellers.

**Sources:** 10 (Modern Stoicism org, Holiday/Wikipedia, Irvine, Pigliucci, Robertson, Stoic Week research, Quartz, Philosophy for Life, Academia.edu)
**Adequacy:** Strong. Covers key figures, organizations, research, criticism, and Silicon Valley phenomenon.

---

## 1. The Revival's Origins

The modern Stoicism revival has intellectual roots in Pierre Hadot's influential work on "philosophy as a way of life" (1995 English translation), which argued that ancient philosophy was primarily a set of spiritual exercises rather than theoretical system-building. This reframing made Stoicism especially attractive: its Roman practitioners had already done the work of translating philosophical principles into daily practices.

The institutional origin is the founding of Modern Stoicism, Ltd in 2012, initially as a collaboration between academic philosophers (Christopher Gill, John Sellars), CBT therapists (Donald Robertson, Tim LeBon), and public intellectuals (Massimo Pigliucci, Jules Evans).

## 2. The Key Figures

### Massimo Pigliucci
Philosophy professor at City College of New York. Published How to Be a Stoic (2017). Runs the blog "Figs in Winter" (formerly "How to Be a Stoic"). Argues for "Stoicism 2.0" — updating the metaphysics (replace logos with laws of nature, drop eternal recurrence) while preserving the ethical core. Key advocate for distinguishing rigorous Stoic philosophy from pop-Stoicism dilution.

### Ryan Holiday
Former marketing director, turned author and popularizer. Books have sold over 10 million copies in 40+ languages. The Obstacle Is the Way (2014) was adopted by NFL teams (New England Patriots, Seattle Seahawks), the Chicago Cubs, and LL Cool J. The Daily Stoic (2016): 2M+ English copies. Runs the Daily Stoic website, email list (millions of subscribers), podcast, and bookstore. Credited by the New York Times with driving the increased popularity of Stoicism. His #1 NYT bestseller series covers the four Stoic virtues.

### William Irvine
Philosophy professor at Wright State University. A Guide to the Good Life: The Ancient Art of Stoic Joy (2009) was the first systematic modern guide to Stoic practice. Introduced negative visualization as the central technique and proposed the trichotomy of control. Criticized by scholars (especially Robertson) for departing from ancient sources, but widely recommended as an accessible entry point.

### Donald Robertson
Scottish CBT therapist. Co-founder of Modern Stoicism. The Philosophy of Cognitive-Behavioural Therapy (2010) is the definitive academic treatment of the Stoicism-CBT connection. How to Think Like a Roman Emperor (2019): #1 philosophy bestseller, 18 languages. Founded Plato's Academy Centre in Greece. Bridges the therapeutic and philosophical streams.

## 3. Stoicon and Stoic Week

**Stoicon** is the largest annual gathering of Stoics worldwide, held since 2013 (online since 2020). The 2025 event was online via Zoom with donation-based tickets. Features academic talks, workshops, and community discussions.

**Stoic Week** is a 7-day experiment in living like a Stoic, with daily readings and exercises. Since 2012, approximately 40,000 people have participated. Results have been consistently positive: on average, a 15% reduction in negative emotions and a 14% increase in life satisfaction after just one week.

The **SABS** (Stoic Attitudes and Behaviours Scale) developed through this research identifies 7 key dimensions of Stoic practice. High SABS scores are linked to greater resilience, lower anger, and higher life satisfaction. 94% of participants reported they would recommend Stoicism as useful life guidance.

## 4. The Silicon Valley Phenomenon

Stoicism gained particular traction in Silicon Valley after Tim Ferriss promoted Ryan Holiday's books and podcast. Holiday has spoken at Google and connected with Jack Dorsey (Twitter), Kevin Rose (Digg), Brad Feld (VC), and other tech figures.

The appeal: Stoicism as an "operating system for high-stress environments." The dichotomy of control maps naturally to startup culture, where outcomes are uncertain but effort and attitude are controllable.

## 5. Three Streams of the Revival

The modern movement is not monolithic. At least three distinct streams:

1. **Academic/philosophical**: Pigliucci, Sellars, Gill. Rigorous engagement with ancient texts, open to updating metaphysics. The Modern Stoicism organization.
2. **Therapeutic**: Robertson, LeBon. CBT practitioners integrating Stoic philosophy. Evidence-based, focused on measurable outcomes.
3. **Popular/self-help**: Holiday, Irvine. Mass-market accessibility. Enormous reach but variable philosophical depth.

## 6. Tensions Within the Movement

Pigliucci has explicitly criticized Silicon Valley Stoicism for emphasizing wealth-as-indifferent while ignoring virtue-as-the-only-good. When the wealthiest segment of society adopts a philosophy that teaches acceptance of external circumstances, there is a risk of using ancient wisdom to justify modern inequality.

Jules Evans describes the movement as a "big, messy tent" with productive but real tensions between scholarly fidelity and popular accessibility.

The fundamental question: can Stoicism survive without its metaphysics? Pigliucci says yes. Traditional scholars worry the ethical system loses coherence without the cosmic logos and the interconnected three-part structure.

## 7. Scale and Impact

- Holiday's books: 10M+ copies sold
- Daily Stoic newsletter: millions of subscribers
- Stoic Week participants: ~40,000 since 2012
- Modern Stoicism: international organization with annual conferences
- Google Trends: "Stoicism" search interest has grown 5-10x since 2014
- TikTok/YouTube: massive Stoic content ecosystem

Stoicism is arguably more widely practiced today than at any point since the Roman Empire.

## 8. What Sources Agree On

1. The revival is real and significant in scale
2. Holiday's popularization has been the largest single driver of awareness
3. The academic-popular tension is genuine and productive
4. The Stoic Week research shows measurable wellbeing benefits
5. Silicon Valley adoption accelerated mainstream interest

## 9. Where Sources Disagree

1. Whether Holiday's popularization helps or harms the tradition (gateway vs. dilution)
2. Whether Stoicism can be meaningfully practiced without its ancient metaphysics
3. Whether Silicon Valley Stoicism is genuine Stoicism or a distortion
4. Whether the movement's male skew reflects the philosophy or the marketing
