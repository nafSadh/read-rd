The Dichotomy of Control

The most famous Stoic doctrine, stated at the opening of Epictetus’s Enchiridion: some things are within our power (opinion, motivation, desire, aversion) and others are not (body, property, reputation, office).[1]CD-01: Philosophy Break, “The Dichotomy of Control” (2023).

This is not passive acceptance but a framework for directing effort. The Stoic invests emotional energy only in what is genuinely under their control.

03

Preferred Indifferents

The Stoics anticipated the obvious objection. Their response: indifferents have “selective value” (axia). Health is “preferred” and sickness “dispreferred” — in normal circumstances, choose health. But happiness does not depend on getting it.[3]CD-05: Routledge Encyclopedia, “Stoicism — The Indifferents.”

A person can be fully happy while sick, poor, and enslaved — if they are virtuous. This is the claim critics find most difficult to accept.

04

Living According to Nature

The Stoic ethical formula: the goal of life is to live in accordance with nature (homologoumenos te physei zen). This means living according to our rational nature, the cosmic order, and our social roles.[4]CD-07: Pigliucci, “From Ancient to New Stoicism: III — Stoic Ethics” (2020).

05

Oikeiosis & Cosmopolitanism

Oikeiosis (self-appropriation) is the Stoic account of moral development. It begins with self-preservation and extends through reason to care for all humanity.[5]CD-08: Stoicism University, “Stoicism Ethics: A Guide to Living Virtuously” (2024).

Hierocles’s concentric circles: self, family, extended family, local community, fellow citizens, all humanity. The ethical task is to draw the circles toward the center.[6]CD-06: Stoic Ethics and Political Citizenship, College of Stoic Philosophers (2023).

06

The Theory of Emotions

Emotions (pathe) are not irrational forces but false judgments — assent to false impressions about what is good or bad. Four primary passions: appetite, fear, pleasure, distress. The Stoic goal: apatheia — freedom from destructive passions, NOT emotional flatness.

Positive emotions (eupatheiai) are preserved: joy, rational wish, caution.

07

Determinism & Responsibility

Stoic physics is deterministic: everything happens through antecedent causes. But the Stoics defended moral responsibility through compatibilism — the first in Western philosophy.

Chrysippus’s cylinder analogy: when pushed, a cylinder rolls because of its shape (internal cause) combined with the push (external cause). Our character is itself a cause.

01

The Dichotomy of Control

Epictetus, Enchiridion 1: some things are within our power (opinion, motivation, desire, aversion) and others are not (body, property, reputation, office). This is the foundation of Stoic practical ethics.[1]CD-01: Philosophy Break, “The Dichotomy of Control” (2023).

Not Passive Acceptance

The dichotomy is not a binary acceptance/rejection framework but a guide for directing effort. The Stoic does not stop trying to change the world — but invests emotional energy only in what is genuinely under their control (their own judgments and choices), not in outcomes.[7]CD-02: The Living Philosophy, “Stoicism’s Dichotomy of Control” (2023).

Irvine’s Trichotomy

William Irvine modified this into a “trichotomy of control” — things we fully control, things we partially control, things we don’t control at all. Scholars (including Robertson) have criticized this modification as departing from the ancient sources without philosophical justification.

Modern Relevance

The dichotomy of control maps naturally to high-uncertainty environments — startup culture, athletics, military leadership — where outcomes are unpredictable but effort and attitude remain controllable. This is why it has become the most widely adopted Stoic practice today.

02

Virtue as the Sole Good

The most radical Stoic claim: virtue (arete) is the only thing that is genuinely good; vice is the only thing that is genuinely bad; everything else — health, wealth, reputation, even life itself — is “indifferent” (adiaphoron) with respect to happiness.[2]CD-03: Orion Philosophy, “A Guide to the 4 Stoic Virtues” (2024).

The Four Cardinal Virtues

Wisdom

Sophia/phronesis: knowledge of what is truly good, bad, and indifferent. The master virtue.

Courage

Andreia: endurance in the face of difficulty. Not just physical but moral courage.

Justice

Dikaiosyne: treating others as they deserve. Fairness, social virtue, political engagement.

Temperance

Sophrosyne: self-control and moderation. Rational governance of appetites and impulses.

The Sage

The Stoic sage (sophos) is a normative ideal — a perfectly virtuous person who never gives assent to false impressions. The Stoics acknowledged the sage may never have existed. Progress toward virtue (prokope) is the realistic goal.[4]CD-07: Pigliucci, “From Ancient to New Stoicism: III — Stoic Ethics” (2020).

03

Preferred Indifferents

The Stoics recognized that calling health, wealth, and life “indifferent” seems counterintuitive. Their response was a formal classification system.[3]CD-05: Routledge Encyclopedia, “Stoicism — The Indifferents.”

The Classification

Selection among indifferents according to their value constitutes “appropriate action” (kathekon) — doing what the situation calls for. The virtuous person selects well among indifferents while recognizing that their happiness does not depend on the outcome.

04

Living According to Nature

The Stoic ethical formula: the goal of life is to live in accordance with nature (homologoumenos te physei zen).[4]CD-07: Pigliucci, “From Ancient to New Stoicism: III — Stoic Ethics” (2020).

Three Dimensions

Rational Nature

Living according to our nature as rational beings — using reason to guide choices rather than being driven by impulse.

Cosmic Nature

Living in harmony with the rational order of the universe (the logos). Nature here is not wilderness but rational structure.

Social Nature

Fulfilling our roles as members of the human community — family, city, cosmos.

The Modern Question

Can “living according to nature” survive without the Stoic cosmic logos? Pigliucci proposes substituting the laws of nature.

05

Oikeiosis & Cosmopolitanism

Oikeiosis (self-appropriation or affinity) is the Stoic account of moral development. It begins with the natural impulse of every living thing to preserve itself and extends through reason to care for others.[5]CD-08: Stoicism University, “Stoicism Ethics: A Guide to Living Virtuously” (2024).

Hierocles’s Concentric Circles

The ethical task is to draw the circles toward the center — to treat strangers with something approaching the concern we naturally feel for ourselves.[6]CD-06: Stoic Ethics and Political Citizenship, College of Stoic Philosophers (2023). This grounds Stoic cosmopolitanism: we are citizens of the cosmos (kosmopolitai) before any particular city.

06

The Theory of Emotions

The Stoics had a distinctive and sophisticated theory of emotions (pathe, traditionally translated “passions”). This is the doctrine most commonly misunderstood.

The Stoic Analysis

Emotions are not irrational forces but false judgments — specifically, assent to false impressions about what is good or bad. The four primary passions: appetite (desire for false good), fear (aversion to false evil), pleasure (elation at false good obtained), distress (depression at false evil occurring).

Apatheia vs. Emotional Flatness

What Stoics Preserve

  • Joy (chara) — rational elation
  • Wish (boulesis) — rational desire
  • Caution (eulabeia) — rational aversion

What Stoics Eliminate

  • Appetite — irrational desire
  • Fear — irrational aversion
  • Pleasure — irrational elation
  • Distress — irrational dejection

The Stoics advocated emotional transformation, not suppression — replacing irrational emotional reactions with appropriate rational responses.

07

Determinism & Responsibility

Stoic physics is deterministic: everything happens through antecedent causes. But the Stoics (especially Chrysippus) defended moral responsibility through compatibilism — the first in Western philosophy.

Chrysippus’s Cylinder

External events trigger our impressions, but we choose whether to assent. The character of the agent is itself a cause. Chrysippus’s analogy: a cylinder rolls because of its shape (internal cause) when pushed (external cause). The push is necessary but not sufficient — the cylinder’s nature determines how it rolls.

“Fate” in the Stoic sense is not external coercion but the rational order of causes. We are part of that order, and our rational responses are themselves causal factors.

Modern Parallel

This makes the Stoics the first compatibilists in Western philosophy — a position still defended today by philosophers who argue that free will is compatible with determinism.

What Sources Agree On

Across 8 sources, five points of strong convergence:

Most Influential

The dichotomy of control is the most practically influential Stoic doctrine.

Most Radical

“Virtue is the sole good” is the most philosophically radical claim.

Most Misunderstood

Apatheia does NOT mean emotional suppression — the most common misreading of Stoicism.

Most Forward-Looking

Stoic cosmopolitanism genuinely anticipated modern universalism and human rights discourse.

Where Sources Disagree

Whether the dichotomy should be modified (Irvine’s trichotomy vs. traditional binary). Whether “virtue is sufficient for happiness” is credible. Whether Stoic cosmopolitanism has practical implications or remains theoretical. Whether apatheia, even properly understood, is psychologically healthy or achievable.
