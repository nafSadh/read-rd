Paradoxes: From Zeno to Gödel

A survey of paradoxes across 2,500 years of intellectual history — from ancient Greek motion puzzles to Gödel's incompleteness theorems, visual impossibilities, and the paradoxes of personal identity.

A paradox is a boundary marker. It shows where a conceptual system reaches its limits — where the rules we use to reason about the world turn back on themselves and produce contradictions, impossibilities, or deep surprises. This survey maps paradoxes across 7 domains and 54 sources: ancient philosophy, formal logic, mathematics, physics, decision theory, personal identity, and visual art. The unifying themes are self-reference, infinity, and the gap between formal systems and the reality they model.

Cross-Cutting Tensions

  • 01 Are Zeno's paradoxes solved? Mathematicians say yes (convergent series). Philosophers say the metaphysical puzzle of continuity persists.
  • 02 Is the Axiom of Choice acceptable? Most mathematicians accept it. Constructivists say Banach-Tarski is a reductio ad absurdum.
  • 03 Does the Chinese Room refute strong AI? Searle says syntax never produces semantics. Dennett and Hofstadter say the system as a whole understands.
  • 04 Is Newcomb's Problem solved? One-boxers and two-boxers have been arguing for 50+ years with no resolution.
  • 05 Are paradoxes failures or features? Logical positivists say linguistic confusion. Hofstadter says the mechanism of consciousness.
  • 06 Is personal identity real? Parfit says no further fact. Common sense says of course you are you.
