# S1: Ancient Paradoxes

> Zeno's paradoxes broke Greek mathematics. The Sorites broke logic. Epimenides planted the seed of self-reference that would eventually produce Godel.

**Sources:** 8 (SEP Zeno, IEP Zeno, Wikipedia, Salmon PDF, SEP Sorites, Wikipedia Sorites, Epimenides Wikipedia, Wolfram MathWorld)
**Adequacy:** Strong. All three ancient paradox families covered.

---

## 1. Zeno of Elea (~490-430 BCE)

At least 8 paradoxes, 4 survive through Aristotle's Physics. Designed to support Parmenides' monism: reality is one, unchanging; motion and plurality are illusions. Not mathematical puzzles but philosophical arguments.

## 2. Achilles and the Tortoise

Tortoise has head start. Achilles must reach each prior tortoise position. Infinite sequence, but the total is a convergent geometric series: 100 + 10 + 1 + 0.1 + ... = 1000/9 meters. Resolution: infinite steps can sum to finite distance/time (Cauchy, 1820s).

## 3. The Dichotomy

Progressive: reach 1/2, then 3/4, then 7/8... never arrive. Regressive: must reach 1/2 before 1, 1/4 before 1/2... infinite regression with no first step. Thomson's Lamp (1954) sharpens this: infinite switching leaves lamp state undefined.

## 4. The Arrow

At each instant, arrow occupies space equal to itself = at rest. If every instant is rest, motion is impossible. Resolution: instantaneous velocity is dx/dt, a limit, not a "state" at an instant. Arrow can be at definite position while having nonzero velocity.

## 5. The Sorites (Heap)

Eubulides of Miletus. Remove grains one by one. When does heap stop being heap? Exposes vague predicates. Four response families: fuzzy logic (Zadeh 1965), supervaluationism, epistemicism (Williamson), contextualism. Practically unsolved.

## 6. Epimenides

"All Cretans are liars." Not genuinely paradoxical (can be simply false). But planted seed for Eubulides' true Liar ("This sentence is false") which IS genuinely paradoxical. Genealogy: Epimenides → Eubulides → Buridan → Russell → Godel → Turing.

## 7. Resolution Timeline

- ~350 BCE: Aristotle's potential/actual infinity distinction
- 1680s: Newton/Leibniz calculus (computational but not rigorous)
- 1820s: Cauchy's limits and convergent series
- 1860s: Weierstrass epsilon-delta
- 1870s: Dedekind cuts, Cantor's set theory
- Open: physical continuity question, Sorites, supertask completion
