# S6: Identity & Persistence Paradoxes

> What makes you you? These paradoxes suggest the question itself might be malformed.

**Sources:** 8 (Wikipedia Teletransportation/Chinese Room, SEP Chinese Room, IEP Chinese Room, Britannica, ScienceMe, LibreTexts, New Statesman)
**Adequacy:** Adequate.

---

## 1. Ship of Theseus (1st century CE)

Plutarch: Athenians preserved Theseus's ship, replacing planks over centuries. Is it the same ship? Hobbes's twist: build a second ship from the removed planks. Which is the "real" Ship of Theseus?

Two main views: (1) Identity tracks functional/spatiotemporal continuity (the maintained ship is the same ship). (2) Identity tracks material composition (the rebuilt ship is the same ship). Neither view is without problems. Extends to: cells replace in your body every 7-10 years; is the corporation that replaced all employees the same corporation?

## 2. Parfit's Teletransporter (1984)

Machine scans body, destroys it, transmits information to Mars, builds perfect replica. Is the Mars person you? Case 1: destruction + reconstruction. Most say "yes, it's me." Case 2: machine malfunctions, doesn't destroy original. Now there are two. Which is you?

Parfit's answer: the question has no answer because personal identity is not what matters. What matters is psychological continuity ("Relation R") — memory connections, personality, intentions. Relation R can hold between you and multiple future persons, or to degrees. Identity is not a binary, all-or-nothing thing.

## 3. Swampman (Davidson, 1987)

Lightning simultaneously kills Davidson and creates a molecule-for-molecule duplicate from swamp matter. Swampman walks to Davidson's office, gives his lectures, kisses his wife. Does Swampman have beliefs? Davidson (who invented the thought experiment) says no: Swampman's brain states have no causal history connecting them to the world. Swampman has never seen a tree, so his states can't be "about" trees, even though they're physically identical to Davidson's.

Most philosophers find this conclusion bizarre. It reveals the tension between internalism (mental content supervenes on brain states) and externalism (mental content depends on causal history).

## 4. The Chinese Room (Searle, 1980)

Searle in a room receives Chinese characters, follows English rulebook, outputs appropriate Chinese responses. The room passes the Turing test. Does the room/Searle understand Chinese? Searle says no: following syntactic rules never produces semantic understanding.

Major objections:
- **Systems reply:** The room as a whole understands (Searle doesn't, but the system does)
- **Robot reply:** Embodiment is required for understanding
- **Brain simulator reply:** What if the room simulates a Chinese speaker's brain?
- **Other minds reply:** How do you know anyone understands?

Searle's response: none of these add anything beyond syntax. Computation is defined syntactically; syntax is not sufficient for semantics.

## 5. AI Relevance

These aren't academic curiosities:
- **Chinese Room → LLMs:** Does GPT-4 understand language or just manipulate symbols? The systems reply is stronger for LLMs than for Searle's room, but the core question persists.
- **Teletransporter → brain uploading:** Would a digital copy of your brain be you?
- **Ship of Theseus → gradual brain-computer integration:** Replace neurons one by one with silicon chips. At what point (if any) does "you" stop being you?
- **Swampman → AI consciousness:** If an AI's internal states have no causal history in the human sense, can it have genuine beliefs?
