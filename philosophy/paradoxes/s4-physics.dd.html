Schrödinger’s Cat

Erwin Schrödinger proposed his famous thought experiment in 1935 in correspondence with Einstein.[1]The Physics Behind Schrödinger’s Cat — National Geographic → The setup: a cat in a sealed box with a radioactive atom, a Geiger counter, and a vial of poison. If the atom decays within an hour, the counter triggers and breaks the vial, killing the cat. Quantum mechanics describes the atom as being in a superposition of decayed and not-decayed states until measured. Does the cat, then, exist in a superposition of alive and dead?

Three Interpretations

Copenhagen

The wave function collapses upon measurement. The cat is alive or dead once the box is opened; the superposition is a mathematical description of our ignorance, not of physical reality.

Many-Worlds

Both outcomes occur. The universe splits: in one branch the cat is alive, in another it is dead. No collapse occurs. Every quantum event creates branching realities.

Decoherence

The environment rapidly entangles with the quantum system, making superposition unobservable at macroscopic scales. The cat is never in superposition because its environment constantly measures it.

Schrödinger’s Intent

Schrödinger thought the scenario was absurd. His point was that Copenhagen must be wrong if it leads to macro-level superpositions. The cat was a critique, not a celebration.

EPR & Bell

Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen published their famous paper in 1935, arguing that quantum mechanics must be incomplete. Their reasoning: if measuring one of two entangled particles instantly determines the state of the other, regardless of distance, then either information travels faster than light (violating relativity) or the particles had predetermined values all along (hidden variables).

Bell’s Theorem (1964)

John Bell proved that any local hidden variable theory makes predictions that differ from quantum mechanics. Specifically, hidden variable theories satisfy a mathematical inequality (Bell’s inequality) that quantum mechanics violates. Nature must choose: locality or the quantum mechanical predictions.

Experimental Verdict

Alain Aspect (1982) and subsequent experiments, culminating in loophole-free tests by Hensen et al. (2015), confirmed that Bell’s inequality IS violated. Nature is nonlocal (or we must abandon other fundamental assumptions like realism or measurement independence). The 2022 Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded to Aspect, Clauser, and Zeilinger for these experimental tests.

Einstein Was Wrong (Sort Of)

EPR was meant to prove quantum mechanics incomplete. Instead, it led to the discovery that nature really is as strange as quantum mechanics says. Einstein’s instinct that something was missing was understandable—but wrong.

Maxwell’s Demon

James Clerk Maxwell proposed this thought experiment in 1867: imagine a container of gas divided into two chambers by a wall with a tiny door. A being (the "demon") operates the door, allowing fast molecules to pass one way and slow molecules the other. The result: one chamber gets hotter, the other colder, without any work being done—an apparent violation of the second law of thermodynamics.

The Resolution: Information Is Physical

The Deep Connection

Maxwell’s Demon established that information is not abstract—it is physical. Entropy and information are two sides of the same coin. This insight connects thermodynamics to computation (Landauer’s principle), to black hole physics (Bekenstein-Hawking entropy), and to the foundations of quantum mechanics.

The Twin Paradox

One of the earliest consequences of special relativity (1905): if one twin travels at near-light speed to a distant star and returns, she will have aged less than her stay-at-home sibling. The apparent paradox is that from each twin’s perspective, the other is moving, so the situation seems symmetric.

The Resolution: Acceleration Breaks Symmetry

The situation is NOT symmetric. The traveling twin must accelerate to leave, decelerate at the destination, accelerate for the return, and decelerate to arrive home. These accelerations change her reference frame. The stay-at-home twin remains in a single inertial frame throughout. In general relativity, the traveling twin’s worldline is shorter through spacetime, meaning she experiences less proper time. This is not a thought experiment: muons created by cosmic rays in the upper atmosphere reach the ground because their relativistic time dilation extends their lifetimes, exactly as predicted.

The Grandfather Paradox

The most famous time-travel paradox: you travel to the past and prevent your grandfather from meeting your grandmother. You are never born. You never travel back in time. Your grandfather meets your grandmother. You are born. You travel back. The logical structure is a causal loop with no consistent resolution.

Three Approaches

Impossibility

Time travel to the past is physically impossible. Most physicists favor this view. The laws of physics simply do not permit closed timelike curves in practice.

Novikov Principle

Self-consistency is enforced by physics. You CAN travel to the past, but events will conspire to prevent paradoxes. You will always fail to kill your grandfather.

Branching Timelines

Killing your grandfather creates a new timeline branch. You continue to exist in the new branch (where you were never born in that timeline) while the original timeline is unaffected.

Information Paradox

Even without violence: bring a book from the future to the past. The author copies it. Where did the information originate? No one created it. This is called a bootstrap paradox.

Olbers’ Paradox

Heinrich Wilhelm Olbers articulated this paradox in 1823, though others had noted it earlier: if the universe is infinite in extent, eternal in duration, and uniformly filled with stars, then every line of sight must eventually terminate at a stellar surface. The entire sky should be as bright as the surface of the Sun. It is not.

The Resolution

The resolution requires multiple facts about the actual universe. The universe is not eternal—it is approximately 13.8 billion years old, so we can only see light from objects within 13.8 billion light-years (the observable universe). The universe is expanding, so distant starlight is redshifted out of the visible spectrum. And stars have finite lifetimes, so not every point in space is radiating.

A Paradox That Predicted Cosmology

Olbers’ paradox is a rare example of a paradox whose resolution preceded the theory that explains it. The dark night sky is direct evidence that the universe had a beginning—a fact not established until the 20th century with Hubble’s observations and the discovery of the cosmic microwave background.

Black Hole Information

Stephen Hawking showed in 1975 that black holes are not truly black—they emit thermal radiation (Hawking radiation) and gradually evaporate. But thermal radiation is featureless; it carries no information about what fell into the black hole. Quantum mechanics insists that information is always conserved. General relativity says the information is trapped behind the event horizon and destroyed when the black hole evaporates. Both theories cannot be right.

Leading Proposals

The AdS/CFT correspondence (Maldacena, 1997) strongly suggests information IS preserved. The holographic principle proposes that all information about the interior is encoded on the event horizon. Recent work on the "Page curve" and quantum extremal surfaces has provided evidence that Hawking radiation does carry information, but the mechanism remains unclear. The information paradox sits at the intersection of quantum mechanics, general relativity, and thermodynamics—making it a key target for any future theory of quantum gravity.

${dIcon}Section 08 — Details

Discovery Engines

Physics paradoxes differ from logical and mathematical paradoxes in a crucial way: they tend to be resolved, and the resolution invariably teaches us something deep about physical reality. This makes them engines of scientific discovery rather than permanent puzzles.

The Pattern

Schrödinger’s Cat

QM is correct at macro scale. Decoherence explains why we don’t see superposition in everyday life.

EPR

Nature really IS nonlocal. Bell’s theorem and experimental tests confirmed quantum entanglement.

Maxwell’s Demon

Information is physical. Entropy and computation are fundamentally linked.

Olbers’ Paradox

The universe had a beginning. The dark sky is evidence for the Big Bang.

The Lesson for Science

When a well-constructed theory produces an apparently paradoxical conclusion, the historical pattern suggests the theory is more likely correct than the intuition. Human intuitions about reality are shaped by everyday experience at human scales. Physical reality is not constrained by those intuitions.
