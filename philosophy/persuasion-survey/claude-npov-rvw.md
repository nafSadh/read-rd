## Pass 1 — Issues Found

### POV (Point of View)

- **P-01 (minor):** S1 Section 04 on the Sophists states their approach is "the historical ancestor of modern dark psychology and manipulation." This frames the Sophists negatively through modern moral categories. The Sophists are a contested group -- some scholars (e.g., Kerferd, Schiappa) have rehabilitated them as serious epistemologists. The framing here aligns with the Platonic/Aristotelian tradition without noting the counter-narrative. **No fix applied** -- the context of a persuasion survey naturally focuses on the power dimension, and the text acknowledges their position ("rhetoric as power, not wisdom") without outright condemnation.
- **P-02 (negligible):** The survey's overall framing treats persuasion as a morally loaded topic (rhetoric vs. manipulation vs. defense). This is appropriate for the subject matter and the Plato-vs-Aristotle framing is explicitly noted as a structuring debate.

### RIGOR

- **R-01 (minor):** S2 states Cambridge Analytica "harvested 87 million profiles without consent." The number 87M is documented (Facebook's own disclosure), but the consent question is more nuanced -- users consented to the initial app, not to the downstream sharing. The phrasing "without consent" is defensible but simplified.
- **R-02 (minor):** S2 cites "Nature Communications Psychology (2024)" for asymmetric learning research. This is a specific enough citation to verify. Acceptable.
- **R-03 (minor):** The "200+ documented biases" claim (S2) is a commonly cited figure but the exact count depends on taxonomy. Adding "approximately" would improve precision. **No fix applied** -- the hedge is implicit in "documented in the literature."

### BIAS

- **B-01 (moderate):** The survey has a defensive/protective orientation -- it frames persuasion techniques primarily as threats to be understood and defended against. This is appropriate for the survey's stated purpose (defense literacy) but readers should note that persuasion research also covers prosocial applications (health communication, education, public safety). The survey does acknowledge Aristotle's neutral framing but leans toward the threat model.
- **B-02 (minor):** Coercive control section (S2) correctly cites Stark (2007) and frames the topic through established academic literature. No sensationalism.

### TONE

- **T-01 (minor):** Terms like "attack surfaces" and "weaponized" (S2 synthesis) import cybersecurity/military metaphors into psychology. This is common in the literature (social engineering inherits from security discourse) but contributes to a threat-centric framing.
- **T-02 (negligible):** The writing maintains academic register throughout. No sensationalism in the Dark Triad or coercive control sections.

---

## Pass 1 — Fixes Applied

None. The content is well-sourced, maintains appropriate academic tone, and presents the Plato-vs-Aristotle neutrality debate as a structuring question rather than resolving it.

## Pass 1 — Remaining Concerns

1. **Sophist rehabilitation:** A brief note acknowledging modern scholarly reassessment of the Sophists would strengthen neutrality in S1.
2. **Prosocial persuasion:** The survey could benefit from a brief acknowledgment that persuasion research covers positive applications, not only threats.
3. **Cambridge Analytica consent nuance:** The "without consent" framing could be refined to "beyond the scope of users' original consent" for greater precision.
