## CHAPTER 10: TWO CULTURES

### Philosophers/Thinkers Covered

- **Jesus of Nazareth** (~4 BCE - 30 CE)
- **Paul** (c. 5-67 CE) - Jewish missionary and theologian
- **Plotinus** (204-270 CE) - Referenced in context of Neoplatonism
- **Ancient Greek philosophers** - Epicureans, Stoics (Athens connection)

### Cultural-Historical Frameworks Introduced

#### Indo-European Culture (4,000 years old)
- **Origins:** Regions around Black Sea and Caspian Sea
- **Spread:** Southeast to Iran/India, southwest to Greece/Spain, west to France/Britain, north to Scandinavia
- **Core Belief:** Polytheism (belief in many gods)
- **Key Characteristic:** Visual/ocular epistemology - "seeing" = understanding
  - Etymology traces: Sanskrit *vidya* → Greek *idea* (Plato) → Latin *video* → Germanic *wissen*
  - Made pictures/sculptures of gods
  - Cyclic view of history (eternal return, seasons)
- **Famous Names Across Languages:**
  - Sky God: Dyaus (Sanskrit) → Zeus (Greek) → Jupiter (Latin) → Tyr (Norse)
  - Gods: Asura (Sanskrit) → Deus (Latin) → Deva/Deus variants
  - Fertility deities: Vani (Sanskrit) → Venus (Latin) → Vaner (Norse)

#### Semitic Culture
- **Origins:** Arabian Peninsula
- **Core Belief:** Monotheism (one God)
- **Key Characteristic:** Auditory epistemology - emphasis on hearing/listening
  - Jewish creed begins "Hear, O Israel!"
  - Prophets began sermons: "Thus spake Jehovah"
  - Religious ceremonies center on reading aloud and reciting
- **Visual Prohibition:** Forbidden to make images of God
  - Old Testament commandment against graven images
  - Continues in Judaism and Islam (Orthodox Christianity also restricts)
- **Linear View of History:** Beginning (creation) → End (Judgment Day)
  - God intervenes in history; history is meaningful, not cyclical
  - Emphasis on historical narrative as sacred (core of holy scriptures)

#### Key Difference: God's Relationship to Creation
- **Indo-European:** Distance between divine and earthly; goal of union/ascent (transmigration of soul, meditation)
- **Semitic:** Distance between God and world; goal of redemption/salvation from sin and death
  - Prayer, sermons, scripture study emphasized over mystical meditation
  - Religion is about relationship, not reunion

### Key Philosophical Concepts

1. **Two Saviors Born in Different Worlds**
   - Plotinus offered Neoplatonic salvation through philosophical contemplation
   - Jesus offered redemption through God's grace and forgiveness (outside Greco-Roman world)

2. **Jesus's Radical Reinterpretation of "Messiah"**
   - Expected: Political/military leader (like King David) to restore Israel's greatness
   - Actual: Spiritual redemption for all humanity; love, forgiveness, mercy as central
   - "Kingdom of God" = compassion for weak/poor, forgiveness, loving neighbors and enemies
   - Distinctive claims: Forgiveness of sins (unprecedented), addressing God as "Father" (Abba - unheard of)
   - Moral teaching: "Love thy neighbor as thyself" + "Love your enemies" + "Turn the other cheek" + "Forgive seventy times seven"

3. **God's Boundless Mercy vs. Human Merit**
   - No one earns God's mercy; we cannot redeem ourselves (contra Greek philosophy)
   - Sinners who repent are more righteous than spotless Pharisees (inversion of moral hierarchy)
   - Ethical demands of Sermon on the Mount show impossibility of human righteousness—purpose is to drive us toward God's forgiveness

4. **The Incarnation vs. Greek Demigods**
   - Jesus = "Perfect God, perfect Man" (not a demigod like Greek heroes)
   - God became human; this is central Christian claim
   - Passion of Christ: suffering servant who bore sins of humanity for atonement
   - Resurrection: not immortality of soul (Greek concept) but resurrection of body (Jewish concept) through God's miraculous power

5. **Paul's Universal Redemption Theology**
   - Christianity is not a Jewish sect but universal message for all humanity
   - "Old Covenant" (God + Israel) → "New Covenant" (God + all mankind through Christ)
   - Universalism: Non-Jews need not become Jews first; no dietary laws required
   - Natural human tendency to seek God; Christ reveals God reaching out to humanity

### Key Questions Posed

1. What happens when a radically different cultural worldview (Christianity, Semitic) enters a dominant civilizational sphere (Greco-Roman, Indo-European)?
2. How can we reconcile linear history with cyclic history? Purpose-driven narrative with eternal recurrence?
3. What is the nature of salvation: ascent through reason/contemplation (Greek) or grace through faith (Semitic)?
4. Is God distant and impersonal (philosophic abstraction) or intimate and personal (who acts in history)?
5. Who deserves redemption? Can sinners be righteous?
6. How does Jesus's teaching differ fundamentally from every other messianic claim?

### What Gaarder Covers Well

- **Cultural Anthropology:** Clear, systematic comparison of Indo-European vs. Semitic worldviews with specific etymological and mythological evidence
- **Historical Context:** Concrete situating of Jesus within Jewish expectations (David's kingdom, political oppression, prophetic tradition)
- **Jesus's Radicalism:** Excellent exposition of how Jesus reframes "Messiah," "Kingdom of God," and moral righteousness
- **Collision of Worldviews:** Vivid portrayal of Paul encountering Greek philosophy in Athens and the tension it creates
- **Universalism:** Good explanation of why Christianity succeeded in spreading beyond Jewish community (Paul's decision)
- **Incarnational Theology:** Clear articulation of the central paradox (God + human in one person) and distinction from pagan demigods

### What He Omits or Oversimplifies

- **Complex Theology of Atonement:** Why Christ's death was *necessary* for redemption is touched lightly; deeper theories of substitution/sacrifice not addressed
- **Resurrection Body:** Resurrection of the body (vs. immortal soul) is mentioned but not deeply explored
- **Creedal Development:** Mentions "first Creed" but doesn't discuss councils (Nicaea 325, etc.) that shaped Christian orthodoxy
- **Women's Role:** Notes women were fervent converts but doesn't explore implications of gender in early Christianity
- **Jewish-Christian Separation:** Treats Christianity's break with Judaism somewhat abstractly; actual historical rupture is complex
- **Pagan Religious Competition:** Mentions Hellenistic religions briefly but doesn't discuss Mithraism, Isis cults, or actual competition
- **Scriptural Interpretation:** Limited discussion of hermeneutics—how early Christians read OT as messianic prophecy
- **Political Power:** Mentions Rome's decline briefly; doesn't analyze how Christianity benefited from imperial patronage

### Notable Framings (Paraphrased)

- **"Two Cultures Colliding":** European civilization has roots in both Indo-European (visual, polytheistic, cyclic) and Semitic (auditory, monotheistic, linear) thought
- **"Hearing vs. Seeing":** Indo-Europeans prioritized sight (idea, vision, sculpture); Semites prioritized hearing (word, prophecy, commandment)
- **"Goethe's Observation":** Those who don't draw on 3,000 years of history are "living from hand to mouth"—understanding roots makes one fully human
- **"The Only Way to Avoid Floating in a Vacuum":** Knowing one's historical roots is existentially necessary

### Connections to Later Philosophy

- **Medieval Christian Philosophy:** Augustine and Aquinas will attempt to synthesize Greek philosophy with Christian theology (the synthesis Gaarder previews here)
- **Enlightenment Critique:** Rationalists will later critique the emphasis on faith over reason; empiricists will question the a priori knowledge from revelation
- **Nietzsche:** Will later savage Christian morality as "slave morality"—inversion of Greek virtue ethics that Gaarder notes here
- **Hegel:** Will see history as progressive unfolding of Spirit (synthesis of linear + progressive history)
- **Existentialism:** Will return to questions of authenticity, freedom, and how to live—echoing Jesus's ethical radicalism
- **Modern Religious Studies:** Gaarder's Indo-European/Semitic dichotomy reflects 19th-century comparative religion (Müller, etc.) now seen as somewhat oversimplified

## CHAPTER 11: THE MIDDLE AGES

### Philosophers/Thinkers Covered

- **St. Augustine** (354-430 CE)
  - Born in Tagaste (North Africa)
  - Traveled: Carthage → Rome → Milan → Hippo
  - Teacher at Carthage, Rome, Milan; bishop of Hippo (last years)

- **St. Thomas Aquinas** (1225-1274 CE)
  - Born in Aquino (between Rome and Naples)
  - Teacher at University of Paris
  - Greatest medieval philosopher

- **Hildegard of Bingen** (1098-1179 CE)
  - Nun, preacher, author, physician, botanist, naturalist
  - Rhine Valley mystic
  - Visionary (saw Sophia/divine wisdom)

- **Albert the Great** (Albertus Magnus)
  - Philosophy teacher of St. Thomas Aquinas
  - Medieval logician

### Historical Timeframe & Periodization

**Gaarder's Elegant "Clock" Metaphor:**
- Midnight (0 AD) = Birth of Jesus
- 1 AM (100 AD) = Paul's missionary journeys
- 1:15 AM (115 AD) = Paul dies in Rome
- 3 AM (300 AD) = Christian church banned, then accepted (Constantine, 313 AD)
- 4 AM (400 AD) = Rome crumbles; Western Empire falls (410), Eastern continues
- 5:30 AM (530 AD) = Plato's Academy closed; Benedictine order founded (529) - **symbolic turning point**
- 11-1 PM (1100-1300 AD) = High Gothic, cathedral schools, first universities (~1200)
- 2 PM (1400 AD) = Cock crows; Middle Ages ebb away; Renaissance begins

**Key Historical Dates:**
- 330 AD: Constantine moves capital to Constantinople ("second Rome")
- 395 AD: Roman Empire divided (Western + Eastern)
- 410 AD: Rome plundered by barbarians
- 476 AD: Western Roman Empire falls; Eastern (Byzantine) continues to 1453
- 529 AD: Plato's Academy closed; Benedictine monastic order founded
- 1200 AD: First universities founded with "faculties" (as today)
- 1453 AD: Constantinople falls to Turks; becomes Istanbul

### Key Philosophical Concepts

#### 1. **The Central Question of Medieval Philosophy**
All medieval philosophy circles one question: **What is the relationship between faith and reason? Between Christian revelation and Greek philosophy?**
- Must we simply believe Christian doctrine?
- Can reason help us understand it?
- Is there contradiction between Bible and reason, or are they compatible?

#### 2. **St. Augustine: Christianizing Plato**
**Background:**
- Initially Manichaean (dualism: good/evil, light/dark, spirit/matter; redemption through spirit transcending matter)
- Influenced by Stoicism (no sharp good/evil divide)
- Culminated in Neoplatonism (all existence is divine)
- Became Christian after inner struggle over problem of evil

**Key Ideas:**
- **Synthesis:** Christianity + Platonic philosophy are compatible
  - Platonic Ideas located in Divine Mind (not eternal, separate realm)
  - This preserves Plato's eternal ideas while affirming biblical creation
- **Evil:** Not a positive force but "absence of God" (Plotinian idea)
  - Evil comes from human disobedience, not from a cosmic principle
  - "Good will is God's work; evil will is falling away from God's work"
- **Reason + Faith:** Reason has limits in religion
  - Christian mysteries accessible only through faith
  - But faith "illuminates" the soul, granting supernatural knowledge of God
  - "Our heart is not quiet until it rests in Thee" (Augustine's famous statement)
- **Problem of Evil Resolved:** All creation is good; evil is privation (absence), not entity
- **Soul/Body Duality:** Insurmountable barrier between God and world (biblical)
  - Yet humans have material bodies ("moth and rust doth corrupt") AND spiritual souls
  - Soul can know God; body belongs to physical world
- **Predestination:** Deeply problematic doctrine
  - Entire human race lost after Fall
  - God predestines who will be saved (not earned; pure grace)
  - Humans have free will but God "foresees" our choices
  - No one deserves redemption; any salvation is God's arbitrary mercy
  - Sophie (rightly) finds this harsh and unfair vs. Socratic common sense
- **History as Philosophy:**
  - First Western philosopher to make history central to philosophy
  - Struggle between "Kingdom of God" and "Kingdom of the World" plays out in history
  - God needs all of history to realize the Kingdom
  - History = development from childhood (Adam) to old age (End Time)
  - "Divine foresight directs the history of mankind from Adam to the end of time as if it were the story of one man who gradually develops from childhood to old age"

**Augustine's "City of God":**
- Two kingdoms struggle within every person
- Kingdom of God more or less present in the Church
- Kingdom of World present in the State (e.g., declining Rome)
- "No salvation outside the Church" became medieval orthodoxy
- Not challenged until Reformation (16th century)

#### 3. **St. Thomas Aquinas: The Great Synthesis of Reason and Faith**
**Background:**
- Lived ~400 years after Augustine
- Arab scholars brought Aristotle to Italy (twelfth century onward)
- Aristotle's works on science, logic, natural philosophy previously unknown in West
- Creates need to reconcile Aristotle with Christianity

**Key Ideas: "Two Paths to One Truth"**
- **Core Thesis:** No conflict between reason and faith; they lead to same truths via different routes
- **Natural Theology:** Truth accessible through reason + senses (e.g., Aristotle's proofs)
- **Revealed Theology:** Truth revealed through Scripture + faith (e.g., incarnation, salvation)
- **Two Paths Don't Contradict:** Example: Thunder and lightning
  - Blind person hears thunder (faith) = truth
  - Deaf person sees lightning (reason) = truth
  - Seeing both reinforces truth; no contradiction
- **Creator and Creation:**
  - Aristotle's "formal cause" (God) sets natural processes going
  - But Bible reveals God's personal nature, love, redemption—info not in philosophy
  - Analogy: Reading *Of Mice and Men* reveals Steinbeck's character, not his biography
  - Nature's creation shows God exists; Scripture tells us *who* God is

**Natural Knowledge (Reason):**
- We can prove God's existence from reason (Aristotle's philosophy)
- We can derive basic moral truths without Scripture ("do unto others")
- Conscience is divinely given to distinguish right from wrong
- The "uncaused cause"; the "formal cause"

**Revealed Knowledge (Faith):**
- Only Scripture teaches: Creation in six days, Jesus as Son of God, Incarnation, Resurrection
- "Verities of faith" (mysteries) only accessible through belief + revelation
- Bible is God's "autobiography" (Augustine's analogy extended)

**Progressive Scale of Existence:**
- Adopts Aristotle's scala naturae: plants → animals → humans → angels → God
- Aligns with Christian hierarchy:
  - Plants: life only
  - Animals: life + sensation
  - Humans: life + sensation + reason (intelligence)
  - Angels: life + sensation + reason + no body (instantaneous knowledge, no need to ponder/reason step-by-step)
  - God: knows all in single coherent vision; time doesn't exist for God ("our now" ≠ "God's now")

**Women & Aristotle's Error:**
- Aquinas uncritically adopted Aristotle's misogyny
  - Woman = incomplete man
  - Only father contributes to reproduction (woman passive, receptive)
  - This aligned with biblical Genesis (woman from Adam's rib)
- **Qualifier (somewhat):** Woman's soul equal to man's; equality exists in Heaven where physical differences cease
- **Note:** Mammalian eggs not discovered until 1827; understandable historical error but still problematic

#### 4. **The Medieval Period as Productive, Not Dark**
Gaarder pushes back against "Dark Ages" label:
- **Negative Legacy:** Decline from Roman high culture
  - Rome had cities with sewers, baths, libraries, architecture
  - Early medieval centuries saw cultural collapse, population decline
  - Rome fell from 1+ million to 40,000 by 600 AD
  - Trade, economy declined; feudalism replaced market economy
  - Ruins pillaged for materials (archaeologists lament)
- **Positive Legacy:** Germination and growth
  - School systems developed (convent schools early, cathedral schools 12th century, universities ~1200)
  - Nation-states established with cities, citizenship, folk culture
  - Fairy tales, folk songs, legends (Romeo & Juliet, Ivanhoe, Pied Piper, Joan of Arc, etc.)
  - Great Gothic cathedrals built (1100-1300s)
  - Intellectual and spiritual depth despite material decline
  - Christianity became unifying force (though conversion gradual)

**Norse Christianity:**
- Norway converted 11th century (late)
- Pre-Christian beliefs persisted under surface
- Pagan elements integrated with Christianity (Scandinavian Christmas customs blend Christian + Old Norse)
- "Married folk grow to resemble each other"—pagan and Christian syncretized

#### 5. **Three Streams of Greek Philosophy Through Middle Ages**
Classical culture "divided like a river into three streams" and reconverged:
- **Western Europe (Roman Catholic):** Neoplatonism (Augustine lineage)
- **Eastern Europe (Byzantine, Constantinople/Byzantium):** Plato
- **Southern (Arabic, North Africa, Spain):** Aristotle
  - Arabs preserved Greek science; were superior in mathematics, chemistry, astronomy, medicine
  - We still use Arabic numerals

**At Renaissance (~1300-1400):**
- Three streams met again in Northern Italy
- Arabic influence from Spain, Greek from Byzantium, Neoplatonic from West
- "Rebirth" of antique culture; Greek civilization survived the "Dark Ages"

### Key Questions Posed

1. Can Greek reason be reconciled with Christian faith, or do they contradict?
2. Is faith enough, or must reason validate theology?
3. Can we locate Platonic Forms within God's mind without compromising creation?
4. Does evil exist as a force, or is it merely absence of good?
5. Why does God predestine some to damnation if all humans are fallen?
6. How can free will coexist with God's foreknowledge?
7. What is history's role? Is it meaningful (linear) or cyclical?
8. Can Aristotelian science and Christian theology coexist?
9. What is the proper hierarchy of being? Where does humanity fit?
10. Should reason or faith be the ultimate arbiter in theological disputes?

### What Gaarder Covers Well

- **Faith-Reason Synthesis:** Excellent exposition of both Augustine's priority of faith + reason's limits, and Aquinas's "two paths" complementarity
- **Historical Positioning:** Clear narrative arc from late antiquity through medieval consolidation
- **Cultural Inheritance:** Elegant metaphor of philosophy as river dividing and reconverging; shows continuity rather than rupture
- **Augustine's Complexity:** Good treatment of his intellectual journey (Manichaean → Stoic → Neoplatonist → Christian) and the problems he grappled with
- **Practical Examples:** Uses contemporary references (Steinbeck, weather) to make abstract philosophical points concrete
- **Aquinas's Logic:** The "two paths," "scale of existence," and natural theology are clearly explained
- **Hildegard of Bingen:** Good example of women's agency in medieval intellectual life (though brief)
- **Medieval Rehabilitation:** Pushes back against Dark Ages stereotype without romanticizing the period

### What He Omits or Oversimplifies

- **Augustine's Development:** Doesn't discuss his earliest works; Gaarder focuses on mature thought
- **Neoplatonism's Details:** Only touches on Plotinus; doesn't discuss later Neoplatonic schools, pagan Neoplatonists
- **Church Councils:** No mention of Nicaea (325), Constantinople (381), Chalcedon (451)—theological development via councils
- **Aquinas's Summa:** Doesn't discuss structure or comprehensiveness of *Summa Theologiae*
- **Mysticism:** No mention of Meister Eckhart, Bernhard of Clairvaux, or contemplative theology
- **Islamic Philosophy:** Mentions Arabs preserved Aristotle but not Al-Ghazali, Averroes, Avicenna—major Islamic philosophers
- **Scholasticism:** Method (quaestio, disputatio) not discussed
- **Monastic Life:** Benedictine order founded but monastic spirituality/practice not explored
- **Heresy:** No discussion of heretical movements (Cathars, Waldensians) or inquisition
- **Women Philosophers:** Only Hildegard; doesn't mention other medieval women thinkers (Héloïse, etc.)
- **Dante:** No mention of *Divine Comedy* as medieval synthesis of theology and poetry
- **Science:** Limited discussion of medieval science despite Aquinas's interest in natural philosophy
- **Politics:** Church-State struggles mentioned but not deeply analyzed

### Notable Framings (Paraphrased)

- **"Medieval Monks Turned Into Book Monks":** Transition from oral to written transmission of knowledge (monastery symbolizes this)
- **"The Clock Analogy":** Makes thousand-year period comprehensible as 10-hour day
- **"River of Civilization":** Culture divided into three streams, each preserving different parts of Greek legacy
- **"One Truth, Two Paths":** Faith and reason are complementary ways of accessing same reality
- **"Absence of God, Not Presence of Evil":** Evil is privatio boni (Augustinian/Neoplatonic, not dualistic)
- **"Aquinas on God's Time":** God sees all moments simultaneously; "now" differs for God vs. humans—subtle response to predestination paradox

### Connections to Later Philosophy

- **Reformation (16th century):** Luther, Calvin challenged Aquinas's synthesis; sola fide vs. faith + reason
- **Descartes (17th century):** Returns to Augustinian method (cogito, seeking certainty in doubt)
- **Leibniz:** "Monadology" echoes medieval scale of being
- **Hegel:** Synthesizes medieval (faith) + Enlightenment (reason) in dialectical philosophy of history
- **Nietzsche:** Critiques Christian morality as weakening (contra medieval virtue ethics)
- **Modern Thomism:** Neo-Thomist revival (20th century) reasserts Aquinas's synthesis
- **Feminist Philosophy:** Later critique of Aquinas/Aristotle on women
- **Philosophy of Science:** Questions about natural theology and cosmological proofs continue
- **Literary Theory:** Dante's medieval synthesis (reason + faith + poetry) influences later aesthetic thought
- **Religious Studies:** Medieval theology shapes contemporary interfaith dialogue and comparative religion

## SYNTHESIS: Two Cultures Meeting the Middle Ages

Gaarder masterfully shows how **Western philosophy is not univocal but hybrid:**

1. **Indo-European** (Greek): Visual, polytheistic, cyclic time, reasoning toward transcendence
2. **Semitic** (Hebrew/Christian): Auditory, monotheistic, linear time, revelation as God reaching down

Medieval philosophers attempt **synthesis:** taking Greek *method* (reason, categories, logic) and applying it to Christian *content* (revelation, redemption, incarnation).

- **Augustine** prioritizes faith but uses Platonic categories
- **Aquinas** systematizes both reason and faith as valid paths

Both show that **intellectual rigor is compatible with religious belief**—a model that shaped Western thought for centuries.

The metaphor of the "river dividing into three streams" captures something profound: **no absolute rupture between ancient and medieval thought, but transformation and transmission through different cultural channels.** The "Dark Ages" were less a collapse than a **redistribution and reorganization** of intellectual life.

Key unresolved tensions:
- Predestination vs. free will (Augustine)
- Universals vs. particulars (medieval realism debate)
- Reason's proper limits (Aquinas's hierarchy of truth)
- Women's status (inherited from Aristotle, not questioned)

These shape subsequent philosophy through the Reformation, Enlightenment, and modern period.
