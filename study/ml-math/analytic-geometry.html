Analytic Geometry

Geometry built on top of algebra. Inner products add length, angle, and distance to vector spaces — enabling projections, orthogonalization, and rotations that underlie PCA, regression, and SVMs.

Deisenroth, Faisal & Ong — Ch 3 (pp 70–95) Raw notes ↗ ← C2 Linear Algebra
§3.1

Norms

A norm assigns a non-negative length $\|\mathbf{x}\|$ to every vector. Three axioms pin down what "length" must mean. Different norms give different shapes for the unit ball — and different geometries for the same vector space.

Unit balls: L1 (Manhattan) vs L2 (Euclidean)
Three axioms (any norm)
Homogeneous · Triangle · Positive definite
$\|\lambda\mathbf{x}\| = |\lambda|\|\mathbf{x}\|$  ·  $\|\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{y}\| \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|+\|\mathbf{y}\|$  ·  $\|\mathbf{x}\|\geq 0$ and $=0$ iff $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}$.
Euclidean norm (default)
$\|\mathbf{x}\|_2 = \sqrt{\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}} = \sqrt{\sum_i x_i^2}$
Used by default throughout MML unless stated otherwise. Also called the $\ell_2$ norm.
Manhattan norm
$\|\mathbf{x}\|_1 = \sum_i |x_i|$
Taxi-cab distance. Not induced by any inner product — shows norms and inner products are distinct concepts.
§3.2

Inner Products

An inner product $\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle$ is a bilinear map that is symmetric and positive definite. It's strictly more structure than a norm: inner products let you measure angles, not just lengths. Any symmetric positive definite (SPD) matrix defines one.

Dot product (standard)
$\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle = \mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{y} = \sum_i x_i y_i$
The default inner product on $\mathbb{R}^n$. The pair $(\mathbb{R}^n, \langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle)$ is a Euclidean vector space.
General inner product
$\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle = \mathbf{x}^\top A \mathbf{y}$
Any SPD matrix $A$ defines a valid inner product. Different $A$ gives different geometry — different angles and distances on the same vectors.
SPD matrix condition
$A = A^\top$ and $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x} > 0 \;\forall\mathbf{x}\neq\mathbf{0}$
Symmetric + all eigenvalues positive. $A_1=\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}9&6\\6&5\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$ is SPD; $A_2=\bigl[\begin{smallmatrix}9&6\\6&3\end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$ is not.
Why this matters for ML
The "kernel trick" (Ch 12) replaces inner products with a kernel function $k(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})$, implicitly computing $\langle\phi(\mathbf{x}),\phi(\mathbf{y})\rangle$ in a high-dimensional feature space without ever forming $\phi(\mathbf{x})$ explicitly. The theory works because kernels are inner products.
§3.3

Lengths & Distances

Every inner product induces a norm: $\|\mathbf{x}\| = \sqrt{\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}\rangle}$. But not every norm comes from an inner product (Manhattan doesn't). The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality is the key result that makes angles well-defined.

Induced norm
$\|\mathbf{x}\| = \sqrt{\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}\rangle}$
Inner product → norm (always). Norm → inner product (only sometimes, via polarization identity).
Cauchy-Schwarz
$|\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|\|\mathbf{y}\|$
Ensures $-1 \leq \frac{\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle}{\|\mathbf{x}\|\|\mathbf{y}\|} \leq 1$, so $\cos\omega$ is always in range. Foundation for the angle formula.
Distance (metric)
$d(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) = \|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\|$
Positive definite · symmetric · triangle inequality. Note: inner product is large when vectors are similar; distance is small. Opposite directions.
§3.4–3.5

Angles & Orthogonality

Once you have an inner product, you can define angles. Orthogonality ($\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle=0$) generalizes perpendicularity and is the geometric heart of projections, the Gram-Schmidt algorithm, and the SVD.

Angle between vectors
$\cos\omega = \dfrac{\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle}{\|\mathbf{x}\|\|\mathbf{y}\|}$
Cauchy-Schwarz guarantees this is in $[-1,1]$. Orthogonal ($\omega=90°$) when numerator = 0.
Orthogonality
$\mathbf{x} \perp \mathbf{y} \iff \langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle = 0$
The zero vector is orthogonal to everything. Orthogonal + unit length = orthonormal. Depends on the choice of inner product!
Orthogonal matrix
$AA^\top = I = A^\top A$
Columns are orthonormal. $A^{-1} = A^\top$ (cheap to compute). Preserves both distances and angles. Called "orthogonal" but "orthonormal" is more precise.
Orthonormal basis (ONB)
$\langle\mathbf{b}_i,\mathbf{b}_j\rangle = \delta_{ij}$
$\delta_{ij}=1$ if $i=j$, else 0. Every basis vector has unit length; all pairs are orthogonal. ONBs make coordinate computation trivial: $\lambda_i = \langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{b}_i\rangle$.
Orthogonality depends on the inner product
Two vectors orthogonal under the dot product ($\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{y}=0$) need not be orthogonal under $\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{y}$ for some other SPD $A$. Geometry is relative to the inner product you choose.
§3.6

Orthogonal Complement

The orthogonal complement $U^\perp$ of a subspace $U$ collects all vectors orthogonal to $U$. It gives a clean decomposition of any vector into a $U$-component and a $U^\perp$-component — the mathematical foundation for normal vectors and hyperplanes.

Definition
$U^\perp = \{\mathbf{v} : \langle\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u}\rangle=0\;\forall\mathbf{u}\in U\}$
$U$ and $U^\perp$ together span the whole space: $\dim(U) + \dim(U^\perp) = \dim(V)$. Any $\mathbf{x}$ decomposes uniquely as $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{u}^\perp$.
Normal vector
$\mathbf{w} \perp U$, $\|\mathbf{w}\|=1$
If $U$ is a plane in 3D, $U^\perp$ is 1D — spanned by a single normal vector $\mathbf{w}$. A hyperplane in $\mathbb{R}^n$ has a 1D orthogonal complement. SVMs (Ch 12) classify via the normal to a hyperplane.
§3.7

Functions as Vectors

Inner products generalize to function spaces via integration. This is the bridge to Fourier analysis, Hilbert spaces, and Gaussian processes.

Function inner product
$\langle u, v \rangle = \int_a^b u(x)\,v(x)\,dx$
If this is 0, the functions are orthogonal. $\sin(x) \perp \cos(x)$ on $[-\pi,\pi]$ — the basis for Fourier series.
Why this matters
Fourier series are exactly projections of functions onto an orthonormal basis of sines and cosines. Gaussian processes (Ch 9 extension) and kernels (Ch 12) live in Hilbert spaces — infinite-dimensional inner product spaces where this integral definition is the inner product.
§3.8

Orthogonal Projections ⭐

The most important section in C3. The orthogonal projection $\pi_U(\mathbf{x})$ is the closest point in subspace $U$ to $\mathbf{x}$. "Closest" means the displacement vector $\mathbf{x} - \pi_U(\mathbf{x})$ is orthogonal to $U$. This single idea underlies linear regression, PCA, and least-squares.

Orthogonal projection onto a 1D subspace

The displacement $\mathbf{x}-\pi_U(\mathbf{x})$ is orthogonal to $U$. The coordinate $\lambda = \langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{b}\rangle/\|\mathbf{b}\|^2$ locates the projection.

1D projection (onto span{b})
$\pi_U(\mathbf{x}) = \dfrac{\mathbf{b}^\top\mathbf{x}}{\|\mathbf{b}\|^2}\,\mathbf{b}$
Coordinate: $\lambda = \mathbf{b}^\top\mathbf{x}/\|\mathbf{b}\|^2$. Length: $\|\pi_U(\mathbf{x})\| = |\cos\omega|\|\mathbf{x}\|$. Same as the trig projection you know from physics.
1D projection matrix
$P_\pi = \dfrac{\mathbf{b}\mathbf{b}^\top}{\mathbf{b}^\top\mathbf{b}}$
$P_\pi^2 = P_\pi$ (idempotent — projecting twice = projecting once). $P_\pi$ is symmetric.
General projection (onto span of B)
$\pi_U(\mathbf{x}) = B(B^\top B)^{-1}B^\top\mathbf{x}$
$B = [\mathbf{b}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{b}_m]$ has full column rank. The coordinates $\boldsymbol{\lambda} = (B^\top B)^{-1}B^\top\mathbf{x}$ come from the normal equations.
General projection matrix
$P_\pi = B(B^\top B)^{-1}B^\top$
The most important formula in C3. Ridge regression adds $\lambda I$: $(B^\top B + \lambda I)^{-1}B^\top$ — improves stability, derived rigorously in Ch 9.
The normal equations
The condition $B^\top(\mathbf{x} - B\boldsymbol{\lambda}) = \mathbf{0}$ gives $B^\top B\boldsymbol{\lambda} = B^\top\mathbf{x}$. This is the normal equations. In Ch 9, with $B = X$ (data matrix) and $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}$ (labels), this becomes least-squares linear regression. The projection matrix $P_\pi = X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top$ is the "hat matrix."
Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization

Given any basis $\{\mathbf{b}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{b}_n\}$, Gram-Schmidt builds an ONB iteratively:

$$\mathbf{u}_1 = \mathbf{b}_1$$ $$\mathbf{u}_k = \mathbf{b}_k - \pi_{\text{span}[\mathbf{u}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{u}_{k-1}]}(\mathbf{b}_k), \quad k=2,\ldots,n$$

Each step: subtract the projection of $\mathbf{b}_k$ onto the already-orthogonalized vectors. Then normalize by $\|\mathbf{u}_k\|$ to get unit length.

Why it works: The projection subtracts the "component already explained" by the previous vectors, leaving only the genuinely new direction. The result always spans the same space: $\text{span}\{\mathbf{u}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{u}_n\} = \text{span}\{\mathbf{b}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{b}_n\}$.

Connection to QR decomposition (Ch 4): Gram-Schmidt is numerically equivalent to QR factorization. $A = QR$ where $Q$ has orthonormal columns and $R$ is upper triangular.

Projection onto affine subspaces

For an affine subspace $L = \mathbf{x}_0 + U$:

  1. Translate: form $\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_0$ and project onto $U$ (the direction space)
  2. $\pi_U(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_0) = B(B^\top B)^{-1}B^\top(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_0)$
  3. Translate back: $\pi_L(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}_0 + \pi_U(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_0)$

This appears in regression (the design matrix includes an intercept, so the column space of $X$ is an affine subspace when centered) and in SVMs (decision boundaries are affine hyperplanes).

§3.9

Rotations

Rotations are the special orthogonal transformations that preserve both lengths and angles and have determinant $+1$ (no reflection). They generalize cleanly from 2D to $n$D via Givens rotations.

Rotation in ℝ²
$R(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$
Positive $\theta$ = counterclockwise. $R(\theta)^\top = R(-\theta) = R(\theta)^{-1}$. In 2D, all rotations commute: $R(\alpha)R(\beta) = R(\alpha+\beta)$.
Rotation in ℝ³ (about $e_3$)
$R_3(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}$
The $e_3$ axis is fixed; rotation happens in the $e_1 e_2$-plane. Similar $R_1, R_2$ matrices for the other axes.
Givens rotation (ℝⁿ)
$R_{ij}(\theta) = I$ with $r_{ii}=r_{jj}=\cos\theta$, $r_{ij}=-\sin\theta$, $r_{ji}=\sin\theta$
Rotates in the $(i,j)$-plane, fixes all other dimensions. Any rotation in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a product of Givens rotations.
3D+ rotations do NOT commute
In 3D, rotating about $x$ then $y$ differs from rotating about $y$ then $x$. This is why Euler angles have gimbal lock and why quaternions exist. Rotation matrices in $\text{SO}(n)$ form a non-abelian group for $n \geq 3$.
