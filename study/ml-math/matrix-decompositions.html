Matrix Decompositions

Every matrix hides a geometry. Decompositions expose it — eigenvalues find the preserved directions, SVD finds the principal axes, Cholesky finds the square root. This chapter is the toolbox that makes Part II computable.

§4.1

Determinant & Trace

Two scalars that summarize an entire matrix. The determinant measures signed volume change — how much the transformation stretches or collapses space. The trace is the sum of diagonal entries, and both connect directly to eigenvalues.

Determinant = signed volume scaling
2×2 Determinant
$\det\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix} = ad - bc$
Signed area of the parallelogram spanned by the two columns.
Characteristic Polynomial
$p_A(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)$
Roots are eigenvalues. Constant term = $\det(A)$; the $(n\!-\!1)$ coefficient encodes $\text{tr}(A)$.
Trace
$\text{tr}(A) = \sum_i a_{ii} = \sum_i \lambda_i$
Sum of diagonal entries = sum of eigenvalues. Cyclic: $\text{tr}(ABC) = \text{tr}(BCA) = \text{tr}(CAB)$.
Eigenvalue Connection
$\det(A) = \prod_i \lambda_i$
Product of all eigenvalues. This is why a zero eigenvalue makes the matrix singular.
Laplace expansion (cofactor expansion)

For any row or column $j$, the determinant expands as: $$\det(A) = \sum_k (-1)^{k+j} a_{kj} \det(A_{kj})$$ where $A_{kj}$ is the $(n\!-\!1)\times(n\!-\!1)$ submatrix with row $k$ and column $j$ removed.

This gives a recursive algorithm: reduce $n\times n$ to $(n\!-\!1)\times(n\!-\!1)$ determinants. For a triangular matrix, the determinant is simply the product of diagonal entries — Gaussian elimination exploits this.

Practical note: Computing $\det$ by Laplace expansion costs $O(n!)$. Real code uses LU decomposition ($O(n^3)$) instead.

Why tr(xyᵀ) = yᵀx matters
For vectors, $\text{tr}(\mathbf{x}\mathbf{y}^\top) = \mathbf{y}^\top\mathbf{x}$ (a scalar). This identity shows up constantly in gradient derivations in Ch 5 and in the MLE for Gaussians in Ch 6.
§4.2

Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

An eigenvector is a direction that a matrix preserves — only its length changes, never its line. The eigenvalue is the scale factor. Finding these special directions is the key to understanding what any linear map fundamentally does.

Eigenvectors: direction preserved, length scaled by λ
Eigenvector equation
$A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}$,   $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$
λ = eigenvalue (scale factor); x = eigenvector (preserved direction). Equivalent to $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.
Find eigenvalues
$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$
The characteristic polynomial has degree $n$, so $n$ roots (complex, possibly repeated). Then solve $(A-\lambda_i I)\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}$ for each.
Algebraic multiplicity
How many times $\lambda_i$ is a root of $p_A$
Always $\geq$ geometric multiplicity (dimension of eigenspace). Equal ⇒ not defective.
Spectral theorem
$A = A^\top \Rightarrow$ real $\lambda_i$, orthogonal eigenvectors
Symmetric matrices always diagonalizable with an ONB of eigenvectors. This is why covariance matrices are tractable.
PageRank connection
Google's PageRank vector is the eigenvector of the link-transition matrix $A$ for eigenvalue $\lambda = 1$. The power method — repeatedly multiply $A\mathbf{x}$, $A^2\mathbf{x}$, … — converges to the dominant eigenvector. Eigenvalues are not just theory; they power global-scale infrastructure.
Why symmetric matrices always have real eigenvalues

Suppose $A = A^\top$ and $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}$ for some complex $\lambda$ and $\mathbf{x}$. Then:

$$\lambda\,\overline{\mathbf{x}}^\top\mathbf{x} = \overline{\mathbf{x}}^\top A\mathbf{x} = (A^\top\overline{\mathbf{x}})^\top\mathbf{x} = \overline{(A\mathbf{x})}^\top\mathbf{x} = \overline{\lambda}\,\overline{\mathbf{x}}^\top\mathbf{x}$$

Since $\overline{\mathbf{x}}^\top\mathbf{x} = \|\mathbf{x}\|^2 > 0$, we get $\lambda = \overline{\lambda}$, meaning $\lambda$ is real. The same argument shows orthogonality of eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues.

§4.3

Cholesky Decomposition

For a symmetric positive definite (SPD) matrix, the Cholesky decomposition is the "square root" — it factors $A = LL^\top$ where $L$ is lower triangular with positive diagonal. Three practical uses make this the workhorse of numerical linear algebra in ML.

Cholesky factorization
$A = LL^\top$
$L$ is lower triangular, positive diagonal. Exists and is unique for SPD $A$. Costs $O(n^3/3)$ — half the LU cost.
SPD condition
$\mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$
Equivalent: all eigenvalues $> 0$. Test: if Cholesky succeeds, $A$ is SPD. Covariance matrices are always SPD (or SPSD).
Three uses in machine learning
1. Solving Ax = b: Factor $A = LL^\top$, solve $L\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{b}$ (forward), then $L^\top\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}$ (backward). Numerically stable.

2. Sampling from a multivariate Gaussian: If $\Sigma = LL^\top$, draw $\mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},I)$, then $\mathbf{x} = L\mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\Sigma)$. This is the standard algorithm in every probabilistic ML library.

3. Checking SPD: Attempt Cholesky — if it fails (negative square root), $A$ is not SPD.
§4.4

Eigendecomposition & Diagonalization

If a matrix has enough linearly independent eigenvectors, it can be written as a triple: change basis → scale → change basis back. This is diagonalization — and it makes matrix powers, exponentials, and many other computations trivial.

Eigendecomposition
$A = PDP^{-1}$
$D = \text{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n)$, columns of $P$ are eigenvectors. Requires $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors.
Spectral decomposition (symmetric)
$A = PDP^\top$
For $A = A^\top$, $P$ is orthogonal ($P^{-1} = P^\top$). Always exists. Each $\lambda_i$ real, each $\mathbf{p}_i$ orthogonal.
Matrix powers
$A^k = PD^kP^{-1}$
$D^k = \text{diag}(\lambda_1^k,\ldots,\lambda_n^k)$ — trivial to compute. Used for Markov chains, PageRank convergence, recurrences.
Geometric picture
$P^{-1}$ → $D$ → $P$
Step 1: $P^{-1}$ rotates to eigenvector coordinates. Step 2: $D$ scales each axis by its eigenvalue. Step 3: $P$ rotates back.
Trace and determinant from eigendecomposition

$$\det(A) = \det(PDP^{-1}) = \underbrace{\det(P)\det(P^{-1})}_{=1}\det(D) = \lambda_1\cdots\lambda_n$$

$$\text{tr}(A) = \text{tr}(PDP^{-1}) = \text{tr}(DP^{-1}P) = \text{tr}(D) = \lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n$$

The second step uses cyclic invariance of trace: $\text{tr}(ABC) = \text{tr}(BCA)$. Both identities confirm: determinant = product of eigenvalues, trace = sum.

Defective matrices cannot be diagonalized
When an eigenvalue's geometric multiplicity (eigenspace dimension) is less than its algebraic multiplicity (root multiplicity in $p_A$), the matrix is defective — there aren't enough eigenvectors to form $P$. The Jordan normal form handles this case, but it's rarely needed in ML.
§4.5

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) ★

The SVD is the fundamental theorem of linear algebra: every matrix — any shape, any rank, real or complex — factors into three matrices with remarkable properties. It generalizes eigendecomposition to non-square matrices and forms the mathematical core of PCA, recommender systems, and least-squares regression.

SVD: every linear map = rotate → scale → rotate
SVD — any matrix
$A = U\Sigma V^\top$
$U \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times m}$ orthogonal; $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ "diagonal" with $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq 0$; $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ orthogonal.
Singular values
$\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^\top A)}$
$A^\top A$ and $AA^\top$ share all nonzero eigenvalues. Right singular vectors $V$ = eigenvectors of $A^\top A$; left $U$ = eigenvectors of $AA^\top$.
Rank via SVD
$\text{rank}(A) = $ number of nonzero $\sigma_i$
The SVD cleanly identifies rank even for near-singular matrices (unlike Gaussian elimination, which is numerically sensitive).
Pseudoinverse
$A^+ = V\Sigma^+ U^\top$
$\Sigma^+$: reciprocals of nonzero singular values. Gives the minimum-norm least-squares solution to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$. Generalizes the inverse to any shape matrix.
SVD generalizes eigendecomposition
For square symmetric SPD matrices: $U = V = P$, $\Sigma = D$ — SVD = spectral decomposition.
For square symmetric matrices: singular values = |eigenvalues|.
For any matrix: SVD always exists, always real, always non-negative $\sigma_i$. No restrictions.
§4.6

Matrix Approximation (Eckart-Young)

The SVD outer-product expansion reveals the "importance" of each rank-1 component of a matrix. Truncating to the top $k$ terms gives the best possible rank-$k$ approximation — a fact formalized by the Eckart-Young theorem (1936).

Outer product expansion
$A = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i \mathbf{u}_i \mathbf{v}_i^\top$
Each term $\sigma_i\mathbf{u}_i\mathbf{v}_i^\top$ is a rank-1 matrix. The singular values $\sigma_i$ give the weight of each term.
Best rank-k approximation
$\hat{A}_{(k)} = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i \mathbf{u}_i \mathbf{v}_i^\top$
Optimal in both spectral and Frobenius norm. No other rank-$k$ matrix is closer to $A$. This is the Eckart-Young theorem.
Approximation error
$\|A - \hat{A}_{(k)}\|_2 = \sigma_{k+1}$
The spectral-norm error is exactly the next singular value. Frobenius error: $\sqrt{\sigma_{k+1}^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2}$.
Compression ratio
Store $k(m+n+1)$ vs. $mn$ entries
For large $m,n$ and small $k$, this can be a 100× compression. The singular values tell you how many terms you need for a given error budget.
Applications of low-rank approximation
Image compression: Pixels form a matrix $A$; store only top-$k$ singular components. Rank-20 often gives visually acceptable quality.

Recommender systems: Movie-user rating matrix $\to$ SVD $\to$ left singular vectors ≈ "movie concepts", right ≈ "user taste vectors". Fill in missing ratings by $\hat{A}_{(k)}$.

PCA (Ch 10): Principal components of centered data $X$ = right singular vectors of $X$. The SVD is PCA.
§4.7

Matrix Phylogeny

Different matrix types have different structures — and each structure unlocks different decompositions. The hierarchy below shows which factorizations are available at each level.

Matrix type hierarchy — each level adds constraints
The key takeaway
SVD: works for every matrix (top of tree).
Eigendecomposition: square non-defective matrices only.
Spectral decomposition: symmetric matrices — orthogonal $P$.
Cholesky: SPD only — but gives the fastest, most stable factorization.

Knowing which factorization is available tells you what algorithms to use and what properties to exploit.
