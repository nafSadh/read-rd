Principal Component Analysis

Given high-dimensional data, what lower-dimensional representation loses the least information? The answer is always the same: project onto the eigenvectors of the data covariance matrix. This chapter derives that answer from two complementary angles — maximize variance; minimize reconstruction error — and shows that the SVD from C4 makes everything computable.

§10.1

Problem Formulation

PCA is a dimensionality reduction method: given $N$ data points in $\mathbb{R}^D$, find a $M$-dimensional linear subspace ($M \ll D$) that preserves as much structure as possible. The first step is always centering — shifting the data so its mean is zero.

Data matrix
$X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$
N data points, D features. Row $n$ is data point $\mathbf{x}_n \in \mathbb{R}^D$.
Centering
$\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_n = \mathbf{x}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}$
Sample mean $\boldsymbol{\mu} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_n \mathbf{x}_n$. Centered matrix $\tilde{X}$ has zero-mean columns. All subsequent analysis assumes centered data.
Orthonormal basis
$B = [\mathbf{b}_1 | \cdots | \mathbf{b}_M] \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times M}$
Represents the target $M$-dimensional subspace $U \subset \mathbb{R}^D$. Columns satisfy $\mathbf{b}_i^\top \mathbf{b}_j = \delta_{ij}$, so $B^\top B = I_M$.
Two criteria (same answer)
maximize variance  or  minimize error
§10.2: maximize projected variance. §10.3: minimize squared reconstruction error. Both yield the top-$M$ eigenvectors of $S$ — this is a theorem, not a coincidence.
Chapter roadmap
§10.2 and §10.3 each derive the same answer from different starting points. §10.4 gives the algorithm. §10.5 handles $N < D$ via the SVD. §10.6 covers model selection (how many components to keep). §10.7 adds a probabilistic generative model.
§10.2

Maximum Variance Perspective

Project the centered data onto a unit vector $\mathbf{b}$. The variance of the projected scalars is $\mathbf{b}^\top S \mathbf{b}$, where $S$ is the data covariance matrix. Maximizing this subject to $\|\mathbf{b}\| = 1$ is a constrained optimization problem whose solution is the leading eigenvector of $S$.

PC1 captures maximum spread; PC2 is orthogonal with smaller variance
Sample covariance matrix
$S = \dfrac{1}{N}\tilde{X}^\top \tilde{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$
Symmetric, positive semidefinite. Entry $(i,j)$ = sample covariance of features $i$ and $j$.
Projected variance
$\text{Var}(\mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{b}^\top S \mathbf{b}$
Variance of the scalars $z_n = \mathbf{b}^\top \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_n$ for a unit vector $\mathbf{b}$. This is the objective to maximize.
Lagrangian
$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{b},\lambda) = \mathbf{b}^\top S\mathbf{b} - \lambda(\mathbf{b}^\top\mathbf{b} - 1)$
Constrained maximization. Setting $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\mathbf{b} = 0$ gives $S\mathbf{b} = \lambda\mathbf{b}$.
Eigenvector condition
$S\mathbf{b} = \lambda \mathbf{b}$
First PC: eigenvector of $S$ for $\lambda_1$ (largest eigenvalue). Optimal variance = $\lambda_1$. Subsequent PCs use the next largest eigenvalues.
Total variance identity
$\text{tr}(S) = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \cdots + \lambda_D$ = total variance of the data. This follows because $\text{tr}(S)$ equals both the sum of diagonal entries (feature variances) and the sum of eigenvalues (C4 §4.1). Keeping $M$ components captures variance $\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_M$ out of a total $\text{tr}(S)$.
Subsequent principal components — why orthogonality is automatic

The $m$-th principal component $\mathbf{b}_m$ is the eigenvector of $S$ corresponding to the $m$-th largest eigenvalue $\lambda_m$, subject to orthogonality with $\mathbf{b}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_{m-1}$.

The Lagrangian approach generalizes straightforwardly. However, the orthogonality constraint is already satisfied automatically: by the spectral theorem (C4 §4.2), eigenvectors of a symmetric matrix for distinct eigenvalues are always orthogonal. When eigenvalues are repeated (degenerate), any orthonormal basis of the eigenspace is equally valid — PCA is non-unique in that case.

Consequence: The matrix $B = [\mathbf{b}_1 | \cdots | \mathbf{b}_M]$ formed from the top-$M$ eigenvectors automatically satisfies $B^\top B = I_M$ — no Gram-Schmidt needed if the eigenvalues are distinct.

§10.3

Projection & Reconstruction Perspective

Alternatively: find the subspace $B$ that minimizes the average squared reconstruction error $\|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_n - \hat{\tilde{\mathbf{x}}}_n\|^2$. The reconstruction is the orthogonal projection back onto the subspace. Minimizing error gives exactly the same eigenvectors as maximizing variance — the two objectives are complementary by design.

Projection onto subspace B and the perpendicular reconstruction error
Projection scores
$\mathbf{z}_n = B^\top \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_n \in \mathbb{R}^M$
Coordinates of $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_n$ in the reduced $M$-dimensional space. Score matrix $Z = \tilde{X}B \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times M}$.
Reconstruction
$\hat{\tilde{\mathbf{x}}}_n = B\mathbf{z}_n = BB^\top \tilde{\mathbf{x}}_n$
$BB^\top$ is the orthogonal projection matrix onto $\text{col}(B)$. Reconstruction lives in $\mathbb{R}^D$ but is constrained to the subspace.
Average reconstruction error
$J = \dfrac{1}{N}\sum_n \|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_n - \hat{\tilde{\mathbf{x}}}_n\|^2$
The quantity to minimize over $B$ (subject to $B^\top B = I_M$).
Minimum error formula
$J_{\min} = \displaystyle\sum_{i=M+1}^{D} \lambda_i$
Sum of the discarded eigenvalues. Minimizing $J$ is equivalent to maximizing $\sum_{i=1}^M \lambda_i$ — same as §10.2.
Equivalence theorem
Because $\text{tr}(S) = \sum_i \lambda_i$ is fixed, minimizing $J = \sum_{i>M}\lambda_i$ is identical to maximizing $\sum_{i\leq M}\lambda_i$. The maximum-variance and minimum-reconstruction-error perspectives are two sides of the same identity. Both solutions: choose $B$ = top-$M$ eigenvectors of $S$.
Deriving J = sum of discarded eigenvalues

Start with $J = \frac{1}{N}\sum_n \|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_n - BB^\top\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_n\|^2 = \frac{1}{N}\sum_n \|(I - BB^\top)\tilde{\mathbf{x}}_n\|^2$.

Expanding using the spectral decomposition $S = U\Lambda U^\top$ and the constraint $B^\top B = I_M$ (with $B$ chosen from columns of $U$), one obtains:

$$J = \text{tr}(S) - \text{tr}(B^\top S B) = \sum_{i=1}^D \lambda_i - \sum_{i=1}^M \lambda_i = \sum_{i=M+1}^D \lambda_i.$$

Minimizing $J$ over all orthonormal $B$ means keeping the $M$ eigenvectors with the largest $\lambda_i$, discarding the rest.

§10.4

Eigendecomposition Algorithm

The standard PCA algorithm: compute the covariance matrix $S$, eigendecompose it, and project the data onto the top $M$ eigenvectors. The projected scores are automatically uncorrelated — PCA decorrelates the data.

Center the data   $\tilde{X} = X - \mathbf{1}\boldsymbol{\mu}^\top$   (subtract sample mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ from each row). All subsequent steps use $\tilde{X}$.
Compute covariance   $S = \frac{1}{N}\tilde{X}^\top\tilde{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{D\times D}$. Symmetric positive semidefinite.
Eigendecompose   $S = U\Lambda U^\top$, with $U = [\mathbf{u}_1|\cdots|\mathbf{u}_D]$ orthogonal and $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_D)$, $\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_D \geq 0$.
Select top $M$   $U_M = U[:,\,1{:}M] \in \mathbb{R}^{D\times M}$   (first $M$ columns = principal components).
Project   $Z = \tilde{X}\,U_M \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times M}$   (score matrix; row $n$ = coordinates of data point $n$ in the new space).
Reconstruct (optional)   $\hat{\tilde{X}} = Z\,U_M^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times D}$   (approximation of centered data in original space).
Score matrix
$Z = \tilde{X}\,U_M \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times M}$
Row $n$ of $Z$ gives the $M$-dimensional representation of data point $n$. Each column is one "principal component score".
Covariance of scores
$\text{Cov}(Z) = U_M^\top S\, U_M = \Lambda_M$
$\Lambda_M = \text{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_M)$. The score covariance is diagonal: PCA decorrelates the data. Variance along PC $i$ equals exactly $\lambda_i$.
Why scores are uncorrelated
$\text{Cov}(Z) = \frac{1}{N}Z^\top Z = \frac{1}{N}(\tilde{X}U_M)^\top(\tilde{X}U_M) = U_M^\top\,S\,U_M = U_M^\top(U\Lambda U^\top)U_M = \Lambda_M.$ The off-diagonal entries of $\Lambda_M$ are zero — different principal component scores have zero sample covariance.
Connection to the spectral theorem (C4 §4.2)

$S = U\Lambda U^\top$ is exactly the spectral decomposition of a symmetric positive semidefinite matrix. The spectral theorem guarantees:

  • All eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ are real and non-negative.
  • Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal; $U$ can always be chosen orthogonal.
  • The decomposition always exists — no "diagonalizability" condition needed beyond symmetry.

This is why $S$ is always tractable for PCA: symmetric matrices are the best-behaved class (C4 §4.7).

§10.5

PCA in High Dimensions

When $N \ll D$ (e.g., 200 patients, 20,000 genes), computing $S \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$ is infeasible. The key insight: instead compute $C = \frac{1}{N}\tilde{X}\tilde{X}^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$, which shares all nonzero eigenvalues with $S$. Equivalently — and most elegantly — apply the SVD directly to $\tilde{X}$.

Small Gram matrix
$C = \dfrac{1}{N}\tilde{X}\tilde{X}^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$
Tractable when $N \ll D$. Nonzero eigenvalues of $C$ = nonzero eigenvalues of $S$ (same $A^\top A$ / $AA^\top$ relationship as in C4 §4.5).
Recovering PCs from eigenvectors of C
$\mathbf{u} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{N\lambda}}\tilde{X}^\top\mathbf{v}$
If $C\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$ ($\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^N$, $\lambda \neq 0$), then $\mathbf{u}$ is the corresponding eigenvector of $S$ normalized to unit length. $\|\tilde{X}^\top\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{N\lambda}$.
SVD of data matrix
$\tilde{X} = U\Sigma V^\top$
Always applicable. Columns of $V$ are the principal components. Singular values relate to eigenvalues: $\lambda_i = \sigma_i^2/N$.
Eigenvalues from SVD
$\lambda_i = \sigma_i^2 / N$
Because $S = \frac{1}{N}\tilde{X}^\top\tilde{X} = V(\Sigma^\top\Sigma/N)V^\top$. The singular values of $\tilde{X}$ encode all spectral information about $S$.
ApproachMatrixSizePrincipal components
Standard ($D \leq N$)$S = \tilde{X}^\top\tilde{X}/N$$D \times D$Columns of $U$ from eigendecomp of $S$
High-dim ($N < D$)$C = \tilde{X}\tilde{X}^\top/N$$N \times N$$\tilde{X}^\top\mathbf{v}/\|\tilde{X}^\top\mathbf{v}\|$ (from eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}$ of $C$)
SVD (always preferred)$\tilde{X} = U\Sigma V^\top$$N \times D$Columns of $V$ directly
PCA = truncated SVD of the data matrix
The SVD route subsumes both the standard and high-dimensional cases. In practice, numerical libraries compute the thin SVD of $\tilde{X}$ and return the first $M$ right singular vectors as the principal components — this is the same result as eigendecomposing $S$, but numerically more stable and often faster (especially via randomized SVD, $O(NDM)$).
Computational cost comparison
  • Eigendecomp of $S$: Form $S$ costs $O(ND^2)$; eigendecomp costs $O(D^3)$. Total: $O(D^2(N+D))$. Infeasible when $D \approx 20000$.
  • Eigendecomp of $C$: Form $C$ costs $O(N^2D)$; eigendecomp costs $O(N^3)$. Total: $O(N^2(D+N))$. Preferred when $N \ll D$.
  • Full SVD of $\tilde{X}$: $O(\min(N,D) \cdot ND)$.
  • Randomized SVD (top $M$): $O(NDM)$ via Halko et al. (2011). The preferred algorithm in practice for large, sparse data.
§10.6

Explained Variance & Model Selection

After running PCA, the question becomes: how many components $M$ to keep? The explained variance ratio gives a principled answer: keep enough components to capture a target fraction (e.g. 95%) of the total variance. The scree plot visualizes the eigenvalue spectrum.

Explained variance ratio
$\text{EVR}(M) = \dfrac{\sum_{i=1}^M \lambda_i}{\sum_{i=1}^D \lambda_i}$
Fraction of total variance captured by $M$ components. Equivalently $(\sigma_1^2+\cdots+\sigma_M^2)/\|\tilde{X}\|_F^2$ using singular values.
Frobenius reconstruction error
$\|\tilde{X} - \hat{\tilde{X}}\|_F^2 = \displaystyle\sum_{i=M+1}^D \sigma_i^2$
Sum of squared discarded singular values. Eckart-Young theorem (C4 §4.6): this is the minimum possible error for any rank-$M$ approximation.
Scree plot — eigenvalue spectrum with elbow after PC2–PC3
Practical selection rules
Elbow method: keep PCs up to the "elbow" — where the eigenvalue curve flattens. Above, that is after PC2–PC3.
Threshold method: keep the smallest $M$ such that $\text{EVR}(M) \geq 0.95$ (or some target).
Likelihood method (PPCA): use cross-validated marginal likelihood — see §10.7.
Compression ratio and practical considerations

Original storage: $N \times D$ floats. After PCA to $M$ components: store $U_M$ ($D \times M$) + $Z$ ($N \times M$) + $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ ($D$) = $M(N+D) + D$ floats.

When $N$ and $D$ are large and $M$ is small, the compression ratio $ND / (M(N+D)+D)$ can be substantial.

Always center; optionally standardize. Without centering, the first PC often points toward the data centroid rather than capturing variance structure. Standardizing (dividing each feature by its standard deviation) switches from the covariance matrix to the correlation matrix — appropriate when features have very different units or scales.

§10.7

Probabilistic PCA

Classical PCA is a computational recipe. Probabilistic PCA (PPCA) recasts it as a latent-variable generative model: observed data $\mathbf{x}$ arises from a low-dimensional Gaussian latent code $\mathbf{z}$ passed through a linear map plus isotropic noise. Classical PCA emerges as the noiseless limit $\sigma^2 \to 0$.

Latent prior
$\mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\, I_M)$
$M$-dimensional standard Gaussian. The latent code lives in the compressed space.
Likelihood (observation model)
$\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(W\mathbf{z} + \boldsymbol{\mu},\; \sigma^2 I_D)$
$W \in \mathbb{R}^{D\times M}$ is the loading matrix. $\sigma^2 > 0$ is isotropic observation noise.
Marginal distribution of x
$\mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu},\; WW^\top + \sigma^2 I_D)$
Obtained by marginalizing out $\mathbf{z}$. Covariance is a rank-$M$ matrix plus scaled identity — structured, low-rank plus noise.
Posterior of z given x
$\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(M^{-1}W^\top(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}),\; \sigma^2 M^{-1})$
$M = W^\top W + \sigma^2 I_M$. Posterior mean is the "encoded" representation of $\mathbf{x}$. Fully tractable — Gaussian in, Gaussian out.
MLE solution — connection to classical PCA
Maximizing the marginal log-likelihood over $W$ and $\sigma^2$ yields: $$W_{\text{ML}} = U_M (\Lambda_M - \sigma^2 I)^{1/2} R$$ where $U_M$ = top-$M$ eigenvectors of $S$, $\Lambda_M = \text{diag}(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_M)$, and $R$ is any orthogonal rotation (free parameter — PPCA is rotation-invariant in latent space). As $\sigma^2 \to 0$: $W_{\text{ML}} \to U_M \Lambda_M^{1/2}$ — classical PCA.
MLE for noise variance
$\sigma^2_{\text{ML}} = \dfrac{1}{D-M}\displaystyle\sum_{i=M+1}^{D}\lambda_i$
Average of the discarded eigenvalues. A large $\sigma^2_{\text{ML}}$ signals that the dropped directions contain significant variance — $M$ may be too small.
Factor analysis comparison
$\Psi = \text{diag}(\psi_1,\ldots,\psi_D)$ (per-feature noise)
Factor analysis relaxes $\sigma^2 I$ to a full diagonal $\Psi$. This breaks the clean eigenvector solution; FA must be solved by EM. Use FA when features have genuinely different noise levels.
EM algorithm for PPCA

When data is incomplete (missing entries), EM is preferred over the closed-form eigendecomp route.

E-step: Compute the posterior mean and covariance of $\mathbf{z}_n$ given the observed entries of $\mathbf{x}_n$. This handles missing data naturally — the model infers the latent code from whatever features are available.

M-step: Update $W$ and $\sigma^2$ to maximize the expected log-likelihood computed in the E-step.

EM converges to the same MLE solution as the eigendecomposition route (Tipping & Bishop, 1999). Its advantage is flexibility with incomplete data, not superior speed for fully observed data.
