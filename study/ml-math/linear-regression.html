§9.1

Problem Setup

The generative model assumes each observation is a noisy linear function of its input. The design matrix $\mathbf{X}$ stacks all inputs into a convenient matrix form, and the i.i.d. Gaussian noise assumption turns the entire likelihood into a tractable Gaussian.

Scalar noise model
$y_n = \mathbf{x}_n^\top \boldsymbol{\theta} + \varepsilon_n$
θ ∈ ℝD are weights; εn ~ N(0, σ²) is i.i.d. Gaussian noise with known variance.
Observation likelihood
$p(y_n \mid \mathbf{x}_n, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathcal{N}(y_n \mid \mathbf{x}_n^\top\boldsymbol{\theta},\, \sigma^2)$
The mean is the linear predictor; σ² is irreducible aleatoric noise.
Design matrix form
$\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon},\quad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})$
X ∈ ℝN×D (row n = xn⊤); y ∈ ℝN (entry n = yn).
Full likelihood
$p(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta},\, \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})$
Multivariate Gaussian with mean vector Xθ and diagonal covariance. Independence of εn gives the product form.
Four assumptions
Linearity: the signal is truly $\mathbf{x}^\top\boldsymbol{\theta}$ (no interaction unless features are expanded).
Homoscedastic noise: $\sigma^2$ is the same for every observation.
Independence: the $\varepsilon_n$ are i.i.d. across observations.
Gaussian noise: not Student-t, not Laplace. When these hold, MLE is optimal. When they fail, estimates can be biased or inefficient.
Scalar vs. matrix form — unpacking the notation

The scalar equation $y_n = \mathbf{x}_n^\top\boldsymbol{\theta} + \varepsilon_n$ says: the $n$-th output is a dot product of the $n$-th input row with the weight vector, plus noise.

Stacking $N$ such equations into matrix form gives $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, where $\mathbf{X}$ is the design matrix. Because the noise vector $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2\mathbf{I})$ (off-diagonal zeros = independence), the joint density factors into a product:

$$p(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_{n=1}^N \mathcal{N}(y_n \mid \mathbf{x}_n^\top\boldsymbol{\theta}, \sigma^2)$$

The covariance matrix $\sigma^2\mathbf{I}$ is diagonal precisely because observations are independent. This structure is what makes the log-likelihood a simple sum of squared residuals.

§9.2

Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Maximizing the log-likelihood over $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ reduces to minimizing the sum of squared residuals — least squares. The MLE solution is the orthogonal projection of $\mathbf{y}$ onto the column space of $\mathbf{X}$, captured by the normal equations.

Log-likelihood
$\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = -\tfrac{N}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \tfrac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2$
The first term is constant in θ. Maximizing ℓ = minimizing the residual sum of squares $\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2$.
Normal equations
$\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}\,\boldsymbol{\theta} = \mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}$
Set $\partial\ell/\partial\boldsymbol{\theta} = \mathbf{0}$. The name "normal" refers to the residual being normal (perpendicular) to col(X).
MLE solution
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_\mathrm{ML} = (\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{X}^+\mathbf{y}$
Exists iff $\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$ is invertible (X has full column rank D). $\mathbf{X}^+ = (\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top$ is the Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse.
MLE for noise variance
$\hat{\sigma}^2_\mathrm{ML} = \tfrac{1}{N}\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_\mathrm{ML}\|^2$
Average squared residual. Biased; the unbiased estimator divides by $N - D$. Plug-in after solving for $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_\mathrm{ML}$.
Geometric interpretation: y projected onto col(X)
MLE = Ordinary Least Squares
The probabilistic model (Gaussian noise) gives a clean theoretical justification for a classic algorithm. The residual vector $\mathbf{y} - \hat{\mathbf{y}}$ is orthogonal to every column of $\mathbf{X}$ — this is why the normal equations are called "normal": the residual is normal (perpendicular) to the column space. The hat matrix $P_\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top$ is symmetric and idempotent ($P^2 = P$), exactly the properties of a projection matrix.
Deriving the normal equations step by step

Expand the least-squares objective $L(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = (\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta})^\top(\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta})$:

$$L(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbf{y}^\top\mathbf{y} - 2\boldsymbol{\theta}^\top\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y} + \boldsymbol{\theta}^\top\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta}$$

Differentiate with respect to $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ (using matrix calculus from C5):

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial\boldsymbol{\theta}} = -2\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y} + 2\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta} = \mathbf{0}$$

This gives the normal equations $\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta} = \mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}$, and solving (when $\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$ is invertible) yields $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_\mathrm{ML} = (\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}$.

When is $\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$ invertible? If and only if $\mathbf{X}$ has full column rank $D$. If $N < D$ (fewer samples than features) or columns are linearly dependent, $\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$ is singular and the MLE is not unique — infinitely many $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ minimize $L$. MAP and Bayesian methods regularize this regime.

§9.3

MAP Estimation — Ridge Regression

When $\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$ is singular or ill-conditioned, the MLE is unstable. Placing a Gaussian prior on $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ shrinks estimates toward zero and regularizes the problem — arriving at ridge regression with a principled interpretation.

Isotropic Gaussian prior
$p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathbf{0},\, b^2\mathbf{I})$
Larger $b^2$ → weaker regularization. As $b^2 \to \infty$, MAP → MLE (prior becomes uninformative).
MAP objective
$L_\mathrm{MAP}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2 + \lambda\|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2$
Ridge (L2) regularization with $\lambda = \sigma^2/b^2$. Minimizing this ⇔ maximizing $\log p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{y})$.
MAP solution
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_\mathrm{MAP} = \left(\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X} + \tfrac{\sigma^2}{b^2}\mathbf{I}\right)^{-1}\!\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}$
The added $\lambda\mathbf{I}$ shifts all eigenvalues up by $\lambda > 0$, making the matrix always positive definite and invertible.
Always exists (λ > 0)
$\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X} + \lambda\mathbf{I} \succ 0$
$\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$ is PSD; adding $\lambda\mathbf{I}$ makes it PD. Works even when $N < D$ or columns of $\mathbf{X}$ are linearly dependent.
Probabilistic justification for ridge regression
Ridge regression was originally proposed as a computational trick to stabilize ill-conditioned normal equations. The MAP derivation gives it a principled probabilistic foundation: the L2 penalty $\lambda\|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2$ is exactly the negative log of a zero-mean Gaussian prior $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, b^2\mathbf{I})$ with $\lambda = \sigma^2/b^2$. The regularization strength encodes the prior-to-noise ratio.
Bias-variance tradeoff — what ridge buys and costs

MLE: unbiased, but high variance when $\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$ is ill-conditioned (small eigenvalues magnify noise).

MAP/Ridge: introduces bias — estimates are shrunk toward zero — but variance is reduced. The total prediction error is bias2 + variance. The optimal $\lambda$ minimizes the sum.

An important contrast: L2 (Gaussian prior) shrinks all coefficients proportionally but never makes them exactly zero. L1 (Lasso, Laplace prior) creates sparsity because its gradient has a discontinuity at zero. Ridge selects nothing; Lasso selects variables.

  • Too small $\lambda$: high variance, overfitting (close to MLE)
  • Too large $\lambda$: high bias, underfitting (all $\theta_j \approx 0$)
  • Optimal $\lambda$: selected via cross-validation or evidence maximization (§9.6)
§9.4

Bayesian Linear Regression

MAP collapses the posterior to a single point. Bayesian linear regression keeps the full posterior distribution over $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, propagating that uncertainty into a predictive distribution over outputs — not just a point estimate, but calibrated confidence.

Conjugate prior
$p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathbf{m}_0,\, \mathbf{S}_0)$
Gaussian prior is conjugate to Gaussian likelihood: the posterior is also Gaussian. Everything stays tractable.
Posterior covariance
$\mathbf{S}_N^{-1} = \mathbf{S}_0^{-1} + \sigma^{-2}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$
Precision = prior precision + data precision. More data → larger $\sigma^{-2}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$ → tighter posterior (smaller $\mathbf{S}_N$).
Posterior mean
$\mathbf{m}_N = \mathbf{S}_N\!\left(\mathbf{S}_0^{-1}\mathbf{m}_0 + \sigma^{-2}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}\right)$
Precision-weighted average of prior information and data information. As $N \to \infty$, data overwhelms prior and $\mathbf{m}_N \to \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_\mathrm{ML}$.
Predictive distribution
$p(y_* \mid \mathbf{x}_*, \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{y}) = \mathcal{N}(y_* \mid \mathbf{m}_N^\top\mathbf{x}_*,\; \mathbf{x}_*^\top\mathbf{S}_N\mathbf{x}_* + \sigma^2)$
Obtained by marginalizing out $\boldsymbol{\theta}$. Predictive variance = epistemic ($\mathbf{x}_*^\top\mathbf{S}_N\mathbf{x}_*$) + aleatoric ($\sigma^2$).
Posterior evolution as data arrives (1D parameter space)
The key payoff: predictive variance decomposition
The predictive variance $\mathbf{x}_*^\top\mathbf{S}_N\mathbf{x}_* + \sigma^2$ splits into two terms:

Epistemic uncertainty $\mathbf{x}_*^\top\mathbf{S}_N\mathbf{x}_*$: uncertainty about $\boldsymbol{\theta}$. Shrinks with more data. Large for test points $\mathbf{x}_*$ far from training distribution (out-of-distribution inputs correctly flagged as high uncertainty).

Aleatoric uncertainty $\sigma^2$: irreducible observation noise. Cannot be reduced by collecting more data.
Sequential (online) Bayesian updating

One elegant property of Bayesian linear regression is that the posterior can be updated incrementally: process data in batches, using the current posterior as the next prior.

  • Initialize with prior $(\mathbf{m}_0, \mathbf{S}_0)$.
  • See batch 1: compute posterior $(\mathbf{m}_1, \mathbf{S}_1)$.
  • Use $(\mathbf{m}_1, \mathbf{S}_1)$ as the new prior; see batch 2: compute $(\mathbf{m}_2, \mathbf{S}_2)$.
  • Repeat. The final posterior is the same regardless of how data is batched.

This is exactly the Kalman filter update rule in disguise — Bayesian linear regression is a Kalman filter for static parameters. The commutativity follows directly from the conjugate Gaussian algebra: the posterior depends on $\sum_n \mathbf{x}_n\mathbf{x}_n^\top$ and $\sum_n \mathbf{x}_n y_n$, which are order-independent sufficient statistics.

MAP is the mean of the posterior — not just the mode

When the prior is $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, b^2\mathbf{I})$ and the posterior is Gaussian, the posterior mean equals the posterior mode (Gaussians are symmetric). So:

$$\mathbf{m}_N = \mathbf{S}_N\bigl(\mathbf{S}_0^{-1}\cdot\mathbf{0} + \sigma^{-2}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}\bigr) = (\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X} + \tfrac{\sigma^2}{b^2}\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y} = \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_\mathrm{MAP}$$

MAP is therefore a special case of Bayesian linear regression: it takes the full posterior but only reports its mean, discarding the uncertainty quantified by $\mathbf{S}_N$. The predictive mean is identical; the predictive variance is not.

§9.5

Feature Maps & the Kernel Trick

Raw inputs may not be linearly related to outputs. A feature map $\phi(\mathbf{x})$ lifts inputs into a higher-dimensional space where linear regression applies. Replacing explicit feature vectors with inner products $k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \phi(\mathbf{x})^\top\phi(\mathbf{x}')$ yields the kernel form — the bridge to Gaussian processes.

Linear basis vs. feature-map basis on the same scatter plot
Feature design matrix
$\boldsymbol{\Phi} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times K},\quad \Phi_{nk} = \phi_k(\mathbf{x}_n)$
All §9.4 formulas carry over with $\mathbf{X} \to \boldsymbol{\Phi}$, $D \to K$. The model is nonlinear in $\mathbf{x}$ but still linear in $\boldsymbol{\theta}$.
Kernel function
$k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \phi(\mathbf{x})^\top\phi(\mathbf{x}')$
Inner product in feature space. Allows working with infinite-dimensional $\phi$ implicitly, as long as $k$ is computable. Any positive definite $k$ is valid (Mercer's theorem).
Kernel (GP) predictive mean
$\mathbb{E}[y_* \mid \cdot] = \mathbf{k}_*^\top(\mathbf{K} + \sigma^2\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{y}$
$\mathbf{k}_* \in \mathbb{R}^N$ with entries $k(\mathbf{x}_*, \mathbf{x}_n)$; $\mathbf{K} \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$ with $K_{nm} = k(\mathbf{x}_n, \mathbf{x}_m)$. Only $N \times N$ inversion needed.
Kernel (GP) predictive variance
$\mathrm{Var}[y_* \mid \cdot] = k(\mathbf{x}_*, \mathbf{x}_*) - \mathbf{k}_*^\top(\mathbf{K} + \sigma^2\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{k}_* + \sigma^2$
Prior variance minus the reduction from data, plus noise. These are the exact Gaussian process regression equations.
Primal-dual duality
There are two equivalent representations of the same predictor:

Primal (feature) form: $f(\mathbf{x}) = \phi(\mathbf{x})^\top\boldsymbol{\theta}$ — learn $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ in $K$-dimensional space, solve a $K \times K$ system.
Dual (kernel) form: $f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{k}_*^\top(\mathbf{K} + \sigma^2\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{y}$ — work directly with data, solve an $N \times N$ system.

Use the primal when $K \ll N$; use the dual when $K \gg N$ or $K = \infty$ (RBF kernel).
Gaussian process connection — the infinite-dimensional limit

A Gaussian process (GP) defines a distribution over functions directly via a kernel, without specifying $\phi$ explicitly. Mercer's theorem guarantees that any valid (positive definite) kernel $k$ corresponds to some (possibly infinite-dimensional) feature map $\phi$.

Bayesian linear regression with feature map $\phi$ and isotropic prior $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \alpha^{-1}\mathbf{I})$ is mathematically equivalent to GP regression with kernel $k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \phi(\mathbf{x})^\top\phi(\mathbf{x}') / \alpha$.

GP regression is the limiting case as $K \to \infty$. The RBF (squared exponential) kernel $k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \exp(-\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}'\|^2 / 2\ell^2)$ corresponds to an infinite-dimensional polynomial feature map. Only the kernel form is tractable for such kernels.

C12 connection: Chapter 12 develops GP regression as a first-class probabilistic model, defining the prior directly on functions via $k$ without reference to any finite $\phi$.

§9.6

Evidence Maximization (Type II MLE)

Bayesian linear regression still requires hyperparameters $\alpha$ (prior precision) and $\sigma^2$ (noise variance). Rather than hand-tuning them, they can be learned by maximizing the marginal likelihood (evidence) — integrating out $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ so the optimization is over hyperparameters only.

Marginal likelihood (evidence)
$p(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \alpha, \sigma^2) = \int p(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \boldsymbol{\theta}, \sigma^2)\, p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \alpha)\, d\boldsymbol{\theta}$
Integrating out θ automatically balances fit vs. complexity (Occam's razor). Tractable because both factors are Gaussian.
Effective parameters
$\gamma = \sum_{i=1}^D \frac{\lambda_i}{\alpha\sigma^2 + \lambda_i}$
$\lambda_i$ are eigenvalues of $\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$. $\gamma \in [0, D]$: number of parameters "determined by data" rather than pinned by the prior.
Optimal prior precision
$\alpha^* = \frac{\gamma}{\mathbf{m}_N^\top\mathbf{m}_N}$
Re-estimated from the posterior mean $\mathbf{m}_N$ (which itself depends on $\alpha$). Requires iterating to convergence.
Optimal noise variance
${\sigma^2}^* = \frac{\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{m}_N\|^2}{N - \gamma}$
Residual variance normalized by effective degrees of freedom $N - \gamma$ (not $N - D$). Accounts for parameters effectively learned from data.
Why the marginal likelihood avoids overfitting
Integrating out $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ imposes an automatic complexity penalty. A hyperparameter configuration that allows too much flexibility (small $\alpha$, small $\sigma^2$) lets $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ fit well — but the evidence averages over the entire prior distribution of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, and most of them overfit. The evidence is low because only a tiny fraction of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ values give a good fit. Jointly maximizing $p(\mathbf{y} | \mathbf{X}, \boldsymbol{\theta})$ over both $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ and $(\alpha, \sigma^2)$ would degenerate: setting $\alpha = 0$ and $\sigma^2 \to 0$ gives infinite likelihood for $N \geq D$.
Iterative update algorithm

Because $\mathbf{m}_N$ depends on $\alpha$ and $\sigma^2$, the optimality conditions are implicit. The algorithm iterates:

  • Initialize $\alpha$, $\sigma^2$ (e.g., $\alpha = 1$, $\sigma^2 = 1$).
  • E-step: compute $\mathbf{m}_N = \mathbf{S}_N(\sigma^{-2}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y})$ and $\mathbf{S}_N^{-1} = \alpha\mathbf{I} + \sigma^{-2}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$.
  • Compute $\gamma$: from eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ of $\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$, compute $\gamma = \sum_i \lambda_i / (\alpha\sigma^2 + \lambda_i)$.
  • M-step: update $\alpha^* = \gamma / (\mathbf{m}_N^\top\mathbf{m}_N)$ and ${\sigma^2}^* = \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{m}_N\|^2 / (N - \gamma)$.
  • Repeat until convergence.

This is a special case of the EM algorithm: $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is the latent variable, $(\alpha, \sigma^2)$ are the parameters. The E-step computes the posterior over $\boldsymbol{\theta}$; the M-step maximizes the expected complete-data log-likelihood, yielding the update formulas above.

Evidence lower bound (ELBO) and variational inference preview

By Jensen's inequality, for any distribution $q(\boldsymbol{\theta})$:

$$\log p(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{X}) \geq \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \boldsymbol{\theta})] - \mathrm{KL}\bigl(q(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \,\|\, p(\boldsymbol{\theta})\bigr)$$

The right side is the ELBO (evidence lower bound). When $q$ is chosen to be the exact posterior $p(\boldsymbol{\theta} | \mathbf{X}, \mathbf{y})$ — as in §9.4, where the posterior is tractable — the ELBO equals the log evidence exactly (KL = 0 means $q$ equals $p$).

For models where the exact posterior is intractable, variational inference optimizes the ELBO over a family of approximate distributions $q$. This foundational idea generalizes C9 to arbitrary latent variable models, including GMMs (C11), VAEs, and modern deep generative models.
