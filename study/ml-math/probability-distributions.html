§6.1

Construction of a Probability Space

Probability theory rests on a triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{A}, P)$. Three Kolmogorov axioms define what "probability" must mean, and everything else follows — including the rules that power Bayesian ML. The formal setup matters because in Part II the "experiment" is often the choice of model parameters over a continuous space $\Omega = \mathbb{R}^d$.

Sample space Ω
All possible outcomes
Discrete (coin flips, word counts) or continuous (temperatures, model parameters $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d$).
Event space A (σ-algebra)
Subsets of Ω we can assign probability to
Must be closed under complements and countable unions. The technicality: on continuous $\Omega$, not every subset admits a sensible probability measure.
Probability measure P
$P: \mathcal{A} \to [0,1]$
K1: $P(A)\geq 0$   K2: $P(\Omega)=1$   K3: $P\!\bigl(\bigcup_i A_i\bigr) = \sum_i P(A_i)$ for disjoint $A_i$.
What follows from the axioms
From the three axioms alone: $P(\emptyset)=0$, $P(A^c) = 1-P(A)$, and $P(A\cup B) = P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)$. No additional assumptions — the entire edifice of probability follows deductively.
§6.2

Discrete and Continuous Probabilities

A random variable $X: \Omega \to T$ maps outcomes to a target space $T$ (usually $T = \mathbb{R}$). Whether $T$ is discrete or continuous determines the representation: PMF vs. PDF. The CDF unifies both.

PMF (discrete)
$p(x) = P(X = x) \geq 0,\quad \sum_x p(x) = 1$
Example: Bernoulli($\mu$): $p(1)=\mu$, $p(0)=1-\mu$. Models a biased coin flip.
PDF (continuous)
$P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b p(x)\,dx,\quad \int p(x)\,dx=1$
Critical: $p(x)$ is NOT a probability — it is a density. It can exceed 1. $P(X=x)=0$ for any specific point.
CDF (both cases)
$F_X(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^x p(t)\,dt$
Always maps $\mathbb{R}\to[0,1]$, monotone non-decreasing, right-continuous. For continuous $X$: $p(x) = dF/dx$.
Marginal (sum rule)
$p(x) = \int p(x,y)\,dy$
For the discrete case replace $\int$ with $\sum$. Marginalizing = summing over all the ways $y$ could have been.
Density vs. probability
$p(x)$ is a density, not a probability. Asking "what is the probability that $X = 1.5$?" yields 0 for any continuous distribution. Only intervals $P(a \leq X \leq b)$ have positive probability. This distinction matters whenever you compare log-likelihoods across different distributions.
§6.3

Bayes' Theorem ⭐

Three rules — conditional probability, product rule, sum rule — are the entire engine of probabilistic reasoning. Combining them yields Bayes' theorem: the mechanism for updating beliefs on evidence. All of Bayesian ML is an application of this single equation.

The four quantities in Bayes' theorem

The evidence $p(y) = \int p(y|x)\,p(x)\,dx$ is the normalizing constant. Many algorithms work with unnormalized posteriors and handle it separately.

Conditional probability
$P(A \mid B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$
Restrict the universe to event $B$, then renormalize. Requires $P(B) > 0$.
Product rule (chain rule)
$p(x, y) = p(y \mid x)\,p(x) = p(x \mid y)\,p(y)$
Chains to $n$ variables: $p(x_1,\ldots,x_n) = p(x_1)\prod_{k=2}^n p(x_k \mid x_1,\ldots,x_{k-1})$.
Sum rule (marginalization)
$p(x) = \int p(x,y)\,dy = \int p(x \mid y)\,p(y)\,dy$
Also written $p(x) = \mathbb{E}_y[p(x \mid y)]$ — marginalizing equals averaging over $y$.
Bayes' theorem
$p(x \mid y) = \dfrac{p(y \mid x)\,p(x)}{p(y)}$
$p(y) = \int p(y|x)\,p(x)\,dx$ is the evidence. In ML: $x\to\theta$ (parameters), $y\to\mathcal{D}$ (data).
Why Bayes' theorem is central to ML
In supervised learning, $x$ plays the role of model parameters $\theta$ and $y$ the role of data $\mathcal{D}$. Bayes gives the mechanism for Bayesian inference: $p(\theta \mid \mathcal{D}) \propto p(\mathcal{D} \mid \theta)\,p(\theta)$. All of Ch 9 (Bayesian linear regression) uses this directly. The evidence $p(\mathcal{D})$ is often intractable, motivating variational inference and MCMC.
§6.4

Summary Statistics and Independence

Rather than working with full distributions, compact descriptions suffice in many settings. The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ is the central object — it is always symmetric and positive semi-definite, directly linking this chapter to the SPD matrices of C3 and the eigendecomposition of C4.

Expected value
$\mathbb{E}_X[g(X)] = \int g(x)\,p(x)\,dx$
Linearity (always): $\mathbb{E}[\alpha X + \beta Y] = \alpha\mathbb{E}[X] + \beta\mathbb{E}[Y]$ — no independence required.
Variance
$\mathrm{Var}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$
Affine: $\mathrm{Var}[aX+b] = a^2\mathrm{Var}[X]$ — shifts don't change spread.
Covariance (two scalars)
$\mathrm{Cov}[X,Y] = \mathbb{E}[XY] - \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$
Positive: tend to move together. Negative: opposite. Zero: no linear relationship (nonlinear dependence may still exist).
Covariance matrix
$\Sigma = \mathrm{Cov}[\mathbf{X}] = \mathbb{E}[(\mathbf{X}-\boldsymbol{\mu})(\mathbf{X}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top]$
Entry $\Sigma_{ij} = \mathrm{Cov}[X_i, X_j]$. Diagonal = individual variances. Always symmetric and PSD (variance is non-negative).
Affine transform — mean
$\mathbb{E}[A\mathbf{X}+\mathbf{b}] = A\boldsymbol{\mu} + \mathbf{b}$
By linearity of expectation.
Affine transform — covariance
$\mathrm{Cov}[A\mathbf{X}+\mathbf{b}] = A\Sigma A^\top$
This formula appears everywhere: every linear layer propagates uncertainty as $A\Sigma A^\top$. Connects to C4 (eigendecomp) and C9 (predictive variance).
Correlation
$\rho(X,Y) = \dfrac{\mathrm{Cov}[X,Y]}{\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}[X]\,\mathrm{Var}[Y]}} \in [-1,1]$
The bound follows from Cauchy-Schwarz (C3). $\pm 1$ = perfect linear relationship; 0 = uncorrelated (not necessarily independent).
Independence
$X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \iff p(x,y)=p(x)\,p(y)$
Implies $\mathrm{Cov}[X,Y]=0$, but NOT vice versa. Exception: for joint Gaussians, zero correlation implies independence.
Uncorrelated ≠ independent (in general)
Correlation captures only linear dependence. $X \sim \text{Uniform}[-1,1]$, $Y = X^2$: $\mathrm{Cov}[X,Y] = 0$ but $Y$ is completely determined by $X$. Independence is strictly stronger — it eliminates all statistical dependence. The Gaussian is the only common distribution where zero correlation suffices for independence.
Covariance matrix = SPD matrix
$\Sigma$ is always symmetric positive semi-definite. The eigendecomposition of $\Sigma$ (Ch 4) reveals the geometry of the distribution: eigenvectors give the principal axes, eigenvalues give the spread along each axis. This is the foundation of PCA (Ch 10).
§6.5

The Gaussian Distribution ⭐⭐

The most important distribution in machine learning. Four reasons: (1) Central Limit Theorem — averages converge to Gaussian; (2) Maximum entropy — Gaussian maximizes entropy for given mean and variance; (3) Tractability — sums, marginals, conditionals of Gaussians are all Gaussian; (4) Conjugacy — Gaussian prior × Gaussian likelihood = Gaussian posterior.

2D Gaussian — contours of constant density (Mahalanobis ellipsoids)

Eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2$ of $\Sigma$ are the axes of the ellipsoid. Eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2$ give the squared semi-axis lengths. Larger $\lambda$ = more spread in that direction.

Univariate Gaussian
$\mathcal{N}(x \mid \mu, \sigma^2) = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\!\left(-\dfrac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$
68-95-99.7 rule: ~68%/95%/99.7% of mass within $\pm1/\pm2/\pm3\sigma$. Standardize: $Z=(X-\mu)/\sigma \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
Multivariate Gaussian ⭐⭐
$\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma) = \dfrac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}\exp\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$
$|\Sigma|^{1/2}$ in the normalizer: larger $\Sigma$ = more spread = lower peak. Exponent is the squared Mahalanobis distance from $\mathbf{x}$ to $\boldsymbol{\mu}$.
Marginal distribution
$p(\mathbf{x}_a) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_a \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_a,\, \Sigma_{aa})$
Partition $\mathbf{X}=(\mathbf{X}_a,\mathbf{X}_b)$. Just read off the diagonal block. Marginalizing a Gaussian = dropping unwanted dimensions.
Conditional mean
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{a|b} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_a + \Sigma_{ab}\Sigma_{bb}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_b - \boldsymbol{\mu}_b)$
Start at $\boldsymbol{\mu}_a$, correct by how far $\mathbf{x}_b$ deviates from its mean. $\Sigma_{ab}\Sigma_{bb}^{-1}$ is the regression coefficient of $a$ on $b$.
Conditional covariance (Schur complement)
$\Sigma_{a|b} = \Sigma_{aa} - \Sigma_{ab}\Sigma_{bb}^{-1}\Sigma_{ba}$
Prior variance minus a PSD correction. Conditioning always reduces variance. $\Sigma_{a|b}$ does not depend on $\mathbf{x}_b$ — unique to the Gaussian.
Affine transform rule
$\mathbf{Y} = A\mathbf{X}+\mathbf{b} \;\Rightarrow\; \mathbf{Y}\sim\mathcal{N}(A\boldsymbol{\mu}+\mathbf{b},\; A\Sigma A^\top)$
Linear operations (projection, rotation, scaling) preserve the Gaussian family. Enables exact inference in linear-Gaussian models.
For joint Gaussians: uncorrelated = independent (unique property)
If $\mathbf{X}$ is jointly Gaussian and $\mathrm{Cov}[X_i, X_j]=0$, then $X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_j$. This holds only for the Gaussian — because it is fully characterized by first and second moments. In all other distributions, zero correlation does not imply independence.
Product of two Gaussians (precision update)

When Bayes' theorem has a Gaussian likelihood and a Gaussian prior, the product of two Gaussian densities is itself (proportional to) a Gaussian:

$$\mathcal{N}(x \mid \mu_1,\sigma_1^2)\cdot\mathcal{N}(x \mid \mu_2,\sigma_2^2) \;\propto\; \mathcal{N}(x \mid \mu,\sigma^2)$$

where the parameters of the product follow a precision update:

$$\underbrace{\frac{1}{\sigma^2}}_{\text{posterior precision}} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sigma_1^2}}_{\tau_1} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sigma_2^2}}_{\tau_2}, \qquad \mu = \sigma^2\!\left(\frac{\mu_1}{\sigma_1^2} + \frac{\mu_2}{\sigma_2^2}\right)$$

In precision notation $\tau_i = 1/\sigma_i^2$:

$$\tau = \tau_1 + \tau_2, \qquad \mu = \frac{\tau_1\mu_1 + \tau_2\mu_2}{\tau_1+\tau_2}$$

Intuition: Two independent Gaussian measurements of the same quantity combine so that (a) precisions add — the result is always tighter than either alone, and (b) the combined mean is a precision-weighted average pulled toward the more precise measurement. This is the foundation of the Kalman filter.

Why Gaussian conditionals are Gaussian

The key insight is that the joint Gaussian density can be written in terms of the quadratic form $(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})$. Partitioning the precision matrix $\Lambda = \Sigma^{-1}$ and completing the square in $\mathbf{x}_a$ shows that:

  • The exponent is quadratic in $\mathbf{x}_a$ (given fixed $\mathbf{x}_b$), so the conditional is Gaussian.
  • The linear coefficient gives the conditional mean formula $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{a|b}$.
  • The quadratic coefficient gives the conditional covariance $\Sigma_{a|b}$.
  • Since $\Sigma_{a|b}$ does not involve $\mathbf{x}_b$, the conditional variance is the same everywhere — the Gaussian is the only common distribution with this property.

This "completing the square" argument is the workhorse of Gaussian process inference and Bayesian linear regression (Ch 9): conditioning on data always yields a closed-form Gaussian posterior.

§6.6

Conjugate Priors & the Exponential Family

In Bayesian inference, computing the posterior $p(\theta \mid \mathbf{x}) \propto p(\mathbf{x} \mid \theta)\,p(\theta)$ generally requires a difficult normalizing integral. Conjugate priors sidestep this: they are chosen so the posterior has the same functional form as the prior — just with updated parameters. The exponential family is the systematic framework for finding conjugate pairs.

Exponential family
$p(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\eta}) = h(\mathbf{x})\exp\!\bigl(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top\boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{x}) - A(\boldsymbol{\eta})\bigr)$
$\boldsymbol{\eta}$: natural parameters   $\boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{x})$: sufficient statistics   $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$: log-partition function (normalizer). Members: Gaussian, Bernoulli, Binomial, Poisson, Gamma, Beta, Dirichlet.
Log-partition function
$A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = \log\!\int h(\mathbf{x})\exp(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top\boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{x}))\,d\mathbf{x}$
$\nabla_\eta A = \mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\phi}(X)]$ (mean of sufficient stats). $\nabla^2_\eta A = \mathrm{Cov}[\boldsymbol{\phi}(X)]$ (always PSD, so $A$ is convex).
Beta-Binomial update rule

Prior: $\mu \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ where $\alpha-1$ acts as "prior successes" and $\beta-1$ as "prior failures."

Likelihood: $x \sim \mathrm{Bin}(n, \mu)$ — $x$ successes in $n$ trials.

Posterior: $\mu \mid x \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha + x,\; \beta + n - x)$

Update rule: just increment the counts — $\alpha_{\text{new}} = \alpha + x$, $\beta_{\text{new}} = \beta + (n-x)$. As $n\to\infty$, the data overwhelms the prior. Posterior mean: $\mathbb{E}[\mu \mid x] = (\alpha+x)/(\alpha+\beta+n)$ — weighted average of prior mean and MLE.

Gaussian-Gaussian conjugacy (precision update)

Prior: $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$    Likelihood: $x \mid \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known.

Posterior after $n$ iid observations (sample mean $\bar{x}$): $\mu \mid \mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_n, \sigma_n^2)$

$$\frac{1}{\sigma_n^2} = \frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n}{\sigma^2} \qquad (\text{precisions add})$$ $$\mu_n = \sigma_n^2\!\left(\frac{\mu_0}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n\bar{x}}{\sigma^2}\right) \qquad (\text{precision-weighted mean})$$

As $n\to\infty$: posterior mean $\to\bar{x}$ (MLE), posterior variance $\to 0$ (certainty).

Sufficient statistics and MLE in the exponential family

In the exponential family, the MLE of $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ satisfies the moment-matching condition:

$$\nabla_\eta A(\hat{\boldsymbol{\eta}}) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{x}_i)$$

That is, fit the model so the expected sufficient statistics equal the empirical sufficient statistics.

  • Gaussian: sufficient statistics are $x$ and $x^2$; matching gives sample mean and variance.
  • Bernoulli / logistic regression: sufficient statistic is $\mathbf{x}$ (the feature vector); the moment-matching condition gives the standard logistic regression gradient equation.

Why A(η) being convex matters: Maximum likelihood in the exponential family is always a convex optimization problem (Hessian = covariance matrix, always PSD). Connects directly to convex optimization (Ch 7).

§6.7

Change of Variables & Inverse Transform

If $Y = g(X)$ is a transformation of a random variable, what is the density of $Y$? The change-of-variables formula answers this, with a Jacobian correction that accounts for how the transformation stretches or squishes probability mass. This connects directly to the determinant of C2 and the Jacobian of C5.

Univariate change of variables
$p_Y(y) = p_X\!\bigl(g^{-1}(y)\bigr)\cdot\left|\dfrac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y)\right|$
Intuition: probability mass is conserved. If the transformation stretches a region by 2×, the density must halve.
Multivariate change of variables
$p_Y(\mathbf{y}) = p_X\!\bigl(g^{-1}(\mathbf{y})\bigr)\cdot\bigl|\det J_{g^{-1}}(\mathbf{y})\bigr|$
$J_{g^{-1}}$ is the Jacobian matrix: $(J)_{ij} = \partial[g^{-1}(\mathbf{y})]_i/\partial y_j$. The determinant measures $d$-dimensional volume stretching — same as $\det(A)$ from C2.
Inverse transform sampling
Draw $U\sim\mathrm{Unif}[0,1]$, return $X = F_X^{-1}(U)$
Works because $P(F_X^{-1}(U)\leq x) = P(U\leq F_X(x)) = F_X(x)$. Limitation: requires $F_X^{-1}$ in closed form (not possible for the Gaussian).
Connection to normalizing flows
§6.7 is the foundation for normalizing flows — deep generative models that chain invertible transformations to map a simple base distribution (usually $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$) into a complex one. Each step applies the change-of-variables formula to track the density. The Jacobian determinant must be cheap to compute — motivating coupling-layer and autoregressive architectures.
