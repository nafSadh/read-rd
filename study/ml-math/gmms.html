Gaussian Mixture Models

A single Gaussian is rarely rich enough to describe real data. GMMs superimpose K Gaussians — each with its own mean and covariance — weighted so they form a valid density. Fitting those weights forces a confrontation with the EM algorithm, latent variables, and the ELBO.

§11.1

Gaussian Mixture Model

A Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) represents a distribution as a weighted sum of $K$ Gaussian components. Each component has its own mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$, covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$, and mixture weight $\pi_k$. The weights form a probability simplex: $\pi_k \geq 0$ and $\sum_k \pi_k = 1$.

1-D GMM — two Gaussian components and their mixture
Mixture Density
$p(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)$
Weighted superposition of $K$ Gaussian components. Valid density because $\sum_k \pi_k = 1$.
Weight Constraints (Simplex)
$\pi_k \geq 0, \quad \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k = 1$
Only $K-1$ weights are free; the $K$-th is determined by the sum-to-one constraint.
Generative Story
$z \sim \text{Cat}(\pi_1,\ldots,\pi_K)$, then $\mathbf{x} \mid z=k \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)$
Latent variable $z$ selects a component; $\mathbf{x}$ is drawn from that Gaussian. Marginalizing over $z$ recovers $p(\mathbf{x})$.
Marginalisation
$p(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_k p(\mathbf{x}\mid z{=}k)\,p(z{=}k)$
Observed density = sum over all possible component origins. The latent $z$ is hidden but reasoned about.
Why more than one component?
A single Gaussian is unimodal and symmetric. Real data is often multimodal (heights of men and women, spectral lines), skewed, or cluster-structured. A $K=2$ GMM already captures bimodal structure; sufficiently large $K$ can approximate any smooth density.
Special cases and parameter count

$K=1$: reduces to a single Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}\mid\boldsymbol{\mu},\boldsymbol{\Sigma})$.

Equal spherical covariances $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k = \sigma^2 I$: isotropic clusters (soft K-means). Taking $\sigma^2\to 0$ recovers hard K-means.

Diagonal $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$: axis-aligned ellipsoids; fewer parameters ($K\cdot D$ instead of $K\cdot D(D+1)/2$).

For a full-covariance GMM in $D$ dimensions with $K$ components, the total free parameter count is: $$p(K) = \underbrace{K \cdot D}_{\text{means}} + \underbrace{K \cdot \tfrac{D(D+1)}{2}}_{\text{covariances}} + \underbrace{(K-1)}_{\text{weights}}$$

§11.2

Parameter Learning via Maximum Likelihood

Given $N$ i.i.d. observations $\mathbf{X} = \{\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_N\}$, the goal is to find $\theta = \{\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k, \pi_k\}_{k=1}^K$ that maximises the log-likelihood. The fundamental obstacle: a log sits outside a sum over $K$ terms, coupling all parameters.

Log-Likelihood
$\ell(\theta) = \sum_{n=1}^{N} \log \left[ \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k \,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k) \right]$
Log of a sum — no closed-form gradient solution. Setting $\partial\ell/\partial\boldsymbol{\mu}_k = 0$ gives equations that depend on $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ itself.
Gradient Obstruction
$\frac{\partial\ell}{\partial\boldsymbol{\mu}_k} = \sum_n \frac{\pi_k \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_n|\boldsymbol{\mu}_k,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)}{\sum_j \pi_j \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_n|\boldsymbol{\mu}_j,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j)} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k)$
The bracketed fraction depends on $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ — equations are coupled and have no closed form.
Why not direct gradient ascent?
  • The log-likelihood is non-convex with many local maxima.
  • Covariance matrices must remain positive definite — a constrained optimisation.
  • Mixture weights must satisfy the simplex constraint $\sum\pi_k=1$.
  • EM provides a structured, constraint-respecting iterative scheme that is simpler and numerically more stable than projected gradient methods.
Degeneracy / singularity
If a single component collapses onto one data point, its covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k \to \mathbf{0}$ and the likelihood blows up to $+\infty$ — a pathological maximum. In practice: regularise $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$ by adding $\lambda I$, or use a conjugate prior (Normal-Wishart) that prevents collapse.

§11.3

EM Algorithm ★

The Expectation-Maximisation (EM) algorithm alternates between two steps, guaranteed to be non-decreasing in log-likelihood at every iteration. The central quantity is the responsibility $r_{nk}$ — the posterior probability that component $k$ generated data point $\mathbf{x}_n$.

EM iteration cycle

The responsibility is a posterior probability computed by Bayes' rule: prior $\pi_k$ times likelihood $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_n|\boldsymbol{\mu}_k,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)$, normalised over all $K$ components.

E-step — Responsibility
$r_{nk} = \dfrac{\pi_k\,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_n|\boldsymbol{\mu}_k,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{K}\pi_j\,\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x}_n|\boldsymbol{\mu}_j,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j)}$
Soft membership: $r_{nk}\in[0,1]$, $\sum_k r_{nk}=1$. No parameters are updated in this step.
Effective Count
$N_k = \sum_{n=1}^{N} r_{nk}$
"Soft" cluster size. $\sum_k N_k = N$. Acts as the denominator in every M-step update.
M-step — Mean Update
$\boldsymbol{\mu}_k \leftarrow \dfrac{1}{N_k}\sum_{n=1}^{N} r_{nk}\,\mathbf{x}_n$
Responsibility-weighted mean. Data points near component $k$'s old mean get higher weight.
M-step — Covariance Update
$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k \leftarrow \dfrac{1}{N_k}\sum_{n=1}^{N} r_{nk}(\mathbf{x}_n-\boldsymbol{\mu}_k)(\mathbf{x}_n-\boldsymbol{\mu}_k)^\top$
Responsibility-weighted covariance. Same form as Gaussian MLE but re-weighted.
M-step — Weight Update
$\pi_k \leftarrow \dfrac{N_k}{N}$
Fraction of data effectively assigned to component $k$. Automatically satisfies the simplex constraint.
Convergence guarantee
The log-likelihood $\ell(\theta)$ is non-decreasing at every EM iteration. It converges to a local maximum (or saddle point). Global optimality is not guaranteed — multiple random restarts are standard practice.

Hard vs. Soft Assignment: K-means and EM

K-means is the limiting case of EM for GMMs with equal spherical covariances $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k = \sigma^2 I$ as $\sigma^2 \to 0$. In that limit responsibilities become binary: $r_{nk} \to 1$ for the nearest cluster, $0$ otherwise.

PropertyK-meansEM for GMMs
Assignment type Hard (0 or 1) Soft ($r_{nk} \in [0,1]$)
Cluster shape Spherical (isotropic) Arbitrary ellipsoid
Covariance Implicit: $\sigma^2 I$, same for all Learned per cluster: $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$
Uncertainty output None — hard labels only Posterior probabilities $r_{nk}$
Optimisation objective Sum of squared distances Log-likelihood $\ell(\theta)$
M-step mean formula $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \frac{1}{|C_k|}\sum_{n\in C_k}\mathbf{x}_n$ $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \frac{1}{N_k}\sum_n r_{nk}\mathbf{x}_n$
Full EM procedure step by step

1. Initialise: choose $K$; set initial $\{\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k, \pi_k\}$ (e.g., from K-means or random).

2. Repeat until convergence:

  • E-step: compute $r_{nk}$ for all $(n,k)$ using current parameters.
  • M-step: update $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$, $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k$, $\pi_k$ using $r_{nk}$.
  • Evaluate log-likelihood $\ell(\theta)$; check for convergence (e.g., $|\Delta\ell| < \epsilon$).

3. Return final parameters $\hat\theta$.

Each M-step update is a closed-form weighted Gaussian MLE, so no inner optimisation loop is needed. This makes EM far simpler to implement than constrained gradient methods.

Latent Variable Perspective & ELBO

The ELBO (Evidence Lower BOund) gives EM its theoretical foundation: EM performs coordinate ascent on the ELBO, a lower bound on the log-likelihood. The E-step tightens the bound; the M-step raises it. Together they guarantee the log-likelihood is non-decreasing.

ELBO geometry — log-likelihood, lower bound, and KL gap

The key identity is a decomposition of the log-likelihood:

Jensen's Inequality (applied)
$\log \mathbb{E}_q[g(z)] \;\geq\; \mathbb{E}_q[\log g(z)]$
Log is concave. Applying this with $g(z)=p(\mathbf{x},z|\theta)/q(z)$ yields the ELBO lower bound.
ELBO Definition
$\mathcal{L}(q,\theta) = \sum_k q(z{=}k)\log\frac{p(\mathbf{x},z{=}k|\theta)}{q(z{=}k)}$
Evidence Lower BOund. Valid for any distribution $q$ over latent $z$.
Decomposition
$\log p(\mathbf{x}|\theta) = \mathcal{L}(q,\theta) + \mathrm{KL}(q(z)\,\|\,p(z|\mathbf{x},\theta))$
Since $\mathrm{KL}\geq 0$, the ELBO is always a lower bound. The gap equals the KL divergence.
EM as Coordinate Ascent
E: $q^*=p(z|\mathbf{x},\theta)$ (KL→0); M: $\theta^* = \arg\max_\theta \mathcal{L}(q,\theta)$
E-step sets $q$ to the posterior — ELBO equals log-likelihood. M-step maximises ELBO over $\theta$.
Why the KL gap matters
After an M-step, $\theta$ has changed so the old $q$ is no longer the exact posterior — a KL gap reopens. The next E-step closes it by recomputing $q=p(z|\mathbf{x},\theta^{\text{new}})$. This alternation is exactly the EM cycle: tighten, raise, tighten, raise ...
Generality: beyond GMMs

The ELBO/EM framework applies to any latent variable model where the posterior $p(z|\mathbf{x},\theta)$ is tractable:

  • Hidden Markov Models: E-step via forward-backward algorithm.
  • Probabilistic PCA: E-step gives low-dimensional projections; M-step updates the loading matrix.
  • Variational Autoencoders (VAEs): the posterior is intractable, so it is approximated by a neural encoder $q_\phi(z|\mathbf{x})$. The ELBO is still maximised — now jointly over $\theta$ (decoder) and $\phi$ (encoder). Same Jensen lower bound, different parameterisation of $q$.

The GMM is the cleanest worked example because the posterior $p(z|\mathbf{x},\theta)$ is a simple categorical distribution (the responsibilities $r_{nk}$).

§11.5

GMMs for Density Estimation

Any smooth probability density on $\mathbb{R}^D$ can be approximated arbitrarily well by a GMM with sufficiently many components $K$. The smoothness of the individual Gaussians is inherited by the mixture — but choosing $K$ is a model-selection problem requiring principled criteria.

Bayesian Information Criterion
$\mathrm{BIC}(K) = -2\,\ell(\hat\theta) + p(K)\log N$
$\ell(\hat\theta)$ = maximised log-likelihood; $p(K)$ = free parameters; $N$ = data points. Choose $K^* = \arg\min_K \mathrm{BIC}(K)$.
Free Parameter Count
$p(K) = K\!\cdot\!D + K\!\cdot\!\tfrac{D(D+1)}{2} + (K\!-\!1)$
Means + covariances + weights (full covariances). Diagonal covariances reduce the middle term to $K\cdot D$.
BIC vs. AIC
AIC uses penalty $2p(K)$ (constant). BIC uses $p(K)\log N$ — grows with $N$, matching the Bayesian model evidence approximation. For large $N$, BIC penalises complexity more strongly and is asymptotically consistent for model selection; AIC is not.
Practical workflow for GMM density estimation

For $K \in \{1, 2, 3, \ldots, K_{\max}\}$:

  • Run EM (multiple random restarts) to obtain $\hat\theta_K$.
  • Compute $\mathrm{BIC}(K)$.

Select $K^* = \arg\min_K \mathrm{BIC}(K)$ and use that GMM as the density estimate.

Limitations:

  • Requires choosing $K$ (BIC helps but does not eliminate ambiguity).
  • All components are Gaussian — cannot capture heavy-tailed sub-populations without many $K$.
  • EM finds local optima; results depend on initialisation.
  • Curse of dimensionality: in high $D$, covariance estimation requires many data points. Diagonal or tied covariances mitigate this.

Nonparametric alternative: Dirichlet Process GMMs allow $K$ to grow with data — avoiding the choice entirely.
