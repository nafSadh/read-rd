Support Vector Machines

SVMs distill classification to a single geometric question: where is the widest street you can draw between two classes? The dual formulation reveals that the answer depends only on pairwise inner products — a structural fact the kernel trick exploits to classify in implicitly infinite-dimensional spaces.

§12.1

Separating Hyperplanes

Binary classification places every training point $\mathbf{x}_n \in \mathbb{R}^D$ on one of two sides with label $y_n \in \{+1, -1\}$. A hyperplane — defined by a weight vector $\mathbf{w}$ and bias $b$ — divides $\mathbb{R}^D$ into two half-spaces and provides the decision boundary. The SVM selects the hyperplane with the largest margin from either class.

Hyperplane
$\{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^D : \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b = 0\}$
w is the normal vector (perpendicular to the hyperplane); b shifts it off the origin. Dimension $D-1$, connecting to C2 affine subspaces.
Prediction Rule
$\hat{y} = \text{sign}(\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}^* + b)$
Positive side $\to$ class $+1$; negative side $\to$ class $-1$. The boundary itself is ambiguous — in practice defaulted to $+1$.
Signed Distance to Hyperplane
$d_n = \dfrac{y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}_n + b)}{\|\mathbf{w}\|}$
Always positive for correctly classified points. The denominator $\|\mathbf{w}\|$ converts the functional margin to Euclidean distance (C3 norm).
Correct Classification
$y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}_n + b) > 0$
The sign of the label matches the sign of the output. The functional margin is unnormalized; rescaling $(\mathbf{w}, b)$ by any $\alpha > 0$ preserves classification.
Scale freedom — the key design choice
If $(\mathbf{w}, b)$ separates the data, so does $(2\mathbf{w}, 2b)$. This non-uniqueness is resolved in §12.2 by fixing a canonical scale: require the closest training point to satisfy $y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + b) = 1$ exactly. This "functional margin = 1" convention pins the scale and simplifies the constraints.
Functional margin vs. geometric margin

The functional margin of point $n$ is $\hat{y}_n = y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + b)$ — it is positive when correctly classified but depends on $\|\mathbf{w}\|$.

The geometric margin divides by $\|\mathbf{w}\|$ to give the true Euclidean distance:

$$\gamma_n = \frac{y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + b)}{\|\mathbf{w}\|}$$

The margin of the classifier is $\gamma = \min_n \gamma_n$ — the distance to the closest point. The SVM maximizes this quantity.

If infinitely many hyperplanes separate the data, maximizing the margin selects the unique "widest street" solution. This maximum-margin principle provides strong generalization bounds via VC theory.

§12.2

Hard-Margin SVM

When the two classes are linearly separable, the hard-margin SVM finds the unique hyperplane that maximizes the margin — the width of the empty band between the classes. This is formulated as a convex quadratic program with a unique global minimum.

Hard-margin SVM geometry: margin, support vectors, decision boundary
Hard-Margin Primal
$$\min_{\mathbf{w},b} \tfrac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 \quad \text{s.t.} \; y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + b) \geq 1$$
Convex QP with $N$ linear constraints. Unique global minimum. Maximizing $2/\|\mathbf{w}\|$ is equivalent.
Margin Width
$\text{margin} = \dfrac{2}{\|\mathbf{w}\|}$
Distance between the two margin hyperplanes $\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}+b=\pm 1$, measured along the normal $\mathbf{w}/\|\mathbf{w}\|$.
Support vectors
The training points for which $y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n + b) = 1$ exactly lie on the margin boundaries. These are the support vectors — the solution depends only on them. Removing any non-support-vector training point leaves $\mathbf{w}^*$ and $b^*$ unchanged. Typically only a small fraction of $N$ points are support vectors.
Why ½‖w‖² instead of 2/‖w‖?

Maximizing $2/\|\mathbf{w}\|$ is equivalent to minimizing $\|\mathbf{w}\|$, which is equivalent to minimizing $\frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2$. The last form is chosen because:

  • It is a smooth, strictly convex objective (the gradient $\nabla_\mathbf{w} \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 = \mathbf{w}$ is linear).
  • Squaring eliminates the square root, simplifying KKT conditions and gradient computation.
  • The factor of $\frac{1}{2}$ cancels when differentiating, giving $\nabla = \mathbf{w}$ with no leading constant.

These choices produce the cleanest Lagrangian (§12.3) and the simplest dual objective.

§12.3

Dual Formulation

Introducing Lagrange multipliers $\alpha_n \geq 0$ and applying KKT stationarity conditions transforms the primal into an equivalent dual quadratic program over the multipliers. The pivotal structural fact: the data appears only through pairwise inner products $\mathbf{x}_n^\top\mathbf{x}_m$ — the gateway to the kernel trick.

Lagrangian
$$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w},b,\boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 - \sum_n \alpha_n\bigl[y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n+b)-1\bigr]$$
One multiplier $\alpha_n \geq 0$ per constraint. Direct application of C7 Lagrangian construction.
KKT Stationarity
$\mathbf{w}^* = \sum_n \alpha_n y_n \mathbf{x}_n \quad;\quad \sum_n \alpha_n y_n = 0$
Setting $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\mathbf{w}=\mathbf{0}$ and $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial b=0$. The first gives $\mathbf{w}$ in the span of training points.
Hard-Margin Dual
$$\max_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}} \sum_n\alpha_n - \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_n\sum_m \alpha_n\alpha_m y_n y_m \mathbf{x}_n^\top\mathbf{x}_m$$
Subject to $\alpha_n \geq 0$ and $\sum_n \alpha_n y_n = 0$. Data appears only as inner products $\mathbf{x}_n^\top\mathbf{x}_m$.
Prediction (Dual Form)
$\hat{y} = \text{sign}\!\left(\sum_n \alpha_n y_n \mathbf{x}_n^\top\mathbf{x}^* + b^*\right)$
Only support vectors ($\alpha_n > 0$) contribute. The sum is sparse — typically over a small subset of training points.
Complementary slackness — the sparsity principle
KKT complementary slackness requires $\alpha_n\bigl[y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n+b)-1\bigr] = 0$ for all $n$. Either $\alpha_n = 0$ (point is outside the margin, irrelevant) or $y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n+b) = 1$ (point is on the margin, a support vector). Most $\alpha_n$ are zero — this is why the SVM solution is sparse.
Why strong duality holds (zero duality gap)

The primal is a convex quadratic program with affine inequality constraints. When the data is linearly separable, the feasible set is non-empty.

Slater's condition (C7): a strictly feasible point exists (any point strictly inside all margin constraints). Slater's condition is sufficient for strong duality in convex programs.

Therefore the dual optimum equals the primal optimum — there is no duality gap. Solving the dual gives the exact primal solution via $\mathbf{w}^* = \sum_n\alpha_n y_n\mathbf{x}_n$.

Recovering $b^*$: from any support vector $\mathbf{x}_s$ with $\alpha_s > 0$, use $b^* = y_s - \mathbf{w}^{*\top}\mathbf{x}_s$. Averaging over all support vectors improves numerical stability.

§12.4

Soft-Margin SVM

When classes overlap or outliers exist, no separating hyperplane exists and the hard-margin SVM is infeasible. Soft-margin SVMs allow margin violations via slack variables $\xi_n \geq 0$, penalizing violations with regularization constant $C > 0$.

Soft-margin SVM: slack variables for margin violators
Soft-Margin Primal
$$\min_{\mathbf{w},b,\boldsymbol{\xi}} \tfrac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 + C\sum_n\xi_n$$
Subject to $y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n+b) \geq 1-\xi_n$ and $\xi_n\geq 0$. C trades margin width against constraint violations.
Hinge Loss
$\ell_\text{hinge}(y,f) = \max\!\bigl(0,\,1 - y \cdot f(\mathbf{x})\bigr)$
Zero when correctly classified outside the margin; grows linearly inside. Soft-margin SVM = L2-regularized ERM with hinge loss (C8 link).
Soft-Margin Dual
$$\max_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}} \sum_n\alpha_n - \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_n\sum_m\alpha_n\alpha_m y_n y_m\mathbf{x}_n^\top\mathbf{x}_m$$
Subject to $0 \leq \alpha_n \leq C$ and $\sum_n\alpha_n y_n=0$. The only change from the hard-margin dual: the box constraint $\alpha_n \leq C$.
C–λ Correspondence
$\lambda = \dfrac{1}{2C}$
Setting $\lambda=1/(2C)$ shows soft-margin SVM = L2-regularized ERM. Large $C$ = small regularization = narrow margin = complex model.
Interpreting the slack variables
$\xi_n = 0$: correctly classified, outside or on the margin.
$0 < \xi_n \leq 1$: inside the margin but correctly classified.
$\xi_n > 1$: misclassified — wrong side of the decision boundary.

KKT analysis of the soft-margin dual: $\alpha_n = 0$ (outside margin), $0 < \alpha_n < C$ (on margin, $\xi_n=0$), $\alpha_n = C$ (inside margin or misclassified, $\xi_n > 0$).
Soft-margin SVM as ERM with hinge loss (C8 connection)

Eliminating the slack variables from the soft-margin primal (by substituting $\xi_n = \max(0, 1 - y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n+b))$) yields:

$$\min_{\mathbf{w},b}\; \frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2 + C\sum_n \max\!\bigl(0,\, 1 - y_n(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x}_n+b)\bigr)$$

This is exactly the C8 empirical risk minimization framework with:

  • Loss: hinge loss $\ell_\text{hinge}(y_n, f(\mathbf{x}_n)) = \max(0, 1 - y_n f(\mathbf{x}_n))$
  • Regularizer: $\frac{1}{2}\|\mathbf{w}\|^2$ (L2 / weight decay)
  • Trade-off: $C = \frac{1}{2\lambda}$ — large $C$ means less regularization

The hinge loss is convex and piecewise linear. It is an upper bound on the 0-1 loss, making it a computationally tractable surrogate for the true classification error.

§12.5

Kernelization

Because the dual and the prediction rule depend on training data only through inner products $\mathbf{x}_n^\top\mathbf{x}_m$, those inner products can be replaced by a kernel function $k(\mathbf{x}_n,\mathbf{x}_m) = \varphi(\mathbf{x}_n)^\top\varphi(\mathbf{x}_m)$. The feature map $\varphi$ need never be computed explicitly.

Kernel trick: non-separable in ℝ² → separable in feature space
Kernel Function
$k(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}') = \varphi(\mathbf{x})^\top\varphi(\mathbf{x}')$
Replace every inner product in the dual with $k(\cdot,\cdot)$. Never compute $\varphi$ explicitly. The feature map may map to infinite dimensions.
Kernelized Prediction
$\hat{y} = \text{sign}\!\left(\sum_n \alpha_n y_n k(\mathbf{x}_n,\mathbf{x}^*) + b^*\right)$
Only support vectors contribute (sparse sum). No explicit $\mathbf{w}$ vector needed — the prediction is fully kernelized.
RBF / Gaussian Kernel
$k(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}') = \exp\!\left(-\dfrac{\|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}'\|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$
Corresponds to an infinite-dimensional feature space via the Taylor expansion of $\exp$. Bandwidth $\sigma$ controls locality: small $\sigma$ = local (complex), large $\sigma$ = smooth.
Polynomial Kernel
$k(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}') = (\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}' + c)^p$
Implicit feature space: all monomials of degree $\leq p$. Degree $p=2$ in $\mathbb{R}^D$ gives $O(D^2)$ features computed in $O(D)$ time.
Mercer's theorem — validity of kernels
A symmetric function $k: \mathbb{R}^D \times \mathbb{R}^D \to \mathbb{R}$ is a valid kernel if and only if the Gram matrix $K \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$ with $K_{nm} = k(\mathbf{x}_n,\mathbf{x}_m)$ is positive semidefinite for every choice of inputs. PSD-ness corresponds to the existence of a valid inner product in feature space (C3 connection).
Why the RBF kernel implies an infinite-dimensional feature space

Write $\|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}'\|^2 = \|\mathbf{x}\|^2 - 2\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}' + \|\mathbf{x}'\|^2$, so:

$$k(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}') = e^{-\|\mathbf{x}\|^2/2\sigma^2} \cdot e^{\,\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}'/\sigma^2} \cdot e^{-\|\mathbf{x}'\|^2/2\sigma^2}$$

Expanding the middle factor using the Taylor series $e^t = \sum_{k=0}^\infty t^k/k!$:

$$e^{\,\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}'/\sigma^2} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}')^k}{k!\,\sigma^{2k}}$$

Each term $(\mathbf{x}^\top\mathbf{x}')^k$ corresponds to a degree-$k$ polynomial kernel, so the full expansion is an infinite sum of polynomial kernels — equivalently, a dot product in an infinite-dimensional feature space.

This is why RBF SVMs can fit arbitrarily complex decision boundaries (as $\sigma \to 0$) and also why the bandwidth $\sigma$ is a critical hyperparameter.

Gram matrix and kernelized QP

Define the Gram matrix $K \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$ with $K_{nm} = k(\mathbf{x}_n,\mathbf{x}_m)$. The kernelized soft-margin dual becomes:

$$\max_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}\; \mathbf{1}^\top\boldsymbol{\alpha} - \tfrac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{\alpha}^\top (K \circ \mathbf{y}\mathbf{y}^\top)\boldsymbol{\alpha} \quad \text{s.t.}\; 0\leq\alpha_n\leq C,\;\mathbf{y}^\top\boldsymbol{\alpha}=0$$

Here $K \circ \mathbf{y}\mathbf{y}^\top$ is the element-wise product (Hadamard product) of the Gram matrix with the outer product of labels. The QP involves no explicit feature vectors — only the $N\times N$ Gram matrix.

Kernel PCA (C4 link): PCA on $K = \varphi(X)\varphi(X)^\top$ gives principal components in feature space. The SVD of the Gram matrix is the computational bridge between kernel methods and dimensionality reduction.

§12.6

Numerical Solution

The SVM dual is a convex QP — it has a unique global optimum, but the naive $O(N^2)$ Gram matrix is infeasible at scale. The Sequential Minimal Optimization (SMO) algorithm of Platt (1998) solves the minimal two-variable subproblem analytically at each step, requiring $O(N)$ memory.

QP Matrix Form
$$\min_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}\; \tfrac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{\alpha}^\top Q\boldsymbol{\alpha} - \mathbf{1}^\top\boldsymbol{\alpha}$$
$Q_{nm} = y_n y_m k(\mathbf{x}_n,\mathbf{x}_m)$. $Q$ is PSD (valid kernel), so the QP is strictly convex with a unique global minimum.
Recovering b*
$b^* = y_s - \sum_n \alpha_n y_n k(\mathbf{x}_n,\mathbf{x}_s)$
Computed from any support vector $\mathbf{x}_s$ with $0 < \alpha_s < C$ (which lies exactly on the margin). Averaged over all such support vectors for stability.
SMO algorithm outline (Platt, 1998)
Step 1: Select $\alpha_n$ that most violates the KKT conditions.
Step 2: Select a second $\alpha_m$ by heuristic (maximize $|E_n - E_m|$, where $E_n = f(\mathbf{x}_n) - y_n$).
Step 3: Solve the two-variable QP analytically (closed form); clip to $[0, C]$; maintain $\sum\alpha_n y_n = 0$.
Step 4: Update $b$ and the error cache. Repeat until KKT convergence.
Sparsity of the solution
After training: $\alpha_n = 0$ for non-support vectors (do not contribute to prediction) and $\alpha_n > 0$ for support vectors (fully determine $\mathbf{w}^*$ and $b^*$). Typically only a small fraction of $N$ training points are support vectors, making prediction fast regardless of $N$.
Why SMO needs only O(N) memory

A naive QP solver requires the full Gram matrix $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$, which costs $O(N^2)$ storage. For $N = 10^6$ this is already $\sim 10^{12}$ entries — impractical.

SMO avoids storing $Q$ by recomputing the two needed rows at each iteration on-the-fly (or caching a small subset). It only stores:

  • The $N$ dual variables $\alpha_n$.
  • The $N$ error values $E_n = f(\mathbf{x}_n) - y_n$ (the error cache).
  • The $N$ training points $\mathbf{x}_n$ and labels $y_n$.

Each two-variable update has a closed-form solution (the constraint $\alpha_n y_n + \alpha_m y_m = \text{const}$ reduces the QP to a 1D quadratic), so no inner numerical solver is needed.

SMO has formally $O(N^2)$ worst-case complexity but superlinear convergence in practice, making it the standard algorithm in production SVM implementations (libsvm, sklearn).
