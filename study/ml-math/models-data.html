§8.1

Data, Models, and Learning

Every supervised learning problem rests on three pillars: data (observed samples), a model (parameterized hypothesis about the data-generating process), and a learning algorithm (the criterion and procedure for fitting the model). The gap between model and reality is the fundamental source of all difficulty in ML.

Training Set
$\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_1,y_1),\ldots,(\mathbf{x}_N,y_N)\}$
$\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^D$ is the feature vector, $y_i$ is the target. Assumed i.i.d. from unknown $p^*(\mathbf{x},y)$.
Parameterized Model
$p(y \mid \mathbf{x}, \boldsymbol{\theta})$
A family of distributions indexed by $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \Theta$. Different families (linear, neural net, GP) impose different structural assumptions.
Expected (True) Risk
$R(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{x},y)\sim p^*}\!\bigl[\ell(f(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\theta}),y)\bigr]$
The ideal objective — but $p^*$ is unknown, so this cannot be computed directly.
Empirical Risk
$R_\text{emp}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \tfrac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \ell\!\bigl(f(\mathbf{x}_i,\boldsymbol{\theta}),y_i\bigr)$
The computable proxy for true risk. Learning = minimizing $R_\text{emp}$.
The model is not the truth
A model is a useful fiction that compresses data into parameters. The entire discipline of statistics and ML is about navigating the gap between the fiction (model) and the real world ($p^*$).
Common loss functions

The choice of loss function determines what "fitting well" means:

  • Squared loss: $\ell(\hat{y},y) = (\hat{y}-y)^2$ — penalizes large errors quadratically; used in regression
  • Absolute loss: $\ell(\hat{y},y) = |\hat{y}-y|$ — robust to outliers; leads to median regression
  • Binary cross-entropy: $\ell(\hat{y},y) = -[y\log\hat{y} + (1-y)\log(1-\hat{y})]$ — for binary classification
  • 0-1 loss: $\ell(\hat{y},y) = \mathbf{1}[\hat{y}\neq y]$ — counts misclassifications; non-differentiable, so rarely optimized directly

Each loss corresponds to an implicit assumption about the noise model in the data-generating process. Squared loss corresponds to Gaussian noise; cross-entropy to Bernoulli outcomes.

§8.2

Empirical Risk Minimization

ERM is the formal statement of supervised learning: find the parameters that minimize average loss on the training set. Most learning algorithms — least squares, logistic regression, SVMs — are special cases of ERM. The central challenge is that minimizing training loss does not guarantee good generalization to unseen data.

ERM
$\boldsymbol{\theta}^* = \underset{\boldsymbol{\theta}}{\arg\min}\; R_\text{emp}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$
The defining equation of supervised learning.
Regularized ERM
$\boldsymbol{\theta}^* = \underset{\boldsymbol{\theta}}{\arg\min}\;\bigl[R_\text{emp}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) + \lambda\,\Omega(\boldsymbol{\theta})\bigr]$
$\Omega(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2$ gives ridge; $\|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|_1$ gives lasso. $\lambda\geq 0$ controls strength.
Generalization Gap
$\text{gap} = R(\boldsymbol{\theta}) - R_\text{emp}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$
How much better the model looks on training data than on new data. Large gap = overfitting.
Bias–Variance Decomposition
$\mathbb{E}_\mathcal{D}[(y-\hat{f})^2] = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Var} + \sigma^2$
Test error decomposes into systematic error, sensitivity to training set, and irreducible noise.
Bias–Variance Tradeoff: decomposing test error vs. model complexity
The bias-variance tradeoff in practice
Increasing model complexity (more parameters, higher polynomial degree, fewer regularization) decreases bias but increases variance. The noise² floor is irreducible — no model can beat Bayes error. The goal of regularization, cross-validation, and early stopping is to locate the optimal complexity without needing to see test data.
Worked example: polynomial regression

Fit degree-$d$ polynomials to $N=10$ points drawn from $y = \sin(x) + \varepsilon$:

  • $d=1$ (linear): high bias, low variance — the curve cannot capture the sinusoidal shape. Underfits.
  • $d=9$ (degree-9 polynomial): zero training error but wild oscillations at test points. Overfits.
  • $d=3$ (cubic): reasonable approximation — balances bias and variance well.

The lesson: the right complexity is not always "more is better." With only $N=10$ points, a degree-9 polynomial has nearly as many parameters as data points and will memorize noise.

Controls for the tradeoff: regularization (penalize $\|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2$); cross-validation to choose $\lambda$ or $d$; early stopping; data augmentation (increases effective $N$); ensemble methods (bagging reduces variance).

§8.3

Parameter Estimation — Maximum Likelihood

MLE answers: given observed data $\mathcal{D}$, which parameter value $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ makes the data most probable? It is the frequentist workhorse — and it is provably equivalent to ERM with the negative log-likelihood as the loss function.

Likelihood Function
$L(\boldsymbol{\theta}\mid\mathcal{D}) = \prod_{i=1}^N p(x_i\mid\boldsymbol{\theta})$
A function of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ for fixed $\mathcal{D}$. Not a probability distribution over $\boldsymbol{\theta}$.
Log-Likelihood (NLL form)
$\ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{i=1}^N \log p(x_i\mid\boldsymbol{\theta})$
Product becomes sum — numerically stable, and gradients of sums are sums of gradients.
MLE
$\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{MLE} = \underset{\boldsymbol{\theta}}{\arg\max}\sum_{i=1}^N \log p(x_i\mid\boldsymbol{\theta})$
Equivalent to ERM with NLL loss: $\arg\min_\theta\bigl[-\tfrac{1}{N}\sum\log p(x_i|\theta)\bigr]$.
Gaussian MLE (univariate)
$\mu_\text{MLE} = \tfrac{1}{N}\sum x_i,\quad \sigma^2_\text{MLE} = \tfrac{1}{N}\sum(x_i-\mu_\text{MLE})^2$
$\mu_\text{MLE}$ is the sample mean (unbiased). $\sigma^2_\text{MLE}$ divides by $N$ — it is biased downward.
MLE = ERM with NLL loss — the bridge between optimization and probability
$$\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{MLE} = \underset{\boldsymbol{\theta}}{\arg\min}\left[-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \log p(x_i\mid\boldsymbol{\theta})\right]$$ This shows MLE is simply ERM where the loss is the negative log-likelihood. Under Gaussian noise, NLL becomes squared loss, so MLE gives ordinary least squares. This is why Chapter 9 (linear regression) begins exactly where this chapter ends.
Gaussian log-likelihood derivation

Assume $x_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ i.i.d. The log-likelihood is:

$$\log L(\mu,\sigma^2\mid\mathcal{D}) = -\frac{N}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^N(x_i-\mu)^2$$

Setting $\partial/\partial\mu = 0$ gives $\mu_\text{MLE} = \frac{1}{N}\sum x_i$. Setting $\partial/\partial\sigma^2 = 0$ gives $\sigma^2_\text{MLE} = \frac{1}{N}\sum(x_i - \mu_\text{MLE})^2$.

Why is $\sigma^2_\text{MLE}$ biased? MLE uses $\mu_\text{MLE}$ computed from the same data, which already "absorbs" some variance. The true residuals $(x_i - \mu_\text{true})$ would be larger on average. The unbiased estimator (Bessel's correction) divides by $N-1$, not $N$.

Properties of MLE
  • Consistency: $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_\text{MLE} \to \boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{true}$ as $N\to\infty$ (under regularity conditions)
  • Asymptotic normality: $\sqrt{N}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_\text{MLE}-\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{true}) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\mathcal{I}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{true})^{-1})$ where $\mathcal{I}$ is the Fisher information matrix
  • Efficiency: Achieves the Cramér-Rao lower bound asymptotically — no unbiased estimator has lower variance in the limit
  • Invariance: If $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ maximizes $L(\boldsymbol{\theta})$, then $g(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})$ maximizes $L(g^{-1}(\cdot))$ for any invertible $g$
  • Bias for finite $N$: MLE is generally biased (as seen with $\sigma^2_\text{MLE}$), though bias vanishes as $N\to\infty$
§8.4

Probabilistic Modeling and Inference

The Bayesian view treats $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ as a random variable rather than a fixed unknown. A prior $p(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ encodes belief before seeing data; the posterior $p(\boldsymbol{\theta}\mid\mathcal{D})$ encodes updated belief after. MAP is a point summary of the posterior; the full Bayesian prediction averages over all $\boldsymbol{\theta}$.

Bayesian update: from prior to posterior via the likelihood
Bayes' Theorem
$p(\boldsymbol{\theta}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{X}) = \dfrac{p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{X},\boldsymbol{\theta})\,p(\boldsymbol{\theta})}{p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{X})}$
Posterior $\propto$ likelihood $\times$ prior. The denominator $p(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{X})$ is constant in $\boldsymbol{\theta}$.
MAP Estimate
$\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{MAP} = \underset{\boldsymbol{\theta}}{\arg\max}\bigl[\log p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{X},\boldsymbol{\theta}) + \log p(\boldsymbol{\theta})\bigr]$
MLE plus a log-prior regularization term. The evidence $p(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{X})$ drops out.
Marginal Likelihood
$p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{X}) = \int p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{X},\boldsymbol{\theta})\,p(\boldsymbol{\theta})\,\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{\theta}$
The normalizing constant. Usually intractable — requires integrating over all $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ weighted by the prior.
Predictive Distribution (full Bayes)
$p(y^*\mid\mathbf{x}^*,\mathbf{X},\mathbf{y}) = \int p(y^*\mid\mathbf{x}^*,\boldsymbol{\theta})\,p(\boldsymbol{\theta}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{X})\,\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{\theta}$
Marginalizes over all $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, weighting by posterior. Gives a distribution over $y^*$, not just a point estimate.
MAP = regularized MLE
A Gaussian prior $p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_0^2 \mathbf{I})$ gives $\log p(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = -\frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2}\|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2 + \text{const}$, so MAP becomes: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{MAP} = \underset{\boldsymbol{\theta}}{\arg\min}\left[-\sum\log p(y_i|\mathbf{x}_i,\boldsymbol{\theta}) + \frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2}\|\boldsymbol{\theta}\|^2\right]$$ This is exactly ridge regression ($L_2$ regularization) with $\lambda = 1/\sigma_0^2$. A Laplace prior gives $L_1$ regularization (lasso). Regularization strength = tightness of prior.
Conjugate priors and sequential Bayesian updating

Conjugate priors make the posterior analytically tractable — same family as prior:

  • Gaussian likelihood (known $\sigma^2$) + Gaussian prior on $\mu$ → Gaussian posterior
  • Binomial likelihood + Beta prior on $p$ → Beta posterior
  • Poisson likelihood + Gamma prior on $\lambda$ → Gamma posterior
  • Multinomial likelihood + Dirichlet prior on $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ → Dirichlet posterior

Sequential learning: the posterior after seeing $N$ data points becomes the prior when the $(N+1)$-th datum arrives: $$p(\boldsymbol{\theta}\mid x_1,\ldots,x_N,x_{N+1}) \propto p(x_{N+1}\mid\boldsymbol{\theta})\,p(\boldsymbol{\theta}\mid x_1,\ldots,x_N)$$ Bayesian learning never needs to store raw data — the posterior is a sufficient summary of all past observations.

MLE vs MAP vs full Bayes for prediction:

  • MLE: $p(y^*|\mathbf{x}^*,\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{MLE})$ — ignores all parameter uncertainty
  • MAP: $p(y^*|\mathbf{x}^*,\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{MAP})$ — uses a single best $\boldsymbol{\theta}$
  • Full Bayes: $\int p(y^*|\mathbf{x}^*,\boldsymbol{\theta})\,p(\boldsymbol{\theta}|\mathbf{y},\mathbf{X})\,\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{\theta}$ — properly accounts for uncertainty; substantially wider predictive intervals when $N$ is small
§8.5

Directed Graphical Models

Directed graphical models (DGMs), or Bayesian networks, provide a visual language for expressing complex joint distributions compactly. The central idea: a DAG encodes conditional independence assumptions, and the joint factorizes into one term per node conditioned on its parents.

Bayesian network: latent chain z₁ → z₂ observed x (plate notation)
DGM Factorization
$p(x_1,\ldots,x_K) = \prod_{k=1}^K p\!\bigl(x_k \mid \text{pa}(x_k)\bigr)$
One conditional per node, conditioned only on its parents $\text{pa}(x_k)$ in the DAG.
Chain Example
$p(A,B,C) = p(A)\,p(B\mid A)\,p(C\mid B)$
$C \perp\!\!\!\perp A \mid B$: conditioning on $B$ blocks the path from $A$ to $C$.
d-Separation: reading off conditional independence

d-Separation is a graphical criterion for determining conditional independence without computing any distributions. Three basic patterns:

  • Chain A → B → C: $A \perp\!\!\!\perp C \mid B$. Observing $B$ blocks information flow from $A$ to $C$. Marginally they are correlated.
  • Fork A ← B → C: $A \perp\!\!\!\perp C \mid B$. Common cause $B$ observed blocks the correlation. Marginally $A$ and $C$ are correlated through $B$.
  • Collider A → B ← C: $A \perp\!\!\!\perp C$ marginally. But conditioning on $B$ opens the path — "explaining away." Classic: given an exam score (collider), ability and difficulty become negatively correlated.

d-Separation rule: $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid Z$ iff every path between $X$ and $Y$ is blocked given $Z$. A path is blocked if it contains a chain/fork where the middle node is in $Z$, or a collider where neither the collider nor any descendant is in $Z$.

Plate notation and the Hidden Markov Model

Plate notation: repeated structure (e.g., $N$ i.i.d. observations) is enclosed in a rectangle ("plate") with a count. Avoids drawing the same subgraph $N$ times and makes the generative story explicit.

Hidden Markov Model (HMM): a chain of latent states $z_1\to z_2\to\cdots\to z_T$, each emitting an observation $x_t$: $$p(z_{1:T},x_{1:T}) = p(z_1)\prod_{t=2}^T p(z_t\mid z_{t-1})\prod_{t=1}^T p(x_t\mid z_t)$$

Why graphical models matter:

  • Compact specification of complex joint distributions over many variables
  • Systematic inference algorithms (belief propagation, variational inference, MCMC) that exploit graph structure
  • Model assumptions become visible — independence assumptions can be checked against domain knowledge
  • Modularity: swap one component (e.g., emission model) without redesigning inference from scratch
§8.6

Model Selection

Having estimated $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ within a fixed model family, the next question is: which family is best? Model selection is the problem of choosing between competing model structures — and it requires criteria that penalize complexity automatically to avoid overfitting the selection process itself.

Model Evidence
$p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{X},\mathcal{M}_k) = \int p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{X},\boldsymbol{\theta},\mathcal{M}_k)\,p(\boldsymbol{\theta}\mid\mathcal{M}_k)\,\mathrm{d}\boldsymbol{\theta}$
A single number per model. Automatically penalizes overly complex models (Occam's razor built in).
Model Posterior
$p(\mathcal{M}_k\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{X}) \propto p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{X},\mathcal{M}_k)\,p(\mathcal{M}_k)$
With uniform model prior, comparison reduces to comparing marginal likelihoods.
BIC
$\text{BIC}(\mathcal{M}_k) = \log p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{X},\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{MAP}) - \dfrac{d_k}{2}\log N$
$d_k$ = number of parameters; $N$ = data size. Select model with highest BIC. Penalty grows with $\log N$.
BMA — Bayesian Model Averaging
$p(y^*\mid\mathbf{x}^*,\mathbf{X},\mathbf{y}) = \sum_k p(y^*\mid\mathbf{x}^*,\mathcal{M}_k,\mathbf{X},\mathbf{y})\,p(\mathcal{M}_k\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{X})$
Average over all models weighted by posterior. Most principled but computationally expensive.
Occam's Razor is built into the marginal likelihood
A complex model (many parameters, diffuse prior) spreads probability mass across many possible datasets — it assigns low probability to any specific observed dataset unless the parameters fit very well. A simpler model concentrates mass on fewer datasets, assigning higher probability to any one of them. The marginal likelihood automatically penalizes complexity without an explicit penalty term.

BIC vs AIC: when to use each

Both criteria trade off fit against complexity, but from different starting points:

$$\text{AIC}(\mathcal{M}_k) = 2\log p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{X},\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{MLE}) - 2d_k$$ $$\text{BIC}(\mathcal{M}_k) = 2\log p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{X},\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{MLE}) - d_k\log N$$

AIC is derived from information theory (Kullback-Leibler divergence to the true model). It is better suited for prediction tasks where the goal is to minimize expected out-of-sample loss.

BIC approximates the Bayesian marginal likelihood via a Laplace approximation around $\boldsymbol{\theta}_\text{MAP}$. It is better for model identification — finding the correct structural form as $N\to\infty$. The penalty $d_k\log N$ grows without bound (unlike AIC's constant 2), so BIC is more aggressive at penalizing large models when $N$ is large.

LOO cross-validation for linear models: Leave-one-out CV (the most thorough form) has a closed-form solution via the hat matrix trick, costing no more than a single fit.
