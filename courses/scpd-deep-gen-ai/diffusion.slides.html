The Big Picture

Two fundamentally different strategies for generation
I
Overview · Paradigms 3

Two Ways to Generate

Autoregressive (Transformers): build output one token at a time, left to right. Each step predicts the next piece from everything before it.

Iterative Refinement (Diffusion): start from pure noise and refine the entire output simultaneously across many small steps.

II
Transformers · Architecture 5

The Transformer Block

A Transformer processes sequences through stacked blocks of self-attention and feed-forward layers.

Self-attention lets every token attend to every other token, capturing long-range dependencies in one pass.

Modern LLMs (GPT-4, Gemini, Claude) stack 32–128 of these blocks with billions of parameters.

Core insight

Attention is a learned routing mechanism — it decides which tokens are relevant to which, dynamically.

Transformers · Generation 6

Autoregressive Generation

LLMs generate one token at a time. At each step, the model sees everything generated so far and predicts a probability distribution over the vocabulary for the next token.

Training Objective P(x_t | x_1, x_2, ..., x_{t-1})

This is maximum likelihood over sequences — the model learns the joint distribution by factoring it into conditional probabilities.

Strength

Handles any sequential modality: text, code, music, even discretized images (e.g. image tokens via VQ-VAE).

Limitation: generation is inherently serial — each token requires a full forward pass. No parallelism at inference time.

III
Diffusion · Intuition 8

The Ink-in-Water Analogy

Imagine dropping ink into water. Over time, the ink diffuses until the water is uniformly colored — all structure is lost.

Now imagine you could reverse time and watch the uniform mixture re-concentrate into the original ink drop pattern.

That's what a diffusion model learns to do. The "ink" is your data (images, audio). The "diffusion" is adding Gaussian noise step by step until it's pure static. The model learns to run this process backward.

Core insight

You don't need to model the data distribution directly. Just learn to undo small amounts of noise — and chain those steps together.

Forward Process 9

The Forward Process

Systematically destroying data with a noise schedule
IV
Forward · Math 10

Adding Noise Step by Step

At each timestep t, a small amount of Gaussian noise is added according to a variance schedule beta_t.

Forward step q(xt | x_{t-1}) = N(xt; sqrt(1-beta_t) * x_{t-1}, beta_t * I)

The variance schedule beta_t typically starts small (~0.0001) and increases to ~0.02 over T=1000 steps.

A key trick: you can jump to any timestep directly without iterating, using the cumulative product alpha_bar_t.

Closed form xt = sqrt(alpha_bar_t) * x0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t) * epsilon

This closed-form sampling is essential for efficient training — you randomly pick a timestep and noise level per training example.

Reverse Process 11

The Reverse Process

Training a neural network to denoise — the UNet backbone
V
Reverse · UNet 12

The UNet Backbone

The denoising network is typically a UNet — an encoder-decoder with skip connections. It takes in a noisy image and the timestep, and predicts the noise.

The encoder progressively downsamples to capture global context. The decoder upsamples back to full resolution, using skip connections to recover fine detail.

Modern diffusion UNets add self-attention layers at middle resolutions and cross-attention for conditioning (e.g., text prompts).

Training Loss (simplified) L = || epsilon - epsilon_theta(xt, t) ||^2

The model predicts the noise that was added. Subtracting the predicted noise from xt gives a cleaner estimate of x0.

VI
Conditioning · CLIP + Cross-Attn 14

From Text to Pixels

Text-to-image models like Stable Diffusion use a text encoder (typically CLIP or T5) to convert the prompt into embeddings.

These text embeddings are injected into the UNet via cross-attention at every resolution level — the image features attend to the text features.

Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) amplifies the text signal: the model runs both with and without the prompt, then exaggerates the difference.

CFG Formula eps_guided = eps_uncond + w * (eps_cond - eps_uncond)

Latent Diffusion (Stable Diffusion) runs this process in a compressed latent space — 8x smaller than pixel space — making it practical on consumer GPUs.

Convergence

Hybrid architectures are blurring the boundary
VIII
Convergence · DiT 19

Diffusion Transformers (DiT)

The UNet isn't the only option. Diffusion Transformers (DiT) replace the UNet with a pure transformer backbone.

Image patches are treated like tokens. The transformer processes them with self-attention, conditioned on timestep and text via adaptive layer norm.

This is the architecture behind Sora (OpenAI), Stable Diffusion 3, and Flux. It scales better with compute than UNets.

Convergence trend

The two paradigms are merging: transformers are the backbone for both language models and the latest diffusion models. The difference is the training objective, not the architecture.

Autoregressive image models (like Parti, LlamaGen) go the other direction — using next-token prediction on discrete image tokens.

Summary 20

Key Takeaways

Transformers (LLMs) model data as sequences and generate one piece at a time. Extraordinary for language, reasoning, and code.

Diffusion models model data as a continuous denoising process and refine everything simultaneously. Extraordinary for images, video, and audio.

Multimodal transformers bridge the gap by encoding images as tokens and feeding them through the same autoregressive framework.

The architectures are converging — DiT uses a transformer backbone for diffusion, while models like Parti use autoregressive generation for images.

The real difference

It's not the architecture — it's the training objective. Next-token prediction vs. noise prediction. Sequential factoring vs. iterative refinement. Both are ways of learning to model distributions.
