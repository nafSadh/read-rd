The Big Question 1

Two Kinds of AI You Use Every Day

When you ask ChatGPT to write an email, it uses one kind of AI.

When you ask Midjourney or DALL-E to make a picture, it uses a completely different kind.

They look similar from the outside — you type words, AI makes something. But under the hood, they work in totally opposite ways.

Think of it this way

One is like a writer composing a sentence word by word. The other is like a sculptor chipping away at a block of marble until a shape emerges.

The Writer · Autocomplete 3

The World's Best Autocomplete

You know how your phone suggests the next word when you're texting? ChatGPT works the same way — just absurdly better.

It reads everything written so far, then picks the most likely next word. Then it reads the whole thing again, picks the next word. And again. And again.

It never sees the whole answer at once. It's always just guessing one word ahead.

The surprising part

That's really all it does. It just does it so well — trained on most of the text on the internet — that the results feel intelligent.

The Writer · Seeing 4

What About AI That "Sees" Photos?

When you upload a photo to ChatGPT or Google Gemini, the AI doesn't suddenly become a different kind of AI. It's still the same writer.

What happens is: the image gets translated into words first (by a separate helper), and then the writer reads those "image words" along with your question.

It's like having a friend who's blind but has someone whispering descriptions of everything they see. The blind friend is still the one doing the thinking.

Same trick

Whether the input is text, a photo, or a PDF — the AI converts everything into a stream of "tokens" (pieces of information) and then predicts what comes next, one piece at a time.

The Sculptor · Intuition 6

Imagine Dropping Ink in Water

Drop ink into a glass of water. It starts as a beautiful, detailed pattern. Then it slowly spreads out until the water is one uniform, murky color. All detail is gone.

Now imagine you could play that video in reverse. You'd watch uniform murk slowly re-form into that beautiful, intricate ink pattern.

That's what image AI does. It starts with pure visual static — like TV snow — and gradually cleans it up, step by step, until a picture emerges.

The trick

The AI was trained by watching millions of photos get slowly destroyed by static. It learned to reverse the destruction. So now it can start from static and "un-destroy" it into a new image.

The Sculptor · Training 7

How Did It Learn to Do This?

Imagine you take millions of photos and deliberately add static to each one — a little at first, then more and more, until it's pure noise.

Then you show the AI a staticky photo and ask: "What did this look like one step less staticky?"

You do this billions of times with different photos and different amounts of static. Eventually the AI gets incredibly good at removing one small step of static at a time.

At that point, you can give it pure static and say "clean this up" — and it will turn that noise into a brand new photo that never existed before.

The magic

It was never taught to "create images." It was taught to remove noise. Creation is what happens when you chain enough noise-removal steps together.

The Sculptor · Steering 8

But How Does It Know What to Draw?

When you type "a cat wearing a top hat," how does the noise-removal machine know to make that?

Your text gets converted into a kind of GPS signal that guides every clean-up step. At each step, the AI doesn't just remove noise randomly — it removes noise in a direction that makes the image look more like what you described.

It's like sculpting marble with a reference photo taped to the wall. Every chip of marble you remove is guided by that reference.

The key

The text is a compass. The denoising is the journey. Without text, you'd get a random image. With text, you get what you asked for.

The text understanding comes from a separate AI that was trained on millions of image-caption pairs from the internet.

The Future 11

They're Starting to Merge

The latest AI systems are starting to combine both approaches. OpenAI's Sora (video AI) uses the sculptor's technique but with the writer's brain architecture.

And writer-style AIs like GPT-4 and Gemini can now generate images too — by translating between their word-based thinking and sculptor-style image generation.

It's like a writer who also learned to paint, or a sculptor who also writes poetry. The tools are different, but the underlying intelligence is shared.

Where it's heading

Soon, the distinction won't matter to users. You'll just say what you want, and the AI will seamlessly write, draw, compose music, or make video — switching techniques under the hood.

Summary 12

The 30-Second Version

ChatGPT / Claude = a writer. It builds answers one word at a time, like the world's best autocomplete. Great for text, code, and conversation.

DALL-E / Midjourney = a sculptor. It starts with TV static and cleans it up step by step until a picture appears. Great for images, video, and audio.

Neither approach is "better." They're different tools for different jobs — like a pen and a chisel.

The amazing part

Both of them learned by reading (or looking at) enormous amounts of human-created work. They're mirrors of human creativity, not replacements for it.
