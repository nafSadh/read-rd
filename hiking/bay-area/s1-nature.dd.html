Section 01

Summit Peaks

Ten summit hikes spanning the full spectrum of Bay Area peak experiences, from a five-minute fire lookout stroll to an all-day 19.5-mile backcountry epic. Each peak offers a distinct character: Mission Peak's crowded sunrise culture, Diablo's 40-county views, St Helena's wine country panoramas, and Rose Peak's permit-required solitude.

Mission Peak Loop (6.2mi RT, 2,135ft, Strenuous)

The Bay Area's most iconic peak hike. A steep fire road climb rewards with 360-degree bay views from Fremont's signature summit. The famous pole at the top marks one of the region's most photographed points. Classic sunrise destination; arrive before dawn on weekends to beat the crowds.

The Experience

Steep, exposed fire road with no shade. Summer temperatures can exceed 90°F. Bring 2+ liters of water minimum. The final push is relentless but the summit panorama justifies every step.

Logistics

Stanford Avenue trailhead, Fremont. Limited parking fills by 7am on weekends. Alternative: Ohlone College trailhead (longer but more parking). Dogs permitted on-leash. No permits needed.

Mt Tamalpais East Peak — Plank Walk (0.5mi RT, 209ft, Easy)

Quick summit walk with fire lookout station. Panoramic views with minimal effort. The lookout station is staffed on weekends and offers interpretive information about the mountain's ecology and fire history.

Mt Tamalpais — Old Railroad Grade (13.8mi RT, 2,342ft, Hard)

Historic railroad route through coastal redwood forest. The "crookedest railroad in the world" operated from 1896 to 1930, carrying tourists to the summit. Sweeping vistas and a completely different experience from the Plank Walk approach. Rich history and stunning scenery make this an all-day classic.

Mount Diablo Summit Trail (7.4mi RT, 2,139ft, Hard)

Far-reaching views spanning 40 counties on clear days. Sierra Nevada mountains visible on exceptional visibility days. The summit visitor center provides geological and ecological context. Bay's most expansive viewpoint, often cited as having one of the largest visible areas from any peak in North America.

Visibility

On the clearest days (typically December–February after storms), views extend to the Sierra Nevada, Lassen Peak, and the Farallon Islands. Bring binoculars.

Route Options

Multiple approach trails: Mitchell Canyon (north), Juniper Trail (south), or drive to the summit and explore shorter loops. The full summit trail from base is most rewarding.

Mount St Helena Trail (9.9mi RT, 2,116ft, Hard)

Highest climbable peak in the Bay Area at 4,342 feet. Views of Napa and Sonoma wine country valleys, with the Sierra visible on clear days. Exposed ridge section with wildflowers in spring. Robert Louis Stevenson honeymooned nearby in 1880.

Rose Peak via Ohlone Trail (19.5mi RT, 5,000ft, Strenuous)

Remote backcountry experience to Alameda County's highest point (3,817 ft). Permit required from East Bay Regional Parks. Wildflower meadows and spectacular isolation. For experienced hikers only. Consider as a two-day backpack with camping at Sunol or Stewart's Camp.

Permit Required

Ohlone Wilderness Trail permit ($2) required. Purchase from East Bay Regional Parks District. The permit system keeps crowds minimal, preserving the backcountry character that makes this hike exceptional.

Black Mountain Loop (5.4mi RT, 1,000ft, Moderate)

South Bay vistas accessible from Monte Bello Open Space. Popular weekend destination with views stretching toward the coast. Connects to multiple other preserves for longer adventures.

Montara Mountain North Peak (7.3mi RT, 1,748ft, Moderate)

Coastal mountain with ocean-to-Diablo 360-degree views. Coastal bluffs and ridge walking with expansive panoramas. The trail traverses through McNee Ranch State Park, one of the most underrated parks on the Peninsula.

Sonoma Mountain Trail (8mi RT, 1,800ft, Hard)

Jack London State Historic Park. Beauty Ranch setting with old redwood groves and 60-mile views on clear days. Literary history adds cultural depth to the hiking experience.

Windy Hill Loop (7.4mi RT, 1,350ft, Hard)

Portola Valley. Fire road climb with Bay Area views, then wooded descent through mixed forest. Varied terrain and shaded return makes this a complete day hike experience.

Section 02

Ancient Redwoods

Seventeen trails through the Bay Area's ancient redwood groves, spanning five distinct park systems. From Muir Woods' accessible boardwalks to Portola's 1,200-year-old giants, these trails pass through ecosystems largely unchanged for millennia. Cathedral-like canopies, fern-carpeted forest floors, and the distinctive scent of coastal redwood bark define these experiences.

Muir Woods — Fern Creek Loop (1.3mi, 36ft, Easy)

Old-growth redwoods with fern-lined creek. Cathedral-like groves in a short, accessible walk. Perfect introduction to Muir Woods for first-time visitors. The loop follows Redwood Creek before climbing gently through a particularly impressive grove.

Main Trail — Redwood Creek (2.0mi, 30ft, Easy)

Flat terrain through cathedral-like old-growth redwoods. The most popular route in Muir Woods. Boardwalked sections are fully wheelchair accessible. Despite crowds, the scale of these ancient trees creates a notable sense of scale.

Canopy View Trail (3.5mi, 842ft, Moderate)

Rise above the redwood canopy for a unique perspective on these giants. Views down into the forest create exceptional photography opportunities. The elevation gain provides a workout absent from the valley-floor trails.

Bootjack Trail Loop (6.3mi, 1,150ft, Moderate)

Multi-trail loop combining old-growth redwoods with chaparral and manzanita. More rugged than main trails with fewer crowds. Connects Muir Woods to the broader Mt Tamalpais trail network.

Big Basin — Redwood Trail (0.7mi, 42ft, Easy)

Father and Mother of the Forest giants plus the historic Chimney Tree. Shortest trail to the most impressive specimens in Big Basin. These trees survived the 2020 CZU Lightning Complex fire, though the surrounding forest was heavily burned.

Post-Fire Recovery

Big Basin was devastated by the 2020 CZU fire. The old-growth redwoods survived but the park infrastructure was destroyed. Limited trails have reopened. Check California State Parks for current access and conditions before visiting.

Skyline to the Sea & Meteor Trail (4.8mi, 912ft, Moderate)

Creek crossings through dense redwood groves with views opening to the coast. Combination route offers variety and water features. Part of the larger 29-mile Skyline-to-the-Sea trail system.

Purisima Creek North Ridge (4.1mi, 1,184ft, Moderate)

Ocean views contrasting with old-growth redwoods. Less crowded than Muir Woods with coastal perspectives. The descent into the redwood canyon is dramatic—transitioning from sunny grassland to deep forest shade in minutes.

Whittemore Gulch & Harkins Ridge (6.6mi, 1,742ft, Moderate)

Pacific vistas and creek canyon walks through redwood corridors. Longer loop with variable terrain and water features. The most comprehensive Purisima experience.

Butano — Little Butano Creek Trail (1.5mi, 200ft, Easy)

Fern canyons with gentle terrain. Secluded redwood groves without crowds. Excellent for families seeking quiet forest immersion.

Butano — Jackson Flats Loop (6.4mi, 1,000ft, Moderate)

Diverse ecosystems from creeks to ridgetops. Wide variety of forest types and views in one comprehensive loop through one of the Peninsula's most undervisited parks.

Butano — Six Bridges Trail (2.2mi, 200ft, Easy)

Six creek crossings with historic bridges. Bat habitat with evening viewing opportunities. A magical creek canyon walk, especially in late afternoon light.

Armstrong Redwoods — Pioneer Nature Trail (1.5mi, minimal, Easy)

ADA accessible path through magnificent old-growth redwoods in Sonoma County. Accessible to everyone including wheelchair users. The Colonel Armstrong Tree (1,400 years old) is the park's centerpiece.

Armstrong Redwoods — Discovery Trail (1.5mi, minimal, Easy)

Self-guided interpretive trail with Braille features. Educational walk suitable for all abilities and ages. Pairs well with the Pioneer Trail for a complete Armstrong experience.

Portola Redwoods — Slate Creek Loop (5.4mi, 1,043ft, Moderate)

Creek crossings beneath the forest canopy. Good water features with redwood scenery and moderate challenge. Less visited than other Peninsula redwood parks.

Portola Redwoods — Peters Creek Loop (11.9mi, 1,030ft, Hard)

Home to 1,200-year-old redwoods on this longer loop. More remote than other redwood trails with ancient grove exploration. The most significant old-growth specimens on the Peninsula outside of Big Basin.

Henry Cowell — Cathedral Redwoods (4.0mi, variable, Moderate)

40-acre old-growth grove within the park. Dense grove with cathedral-like canopy and peaceful forest floor walks. Near Santa Cruz, making it a good complement to a coast visit.

Section 03

Cascading Waterfalls

Nine waterfall hikes spanning the full range of Bay Area water features, from easy family loops to strenuous backcountry epics. These are temporal experiences—they exist fully only November through April, when coastal storms deliver the precipitation that animates creeks. Timing a visit to coincide with peak flow significantly enhances the experience.

Cataract Falls Trail (2.9mi, 1,053ft, Hard)

The most dramatic waterfall hike in the Bay Area. A mile-long cascade drops through a narrow redwood gully on Mt Tamalpais. Water flows at full force only November through May; peak occurs late January and early March. The trail climbs steeply through old-growth forest with multiple vantage points along the cascade.

Timing

Visit 2–3 weeks after major storms for peak flow. June through October the cascade dries to a trickle. The difference between peak and off-season is dramatic.

Technical

Steep, rocky trail with potential creek crossings. Sturdy boots essential. Not suitable for young children or inexperienced hikers during high water.

Access

Fairfax–Bolinas Road trailhead. Very limited parking fills early on rainy-season weekends. Arrive before 8am or try weekday visits.

Ecosystem

Old-growth redwoods, Douglas fir, tanoak, and sword ferns. The steep gully creates a microclimate that supports species rarely seen elsewhere in Marin.

Nora Trail (3.7mi, 807ft, Moderate)

Brook waterfall with bay and ocean views. Less crowded than Cataract Falls with similar beauty and added vistas. A solid alternative when Cataract parking is full.

Alamere Falls (13.2mi, 1,784ft, Strenuous)

A rare tidefall—40-foot falls dropping directly onto Wildcat Beach in Point Reyes. Tide-dependent access to the falls base creates logistical complexity. All-day adventure through diverse Point Reyes terrain. One of only two tidefalls in California.

Tide Planning

The falls are accessible only at low tide. Check tide tables and plan arrival for 1–2 hours before low tide. Rising tides can trap hikers against the cliff. Never approach during storm swells.

Berry Creek Falls Loop (9–12mi, 2,100ft, Hard)

Four waterfalls including the 70-foot main cascade at Big Basin. Multiple drops and water features throughout. The most comprehensive waterfall experience in the Bay Area when water is flowing. Post-2020 fire access may be limited; check conditions.

Waterfall Loop — Uvas Canyon (1.5mi, 393ft, Easy)

Three waterfalls in a short accessible loop. Dog-friendly trail perfect for families. The easiest multi-waterfall experience in the Bay Area.

Uvas Waterfall & Contour Loop (2.7mi, 500ft, Moderate)

Four waterfalls along Swanson Creek. More comprehensive experience than the main loop with creek-side walking through oak woodland.

Triple Falls Trail (2.5mi, variable, Moderate)

Secluded triple waterfall off the main Uvas Canyon trails. Less visited with quiet canyon experience and stacked water features. Worth the short detour from the main loop.

Brooks Falls Loop (2.4mi, 639ft, Moderate)

175-foot seasonal falls with three distinct drops in San Pedro Valley, Pacifica. San Mateo County's tallest waterfall when flowing. Dramatic water display visible from multiple viewpoints along the trail.

Murietta Falls Trail (12.3mi, 4,229ft, Strenuous)

100-foot falls, the highest waterfall in the Bay Area. Remote Ohlone Wilderness access requires a full day and excellent fitness. Permit required. The falls flow only during wet season, making timing critical.

Why Go

  • Highest waterfall in the Bay Area
  • True wilderness solitude
  • Spectacular when flowing
  • Wildflower meadows en route

Considerations

  • 4,229ft elevation gain is serious
  • Permit required ($2)
  • Dry most of the year
  • 12+ mile round trip
Section 04

Seasonal Wildflowers

Seven routes through the Bay Area's most spectacular wildflower displays. The region's unique serpentine grasslands support endemic species found nowhere else on Earth, while coastal headlands and mountain meadows create seasonal color spectacles from March through May. Timing is everything—peak bloom lasts only 2–4 weeks at each location.

Chimney Rock Trail (1.8mi, 160ft, Easy)

Point Reyes coastal cliffs with peak bloom April through May. Whale watching season coincides with wildflower peak, creating a dual attraction. Douglas iris, lupine, and coastal paintbrush carpet the headlands above the Pacific. The lighthouse views add dramatic backdrop to the flower show.

Peak Bloom

April–May. Douglas iris dominates early; lupine and paintbrush take over in late April. Check Point Reyes wildflower reports for current conditions.

Wildlife Bonus

Gray whale migration (January–April) overlaps with early bloom. Elephant seals visible from nearby overlooks. Harbor seals in Drake's Bay.

Coyote Ridge (2.6mi, 1,000ft, Moderate)

Serpentine grasslands with rare endemic butterfly species. Requires Butterfly Pass permit (limited). Unique geology supports the Bay checkerspot butterfly and several plant species found only on serpentine soils. One of the Bay Area's most ecologically significant hikes.

Permit Required

Butterfly Pass permits are limited and required during bloom season. The serpentine habitat is extremely fragile. Stay on designated trails. No dogs permitted. The permit system protects one of the rarest ecosystems in North America.

Edgewood Trail Loop (3.3mi, 587ft, Moderate)

Serpentine grasslands in Redwood City with guided interpretive hikes March through May. Seasonal diversity with peak bloom in spring. Educational programs led by knowledgeable docents add depth to the visual experience. Over 400 plant species recorded in the park.

Mt Diablo via Mitchell Canyon (13.2mi, 3,529ft, Strenuous)

Endemic globe lilies and lupine meadows create a wildflower spectacle on the way to the summit. All-day summit hike with spring bloom rewards. The Mitchell Canyon entrance accesses the mountain's most botanically diverse face. Over 900 plant species recorded on Mt Diablo.

Sunol Regional Wilderness (6–19mi, up to 1,800ft, Moderate–Hard)

Little Yosemite gorge with creek views and grassland wildflowers. Variable length options from short walks to all-day adventures. The gorge is named for its Yosemite-like rock formations along Alameda Creek. Spring brings both water features and bloom.

Russian Ridge Loop (2.7mi, 465ft, Easy)

Poppies, lupine, and owl's clover create colorful spring meadows on the ridgeline above Skyline Boulevard. Ocean views from the ridge add dramatic perspective. Easy-access peak bloom destination requiring minimal effort for maximum reward.

Donner Canyon Falls Loop (5.4mi, 1,161ft, Moderate)

Waterfalls plus wildflowers plus colorful rock formations on Mt Diablo's north face. Complete mountain experience combining water, flowers, and geology. The only Bay Area hike that reliably delivers all three elements in a single outing during spring.

Wildflower Season Planning

Bay Area wildflower season depends on winter rainfall. Wet winters produce spectacular blooms 6–8 weeks later. Track conditions via CalFlora, iNaturalist, and park district social media. Peak bloom typically lasts only 2–4 weeks per location. Serpentine grasslands bloom later than coastal sites.
