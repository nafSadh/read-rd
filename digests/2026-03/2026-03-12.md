## Research Highlights

### Epistemic Gain, Aleatoric Cost: Uncertainty Decomposition in Multi-Agent Debate for Math Reasoning
**Category:** Research Paper | **Source:** arXiv

Presented at AAMAS 2026, this paper formalizes a critical distinction in multi-agent LLM reasoning: epistemic uncertainty (reducible through more information or debate) versus aleatoric uncertainty (irreducible variance inherent to the problem). The authors demonstrate empirically that multi-agent debate reliably resolves epistemic uncertainty in mathematical reasoning tasks, but can amplify aleatoric costs when agents confidently converge on wrong answers. The framework gives practitioners a principled basis for deciding when adding agents to a pipeline will improve outcomes and when it will add noise.

[Read more](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08102)

### Graph-Theoretic Agreement Framework for Multi-Agent LLM Systems
**Category:** Research Paper | **Source:** arXiv

This work applies graph theory to the problem of consensus in collections of LLM agents, formalizing how communication topology affects whether and how quickly a multi-agent system converges on a shared answer. The authors prove convergence conditions for several graph structures — including fully connected, ring, and hierarchical topologies — and show that sparse graphs with high clustering coefficients often outperform dense graphs due to reduced redundancy and lower context contamination. The results have direct implications for designing scalable multi-agent reasoning pipelines.

[Read more](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.00121)

## Agent & Tool Use

### Uncertainty Decomposition as a Design Tool for Ensemble Agent Architectures
**Category:** Research Paper | **Source:** arXiv

The practical value of the Epistemic Gain / Aleatoric Cost framework extends well beyond mathematical reasoning. For engineers building ensemble or debate-based agent architectures, the paper provides a decision rule: measure whether disagreement between agents is patterned (epistemic) or random (aleatoric) before adding debate rounds. Adding debate when uncertainty is primarily aleatoric increases latency and cost without improving accuracy, and in some cases degrades it. This is one of the few pieces of theoretical work in the multi-agent space that translates directly into actionable architectural guidance.

[Read more](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.08102)
