01

The MDB System

Architecture of Development Finance

The multilateral development bank system — World Bank Group, regional development banks (ADB, AfDB, IDB, EBRD, AIIB, NDB), and specialized institutions — constitutes the backbone of international climate finance. The 2023 Joint Report on MDB Climate Finance shows these institutions delivered $74.7 billion to low- and middle-income countries, up from $60.7 billion in 2022. An additional $50.3 billion went to high-income economies. Every MDB hit a record high for the year.

MDBs matter for climate finance for a specific reason: leverage. Every dollar of shareholder capital generates multiple dollars of lending through the banks' AAA credit ratings and ability to borrow cheaply on capital markets. The G20-commissioned Independent Expert Group estimates that reform could double or triple MDB overall finance — which is why the NCQG and the Baku-to-Belém Roadmap both assign MDBs a central role.

The Leverage Advantage

MDBs' unique value proposition is leverage. Through their AAA credit ratings, they can borrow at low rates and lend to developing countries at below-market terms. Each dollar of paid-in capital from shareholders generates roughly $2-4 of lending. This multiplier effect is why the WRI and other institutions see MDB reform as the most plausible pathway to scaling climate finance from $116B to $300B — reforms that change capital adequacy rules effectively create money without requiring new appropriations from national legislatures.

But the leverage also creates a structural bias toward lending over granting. MDB finance counts toward climate finance targets regardless of whether it's a grant, concessional loan, or near-market-rate loan. This incentivizes MDBs to maximize the volume of climate-tagged lending rather than ensuring the most effective use of scarce concessional resources — particularly problematic for adaptation in the poorest countries, where debt capacity is already stretched.

MDBs are the backbone of international climate finance because of leverage — each dollar of shareholder capital generates multiple dollars of lending. But that same leverage mechanism creates a structural bias toward loans over grants, deepening the debt burden of the countries they're meant to help.
02

The Evolution Roadmap

Banga's Reforms

When Ajay Banga replaced climate-skeptic David Malpass as World Bank President in 2023, it was widely seen as a reset. The Bank adopted a new mission — "a world free of poverty on a livable planet" — explicitly linking development and climate. At COP28, Banga announced the flagship commitment: 45% of annual financing devoted to climate-related projects by FY2025, up from 35%. He estimated this would "put to work more than $40 billion per year."

The WRI's assessment of the Evolution Roadmap notes additional reforms: climate-resilient debt clauses that pause interest payments for eligible countries hit by natural disasters, Paris alignment for all new operations, and a commitment to balance IBRD and IDA resources equally between mitigation and adaptation.

Other MDBs followed suit. The Inter-American Development Bank announced its own reform plan in March 2024. The Asian Development Bank raised its target to 50% climate finance by 2030. The collective signal was clear: MDBs were moving climate from the periphery to the center of their operations.

The Demand-Side Problem

But CGDev's analysis identifies a tension at the heart of MDB climate ambition: the World Bank is a demand-driven organization. When CGDev reviewed surveys of 43 client countries conducted in 2020-2021, climate change was ranked as a top priority by fewer than 6% of respondents. Many borrowing countries want the Bank to help with growth, infrastructure, health, and education — not all of which have climate components.

Country ownership is part of the Bank's DNA. For many major emitting countries, the World Bank is not and will not be a significant source of climate finance — China and India don't borrow from the Bank for their energy transitions. The 75 IDA-eligible countries account for only 3.6% of global emissions. This creates a fundamental tension: the countries the Bank serves most are not the ones driving emissions, and the high-ambition climate targets may divert resources from poverty-focused programs that lack any discernible climate link.

Banga has been persuasive about the link between development and climate. But the World Bank is demand-driven, and many client countries rank climate change well below other priorities. A 45% climate target in a demand-driven institution creates pressure to label everything as climate — which is exactly what the data shows happening.
03

The 45% Target: Real or Greenwash?

The Evidence

The Center for Global Development has produced the most rigorous independent assessment of what the World Bank's climate portfolio actually contains. Their scraping of project documents from FY23 and FY24 — replicating an earlier study of 2,554 projects from 2000-2022 — reveals a persistent pattern.

Of 925 projects that reported thematic areas, 825 were tagged as climate-related. That's 89% — far higher than the 45% target suggests. But when you look at the share of each project actually allocated to climate, the picture changes dramatically: 630 climate-tagged projects had less than 10% of their loan value directed to climate activities. The total climate-tagged spending was $55 billion out of $153 billion for the two years — roughly one-third, not 45%.

CGDev's conclusion is blunt: the pattern of "labeling a little bit of a lot of projects as climate-focused continues." The 45% target incentivizes broad, shallow tagging rather than deep, meaningful climate investment. The Bank launched a "climate outcome platform" at COP28 to track results rather than just inputs — but the timetable for implementation remains unclear.

The Rhetorical Retreat

Perhaps most telling: CGDev tracked the frequency of the word "climate" in Banga's official speeches. In 2024: 3.33 mentions per speech. In 2025 (through the date of analysis): 0.33 per speech — a 90% decline. This rhetorical shift isn't incidental. It reflects the reality that a climate-dedicated capital increase is "extremely slim given the current policy stance of the World Bank's largest shareholder." The US under Trump is not going to fund a climate-focused expansion of the Bank.

The 45% target has become a case study in Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. The incentive is to tag broadly and shallowly rather than invest deeply in genuine climate outcomes.
04

Green Climate Fund

The Flagship Climate Fund

The Green Climate Fund occupies a unique position: it's the largest operating entity of the UNFCCC Financial Mechanism, specifically mandated to support developing countries' climate action. At COP29, total pledges for the 2024-2027 programming cycle reached $13.6 billion from 34 countries and one region. Sweden's $763 million pledge was the standout contribution.

What distinguishes GCF from MDB climate finance is its 50/50 balance between adaptation and mitigation — a deliberate policy choice that MDBs have not replicated. GCF has invested $7.3 billion in adaptation, allocating at least half to the most vulnerable countries (SIDS, LDCs, African states) — currently 64% of the adaptation portfolio. It has also invested $1.6 billion in private-sector adaptation projects.

Scale vs. Need

GCF's ambition is real but its scale is tiny relative to need. The NCQG envisions tripling annual outflows from UNFCCC climate funds by 2030. Even tripled, the four multilateral climate funds (GCF, Adaptation Fund, CIF, GEF) would provide approximately $10 billion annually — less than 4% of the $300 billion target. These funds serve a critical catalytic function, especially for the most vulnerable countries, but they cannot be the primary vehicle for scaling climate finance.

GCF's third replenishment cycle kicks off in late 2027. Under the current US administration, no American contribution is expected. The GEF's 9th replenishment begins July 2026 with the same constraint. Whether other donors compensate — or whether these funds shrink — will be a key indicator of whether the post-US climate finance architecture can sustain itself.

Complementarity Efforts

At COP29, the four multilateral climate funds committed to strengthening complementarity and coherence — aligning access procedures, reducing fragmentation, and enabling a broader range of financiers to participate. This is a response to a persistent critique: that the landscape of climate funds is fragmented, duplicative, and difficult for developing countries to navigate. Whether this commitment translates into simpler access remains to be seen.

GCF is the only major multilateral fund that genuinely balances adaptation and mitigation 50/50. This makes it irreplaceable for vulnerable countries — but its total scale is less than 4% of the NCQG target. It's a proof of concept, not a solution at scale.
05

Capital Adequacy Reform

Unlocking the Balance Sheet

The most technically promising pathway to scaling MDB climate finance involves changing how MDBs manage their capital. The G20-commissioned Independent Review of MDB Capital Adequacy Frameworks, along with the Independent Expert Group on Strengthening MDBs, identified specific reforms: adjusting capital adequacy ratios to unlock more lending from existing capital, pioneering hybrid capital instruments, and leveraging callable capital more aggressively.

WRI estimates that if these reform proposals are fully implemented alongside capital increases and concessional window replenishments, MDB overall finance could double or triple. If MDBs then directed roughly half toward climate — consistent with current trajectory — MDB climate finance could reach approximately $195 billion per year. That would be a transformative contribution toward the $300B NCQG.

The Political Constraint

But balance sheet reform requires shareholder agreement, and major changes to capital adequacy rules or new capital injections require legislative approval in donor countries. The US is the World Bank's largest shareholder. Under the current administration, a climate-dedicated general capital increase is essentially off the table. The IDA21 replenishment — the concessional arm serving the poorest countries — faces the same headwind.

This creates a paradox: the most technically sound pathway to scaling climate finance (MDB reform) requires approval from a shareholder (the US) that has explicitly withdrawn from climate finance commitments. The question is whether other major shareholders — EU member states, Japan, UK, and potentially China — can drive reform forward despite US disengagement.

MDB capital adequacy reform is the closest thing to a free lunch in climate finance — unlocking more lending from existing capital without new appropriations. But it requires shareholder consensus that the US is currently blocking.
06

The Adaptation Shortfall

Mitigation Dominates

The MDB Joint Report reveals a stark imbalance. In high-income economies, 94% of MDB climate finance went to mitigation, with adaptation co-finance at just 3%. Even in low- and middle-income countries, where adaptation needs are most urgent, mitigation consistently outpaces adaptation.

The Paris Agreement states that "the provision of scaled-up financial resources should aim to achieve a balance between adaptation and mitigation." Some MDBs have tried: the World Bank committed to equal IBRD/IDA resources for both. GCF maintains its 50/50 split. The Asian Development Bank set an explicit adaptation target. But across the system, the structural bias remains.

Why Mitigation Wins

The adaptation shortfall is not arbitrary — it reflects structural features of development finance. Mitigation projects (solar farms, wind installations, grid upgrades) generate measurable, tradeable outcomes (tons of CO2 avoided). They attract private co-financing. They can be standardized across countries. Adaptation, by contrast, is context-specific (flood barriers in Bangladesh look nothing like drought resilience in the Sahel), harder to measure, and typically delivers public goods rather than financial returns. Private co-finance mobilization ratios for adaptation are far lower than for mitigation.

The result is a system that channels money toward reducing future emissions in middle-income countries — which is important — while chronically underinvesting in helping the poorest countries survive the climate impacts already locked in. The S8 deep-dive on the adaptation gap explores this dynamic in detail.

MDB adaptation finance lags mitigation not because adaptation matters less, but because mitigation is easier to measure, standardize, and attract private co-financing for. Fixing this requires deliberately redesigning incentive structures — not just setting targets.
07

Reform Without US Leadership

The Shareholder Problem

The US holds approximately 16% of World Bank voting shares — enough to block major structural changes that require supermajority approval. Japan (7.6%), Germany (4.2%), France (3.8%), and the UK (3.8%) are the next largest shareholders. China holds 5.7% following the 2018 capital increase.

Under the current administration, the US has withdrawn from Paris, canceled climate finance commitments, and signaled no appetite for climate-dedicated MDB reform. At the 2025 World Bank Annual Meetings, Big Shift Global reported there was "barely a whisper of climate during any of the flagship events." A decision on whether to resume gas exploration financing hangs in the balance.

Can Others Lead?

The Brookings analysis notes that the Independent High-Level Expert Group (IHLEG), co-chaired by Amar Bhattacharya, has been advancing the Baku-to-Belém Roadmap — but the political weight of this work depends on whether major shareholders translate it into governance decisions. European shareholders collectively hold enough votes to influence Bank direction, but they're simultaneously cutting their own ODA budgets. Japan has maintained relatively stable development finance but cannot single-handedly drive reform.

China's position is pivotal. It has increased its role in MDB governance through the AIIB (which it dominates) and the New Development Bank (BRICS bank). If China stepped more aggressively into World Bank reform — supporting capital increases, pushing for climate targets, facilitating Paris alignment — it could partially fill the US leadership vacuum. But China has historically preferred to build parallel institutions rather than reform Western-led ones.

The Fossil Fuel Test

Big Shift Global and civil society watchdogs have framed the test simply: are MDBs aligned with Paris or not? Despite commitments to Paris alignment, the World Bank is reportedly considering a return to gas exploration financing. Indirect financing through financial intermediaries may already be funding fossil fuel projects "through the backdoor." If MDBs claim climate leadership while continuing to finance fossil fuel infrastructure — even indirectly — the credibility of the entire MDB climate finance framework is undermined.

The MDB reform agenda was designed for a world where the US led. In 2025-2026, it must survive in a world where the US is absent. Whether Europe, Japan, and China can collectively drive reform — while simultaneously cutting their own climate finance budgets — is the open question at the heart of multilateral climate finance.
