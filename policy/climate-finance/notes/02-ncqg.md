# S2: The $100B Pledge & the NCQG

> The world's governments took fifteen years to triple an inadequate number, then celebrated it as a breakthrough while the people it was meant to serve called it an insult.

**Sources:** 8 (UNFCCC, IISD, UNCTAD, WRI, NRDC, CGDev, CPI, UN News)
**Adequacy:** YES

---

## 1. The Broken Promise: $100B/Year

The $100 billion per year target was born at Copenhagen in 2009, a political number conjured to save a failing summit rather than derived from any assessment of actual need. Developed countries pledged to mobilize $100B annually by 2020 for developing country climate action. They didn't meet it on time — the target was first reached in 2022, two years late, and only by counting loans, guarantees, and mobilized private finance alongside grants.

The accounting itself was contested. What "counts" as climate finance? Loans at near-market rates? Projects with tenuous climate connections? The CGDev study of 2,500+ World Bank projects found hundreds of climate-tagged activities with little actual climate relevance. The $100B was always as much an accounting exercise as a financial commitment.

## 2. The NCQG: $300B or $1.3T?

At COP29 in Baku (November 2024), after negotiations that ran into double overtime and featured walkouts by least developed countries and AOSIS, governments agreed the New Collective Quantified Goal:

- **Core target:** $300B/year by 2035, with developed countries "taking the lead"
- **Broader ambition:** All actors to scale up to $1.3T/year by 2035
- **Sources:** Public, private, bilateral, multilateral, plus "alternative sources"
- **New feature:** Developing countries "encouraged" to contribute voluntarily

The $300B is a tripling of the previous $100B goal — a framing developed countries preferred. But the Independent High-Level Expert Group (IHLEG) had recommended $390B from public sources alone. UNCTAD estimated the target should be $900B from 2025, rising to $1.46T by 2030. India called $300B a "paltry sum." SIDS representatives said they were "literally sinking."

## 3. The Baseline Math

How far is $300B from current reality? NRDC's analysis shows:
- 2022 baseline: $115.9B (under previous accounting)
- If all MDB climate finance counted (not just developed country share): $135.1B
- Annual growth rate needed: 6.3-8.4% depending on methodology
- MDBs committed $120B by 2030; bilateral gap ~$105B; multilateral funds ~$10B

CPI's Global Landscape shows total climate finance (all sources, not just international) hit $1.9T in 2023, exceeding $2T in 2024. Private finance exceeded $1T for the first time. But a stark disparity persists: emerging economies can't access affordable capital at scale.

## 4. The Baku-to-Belém Roadmap

The Roadmap is the operational bridge between the NCQG pledge and delivery. It calls for scaling up grants, concessional finance, and non-debt-creating instruments. It mandates the COP29/30 Presidencies (Azerbaijan and Brazil) to present a menu of options at COP30.

Key elements: MDB reform trajectories, bilateral scaling pathways, innovative sources (international taxes, SDR rechanneling), and private finance mobilization. The WRI analysis identifies specific pathways showing $300B is reachable if MDB reforms hold and bilateral contributions grow at historical rates — but this assumed continued US engagement.

## 5. Structural Flaws

CGDev's post-COP29 analysis identified critical shortcomings:
- No lessons learned from $100B (which incentivized lending, greenwashing, ODA diversion)
- No grant equivalent measurement — a dollar of loans counts the same as a dollar of grants
- No inflation adjustment — $300B in 2035 dollars is far less than $300B in 2024 dollars
- No adaptation subgoal — mitigation will likely dominate unless explicitly protected
- Developing country voluntary contributions risk diluting the $300B target rather than adding to it

## 6. What Sources Agree On
- $300B is insufficient for actual needs but represents real political progress
- The $1.3T broader target is closer to what's needed
- MDBs are the most plausible scaling mechanism
- Grant vs. loan distinction is critical and unresolved
- The Baku-to-Belém Roadmap is the key operational document

## 7. Where Sources Disagree
- **Whether $300B is achievable:** WRI/NRDC models show pathways; CGDev is skeptical given incentive structures
- **Whether developing country contributions help or dilute:** NRDC sees it as welcome broadening; CGDev warns it makes the $300B easier to hit on paper while reducing pressure on developed countries
- **Role of private finance:** CPI celebrates private crossing $1T; critics note private flows favor mitigation in middle-income countries, not adaptation in LDCs
- **China counting:** If China's $3.1B/yr is counted, it fills part of the gap but may disincentivize reporting
