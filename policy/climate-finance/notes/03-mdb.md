# S3: Multilateral Funds & MDB Reform

> The world's development banks sit at the center of climate finance — and at the center of a credibility crisis about whether "climate finance" means what it says.

**Sources:** 9 (WRI ×2, GCF, CGDev ×2, Big Shift Global ×2, MDB Joint Report, World Bank)
**Adequacy:** YES

---

## 1. The MDB System

Multilateral development banks are the largest channel for international climate finance, delivering $74.7 billion to low- and middle-income countries in 2023 — up from $60.7B in 2022. Every MDB hit a record high. The World Bank alone committed 45% of its financing to climate by FY2025. At COP29, MDBs collectively committed to $120B in climate finance for LMICs by 2030.

But MDB climate finance is overwhelmingly mitigation-focused. In high-income economies, 94% of MDB climate finance went to mitigation; adaptation co-finance was just 3%. Even in LMICs, the imbalance persists — adaptation requires context-specific, harder-to-measure interventions that don't attract private co-financing at scale.

## 2. The Greenwashing Question

CGDev's analysis of 2,500+ World Bank projects found hundreds of climate-tagged activities with little climate relevance — including teacher training in Guyana and civil service payment automation in Afghanistan. The pattern continues in FY23-24: 630 out of 925 climate-tagged projects had less than 10% of loan value actually allocated to climate. The 45% target creates "enormous pressure to tag financing with a climate benefit, no matter how tenuous the link."

Most damning: Banga's speeches mentioned "climate" 3.33 times per speech in 2024, dropping to 0.33 in 2025. The rhetorical retreat signals that a climate-dedicated capital increase is essentially off the table under the current US policy stance.

## 3. The Green Climate Fund

GCF is the largest UNFCCC operating entity. Pledges for its 2024-2027 programming cycle stand at $13.6B from 34 countries. Sweden alone pledged $763M at COP29. GCF maintains a 50/50 balance between adaptation and mitigation, with 64% of adaptation going to the most vulnerable countries. It has invested $7.3B in adaptation and $1.6B in private sector adaptation.

But GCF's scale is tiny relative to need. Even tripled by 2030 as the NCQG envisions, multilateral climate funds would provide ~$10B annually — less than 4% of the $300B target.

## 4. Capital Adequacy Reform

The G20-commissioned Independent Review of MDB Capital Adequacy Frameworks (CAF) proposed changes that could unlock significantly more lending capacity without new capital injections. If combined with shareholder capital increases and concessional window replenishments, MDB overall finance could double or triple, with climate finance potentially reaching ~$195B/yr.

But capital increases require shareholder approval — and the US, as the World Bank's largest shareholder, has signaled no appetite for this under the current administration.

## 5. What Sources Agree On
- MDBs are the most plausible scaling mechanism for reaching $300B
- Current climate tagging is unreliable and incentivizes greenwashing
- Adaptation finance lags mitigation within MDB portfolios
- Reform is underway but insufficient without new capital

## 6. Where Sources Disagree
- **Whether 45% is real:** WB frames it as ambitious commitment; CGDev shows it's inflated by tenuous tagging
- **MDB reform vs. transformation:** WRI sees reform trajectory as positive; Big Shift Global says MDBs "not fit for purpose" while still financing fossil fuels
- **Fossil fuel backdoor:** Big Shift warns of indirect fossil fuel financing through financial intermediaries; WB maintains Paris alignment
- **Who drives reform without US:** Some see EU/Japan stepping up; others doubt reform momentum can survive without largest shareholder
