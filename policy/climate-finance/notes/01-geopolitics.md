# S1: Geopolitics of Climate Finance

> The architecture of international climate finance is being reshaped less by climate science than by great-power competition, domestic political cycles, and the rise of new coalitions demanding a different deal.

**Sources:** 12 (E3G, WEF, Penn/PICO, Wellington, ODI, SEI, Heinrich Böll, Brookings, NPR, White & Case, Taylor & Francis, TRENDS Research)
**Adequacy:** YES

---

## 1. The US Withdrawal Cascade

Trump's January 2025 executive order didn't just restart the Paris withdrawal clock — it triggered a cascade of financial exits with no precedent in climate diplomacy. Within weeks, the State Department's climate negotiation office was dissolved. By March, the US had formally withdrawn from the FRLD board, canceled JETP pledges worth over $4 billion to South Africa, Indonesia, and Vietnam, and rescinded the International Climate Finance Plan. No US representative attended the June 2025 Bonn intersessionals — and delegates reportedly felt relief, since US participation under Trump would likely have been obstructionist (Heinrich Böll).

The financial void is deeper than the Paris withdrawal alone. The GEF 9th replenishment begins July 2026, GCF's third cycle kicks off late 2027, and the FRLD's first replenishment is slated for early 2028 — none will see American contributions under this administration. The US had pledged $50 million to the chronically underfunded Adaptation Fund; it never materialized.

But here's the compounding problem: other developed countries are not filling the gap. They're cutting too. ODA fell 9% in 2024 and OECD projects a further 9-17% drop in 2025. Germany alone is cutting $9.2 billion, France $2.6 billion, the UK $2.2 billion (Focus 2030). The US withdrawal has created political cover for austerity rather than catalyzing compensatory increases.

## 2. China's Strategic Ambiguity

China occupies the most consequential ambiguous position in climate finance. It is simultaneously the world's largest emitter, the world's largest deployer of renewable energy, and a major — but uncounted — provider of South-South climate finance. At COP29, China announced it had provided RMB 177 billion (~$24.5 billion) in climate finance to developing countries since 2016, averaging $3.1 billion per year (NRDC). But because the NCQG allows developing countries to voluntarily count their contributions, China can choose to report — or not — depending on what serves its negotiating position.

In October 2025, Beijing expanded export controls on rare earths and related technologies, including an extraterritorial licensing rule for foreign products containing even 0.1% Chinese-origin rare earth content (WEF). The materials enabling the green transition are also instruments of geopolitical leverage. The US, in turn, has imposed tariffs and technological protectionism that fragment the global clean energy supply chain.

The paradox, as the WEF Global Future Councils noted, is that rivalry between the superpowers has already accelerated clean energy manufacturing. Competition has catalyzed investment in critical minerals, reshored green industry, and sharpened focus on resilience. The question is whether this competitive acceleration can persist without cooperative guardrails — and whether the Global South gets squeezed between the two.

## 3. EU: Steady Rhetoric, Eroding Budgets

The EU has positioned itself as the "stay the course" actor. Commission President von der Leyen declared after the US withdrawal that Europe would "keep working with all nations that want to protect nature and stop global warming." EU Climate Commissioner Hoekstra and Council President Costa made similar statements. But rhetoric is outpacing resources. The EU's own member states are among those cutting bilateral aid most aggressively — France down, Germany's historic cuts, UK's sustained reductions.

At the same time, the EU is exploring Article 6 carbon credits to meet its 2040 climate target — a move Carbon Market Watch calls outsourcing emissions reduction rather than domestic action. The EU Green Deal Industrial Plan was partly a response to the US Inflation Reduction Act's subsidies, creating a three-way green industrial competition. But this competition is largely about domestic manufacturing advantage, not international climate finance.

## 4. Global South: Leverage Through Coalitions

The most significant structural shift is the Global South's growing capacity to shape outcomes rather than merely receive them. At COP29, India called the $300 billion NCQG a "paltry sum." SIDS representatives warned they were "literally sinking." Bolivia and Nigeria labeled it "insultingly low" (UN News). These are not just rhetorical moves — they represent coordinated negotiating blocs with the power to block or shape consensus.

South-South cooperation is emerging as a parallel track. Nations exporting renewable energy know-how and early-stage climate finance are shifting the narrative from dependency to capability (WEF). Regional experimentation — a Southeast Asian climate data grid, an East African soil-restoration corridor, a Latin American carbon credit alliance — can pilot governance models that later scale outward. The implication is that inclusive sustainability can survive amid geopolitical fragmentation, as long as new nodes of cooperation are nurtured.

The Bridgetown Initiative, championed by Barbados Prime Minister Mia Mottley, has pushed MDB and international financial architecture reform onto the G20 agenda. Debt-for-climate swaps are gaining traction. The political agency of climate-vulnerable states has never been higher, even as their material circumstances deteriorate.

## 5. Great-Power Competition Crowding Out Climate

E3G identifies a persistent pattern: geopolitical crises sideline climate action. In 2024 and 2025, the global agenda was dominated by Ukraine, Middle East conflicts, trade wars, and defense spending surges. Multiple countries raised defense budgets above 2% of GDP, with Germany proposing NATO target 5% (Wellington). This "crowding out" effect is structural, not temporary — as long as great-power competition remains the dominant frame, climate competes for attention and budgets.

The Penn/PICO Global Climate Trends Report captures the cascading dynamics: public debt exceeding 100% of GDP in 80+ countries, interest rates above 3% for six consecutive quarters, governments facing trade-offs between debt servicing and resilience investment. Climate finance doesn't operate in isolation — it competes with every other claim on shrinking fiscal space.

## 6. What Sources Agree On

- The US withdrawal is the single biggest disruption to the climate finance architecture since Paris
- Other donors are not compensating — they're also cutting
- China's role is pivotal but strategically ambiguous
- Global South coalitions are more organized and assertive than ever
- Geopolitical fragmentation is structural, not cyclical

## 7. Where Sources Disagree

- **US absence as net positive or negative:** One Taylor & Francis paper argues US participation has been destabilizing — repeated cycles consume diplomatic energy and constrain ambition. Most other sources treat withdrawal as unambiguously harmful.
- **Competition as accelerant:** WEF sees US-China rivalry driving clean tech investment; E3G sees it crowding out cooperative climate action. Both may be simultaneously true.
- **South-South capacity:** WEF and ODI are optimistic about regional experimentation replacing centralized frameworks. Wellington and TRENDS are more pessimistic, seeing fragmentation as primarily destructive.
- **EU credibility:** EU positions itself as the climate leader; civil society groups and data on ODA cuts suggest the gap between rhetoric and resource commitment is widening.
