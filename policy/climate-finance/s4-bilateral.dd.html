01

ODA in Freefall

The Numbers

The Focus 2030 analysis of 2024 final data documents a historic contraction. Total ODA from DAC countries fell to $212.1 billion, down 7.1% in real terms from 2023. The US remains the largest donor ($63.3B) but bilateral aid fell 4.7% globally. In-donor refugee costs — which count as ODA but never leave the donor country — accounted for $27.9B (13% of total).

The 2025 outlook is worse. Ten major European donors are cutting ODA by $18 billion compared to 2023: Germany ($9.2B), France ($2.6B), UK ($2.2B), and others. The OECD projects total ODA will drop 9-17% from 2024 to 2025. This comes atop the 2024 decline, meaning ODA could contract by 15-25% over two years — an unprecedented retreat.

The Heinrich Böll Foundation places this in climate finance context: most bilateral climate finance is drawn from ODA budgets. When ODA contracts, climate finance contracts with it — unless countries make explicit additional allocations, which none are doing.

ODA is contracting at the exact moment climate finance commitments are scaling up. The math doesn't work unless bilateral climate finance is genuinely additional to development budgets — and the evidence says it isn't.
02

Debt-for-Climate Swaps

The New Wave

Debt-for-nature and debt-for-climate swaps have experienced a renaissance since Belize's 2021 breakthrough. The CPI Primer documents The Nature Conservancy's Nature Bonds Program as the driving force: six deals across Seychelles (2016), Belize (2021), Barbados (2022), Gabon (2023), Bahamas (2024), and Ecuador (2024).

Ecuador's Galápagos swap was the world's largest: Credit Suisse helped the government buy back $1.6B of debt for $644M, saving roughly $1B in repayments over 17 years. In return, Ecuador committed $18M annually for 20 years to Galápagos marine conservation. A second Ecuador swap in December 2024 raised $450M for Amazon conservation — the first land-based TNC Nature Bond.

The ORF analysis places this in context: 58 of the most climate-vulnerable developing countries have nearly $500B in debt servicing due in four years. They spend 5× more on debt than climate action (projected 7× by 2025). Swaps address both crises simultaneously — but at a fraction of the scale needed.

Limitations

Swaps are complex, slow to negotiate, and niche. Barclays projects the market could reach $800B, but current deals total a few billion. The ECDPM notes the EU has been shy on commercial swaps, leaving leadership to the US (now withdrawn). Transparency concerns persist — Ecuador's Galápagos Life Fund was incorporated in Delaware, a low-tax jurisdiction, without disclosing its project portfolio.

Debt-for-climate swaps are the most promising innovation in bilateral climate finance — but they're a tool for countries with tradeable debt at a discount, not a systemic solution. They supplement grant finance; they cannot replace it.
03

JETP Cancellations

Dismantling the Partnerships

Just Energy Transition Partnerships (JETPs) were the Biden administration's flagship bilateral climate finance mechanism — multi-billion dollar packages negotiated with South Africa, Indonesia, Vietnam, India, and Senegal to accelerate their transition away from coal. The US pledged over $1B to South Africa and over $3B to Indonesia and Vietnam combined.

In March 2025, the Trump administration canceled all US financial participation. This wasn't just a funding cut — it unwound years of negotiated country platforms that integrated energy planning, workforce transition, and investment mobilization. The Heinrich Böll Foundation documents that the withdrawal cancels both grant projects and pledged financial commitments.

The JETP model represented a shift from project-based to systemic climate finance — whole-economy transition planning backed by coordinated bilateral and multilateral resources. Its cancellation signals that the US is not just withdrawing from climate pledges but from the institutional architecture that was meant to deliver them.

JETPs were not just money — they were negotiated frameworks for systemic energy transition in major emitting developing countries. Their cancellation destroys institutional architecture that took years to build and cannot be quickly replaced.
04

Bilateral Climate ODA

The Data

The OECD's climate development finance data shows bilateral climate-related ODA represented 35.6% of total allocable ODA in 2023-24, with mitigation-only activities dominating. Climate mitigation is more likely to be tagged as the principal objective, while adaptation is more often tagged as "significant" — a secondary consideration rather than the driving purpose.

Regional disparities are stark. In 2021-22, Africa received the least climate-related share of ODA (33%) despite being most vulnerable. Asia received 42%, Latin America 43%, Oceania 46%. In absolute terms, Africa was the largest ODA recipient ($47B) but received less climate finance ($15B) than Asia ($18B). The pattern reflects a structural preference for mitigation projects in middle-income countries over adaptation in the poorest.

Africa receives the largest total ODA but the lowest climate share — because mitigation dominates bilateral climate finance, and mitigation projects concentrate in middle-income countries with larger energy sectors. The poorest continent, most in need of adaptation, gets the least climate-specific support.
05

The Blurring Line

When Everything Is Climate

The OECD estimates that bilateral ODA spending on "global public goods" — primarily climate — has grown from 40% of bilateral ODA in 2008-12 to 64% in 2018-22. This reflects both genuine integration of climate into development and strategic relabeling of existing programs. The result is that climate finance and development finance are increasingly indistinguishable — which makes additionality assessment nearly impossible.

The CGDev argued for the NCQG to clarify the climate-development boundary and measure additionality through grant-equivalent terms. This didn't happen. The risk: as ODA shrinks, an ever-larger share is relabeled as climate finance, giving the appearance of climate ambition while actually reducing both development and climate resources.

When 64% of bilateral ODA is tagged as "global public goods" primarily due to climate, the concept of additionality becomes meaningless. Climate finance appears to grow while both development and climate resources actually shrink.
06

Innovative Instruments

Beyond Traditional ODA

Innovation in bilateral climate finance extends beyond debt swaps. The Germany-Egypt bilateral swap supports Egypt's NWFE-EP energy platform — a country-level transition plan backed by converted debt. IMF Special Drawing Rights rechanneling could provide non-debt-creating finance if politically feasible. Sustainability-linked sovereign bonds (SLBs) tie borrowing costs to environmental outcomes.

The UNDP estimates 20 countries are actively seeking debt-for-climate swaps. Lao PDR is working with UNDP to explore restructuring. The pipeline is growing, but execution requires credit enhancement from MDBs (typically covering ~70% of risk), experienced NGO intermediaries like TNC, and political will from creditors.

These instruments matter because they create fiscal space without new appropriations — a critical feature when legislatures won't approve additional ODA. But they're supplements, not substitutes. The NCQG framework acknowledges "alternative sources" but sets no specific targets for innovative instruments, leaving their contribution uncertain.

Innovative instruments — debt swaps, SDR rechanneling, SLBs — matter most when traditional ODA is contracting. They create fiscal space without new appropriations. But they're supplements for the countries that qualify, not a system-wide solution.
