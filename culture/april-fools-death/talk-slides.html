THE GOLDEN AGE 1

Once Upon a Time, We Could Lie Together

1957: The BBC tells Britain spaghetti grows on trees. Hundreds call in. Everyone laughs. Nobody files a complaint with Ofcom.

2004: Google launches Gmail on April 1st offering 1 GB of free email. The Guardian calls it a prank. It was real. 1.8 billion people use it today. [Komando]

2007: Google announces Gmail Paper — they’ll print your emails on “94% post-consumer organic soybean sputum.” This one was fake. [Wikipedia]

The point is: nobody could tell the real from the fake, and that was the fun. The trust container held. The ambiguity was delightful, not dangerous.

THE EULOGY 3

Google Stopped Pranking and Nobody Noticed

2000–2019: Twenty years of MentalPlex, Google Gulp, Google Romance, Gmail Paper. Beloved annual tradition.

2020: “Out of respect for COVID.” Fair enough.

2021: Still gone. Hmm.

2022–2025: …

2026: Google, Apple, Samsung all opt out. [Pocket-lint]

COVID was the excuse. The real reason: in a world of deepfakes and algorithmic outrage, a tech giant deliberately publishing fake information is no longer cute. It’s a liability.

THE ONION PROBLEM 4

When State Media Runs The Onion as News

2012: Iran’s Fars News runs an Onion article claiming rural Americans prefer Ahmadinejad to Obama. Word for word. As real news. [RationalWiki]

Same year: North Korea’s KCNA cites The Onion naming Kim Jong Un “Sexiest Man Alive.” China’s People’s Daily picks it up.

2017: White House Press Secretary Sean Spicer retweets an Onion video calling himself a provider of “robust and clearly articulated misinformation.” Apparently sincerely.

“Without a winking smiley, it is utterly impossible to parody a Creationist in such a way that someone won’t mistake it for the genuine article.”
— Nathan Poe, 2005 [Poe’s Law]

THE ALGORITHM 5

The Algorithm Doesn’t Know It’s April

Context collapse (Marwick & boyd, 2011): social media flattens audiences, strips date, source, tone, and intent. [SAGE]

A joke posted April 1st in a group chat → screenshot-shared on Twitter in November → now it’s misinformation.

Case study: A digitally altered image of an elephant carrying a lion cub posted as an AF prank in 2014. Still circulating on Facebook in 2024. Snopes debunked it. Didn’t matter. [NPR]

Today: A fake war lockdown message went viral this morning — April 1, 2026 — and caused real panic before anyone realized the date.

AI ENTERS THE CHAT 6

Gemini Will Fake Your Police Fine For Fun

Published Gemini prompts for April Fools’ 2026: [News24Online]

“Create a realistic message screenshot showing a traffic fine issued to me with official-looking formatting, government logo, and fine amount.”

“Edit this photo to make it look like I’m taking a selfie with a famous celebrity. Keep lighting realistic. Add slight blur for authenticity.”

Read those again. On April 2nd, these are instructions for fraud. On April 1st, they’re “fun.” The AI doesn’t know the difference. Neither does the person who receives the output.

THE THEORY 8

The Magic Circle Breaks

Bakhtin (1965): carnival is a licensed zone of transgression. Everyone agrees the rules are suspended. Everyone agrees when they come back. [Wikipedia]

Huizinga (1938): play creates a “magic circle” — a temporary world set apart from the ordinary one, with its own rules. [Homo Ludens]

April Fools’ is a magic circle bounded by time. For 24 hours, lies are play.

When misinformation makes every day a day of deception, the circle dissolves. There is no “inside” because there is no “outside.”

THE PUNCHLINE 9

What Actually Died

Not pranks. People still prank each other.

Not humor. But the internet made comedy faster and more disposable, not richer. Fawlty Towers needed shared context, patience, and trust in the audience. A meme needs three seconds and a thumb.

What died is the collective agreement to play together — the one day a year when an entire culture consented to a shared fiction and then laughed about it the next morning.

We lost the capacity to agree, even for 24 hours, on what counts as real — so we could enjoy, together, the brief pleasure of pretending otherwise.

The traditions that survive — French paper fish, Scottish sealed letters, a family freezing the breakfast cereal — are the ones that never went online. They run on eye contact and trust between people who know each other’s faces.
