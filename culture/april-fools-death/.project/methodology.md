# Methodology — Misinformation Ate April Fools

## Research Question
How did the rise of misinformation, post-truth politics, and synthetic media erode the cultural conditions that made April Fools' Day work — and what does that loss reveal about trust, play, and shared reality?

## Scope & Dimensions
Eight axes, each getting a one-page treatment in a unified deep-dive:

1. **Licensed Transgression** — Bakhtin's carnival, history of sanctioned deception
2. **The Trust Prerequisite** — pranks require a shared reality baseline to subvert
3. **The Corporate Retreat** — Google stopping in 2020, brand risk calculus
4. **Satire Collapse** — "the Onion problem," real headlines indistinguishable from parody
5. **Platform Mechanics** — algorithmic context-stripping (date, source, intent)
6. **Deepfakes & Synthetic Media** — when "just a prank" becomes dangerous
7. **Cultural Divergence** — is this a global or Western/anglophone phenomenon?
8. **The Mourning of Play** — what's lost when a culture can't safely pretend together

## Source Strategy
- Discourse survey: what scholars, journalists, and cultural critics are saying
- Web search for academic papers, longform journalism, cultural criticism
- Primary data: brand participation trends, Google's April Fools' timeline (2000–2019, stopped 2020)
- Cross-cultural sources for section 7

## Reading & Synthesis Approach
Discourse survey — synthesizing arguments, not reading individual papers at depth.

## Voice & Style
**Storytelling first.** Every section opens with a story, anecdote, or vivid scene — the BBC spaghetti harvest, the Google engineer who killed Gmail Paper, the Onion article that Iran ran as news. Theory and analysis follow from narrative, never the other way around. The tone is essayistic, slightly wry, intellectually serious but never dry. Think: a great longform piece in The Atlantic or a well-crafted newsletter essay. No academic hedge-speak.
