# LGROTG

It started as a typo.

Rafaela Souza, a 34-year-old transit planner in São Paulo, was trying to type "let's get rid of the gridlock" in a WhatsApp group about her commute. Autocorrect ate half the sentence. What came out was:

*lgrotg*

Her friend Tomás replied: *let's get rid of the governments?*

Rafaela sent a laughing emoji and went back to her spreadsheet. She didn't delete the message. She should have deleted the message.

---

Within 72 hours, LGROTG was a hashtag in eleven languages. Within a week, someone in Lagos had printed it on a t-shirt. Within a month, a political science professor at Sciences Po had published a paper titled "LGROTG: Memetic Nihilism or Post-Institutional Yearning?" which nobody read but everyone cited.

The strange thing was that nobody agreed on what it meant. In Seoul, LGROTG was a student protest chant against housing policy. In Berlin, anarchists spray-painted it on the Bundestag. In Ohio, a retired electrician named Gary put it on his lawn sign, and when a local reporter asked what it stood for, he said, "I don't know. But I feel it in my bones."

Rafaela watched all of this from her apartment with the quiet horror of someone who has accidentally started a fire by sneezing near a candle.

---

The first actual LGROTG happened in a town called Buen Retiro, population 9,200, in the Bolivian highlands. The mayor had been in office for nineteen years. He had won five elections, each with a larger margin than the last, which was suspicious only if you noticed that the ballot boxes were stored in his brother-in-law's garage.

The brother-in-law, Ramiro, was the more important figure. He held the municipal road maintenance contract, the market stall licensing fees, and a handshake agreement with the regional water authority that nobody had ever seen in writing. The mayor was the face. Ramiro was the skeleton.

On a Tuesday in March, 4,000 residents simply showed up at the municipal building and sat down. They didn't storm it. They didn't break anything. They brought food. They brought children. An elderly woman named Doña Carmen brought a folding chair and a thermos of api and said to the security guard, "We're not leaving until he does."

On Wednesday, Ramiro called the police. The police chief was the mayor's cousin — the family tree and the org chart were the same document in Buen Retiro. Twelve officers arrived. They arrested Carmen's nephew, Diego, a 22-year-old agronomy student whose specific crime was sitting on the front steps and declining to move. They put him in a truck and drove him to the station in Vallegrande, two hours away. Someone filmed it. The video was on Twitter by noon.

By Thursday, 6,000 people were in the square. People had come from Mataral, from Postrervalle, from towns that had their own mayors to resent. Somebody brought a generator and a projector and played the arrest video on the side of the municipal building on a loop. Doña Carmen, who had not moved from her chair in forty hours, looked at the growing crowd and said to nobody in particular, "This is more people than vote."

The mayor left on Friday. Not because he conceded. Because three of his own council members crossed the square, sat down next to Carmen, and asked her what they should do now. Ramiro held out longer. He threatened to cut the water. A woman named Estela, who worked at the treatment plant and had been quietly keeping it running for eleven years while Ramiro billed the municipality for "maintenance consulting," said: "Cut it with what? You don't know where the valves are." Ramiro left on Saturday. He took the filing cabinet with the contracts. Nobody cared. Estela knew the pipe routes from memory.

Diego came home from Vallegrande on Sunday with a bruised rib and a face that was already on murals in three countries. He didn't want to be on murals. He wanted to finish his degree.

---

What happened next in Buen Retiro was supposed to be chaos. The newspapers were ready for chaos. A town without a government would collapse — the water would stop, the roads would crack, the criminals would feast.

Instead, Doña Carmen called a meeting.

Two hundred people showed up. They met in the covered market because it was the only building big enough. Carmen stood on a crate and said, "Who here knows how the water treatment plant works?" Estela raised her hand. "Good," she said. "You're in charge of water. Who knows about the school budget?" A teacher named Yolanda raised her hand. "You're on it. Who wants to handle trash collection?" Silence. Carmen looked around. "Nobody? Okay, we'll take turns. Starting with my nephew."

Diego groaned. Everybody laughed. He took out the trash for two months with a bruised rib.

They called it a *cabildo abierto* — an open council, an old Spanish colonial term repurposed for something the colonies had never actually practiced. Anyone could attend. Anyone could speak. Decisions required a two-thirds majority of everyone present. Meetings happened on Sundays, in the market, after church or after sleeping in, depending on your disposition.

For five months, it worked beautifully. Then it almost died.

---

The Quispe family and the Mamani family had been arguing about a parcel of land near the new road for longer than anyone could remember. Under the mayor, it hadn't mattered — Ramiro had simply awarded the road easement to whichever family had paid him most recently, which meant both families hated each other *and* Ramiro, which was at least a unifying sentiment.

Now there was no Ramiro. The cabildo had to decide. And the cabildo split.

The Quispes had more people. The Mamanis had a better claim — a deed from 1974, hand-typed, which may or may not have been legitimate but which certainly existed. The Sunday meetings, which had been running at comfortable two-hour marathons, stretched to four, then five hours. People started leaving early. Then they started not coming at all. Attendance dropped from 300 to 90.

Yolanda, the teacher, stopped coming after a Quispe cousin called her a Mamani puppet in the market on a Wednesday. Estela kept the water running but refused to speak at meetings. Diego, who had become a kind of accidental secretary, told his aunt that maybe they should just hold an election and put someone in charge again.

Carmen, for the first time, didn't have an answer. She sat in her folding chair and said nothing for an entire Sunday meeting while two men shouted over each other about a property line that was, in the grand scheme of things, fourteen meters of scrubland.

The thing that saved Buen Retiro was not wisdom. It was embarrassment. Pilar — the doctoral student from La Paz who had arrived to study the town's collapse and instead studied its success — had become a semi-permanent fixture. She'd been there four months. She knew everyone. And on the worst Sunday, the one where the Quispe patriarch called the Mamani grandmother a thief and the Mamani grandmother threw a sandal at him, Pilar stood up and said: "You know the whole world is watching, right? There's a BBC documentary crew coming in two weeks."

This was true. It was also, as Pilar would later admit in her dissertation, a form of manipulation that she was not entirely comfortable with but which seemed preferable to watching the experiment implode over fourteen meters of scrubland.

They brought in a mediator. A retired judge from Cochabamba named Dr. Salazar who had no connection to either family and who charged nothing because he said, "I've been waiting my whole career to see a town that actually wants to solve its own problems." It took three Sundays. The compromise was ugly — a shared-use easement that neither family loved — but both families accepted it, publicly, in the market, in front of the BBC crew that had indeed arrived and was filming everything with the grave seriousness that the British bring to other people's experiments in democracy.

Attendance climbed back to 200. Never 300 again. But 200 who kept showing up.

---

Six months later, Pilar published her paper. Its title was: "Buen Retiro and the Paradox of Subtracted Government: How Removal Produced Participation." The section on the land dispute was the longest chapter. Her advisor wanted her to cut it because it "complicated the narrative." She refused. It got her tenure.

Rafaela read about it on Twitter, sitting in traffic on Marginal Pinheiros, and laughed so hard she missed her exit.

---

The second LGROTG happened in Reykjavík, which was fitting, because Icelanders had already crowdsourced a constitution once, in 2011, before their parliament quietly shelved it.

A software developer named Jón Sigurðsson — yes, the same name as the independence hero, which he was very tired of hearing about — built a platform he called *Þing*, after the ancient Norse parliament. It was absurdly simple: any citizen could propose a policy. If 5,000 people endorsed it within 30 days, it went to a public deliberation period. If it survived deliberation — meaning a supermajority still supported it after hearing structured opposition arguments — it became binding.

The first six proposals were uncontroversial. Renewable energy targets. A noise ordinance for downtown Reykjavík. Parental leave adjustments. The Althing — the oldest parliament in the world, as they will tell you within thirty seconds of meeting you — watched with the tolerant amusement of a parent watching a child play with a toy stove.

The seventh proposal was a fisheries quota reduction. And that's when the stove caught fire.

Nordvik Holdings — not the largest fishing company in Iceland, but the most politically connected — noticed the proposal three days before the endorsement threshold. Within 48 hours, 8,400 new accounts endorsed a counter-proposal that would *increase* quotas. The accounts had Icelandic names, Icelandic profile photos, and had been created between 2 and 4 AM on a Tuesday. Jón, who was a developer and therefore paranoid about exactly this kind of thing, noticed the pattern immediately. He flagged it publicly.

Nordvik's response was elegant in its shamelessness. They didn't deny creating the accounts. They denied that the accounts were fake. "These are concerned citizens," their spokesperson said, "exercising their democratic right to participate." When a journalist pointed out that 8,400 people — roughly 2.5% of Iceland's population — had apparently developed passionate opinions about fisheries policy at 3 AM on the same Tuesday, the spokesperson said, "Icelanders are early risers."

Jón pulled the counter-proposal. Nordvik called him an autocrat. An op-ed in *Morgunblaðið* accused him of "digital gatekeeping." A Twitter account called @RealÞing — which had the production values of a professional communications firm and the grassroots authenticity of a Super Bowl ad — posted daily criticisms of the platform's "elitism."

For two weeks, it worked. Support for *Þing* dropped. Jón's inbox filled with death threats, which in Iceland meant strongly worded emails with correct grammar.

Then he did the thing that nearly killed the platform and ultimately saved it: he required national ID verification. Kennitala. The ten-digit number every Icelander has from birth. Real identity, real person, one account.

Participation dropped by 40%. Nearly half the user base either couldn't be bothered with verification or, more tellingly, couldn't verify at all. Jón watched the numbers fall and told a reporter, "I'd rather have 60% of a real country than 100% of a fictional one," which was a good line that he'd been saving for three days and which he was nevertheless genuinely terrified to say out loud.

The fisheries proposal went back up. It passed in eleven days. Nordvik sued. The suit was thrown out on the grounds that *Þing* was a voluntary platform and the Althing hadn't actually adopted its proposals. Except — and this was the part that made the Althing nervous — the fishermen had. The boats were following *Þing*'s quotas, not parliament's. Not because anyone forced them, but because the fishermen had endorsed the proposal themselves and considered it more legitimate than the one a handful of parliamentarians had negotiated behind closed doors with, well, Nordvik.

By the following spring, the prime minister went on television and said, with the resigned smile of a man who has been outmaneuvered by his own citizenry, "Well. It appears the people have decided to govern themselves. I suppose that's what we always said we wanted." He paused. "I'd like the record to show that I am in favor of this, now that it's inevitable."

Nobody laughed. A few people clapped. Jón, watching from his apartment, thought: *this is either the best moment in Icelandic democracy or the moment before the worst one.* He went to bed and couldn't sleep.

The third LGROTG was in Kerala, which surprised no one who knew anything about Kerala.

A network of neighborhood assemblies — *kudumbashrees*, originally women's self-help groups — began handling local governance directly. They allocated budgets. They mediated disputes. They ran vaccination drives. They had been doing this, in various forms, since 1996. The internet just noticed.

Three months after the international attention arrived, so did the first scandal. A neighborhood treasurer in Thrissur was caught skimming funds — small amounts, 40,000 rupees over eight months, siphoned into a second account her husband used for a motorcycle repair business. In a state government, this would have taken years to surface and longer to resolve. The assembly caught it in three weeks. Not through an audit. Through a grandmother named Lakshmi who noticed that the monthly tea budget kept going up while the quality of the tea kept going down.

The treasurer was removed. The money was repaid. Lakshmi was offered the treasury role and declined on the grounds that she was 74, her eyesight was going, and she had "no patience for spreadsheets or thieves." The assembly gave her a ceremonial vote of thanks and appointed a 28-year-old accountant who was terrified of Lakshmi, which was, everyone agreed, exactly the right dynamic for fiscal oversight.

When the state government sent a bureaucrat to reassert control, the bureaucrat attended three assembly meetings, was given excellent tea — the quality had improved — and a thorough accounting of every rupee spent, including the stolen ones and their recovery. He went back to Thiruvananthapuram and filed a report recommending that the state government study the assemblies' accounting practices. His supervisor buried the report. Lakshmi, somehow, obtained a copy and read it aloud at the next meeting. Nobody asked how she got it. In Kerala, grandmothers have their sources.

---

The political scientists scrambled. This wasn't in the models. Backsliding was in the models. Polarization was in the models. Executive aggrandizement had its own Wikipedia page and three dedicated research institutes. But *subtraction*? A global movement where the governing impulse moved downward — from capitals to towns, from parliaments to market squares, from professional politicians to Doña Carmen with her thermos?

There was no theory for this. There were only thermoses.

The honest scholars noted, quietly, that it was also fragile. Buen Retiro had nearly collapsed over a land dispute. *Þing* had nearly been captured by a fishing company. The Kerala assemblies worked because Kerala had the literacy rate, the civic infrastructure, and the decades of practice — you couldn't airlift the model to Bihar and expect the same results. Every success story had a scar. Every scar had a lesson. And the lesson was never "this is easy." The lesson was: *this is possible, if you don't leave.*

---

Two years after the typo, Rafaela was invited to give a TED talk. She said no. Then she was invited to appear on a panel at the World Economic Forum in Davos. She said no. Then Doña Carmen called her on the phone — they'd never spoken, but Carmen had gotten her number from Pilar the doctoral student — and said, "Mija, come to Buen Retiro. See what you started."

Rafaela flew to Bolivia. She sat in the covered market on a Sunday and watched 200 people argue about whether to build a new footbridge over the Río Chico. The argument lasted two hours. It was passionate, specific, occasionally funny, and conducted entirely without reference to party affiliation, ideology, or national politics. A 16-year-old boy gave the most persuasive speech, using hand-drawn maps. The vote was 143 to 57. The bridge would be built.

She noticed things. The mural of Diego on the wall outside — he hadn't wanted it, but someone had painted it while he was in Vallegrande and he'd given up trying to have it removed. The word *TRAIDOR* spray-painted on the market's back wall during the land dispute, which nobody had painted over because Carmen said it should stay. "We should remember what we almost became," Carmen said. The Quispe patriarch and the Mamani grandmother, both present, sitting on opposite sides of the market, both voting on the bridge, not speaking to each other but not leaving either.

Afterward, Carmen poured her a cup of api and said, "You know what the secret is?"

"No."

"Nobody here is trying to be president." She paused. "Also, we almost failed. Twice. The second time, I thought it was over." She looked at the crowd dispersing into the afternoon, carrying their folding chairs. "The ones who stayed through the bad part — those are the ones who matter. Anyone can show up for the miracle. Staying for the argument about the scrubland, that's the thing."

---

Rafaela went home. She didn't write a book. She didn't start a movement. She went back to her transit spreadsheets and her overcrowded bus route and her WhatsApp group, where Tomás had recently typed "lgrotg" again, this time on purpose, about a parking regulation.

She sent back a single emoji: 🪑

Tomás understood. It meant: *bring a folding chair. And don't leave when it gets boring.*
