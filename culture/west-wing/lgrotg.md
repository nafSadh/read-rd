# LGROTG

It started as a typo.

Rafaela Souza, a 34-year-old transit planner in São Paulo, was trying to type "let's get rid of the gridlock" in a WhatsApp group about her commute. Autocorrect ate half the sentence. What came out was:

*lgrotg*

Her friend Tomás replied: *let's get rid of the governments?*

Rafaela sent a laughing emoji and went back to her spreadsheet. She didn't delete the message. She should have deleted the message.

---

Within 72 hours, LGROTG was a hashtag in eleven languages. Within a week, someone in Lagos had printed it on a t-shirt. Within a month, a political science professor at Sciences Po had published a paper titled "LGROTG: Memetic Nihilism or Post-Institutional Yearning?" which nobody read but everyone cited.

The strange thing was that nobody agreed on what it meant. In Seoul, LGROTG was a student protest chant against housing policy. In Berlin, anarchists spray-painted it on the Bundestag. In Ohio, a retired electrician named Gary put it on his lawn sign, and when a local reporter asked what it stood for, he said, "I don't know. But I feel it in my bones."

Rafaela watched all of this from her apartment with the quiet horror of someone who has accidentally started a fire by sneezing near a candle.

---

The first actual LGROTG happened in a town called Buen Retiro, population 9,200, in the Bolivian highlands. The mayor had been in office for nineteen years. He had won five elections, each with a larger margin than the last, which was suspicious only if you noticed that the ballot boxes were stored in his brother-in-law's garage.

On a Tuesday in March, 4,000 residents simply showed up at the municipal building and sat down. They didn't storm it. They didn't break anything. They brought food. They brought children. An elderly woman named Doña Carmen brought a folding chair and a thermos of api and said to the security guard, "We're not leaving until he does."

The mayor left on Thursday. Nobody replaced him.

---

What happened next in Buen Retiro was supposed to be chaos. The newspapers were ready for chaos. A town without a government would collapse — the water would stop, the roads would crack, the criminals would feast.

Instead, Doña Carmen called a meeting.

Two hundred people showed up. They met in the covered market because it was the only building big enough. Carmen stood on a crate and said, "Who here knows how the water treatment plant works?" A man named Esteban raised his hand. "Good," she said. "You're in charge of water. Who knows about the school budget?" A teacher named Yolanda raised her hand. "You're on it. Who wants to handle trash collection?" Silence. Carmen looked around. "Nobody? Okay, we'll take turns. Starting with my nephew."

Her nephew groaned. Everybody laughed.

They called it a *cabildo abierto* — an open council, an old Spanish colonial term repurposed for something the colonies had never actually practiced. Anyone could attend. Anyone could speak. Decisions required a two-thirds majority of everyone present. Meetings happened on Sundays, in the market, after church or after sleeping in, depending on your disposition.

---

Six months later, a doctoral student from the University of La Paz arrived in Buen Retiro to study its collapse. She found the water running, the school open, and the roads in better condition than before. She also found that trash collection had become the most hotly contested topic in town — not because nobody wanted to do it, but because three families were now competing for the contract, and the open council meetings about waste management had become genuinely riveting civic theater.

The doctoral student's name was Pilar. She was supposed to stay two weeks. She stayed four months. She wrote a paper that got her tenure. The paper's title was: "Buen Retiro and the Paradox of Subtracted Government: How Removal Produced Participation."

Rafaela read about it on Twitter, sitting in traffic on Marginal Pinheiros, and laughed so hard she missed her exit.

---

The second LGROTG happened in Reykjavík, which was fitting, because Icelanders had already crowdsourced a constitution once, in 2011, before their parliament quietly shelved it.

A software developer named Jón Sigurðsson — yes, the same name as the independence hero, which he was very tired of hearing about — built a platform he called *Þing*, after the ancient Norse parliament. It was absurdly simple: any citizen could propose a policy. If 5,000 people endorsed it within 30 days, it went to a public deliberation period. If it survived deliberation — meaning a supermajority still supported it after hearing structured opposition arguments — it became binding.

The Icelandic parliament, the Althing (the oldest in the world, as they will tell you within thirty seconds of meeting you), tried to ignore it. Then *Þing* passed a fisheries policy that was better than anything the Althing had produced in a decade, and the fishermen started following *Þing*'s rules instead of parliament's.

By the following spring, the prime minister went on television and said, with the resigned smile of a man who has been outmaneuvered by his own citizenry, "Well. It appears the people have decided to govern themselves. I suppose that's what we always said we wanted."

---

The third LGROTG was in Kerala, which surprised no one who knew anything about Kerala.

A network of neighborhood assemblies — *kudumbashrees*, originally women's self-help groups — began handling local governance directly. They allocated budgets. They mediated disputes. They ran vaccination drives. When the state government sent a bureaucrat to reassert control, the bureaucrat attended three assembly meetings, was given excellent tea and a thorough accounting of every rupee spent, and went back to Thiruvananthapuram to report that the assemblies were, annoyingly, doing a better job than his department.

Nobody called it LGROTG in Kerala. They called it *janakīya āsūtraṇam* — people's planning. It had been going on, in various forms, since 1996. The internet just noticed.

---

The political scientists scrambled. This wasn't in the models. Backsliding was in the models. Polarization was in the models. Executive aggrandizement had its own Wikipedia page and three dedicated research institutes. But *subtraction*? A global movement where the governing impulse moved downward — from capitals to towns, from parliaments to market squares, from professional politicians to Doña Carmen with her thermos?

There was no theory for this. There were only thermoses.

---

Two years after the typo, Rafaela was invited to give a TED talk. She said no. Then she was invited to appear on a panel at the World Economic Forum in Davos. She said no. Then Doña Carmen called her on the phone — they'd never spoken, but Carmen had gotten her number from Pilar the doctoral student — and said, "Mija, come to Buen Retiro. See what you started."

Rafaela flew to Bolivia. She sat in the covered market on a Sunday and watched 300 people argue about whether to build a new footbridge over the Río Chico. The argument lasted two hours. It was passionate, specific, occasionally funny, and conducted entirely without reference to party affiliation, ideology, or national politics. A 16-year-old boy gave the most persuasive speech, using hand-drawn maps. The vote was 212 to 88. The bridge would be built.

Afterward, Carmen poured her a cup of api and said, "You know what the secret is?"

"No."

"Nobody here is trying to be president."

---

Rafaela went home. She didn't write a book. She didn't start a movement. She went back to her transit spreadsheets and her overcrowded bus route and her WhatsApp group, where Tomás had recently typed "lgrotg" again, this time on purpose, about a parking regulation.

She sent back a single emoji: 🪑

Tomás understood. It meant: *bring a folding chair*.
