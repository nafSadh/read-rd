# The Folding Chair

Nobody elects the person who brings the chair.

That's the thing. In every occupation, every sit-in, every movement that begins with people refusing to leave, there is someone who arrives first. They don't arrive because they're ambitious. They arrive because they're organized, or angry, or they happen to live closest to the municipal building. They bring a folding chair because they have a bad knee, or because their mother taught them to always carry one, or because they are the kind of person who plans for things.

And then they don't leave. And the chair becomes a position.

---

Kostas Papadimitriou was sixty-seven years old, had forearms like anchor chain, and had built ships in the Skaramagas yards for forty years before his pension was cut in half by the second memorandum. He lived in Ano Poli, the old upper town of Thessaloniki, where the streets were too narrow for cars and the municipal government had, in the long tail of the debt crisis, simply stopped. The streetlights went dark in October. The trash stopped being collected in November. By January, a pipe burst on Odos Kleious and the city's response was a recorded message that said the department was experiencing "temporary service limitations," which had been the message since 2019.

Kostas organized a cleanup. Fifteen people, a Saturday morning, rubber gloves and trash bags. Afterward they sat in the plateia and someone said, "We should do this every week." Kostas brought a folding chair to the debrief because his knee was shot. He sat in it. He stayed.

By the third month, they had a name — the Ano Poli Assembly — and a meeting space: a shuttered kafeneío whose owner, an old friend of Kostas's, let them use it rent-free in exchange for maintaining the building. They had rerouted the burst pipe themselves. They had organized a rotating patrol after a string of burglaries. They were, in every practical sense, the government of a neighborhood the government had forgotten.

Kostas chaired every meeting. The word is unavoidable.

---

Here is how Kostas ran a meeting. Someone would propose something — a community garden on the vacant lot near the old wall, say. Kostas would listen. He'd nod. He'd wait for the person to finish. And then he'd ask three questions.

The questions were never hostile. They were practical, specific, and devastating. "Who's going to water it in August?" "Does the lot actually belong to the city or to the church?" "How many of us have grown anything besides opinions?"

The room would laugh. The proposer would falter. The proposal would die of natural causes on the floor. Kostas hadn't said no. He'd never said no to anything in his life. He'd just asked three questions, in a tone that suggested he genuinely wanted answers, and the absence of answers did the work.

People started calling this "the Kostas test." They meant it affectionately. They checked with him before proposing things. Not because he'd asked them to — he would have been appalled by the suggestion — but because they'd watched what happened when they didn't.

In the sixth month, a woman named Dimitra raised her hand.

Dimitra was thirty, an urban planner who'd moved to Ano Poli for the cheap rent and stayed for the assembly. She had the specific frustration of someone who'd studied governance academically and was now watching it happen in a kafeneío, recognizing every pattern she'd read about and unable to say so without sounding like a lecturer.

She said: "I think we should rotate the chair."

Kostas agreed immediately. "Of course," he said. "Who wants to go next week?"

Silence.

Not hostile silence. Not embarrassed silence. The silence of forty people who had watched Kostas prepare the agenda, manage the discussion, keep the minutes, lock the kafeneío at midnight, and mediate a dispute between two neighbors about a shared courtyard wall — all unpaid, all on his own time, all while his knee got worse. Nobody wanted that job. They wanted Kostas to keep doing it. They just also wanted to believe that nobody was in charge.

Dimitra chaired the following week. The meeting ran forty minutes over. Two agenda items were skipped. A man named Yorgos gave a twenty-minute speech about pigeons — specifically about the pigeons on his balcony, their habits, their apparent contempt for his laundry, and his proposal that the assembly allocate funds for netting. Kostas would have cut him off at three minutes. Dimitra couldn't figure out how to do it without being rude, because Yorgos was seventy-four and lonely and the meetings were, she suspected, the highlight of his week.

Dimitra chaired one more time, then stopped. "I don't have the bandwidth," she said, which was true and also not the whole truth.

Kostas was back in his chair the third week. Nobody discussed it. The agenda was tight. The meetings ended on time. Yorgos got three minutes for pigeons and seemed content.

---

Eight hundred kilometers south and four years earlier, a woman named Aminata Ndiaye fixed a water crisis in the Parcelles Assainies neighborhood of Dakar by organizing fifty women with folding chairs to sit in the lobby of the water authority until the taps came back on. It took three days. She brought a thermos of café Touba and a bag of fataya that her sister-in-law had fried that morning. She did not bring a manifesto.

The taps came back on. All seven days a week, for the first time in two years.

Aminata could have stopped there. She ran a phone repair shop on the main road — a folding table, a magnifying glass, a box of parts, and a reputation for honesty that had taken fifteen years to build. She had a business. She had three children. She did not need a second career in municipal governance.

She started a neighborhood council anyway. Saturday meetings, open to all, decisions by consensus. She kept the minutes in a notebook with a blue cover. She posted the agenda on the wall of her repair shop on Mondays so people could add items. She translated between Wolof and French when the older residents and the younger ones couldn't follow each other. She remembered what had been decided three weeks ago when no one else could.

She was the institutional memory of an institution that was supposed to have no center. This is the role that nobody applies for and nobody can replace: the person who remembers.

---

Eight months in, a man named Ousmane proposed using the community savings pool to repair a section of road near the school. The road was genuinely dangerous — two children had fallen from their bikes on the broken asphalt, one badly enough to need stitches. Twenty-three people voted for the repair. Nineteen voted against, preferring to save the money for a second water storage tank. Aminata had argued for the tank. Aminata lost.

The road was not repaired.

This is the part that matters, so read it slowly. Aminata did not block the vote. She did not overrule anyone. She did not stand up and say, "I've decided otherwise." What she did was smaller, quieter, and more complete.

The savings pool was in a bank account that Aminata had opened. Aminata was the sole signatory. This was not a power grab. It was a Tuesday afternoon when the account needed to be opened and Aminata was the one who went to the bank, because Aminata was always the one who went to the bank, the way she was always the one who kept the minutes and posted the agenda and translated between Wolof and French. Power accretes to the person who shows up on Tuesday.

She didn't refuse to release the funds. She said the bank needed additional paperwork. Then she said the paperwork was being processed. Then the rainy season came, and you can't pave a road in the rain, and by the time the ground dried the council had moved on to other things.

The water tank was built in November. It was the right decision. The city did cut the water again in December, and the tank kept the neighborhood supplied for nine days. Aminata had been right. She was right the way autocrats are right — about the outcome, not the process. The council had voted. The vote had not mattered. And nobody noticed except Ousmane, who stopped coming to meetings, and his wife, who still came but never spoke.

---

There is a pattern here. It has three stages. A political anthropologist in Accra named Kwame Asante described them in a paper that was widely cited in departments of political science and read by nobody in any of the movements it described, because the movements were too busy governing to read about governance.

The stages are not a theory. They are a time-lapse.

The first stage is the empty chair. Authority has collapsed or been removed. The space is open. This stage is euphoric and terrifying, like the first seconds of freefall before you remember whether you packed the parachute. It lasts between two weeks and three months.

The second stage is the working chair. Someone sits, because someone must. They sit because they are competent, willing, and physically present — the trinity of accidental power. They do not want to rule. They want to fix the pipe, chair the meeting, open the bank account. They do the work that no one else wants to do, and the work comes with a view of the room that no one else has, and the view becomes a vantage point, and the vantage point becomes a position. They may not notice this happening. They are too busy preparing the agenda.

The third stage is the permanent chair. The sitter has become load-bearing. Removing them would collapse the structure — not because they've engineered their own indispensability, but because nobody learned the job while they were doing it. The chair no longer folds. It does not fold up and go home at night. It is the room's fixed point, and the room orients around it the way iron filings orient to a magnet: not by command, but by field.

By stage three, the folding chair is a throne. The person in it may be kind. They may be wise. They may be Carmen with her thermos, or Kostas with his bad knee, or Aminata with her blue notebook. It doesn't matter. The geometry is the same. One person, one chair, one center. The rest of the room is audience.

---

The counterargument writes itself, and it's a good counterargument, so let's hear it out.

It says: *This is not authoritarianism.* Carmen is not Orbán. Kostas is not Erdoğan. A retired shipwright who chairs a neighborhood assembly is not a threat to democracy. He *is* democracy, in its most local and lived form. Comparing him to a strongman is offensive, reductive, and exactly the kind of false equivalence that makes political science useless to actual communities.

The counterargument is correct. Kostas is not Orbán. The comparison is absurd on its face.

But the question isn't whether Kostas is a tyrant. The question is what happens when Kostas dies.

---

Kostas didn't die. He had a heart attack, on a Thursday in March, in the kafeneío, during a meeting about parking. He was arguing that the vacant lot on Odos Kassandrou should remain open for elderly residents — specifically, for himself — when his left arm went numb and he slid out of his chair and Yorgos, the pigeon man, who had been a paramedic in 1974, caught him before he hit the floor.

He survived. He was in the hospital for two weeks. The assembly met once without him. The meeting lasted twenty minutes because nobody knew the agenda, and the agenda was in Kostas's notebook, and Kostas's notebook was in Kostas's coat pocket, and Kostas's coat was at the hospital.

They did not meet again until he came back.

Dimitra visited him in the hospital. She brought oranges and the sentence she'd been carrying for a year. She said: "We need a constitution."

Kostas, from his hospital bed, said: "We said we wouldn't have one."

Dimitra peeled an orange. "We said a lot of things."

---

They wrote rules. It took four Sundays. The rules were handwritten on butcher paper and taped to the wall of the kafeneío because Dimitra said, correctly, that rules nobody could see were rules nobody would follow.

Term limits for the facilitator: three months, no consecutive terms. A rotation schedule, posted on the wall. A treasurer who was not also the secretary. Written procedures for when the facilitator was absent, which was a polite way of saying "written procedures for when Kostas has another heart attack." Minutes kept in a shared notebook, not a personal one. The bank account — Dimitra added this, thinking of Aminata, whom she'd never heard of — required two signatories.

Kostas voted for all of it. He voted for it because he was a good man, because his heart attack had scared him in ways he couldn't articulate, and because, if he was honest with himself in the way that men of his generation rarely were, he was tired. He had been sitting in the chair for two years. His knee was worse. He wanted to go home at nine.

The assembly survived. It was less efficient. Meetings ran long. Facilitators who were not Kostas let Yorgos talk about pigeons for fifteen minutes. Agenda items were sometimes forgotten. One facilitator, a well-meaning 24-year-old named Stavros, accidentally scheduled two meetings on the same evening and spent both of them apologizing.

But it survived. It survived because the thing that kills movements is not incompetence. The thing that kills movements is the single point of failure that everyone pretends isn't there until the single point of failure is on the floor of a kafeneío with a numb left arm.

---

In Dakar, they did not write a constitution. Aminata is still in her chair. The water tank works. The road was eventually repaired — eighteen months late, with money from a different fund, after the original savings pool had been quietly redirected. Ousmane still doesn't come to meetings. His wife started coming again. She sits in the back.

The council functions. It functions well. When residents are asked, they say Aminata is doing a wonderful job. They are not wrong. She is doing a wonderful job. The chair doesn't fold anymore, but the water runs, and in Parcelles Assainies that counts for more than political theory.

---

There is a version of this story that ends in despair. It goes: all power concentrates. All chairs become thrones. All revolutions eat themselves. History is a wheel and the best you can hope for is a kind king with a good thermos.

That version is seductive and almost right, which is the most dangerous kind of wrong.

What it misses is the butcher paper.

Ano Poli wrote rules on the wall and survived Kostas's heart attack. Buen Retiro nearly died over scrubland and came back with 200 people who kept showing up. The pattern is not that the folding chair always becomes a throne. The pattern is that the chair *always tries* to become a throne, the way water always tries to flow downhill, and the question is whether anyone builds a dam before the valley floods.

The dam is boring. The dam is term limits and two signatories and a rotation schedule and long meetings where a 24-year-old facilitator accidentally schedules two events on the same night. The dam is not inspiring. Nobody spray-paints the dam on the side of a building. Nobody flies to Bolivia to see the dam.

But the dam is the difference between Ano Poli and Parcelles Assainies. Between a neighborhood that survived its founder's heart attack and one that can't survive its founder going on vacation.

The folding chair becomes a throne not through ambition but through exhaustion. Not through conspiracy but through competence. Not through malice but through the simple, structural fact that the person who does the work gets the power, and the person who gets the power does more work, and the cycle tightens until the chair and the person are the same object and neither one can fold.

The antidote is not vigilance. Vigilance is exhausting, and exhaustion is the disease.

The antidote is furniture. More chairs. Worse chairs, maybe — chairs that wobble, chairs held together with tape, chairs that let Yorgos talk too long about pigeons. But chairs that someone else is sitting in, doing the work badly, learning the job slowly, making mistakes that a room full of patient adults can absorb.

The price of distributed power is distributed incompetence. The price of distributed incompetence is long meetings. The price of long meetings is a civilization that might, against every structural incentive, actually govern itself.

It is not a good deal. It is the only deal on the table.

---

Dimitra, in Thessaloniki, was training a new facilitator. Eleni was twenty-two, nervous, and had just let Yorgos talk about pigeons for fifteen minutes while the room slowly lost the will to live. The meeting had ended twenty minutes late. Two agenda items had been deferred. Eleni was mortified.

"You let him go too long," Dimitra said. "Next time, cut him at five. He won't mind. He just likes to talk."

"Kostas would have cut him at three."

"Kostas was better at this than you."

Eleni looked at the chair — Kostas's old chair, the folding one with the torn seat that he'd brought from home two years ago and that nobody had replaced because replacing it felt, obscurely, like a betrayal. "Then why aren't we letting Kostas do it?"

Dimitra picked up the chair. She folded it. She leaned it against the wall.

"Because the whole point," she said, "is that it folds."
