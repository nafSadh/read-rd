I
§1 · Definition 3

The Slow Erosion

Democratic backsliding is the gradual weakening of democratic institutions by leaders who were democratically elected.

It doesn’t look like a military coup. It looks like legal actions — executive orders, judicial appointments, media regulations — that individually seem routine but cumulatively concentrate power.

Nancy Bermeo (2016) identifies the dominant modern pattern: executive aggrandizement — elected leaders weakening checks on their power one by one, through institutional changes that hamper opposition.

The key insight

Democracies rarely die overnight. They are dismantled in daylight, one norm at a time, by people who won elections.

II
§2 · V-Dem 2026 Report 5

The State of Democracy in 2025

The V-Dem Institute (Univ. of Gothenburg) produces the world’s largest democracy dataset: 202 countries, data from 1789, 4,200+ scholars.

Their 2026 report — titled “Unraveling the Democratic Era?” — finds that the gains of the third wave of democratization since the mid-1970s are almost entirely erased.

44 countries are currently autocratizing (home to 41% of world population — a record). Only 18 countries are democratizing (just 5% of population).

For the second year running, autocracies outnumber democracies globally: 92 to 87.

Nine straight years

International IDEA confirms: 2024 was the ninth consecutive year where more countries declined than improved.

§2 · The American Case 6

The U.S.: “Unprecedented” Speed

For the first time in over 50 years, the U.S. has been downgraded from “liberal democracy” to “electoral democracy” by V-Dem.

The Carnegie Endowment finds the pace of executive dominance “striking” compared to other backsliding cases. Trump’s team works to weaken checks across multiple levels simultaneously.

The Freedom in the World index (2026) gave the U.S. its lowest score (81/100) since the 100-point system began in 2002.

The Polity data series now labels the United States an anocracy — a regime between democracy and autocracy.

V-Dem’s Director

“The U.S. democracy is currently in a much faster deterioration process than any other democracy in modern times.” — Staffan I. Lindberg

III
§3 · The Playbook 8

Five Steps to Hollow a Democracy

1. Discredit the press. Label unfavorable coverage “fake news.” Create state-friendly alternatives. Defund public media.

2. Gut the judiciary. Pack courts with loyalists. Transfer independent judges. Ignore rulings you don’t like.

3. Weaponize prosecution. Investigate opponents. Pardon allies. Politicize law enforcement.

4. Erode electoral integrity. Gerrymander. Restrict voting access. Challenge results preemptively.

5. Normalize it. Do it openly. Do it fast. Make the outrageous routine. Exhaust the capacity for outrage.

Every step is individually defensible. “We’re just holding the media accountable.” “We’re just appointing qualified judges.” The pattern only emerges in aggregate.

§3 · Target #1 9

Freedom of Expression: The First Casualty

V-Dem finds that freedom of expression shows the most drastic global decline and is the most common target among autocratizing leaders over the past 25 years.

The RSF World Press Freedom Index (2025) classified the global situation as “difficult” for the first time in its history.

The pattern is consistent: first you attack the press, because controlling information is prerequisite to everything else. If citizens don’t know what’s happening, they can’t resist it.

In 2025, the AP was banned from the White House briefing room. The administration launched a “Media Bias Portal” with an “Offender Hall of Shame.” ICE agents physically attacked journalists.

Why expression first?

You can’t erode institutions in secret if someone is watching. Silencing the watchers comes first.

IV
§4 · Hungary 11

Hungary: The Template

Viktor Orbán, elected in 2010, rewrote Hungary’s constitution with his two-thirds parliamentary majority. He packed courts, captured media, redirected state advertising to allied outlets, and built what he openly calls an “illiberal democracy.”

During COVID-19, Orbán adopted emergency laws allowing government by decree indefinitely. He used the crisis to consolidate power further, drawing accusations of democratic backsliding on rule of law, corruption, and civil rights.

Hungary remains in the EU, holds elections, and maintains all the formal structures of democracy. The buildings are still there. The titles are still on the doors. But the substance has been hollowed out. V-Dem now classifies Hungary as an electoral autocracy.

Why Hungary matters

It proved that a European democracy with EU membership could be dismantled from within, legally, by a democratically elected leader — and that the international community would watch it happen.

§4 · Comparative 12

Same Playbook, Different Accents

Turkey: Erdoğan used a failed coup attempt (2016) to purge 150,000+ civil servants, judges, teachers. Independent media virtually eliminated.

India: Under Modi, India lost its V-Dem “democracy” classification. Press harassment, NGO restrictions, and religious polarization accelerated since 2014.

Brazil (Bolsonaro era): Attacked courts, mimicked Trump’s rhetoric, questioned election integrity. Supporters stormed government buildings in Jan 2023.

What connects them: all were democratically elected. All used legal mechanisms. All targeted the same institutions in roughly the same order.

Carnegie: “Common tactics include weakening the independent judiciary, politicizing prosecutorial power, undermining electoral fairness, censoring media, and co-opting private businesses.”

V
§5 · Democratizers 14

18 Countries Going the Other Way

Brazil is democratizing after Bolsonaro. Lula’s government is rebuilding institutions, though the process is fragile.

Poland reversed years of PiS-led backsliding after the 2023 election. Media independence and judicial reforms are being restored.

Botswana, Guatemala, and Mauritius are the three new democratizers identified in the 2025 V-Dem data.

In most democratizing countries, media freedom is improving — the inverse of the backsliding pattern, confirming that press freedom is the canary in the mine.

Pattern recognition

In 1975, Spain, Portugal, and Greece were all dictatorships. Within five years, all three were democracies. The Berlin Wall fell in a single night. Things that seem permanent aren’t.

§5 · What Works 15

What Resists Backsliding?

Strong institutions that can’t be captured by a single leader. Independent courts with tenure protections. Distributed power across levels of government.

Social trust — citizens who believe their neighbors are acting in good faith, even when they disagree. Stokes (Chicago) finds this is the hardest factor to rebuild once lost.

Political elites committed to norms. When politicians restrain themselves not because they must but because they understand the system depends on it.

Voter engagement. Research shows that when candidates violate democratic norms, significant numbers of voters switch away — but only if turnout is high enough for this to matter.

Press freedom. In every democratizing country in the V-Dem data, media freedom is improving. It is both an indicator and a cause.

Stokes’s formula

Effective resistance to democratic erosion requires three things simultaneously: strong institutions, social trust, and political elites committed to democratic norms.

CLOSING 16

The Temperature Keeps Climbing

Democratic backsliding is not a single event. It is a process — gradual, legal, and designed to look routine until it isn’t.

The hardest part is not the fixing. The hardest part is the noticing — recognizing the pattern while the individual steps still seem defensible.

The data is unambiguous: nine consecutive years of net democratic decline. The third wave of democratization nearly erased. Autocracies outnumbering democracies for the second year running.

But the data also shows recovery is possible. Poland reversed. Brazil is rebuilding. Botswana just started. The story has never been unidirectional.

The question

Democracy doesn’t die when someone takes it. It dies when not enough people notice it leaving.
